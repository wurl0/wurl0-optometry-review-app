## C6-01 — Management of Visual Disorders
*Personal study notes | TOS: C.6 | 150 items*

---

## The Simple Version

Management of visual disorders in optometry follows a fixed priority: fix the optics first, then address the muscle problem, then train the system, then refer for surgery if none of that works. This order matters because most binocular disorders are at least partly driven by uncorrected refractive error, and prism or therapy on top of a wrong prescription is treating the wrong thing.

The three main areas here are phorias (latent deviations), tropias (manifest deviations), and amblyopia (reduced VA not explainable by optics alone). Each has its own management ladder, but all three start at the same first rung: correct the refractive error.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "A child has esotropia. The parent wants surgery. What do you do first?" (Full cycloplegic refraction, because if the deviation is accommodative, the right glasses may straighten the eyes completely — and surgery on an uncorrected hyperope is treating the symptom, not the cause.)

---

## But Why?

**Why correct the refractive error before anything else?**
Uncorrected hyperopia drives accommodation, and accommodation drives convergence via the AC/A ratio. An eye that is over-accommodating will over-converge. Giving glasses removes the accommodation demand, which removes the convergence excess. Without that step, the full deviation cannot be assessed, and any prism or VT prescribed will be based on an inflated angle.

**Why does occlusion therapy work for amblyopia?**
The amblyopic eye is not blind — its retina and visual pathway are intact. The problem is cortical: the brain has learned to suppress the amblyopic eye's input because it is blurrier than the fellow eye. Covering the good eye forces the brain to use the suppressed eye, re-opening the cortical representation of that eye through use-dependent plasticity.

**Why does atropine penalization work?**
Atropine paralyzes accommodation in the good eye, making it blurry for near tasks. The amblyopic eye then provides clearer near vision and the brain is nudged toward using it. The mechanism is the same as patching (force use of the amblyopic eye) but without a patch — useful for children who remove patches or have patching-induced contact dermatitis.

**Why does simply correcting the refractive error sometimes resolve amblyopia on its own?**
In anisometropic amblyopia, the brain suppresses the eye with the worse image — not because of strabismus, but because of chronic image disparity. Providing the correct lens equalizes the retinal image quality. The brain no longer has a reason to suppress one eye, and cortical plasticity allows some recovery, especially during the sensitive period.

**Why is patching alone insufficient for amblyopia?**
Patching improves monocular VA by forcing use of the amblyopic eye, but it does not rewire the binocular cortical circuits that process binocularity and fusion. If the underlying strabismus or anisometropia is not addressed, the amblyopia returns when the patch is removed. VT combined with patching addresses both the monocular acuity deficit and the binocular dysfunction.

| Pleoptics | Greek *pleion* (more) + *ops* (eye/vision) | Technique using intense light stimulation of the non-foveal area to rehabilitate foveal fixation in amblyopia with eccentric fixation |
| Anisometropia | Greek *anisos* (unequal) + *metron* (measure) + *ops* | Clinically significant difference in refractive error between the two eyes |
| Aniseikonia | Greek *anisos* + *eikon* (image) | Unequal image size between the two eyes, causing binocular fusion difficulties |
| Isekonic / iseikonic lens | Greek *isos* (equal) + *eikon* | Lens designed to equalize retinal image size without altering optical power |

> Lookalike trap: Amblyopia ≠ suppression. Suppression is the active cortical inhibition that causes amblyopia. Amblyopia is the end result (reduced VA). A patient can have suppression without amblyopia (facultative suppression, no persisting effect). Amblyopia requires the suppression to be obligatory.

### How It Works — The Decision Framework

The management of any visual disorder follows the same four-rung ladder. The rungs must be climbed in order.

**Rung 1: Correct the refractive error**
- Always first
- Clears the retinal image
- Removes accommodative demand (reduces AC/A-driven convergence in CE and accommodative esotropia)
- In anisometropic amblyopia, may resolve the amblyopia partially or fully without patching
- Prism or VT prescribed before the refractive error is corrected is based on the wrong angle

**Rung 2: Prism prescription**
- Used when Sheard's or Percival's criterion is not met after optical correction
- Compensating prism: reduces the demand on fusional vergence without training it
- Direction: base opposite to the deviation tendency (exo → BI; eso → BO; hyperphoria → BD before the higher eye)
- Limitation: does not strengthen the vergence system; vergence ranges remain unchanged; symptoms may return if prism is discontinued

**Rung 3: Vision therapy (VT)**
- Trains the vergence system to expand the zone of clear single binocular vision (ZCSBV)
- Indicated for CI, FVD, accommodative insufficiency, and some CE cases
- More durable than prism because it builds actual vergence capacity
- For amblyopia: active therapy (patching, penalization, CAM, OVT, perceptual learning)
- Combines sensory and motor activities; addresses both monocular and binocular function

**Rung 4: Surgical referral**
- For large-angle constant tropias where optical and VT management is insufficient
- Accommodative esotropia: surgery is avoided if the full hyperopic Rx resolves the angle
- Large exotropia: surgery is the primary treatment when deviation is constant and large
- Always refer to ophthalmology

### Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

---

**Rule: Sheard's Criterion**

```
Compensating fusional vergence recovery ≥ 2 × phoria
If criterion is NOT met: patient is symptomatic
```

**What it states in plain language:**
The vergence reserve available to compensate for a phoria must be at least twice the size of the phoria. If your phoria uses more than half your available reserve, you will develop symptoms.

**What each part means:**
- Compensating fusional vergence = the vergence in the direction OPPOSITE to the deviation
 - For exophoria: the compensating vergence is the BI (negative) fusional vergence (how far BI you can push before diplopia)
 - For esophoria: the compensating vergence is the BO (positive) fusional vergence
- Recovery value (not break) is used as the measure of functional reserve — the point at which the patient can re-fuse, not the point at which they lose fusion
- Phoria = the size of the latent deviation (in Δ)

**Where it comes from:**
Charles Sheard (1920) observed that many symptomatic patients had normal phoria magnitudes but insufficient vergence reserves. He proposed the 2:1 ratio as a clinical threshold, reasoning that any demand requiring more than half the total reserve would be sustained at the margin, creating fatigue and symptoms.

**The analogy:**
Think of the phoria as a car constantly pulling left, and the fusional vergence as the force you apply to the steering wheel to keep it straight. If the pull is strong enough that you're already using more than half your steering strength just to maintain the lane, you'll fatigue quickly. If the pull only needs a quarter of your strength, you can sustain it comfortably all day.

**Why this form and not another:**
The 2:1 ratio sets a practical comfort margin. A ratio of 1:1 would mean the patient is always using 100% of reserve — technically possible but functionally exhausting. The 2:1 ratio leaves half the reserve as a safety buffer for visual tasks that increase demand (fatigue, illness, stress).

**What it does NOT explain:**
- Sheard's criterion does not apply to vertical phorias, where even small amounts cause significant symptoms
- It does not account for asymmetric vergence ranges (a patient with a large blur-to-break gap may have an unusual profile)
- When the ZCSBV is small and asymmetric, Percival's criterion may be more informative

**Cross-subject connections:**
- Binocular Vision (B5): Sheard's criterion is used alongside Morgan's norms to identify binocular vision disorders from OEP data
- VT (C7): The goal of VT for vergence disorders is to expand the recovery value until Sheard's is met and sustained

**Reading the Result:**

| Finding | Criterion | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Recovery ≥ 2× phoria | Criterion MET | Likely asymptomatic; monitor |
| Recovery < 2× phoria | Criterion NOT MET | Symptomatic; prescribe prism or VT |
| Prism to prescribe (Sheard's) | (2 × phoria − recovery) ÷ 3 | Minimum prism to bring patient into criterion |

---

**Rule: Percival's Criterion**

```
The demand point (phoria) must fall within the middle third of the zone of clear single binocular vision (ZCSBV)
Middle third = between 1/3 BO and 1/3 BI of the total fusional vergence range
```

**What it states in plain language:**
The patient's resting eye position (phoria) must sit in the comfortable center zone of their available vergence range. If it falls in the outer one-third of either the BO or BI side, they will be symptomatic.

**Where it comes from:**
Percival (1892) proposed that comfort is greatest when the visual demand falls in the middle of the available range — neither near the BO limit nor the BI limit. It is geometric rather than ratio-based (unlike Sheard's).

**When to use Percival's over Sheard's:**
Percival's is more useful when the ZCSBV is small or asymmetric — common in FVD patients where both BO and BI ranges are reduced. In these cases, a normal-sized phoria can still violate Percival's because the entire range is compressed.

**Prism to prescribe (Percival's):**
```
If demand is in the outer BO third: prescribe BI prism = (1/3 × BO break) − (2/3 × BI break)
If demand is in the outer BI third: prescribe BO prism = (1/3 × BI break) − (2/3 × BO break)
```

**Cross-subject connections:**
- OEP 21 Steps (C5): The vergence ranges (#16B, #17B) are the source data for applying both criteria

---

**Formula: Amplitude of Convergence**

```
Amplitude of Convergence = (NPD × 10) ÷ (NPC + 3)

Where:
 NPD = near pupillary distance in mm
 NPC = near point of convergence in cm
 +3 = correction for the distance from the spectacle plane to the center of rotation (≈ 27 mm → 3 cm)
 Result = in prism diopters (Δ)
```

**What it states in plain language:**
The maximum convergence the eyes can achieve, expressed in prism diopters, calculated from how close the eyes can converge (NPC) and how far apart they are (NPD).

**What each part means:**
- NPD: the interpupillary distance during near fixation (smaller than far PD because the eyes converge)
- NPC: how close the object gets before one eye breaks away (in cm, measured from the spectacle plane)
- +3: accounts for the distance between the spectacle plane and the center of rotation (~27 mm ≈ 3 cm); ensures the denominator represents the actual working distance from the ocular center of rotation, not the spectacle plane

**Reading the Result:**

| NPC value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 5 cm | Convergence excess (over-convergence) |
| 5–8 cm (accommodative target) | Normal per Scheiman and Wick (2008) |
| 6–10 cm (penlight target) | Normal range |
| > 10 cm | Convergence insufficiency |

### Comparisons That Matter

**Patching vs. atropine penalization:**

| Feature | Occlusion (patching) | Atropine penalization |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Physically blocks light from good eye | Paralyzes accommodation in good eye, blurring near vision |
| VA improvement | Effective for both distance and near in amblyopic eye | More effective for near tasks in amblyopic eye |
| Compliance | Requires child cooperation; patches can be removed | No patch needed; once applied, cannot easily be removed |
| Risk | Rebound amblyopia if over-patched | Pupil dilation, photophobia, cycloplegia in good eye |
| Board clue | Conventional = patch good eye; inverse = brief patch of amblyopic eye | "Pharmacologic penalization" on the board = atropine |

---

**Strabismic vs. anisometropic vs. deprivation amblyopia:**

| Feature | Strabismic | Anisometropic (refractive) | Deprivation (form-vision) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cause | Suppression from strabismus | Image quality disparity from unequal refraction | Physical obstruction of light (cataract, ptosis, corneal opacity) |
| Fixation | Often eccentric | Usually foveal | Varies; often eccentric |
| Amblyopia depth | Moderate to deep | Mild to deep | Most severe |
| Prognosis | Good if treated early | Good; refractive correction alone may resolve | Worst prognosis; must remove opacity urgently |
| First step | Correct RE; assess angle | Full refractive correction | Remove obstruction (surgery) THEN correct RE |
| Board clue | Suppression is the driver | Anisometropia ≥ 2D (Borish) is the risk threshold | Congenital cataract = treat immediately |

---

**Sheard's vs. Percival's:**

| Feature | Sheard's Criterion | Percival's Criterion |
|---|---|---|
| Rule | Compensating recovery ≥ 2× phoria | Phoria must fall within middle 1/3 of vergence range |
| Based on | Ratio (phoria vs. reserve) | Geometry (position within range) |
| Uses | Primary criterion for symptomatic phoria | Secondary; used when ZCSBV is small or asymmetric |
| Prism amount | (2 × phoria − recovery) ÷ 3 | Based on BO and BI break values |
| Board clue | "Sheard's" = 2× rule | "Percival's" = middle third of range |

### Memory Anchors

**The management order:**
"Refract → Prism → Train → Refer"
(Correct the optics → prescribe compensating prism → vision therapy → surgery)

**Sheard's in one sentence:**
"Reserve must be at least double the phoria. If not: prescribe (2Ph − R) ÷ 3 prism."

**Prism direction rules:**
- Exo → BI (base-in, because the eyes are drifting out and you're pointing them back in optically)
- Eso → BO (base-out, because the eyes are drifting in and you're correcting toward the outside)
- Hyperphoria → BD before the HIGHER eye (base-down on the eye that is riding up)

**Amblyopia types by first-step treatment:**
- Deprivation → Remove the opacity FIRST (then patch)
- Strabismic → Glasses FIRST (then patch; surgery for non-accommodative angle)
- Anisometropic → Glasses FIRST (then patch if VA gap remains after 8–12 weeks)

**Pleoptics = eccentric fixation only:**
Pleoptics is only indicated when the amblyopic eye has eccentric fixation. It is no longer the preferred method because gains in VA are inferior to conventional patching and it requires specialized, expensive equipment.

**CE mnemonic:**
CE = Convergence Excess = Correction first (full hyperopic Rx, then plus add)

## Board Traps

1. **Full hyperopic correction for accommodative esotropia, not under-correction** — the instinct is to give less plus to "avoid over-minusing," but for accommodative esotropia the full cycloplegic Rx is the treatment. Under-correcting the hyperopia leaves the accommodation demand in place, preserving the deviation.

2. **Never under-correct the amblyopic eye** — giving a reduced Rx to avoid "stressing" the amblyopic eye is wrong. The amblyopic eye needs the clearest possible retinal image. Under-correction perpetuates suppression.

3. **Surgery does not treat amblyopia** — surgery straightens the eyes (motor alignment) but does not restore VA. Amblyopia treatment (patching, penalization, VT) must still be done before or after surgery to restore acuity.

4. **Pleoptics is for eccentric fixation, not all amblyopia** — board stems that describe pleoptics as first-line for all amblyopia are wrong. It is specifically indicated for amblyopia with eccentric fixation, and even then its results are inferior to patching per the source.

5. **Alternating strabismus usually does not cause amblyopia** — because each eye gets used regularly, the brain does not persistently suppress one eye. Shallow amblyopia may occur in the more frequently deviating eye, but severe amblyopia is not the typical presentation.

6. **Sheard's uses the RECOVERY value, not the BREAK** — the recovery value represents the functional range the patient can sustain. The break value represents the absolute limit, which is not clinically useful for day-to-day functioning.

7. **Prism before non-dominant eye** — when prescribing split prism, the prism should be placed before the non-dominant eye when possible.

8. **DI vs. CN6 palsy** — divergence insufficiency (esophoria at far only) must be differentiated from sixth nerve palsy. DI is comitant; CN6 palsy is incomitant (worse in the field of the paretic muscle). Rule out CN6 palsy before prescribing BO prism for a patient with sudden-onset distance esophoria.

9. **Rebound amblyopia** — over-patching can cause the previously good eye to become amblyopic. The patch duration must be appropriate for age and depth of amblyopia. Follow-up schedules should check VA in both eyes at every visit.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 7-year-old presents with 20/20 OD and 20/60 OS. Examination shows 4 Δ esotropia OU with +3.50D hyperopia. Cycloplegic refraction confirms +3.50D OU. What is the most appropriate first management step?

- A. Patch the right eye for 6 hours/day
- B. Prescribe full cycloplegic correction (+3.50D) and reassess
- C. Refer immediately for strabismus surgery
- D. Prescribe BO prism to compensate for the esotropia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Prescribe full cycloplegic correction (+3.50D) and reassess.**

This is a classic accommodative esotropia presentation: esotropia with significant hyperopia. The uncorrected hyperopia drives accommodation, which drives convergence via the AC/A ratio, producing the esotropia. The first step is full hyperopic correction — the glasses may resolve the deviation completely. Patching (A) is premature before the refractive error is addressed; the amblyopia in OS may partially or fully resolve with correction alone. Surgery (C) is premature; surgery on an uncorrected accommodative esotropia treats the symptom without removing the cause. Prism (D) would compensate the deviation without removing the accommodative drive; the esotropia would persist when the prism is removed.

</details>

---

**2.** A patient has 8 Δ exophoria at near. BI recovery at near (#17B) is 10 Δ. The examiner wants to prescribe compensating prism. How much prism is needed according to Sheard's criterion, and in which direction?

- A. 2 Δ BI
- B. 2 Δ BO
- C. 4 Δ BI
- D. No prism needed; criterion is met

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**A. 2 Δ BI.**

Sheard's criterion: BI recovery (10 Δ) must be ≥ 2 × phoria (2 × 8 = 16 Δ). It is not met (10 < 16). Prism needed = (2 × 8 − 10) ÷ 3 = (16 − 10) ÷ 3 = 6 ÷ 3 = 2 Δ. Direction: exophoria → BI prism (base-in compensates for the outward deviation). B is wrong direction. C over-estimates the amount. D is incorrect because the criterion is clearly not met.

</details>
