## 1. Examination Sequence Overview

Primary care optometry follows one logical sequence. Each step informs the next — none are independent.

| Phase | Area | Key Tests |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Case History | Chief complaint, LOFDAR, POMR |
| 2 | Refraction | VA, retinoscopy, subjective refraction |
| 3 | Binocular / Accommodative Status | NPC, phorias, vergences, PRA/NRA |
| 4 | Ocular Health | External exam, slit lamp, ophthalmoscopy, IOP |

**Clinical Protocol:** Hand hygiene before and after every patient. Disinfect instruments between patients using 70% isopropyl alcohol or 0.5% sodium hypochlorite. IPAO/COVID-19 guidelines require PPE, health screening, and physical distancing.

## 2. Case History

### Definition and Purpose

Case history is the patient's account of their visual and ocular problems. It builds rapport, organizes exam direction, and generates initial impressions before any testing begins.

### 4 POMR Components (Weed, 1968–1969) — "CAMP"

| Letter | Component | Covers |
|---|---|---|
| C | Chief / Problem List | Complaints the patient experiences or observes |
| A | Assessment / Initial Treatment Plan | Provisional diagnosis and management plan |
| M | Medical Records (Defined Data Base) | Patient profile, history, medications |
| P | Progress Notes | Follow-up records and response to treatment |

### Essential Elements of Case History

1. Patient profile (name, age, address, gender, occupation, education)
2. Chief complaint — primary reason for visit
3. Visual and ocular history (present and past)
4. General health history
5. Medication usage and allergies
6. Family ocular and medical histories
7. Occupational and recreational visual requirements

**Clinical significance of patient profile fields:**

| Field | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Age | Influences diagnosis, prescription, treatment (e.g., presbyopia at 40+) |
| Address | Socio-economic status, disease prevalence in area |
| Gender | Disease predilection differs by sex |
| Occupation / Hobby | Determines lens type, amount of correction, disease predilection |
| Education | Guides communication style and IQ-appropriate explanations |

### Chief Complaint — Asthenopia

Asthenopia: uncomfortable, painful, irritable vision from any form of functional eyestrain.

Sources of ocular complaints:

| Source | Cause |
|---|---|
| Accommodative | Uncorrected refractive error |
| Motor | Muscular deficiency |
| Integrative | Combination of refractive error AND muscular deficiency |

Memory hook: **AMI** — Accommodative, Motor, Integrative.

### LOFDAR — Describing Any Complaint

Use LOFDAR to fully characterize any complaint during history:

- **L** — Location (laterality: right, left, both; where on the eye)
- **O** — Onset (sudden or gradual)
- **F** — Frequency (how often)
- **D** — Duration (how long each episode lasts)
- **A** — Alleviating / Aggravating factors
- **R** — Related / Associated signs and symptoms

### Headache Location and Refractive Cause

| Location | Most Likely Cause |
|---|---|
| Frontal | Hyperopia |
| Occipital | Presbyopia or convergence insufficiency |
| Temporal | Astigmatism |
| Parietal | Tension headache |

### Common Complaints and Probable Causes

| Complaint | Most Likely Cause | Important Note |
|---|---|---|
| Blurred vision | Uncorrected refractive error, cataract, diabetic retinopathy | Ask: when does it occur? |
| Headache | Uncorrected refractive error, stress, migraine | Location and triggers matter |
| Diplopia | Weak EOM, high astigmatism, uncorrected refractive error | Monocular vs. binocular |
| Photophobia | Albinotic / lightly pigmented eyes, retinal degeneration | Also: uveitis, corneal abrasion |
| Itching | Allergy, dry eye | Rule out seasonal pattern |
| Floaters | Usually physiologic; vitreous hemorrhage, retinal detachment | Sudden onset = urgent referral |

### Systemic Effects on Refractive State

| Blood Sugar | Lens Effect | Refractive Change |
|---|---|---|
| High (hyperglycemia) | Lens swells | MORE MYOPIC / less hyperopic |
| Low (hypoglycemia) | Lens shrinks | MORE HYPEROPIC / less myopic |

Memory rule: **HIGH sugar = INFLAMED lens = MORE MYOPIC** (focal point shifts anterior).

### Communication Skills — 8 Key Behaviors

1. Greet with a smile; introduce yourself before asking the patient's name
2. Use open-ended questions; move from general to specific
3. Active listening — attend to verbal and non-verbal cues
4. Silence — allow time to respond; avoid rushing
5. Empathy and warmth — treat the patient as a person, not a clinical problem
6. Sensitivity to psychosocial and personal issues
7. Clear and concise questions in layman's terms
8. Avoid repetition and redundancy (especially with children and elderly)

## 3. Visual Acuity

### Definition

Visual acuity (VA) is the clarity or sharpness of vision — the ability to discriminate the contour and detail of objects. From the Latin *acuitas* (sharpness). Dr. Herman Snellen (1834–1908), Dutch ophthalmologist, created the standard test chart.

### Forms of Visual Acuity — Lowest to Highest

| Form | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum Visible (Detection) | Recognizing a figure against its background | Detecting a dot on a blank field |
| Minimum Separable (Resolution) | Distinguishing two lines as separate; normally 5' of arc | Tumbling E, Landolt C — **Snellen standard** |
| Minimum Cognoscible / Legible (Recognition) | Threshold for identifying letters | Snellen chart, Sloan letters |
| Minimum Separable Angle | Smallest angle subtended at nodal point for two points seen as separate | Grating acuity |

Memory hook: **VSCS** — Visible, Separable, Cognoscible, Separable Angle.

**Board note:** Snellen acuity is most often classified as minimum separable. Some sources define it as minimum cognoscible.

### VA Terminology

- **Unaided VA** — vision without any correction; baseline data
- **Habitual VA** — vision with the patient's current correction
- **Optimal VA** — best corrected VA after full refraction
- **Pinhole VA** — acuity through a pinhole disc; bypasses refractive error

### Snellen Chart Design Standards

| Parameter | Standard |
|---|---|
| Background / contrast | Black letters on white; 80% contrast |
| Luminance at patient's eye | 20 foot-candles (20 fc) |
| Test distance | 20 feet (6 metres) |
| Letter stroke width | 1/5 of the 5-minute square |
| Letter spacing | 1/2 the width of the square |
| Row separation | 1/2 the size of the square |
| Minimum letters per row (20/50 and smaller) | 5 letters |
| Scoring stop point | Row where more than half the letters are missed |
| Easiest letter | L |
| Hardest letter | B |

Snellen chart construction formula: `Tan 5' = X/distance`. The basis is a square of 5' of arc in each dimension with letters formed in squares each subtending 1' of arc.

### Types of Snellen Charts

- Standard Snellen (letters)
- ETDRS (Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study) — gold standard for research
- Landolt C — resolution standard
- E-test (tumbling E) — for preliterate or non-verbal patients
- Bailey-Lovie — logarithmic spacing

### Snellen Notation Systems

| System | Format | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Imperial (English) | Feet | 20/20 |
| Metric | Metres | 6/6 |
| Decimal (International) | Decimal | 1.0 |
| LogMAR | Log of minimum angle of resolution | 0.0 |

Conversion: 20 feet = 6 metres. Decimal = numerator ÷ denominator. 20/50 = 0.4; 20/200 = 0.1.

### Expected VA for Uncorrected Refractive Error (Borish)

| Snellen VA | Uncorrected Sphere Error | Uncorrected Cylinder Error |
|---|---|---|
| 20/20 | < 0.25 D | < 0.25 D |
| 20/30 | 0.50 D | 1.00 D |
| 20/40 | 0.75 D | 1.50 D |
| 20/60 | 1.00 D | 2.00 D |
| 20/80 | 1.50 D | 3.00 D |
| 20/120 | 2.00 D | 4.00 D |
| 20/200 | 2.00–3.00 D | 5.00 D |

### Procedure: Distance VA

1. Patient at 20 feet (6 m) from chart under 20 fc illumination
2. Occlude the LEFT eye; test OD first
3. Patient reads from largest letter downward
4. Record the last row where more than half the letters are correct
5. Note any missed letters: 20/30-1 (missed one), 20/30+2 (read two extra)
6. Repeat for OS; then test OU last
7. Record: `OD 20/30-1 | OS 20/20 | OU 20/20`

### Subnormal Vision Techniques — In Order

| Step | Technique | Procedure | Recording |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Move patient closer | Move until they can read the largest letter; min 0.5 m | 20/200 at ___ ft |
| 2 | Counting Fingers (CF) | Expose fingers at ~1 ft | OD CF at 3 ft |
| 3 | Hand Motion (HM) | Ask if patient sees hand movement | OD HM at 4 ft |
| 4 | Light Projection (LP) | Penlight in 4 quadrants at 20 inches | OD LP |
| 5 | Light Perception (LP) | Direct penlight — can patient detect light? Monocular | OD LP / NLP |

### Factors Affecting VA

**Environmental:** Intensity of illumination, spectral nature of light, surrounding field intensity, region of retina stimulated, lateral illumination and glare, distance of test target.

**Ocular:** Lid pressure, tear film, pupil size (optimal 2–4 mm in light), presence of ametropia, retinal mosaic, clarity of optical media.

## 4. Preliminary Examination

### Purpose

Assesses visual abilities, ocular health, and related systemic health status — provides a complete initial view of the patient before refractive testing begins.

---

### 4.1 Interpupillary Distance (IPD / PD)

Distance between the centres of the entrance pupils. Also called Interocular Distance (IOD) or Pupillary Distance (PD). Recorded in millimetres with distance PD first (e.g., 64/61).

**Clinical significance:**
- Places optical centres of spectacle lenses accurately to avoid unwanted prismatic effect
- Errors in PD produce induced prism and cause eyestrain
- Distance PD is always larger than near PD

**Typical values:**
- Distance PD: 60–68 mm
- Near PD: 3 mm less than distance PD

**Methods:**
1. **Anatomical Method** — using a millimetre ruler
 - Close your RIGHT eye; patient fixates on your open LEFT eye
 - Align zero to the nasal border of patient's RIGHT pupil
 - Read at temporal border of patient's LEFT pupil
 - Close your LEFT eye; patient fixates your RIGHT eye (near PD)
2. **Catoptric Method** — using the corneal reflection of a penlight

**Near PD Formula:**

```
NPD = (DPD × WD) / (WD + 27)
```

The constant **27** = 13.5 mm (behind cornea) + 13.5 mm (to spectacle plane).

**Proportional formula:**
```
NPD = (DPD / WD) × (WD - 27)
```

**Indications for monocular PD measurement:**
- Unilateral strabismus
- Facial asymmetry
- High refractive error (horizontal tolerance: 2 mm; vertical tolerance: 1.2 mm)
- Multifocal dispensing

---

### 4.2 Near Point of Convergence (NPC)

The point where visual lines intersect at maximum convergence — measures the maximum convergence ability.

**Instrument:** RAF Rule (Royal Air Force) — primary instrument; target placed at 45°. Also: penlight, pencil, Wolff ball.

**Normal values (Scheiman and Wick, 2008):**

| Target Type | Break | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| Accommodative target | 5 cm ± 2.5 | 7 cm ± 4 |
| Penlight (non-accommodative) | 7 cm ± 4 | 10 cm ± 5 |

**Amplitude of Convergence Formula:**

```
Amplitude of Convergence = (NPD × 10) / (NPC + 3)
```

Example: NPD = 60, NPC = 7 → (60 × 10)/(7 + 3) = 600/10 = **60 prism dioptres**

---

### 4.3 Pupil Size Evaluation

| Condition | Typical Size |
|---|---|
| Room light | 4.0 mm |
| Near darkness | 6.5 mm |
| Direct light | 3.0 mm |

**Anisocoria:** Difference in pupil size between the two eyes.
- Physiologic anisocoria: <0.5 mm difference, normal in ~20% of population
- Pathologic: >1 mm, associated with CN III palsy, Horner's syndrome, Adie's tonic pupil

---

### 4.4 Fixation Test / Corneal Reflex Test (Hirschberg Method)

Determines the presence of strabismus. Light held at 40 cm. The corneal reflex in the normal eye is slightly nasal (0.5 mm nasal to pupil center) because the visual axis and optical axis are not coincident.

**Interpretation of reflex position:**

| Reflex Position | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Slightly nasal | No deviation (normal) |
| Extremely nasal | Exodeviation |
| Temporal | Esodeviation |

**Hirschberg quantitative equivalents:**

| Corneal Reflex at Pupil Edge | Prism Dioptres | Degrees |
|---|---|---|
| Pupil edge | ~10Δ | 5° |
| Mid-iris | ~30Δ | 15° |
| Limbus | ~60Δ | 30° |
| Beyond limbus | ~90Δ | 45° |

**Angle kappa:** The angle between the visual axis and the pupillary axis. A positive angle kappa gives the appearance of exotropia (pseudostrabismus).

---

### 4.5 Version Test / Ocular Motility

Tests conjugate eye movements using the 6 extraocular muscles. Uses H-pattern (6 diagnostic gaze positions plus primary position).

**Governing laws:**
- **Sherrington's Law of Reciprocal Innervation:** When an agonist contracts, the antagonist relaxes
- **Hering's Law of Equal Innervation:** Both eyes receive equal simultaneous innervation to yoke muscles

---

### 4.6 Pupillary Reflexes

**Direct Light Reflex (DLR):** Shining light into an eye causes that eye's pupil to constrict. Afferent: CN II; efferent: CN III.

**Indirect / Consensual Light Reflex (ILR):** Shining light into one eye causes the opposite eye's pupil to constrict. Tests the consensual pathway via the optic chiasm.

**Near / Accommodative Reflex (PNR):** Pupil constricts with near fixation. Part of the near triad: accommodation + convergence + miosis.

**Sample recording:** `DLR = 3+/3+ | ILR = 2+/2+ | PNR = 2+/2+ | PERRL (or PERLA)`

**Grading:**
- 4+ = brisk, very rapid constriction
- 3+ = normal, moderately rapid
- 2+ = sluggish
- 1+ = barely detectable
- 0 = absent

---

### 4.7 Swinging Flashlight Test (Marcus Gunn Pupil / RAPD)

Detects unilateral retinal or optic nerve anomalies anterior to the lateral geniculate nucleus. Rapid, inexpensive, reliable.

**Procedure:** Swing light alternately between eyes in a darkened room, allowing 1–2 seconds per eye.

**Positive test (Marcus Gunn Pupil / RAPD):** Paradoxical DILATION of the impaired eye when the light swings to it. This indicates an afferent pupillary defect — the affected eye's optic nerve transmits a weaker signal, so when the light moves to it, both pupils dilate relative to their state when the good eye was illuminated.

---

### 4.8 Color Vision Testing

**Types of color vision deficiency:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Monochromacy | Only one type of cone (or rod monochromacy — no cones) |
| Dichromacy | Two cone types functional; one absent |
| Anomalous Trichromacy | All three cone types present but one shifted in spectral sensitivity |

**Dichromacy types:**

| Type | Cone Absent | Color Axis |
|---|---|---|
| Protanopia | Red (L-cone) | Vertical |
| Deuteranopia | Green (M-cone) | Slant |
| Tritanopia | Blue (S-cone) | Horizontal |

**Anomalous trichromacy:** Protanomaly (red weak), Deuteranomaly (green weak, most common overall), Tritanomaly (blue weak).

**Acquired color vision deficiencies:**

| Lesion Location | Deficiency Type |
|---|---|
| Choroid | Yellow-blue (tritan) |
| Retina | Yellow-blue OR red-green |
| Optic nerve | Red-green |

#### Ishihara Pseudoisochromatic Plates (1917)

Tests red-green color deficiencies. Standard version: 38 plates.

| Plates | Type | Normal | Deficient |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plate 1 | Demonstration | Number | Same number (everyone sees this) |
| 2–7 | Transformation | Correct number | A different number |
| 8–13 | Vanishing | Number present | Cannot see the number |
| 14–15 | Hidden design | No number | A number appears (diagnostic) |
| 16–17 | Diagnostic severity | Varies | Determines severity |
| 18–24 | Tracing | Tracing path | For illiterate / children |

**Procedure:**
- Patient VA must be better than 6/60 (0.1)
- Good illumination (Macbeth Easel Lamp recommended)
- Test monocularly
- Hold chart 75 cm from patient, perpendicular to visual axis
- Allow 3–5 seconds per plate
- Patient may trace with a cotton tip if unable to identify numbers
- **Scoring:** 4 or fewer incorrect = normal; 5 or more incorrect = defective red-green color vision
- Example recording: 17/18 (normal); 10/18 (defective)

**Board trap:** Ishihara tests ONLY red-green deficiencies. It does NOT test blue (tritan) defects.

#### Hardy Rand Rittler (HRR) Test

Tests both red-green AND blue-yellow (tritan) deficiencies. More comprehensive than Ishihara.

#### Farnsworth D-15

- 16 color caps (one fixed reference)
- Tests for color confusion axes
- Failure criterion: **2 or more crossings involving caps at least 4 numbers apart**
- Color axes: Protan = vertical, Deutan = slant, Tritan = horizontal

#### Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue (FM-100)

- 85 color caps arranged in 4 trays
- Gold standard for mild CVD and acquired color deficiencies
- Tests the full color discrimination spectrum

---

### 4.9 Visual Field

The entire area seen when the eye is directed on a single point, including peripheral vision.

**Confrontation test:** Gross screening method comparing patient's field to examiner's.

#### Amsler Grid

- Held at **33 cm** from patient; patient wears habitual near correction
- Patient fixates the center dot and observes all 4 corners, line straightness, and missing or blurry squares
- Each small square subtends 1° of arc at 33 cm; full grid covers the central 10° (20° total diameter)

| Finding | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Metamorphopsia | Lines appear wavy or distorted |
| Macropsia | Squares appear larger than normal |
| Micropsia | Squares appear smaller than normal |
| Scotoma | Area of missing squares |

---

### 4.10 Near Point of Accommodation (NPA) — Amplitude of Accommodation

**Push-up technique:** Move a near target toward the eye until it first blurs.

```
Amplitude = 100 / NPA distance (in cm)
```

Example: NPA at 25 cm → 100/25 = **4.00 D**

**Hofstetter Formulas:**

| Type | Formula |
|---|---|
| Minimum | 15 − 0.25 × age |
| Average | 18.5 − 0.30 × age |
| Maximum | 25 − 0.40 × age |

Quick check at age 40: Minimum = 15 − (0.25 × 40) = **5.00 D**

**Donders Table (key values):**

| Age | Expected Amplitude |
|---|---|
| 10 | 14.0 D |
| 20 | 10.0 D |
| 30 | 7.0 D |
| 40 | 4.5 D |
| 50 | 2.5 D |
| 60 | 1.0 D |

---

### 4.11 Dominant Eye Test

Tests perceptual or motor superiority of one eye.

- **Ring Test:** Patient fixates examiner's nose through a ring formed by their fingers
- **Hole-in-a-Card Test:** Patient sights through a small hole in a card
- **Box Test:** Patient aligns two pipe cleaners

---

### 4.12 Amblyopia

Current definition: unilateral or bilateral decrease of visual acuity caused by pattern vision deprivation or abnormal binocular interaction for which no obvious cause can be found by physical examination and which cannot be corrected by optical or surgical means, but is reversible by therapeutic measures.

Criteria: difference of >2 lines between two eyes, OR VA <6/9.

## 5. External Ocular Health Examination

### 7 Rules for Recognizing Healthy Eyes

1. The cornea should be clear
2. The pupil should be black
3. The sclera should be white
4. The eyelids should open and close properly
5. The eyelashes should point away from the eye
6. Children's eyes should appear straight
7. The vision should be normal

### Areas Assessed in External Exam

Facial expressions and symmetry, skin color and texture, hair color and texture, gross eye movements and position, head position (a tilt or turn may indicate EOM compromise), eyelid position.

---

### Corneal Anomalies

**Corneal opacity classification:**

| Type | Description | Effect on Vision |
|---|---|---|
| Nebula | Slight haze; seen only by oblique illumination | Seldom obstructs vision |
| Macula | Moderately dense, circumscribed whitish opacity | May reduce VA |
| Leukoma | Opaque white spot | Significant VA loss |

**Keratoconus:** Progressive ectatic disorder causing central or paracentral corneal thinning and anterior protrusion. Characteristic Munson's sign (V-shaped indentation of lower lid on downgaze). On retinoscopy: scissors motion.

---

### Conjunctival Anomalies — Conjunctivitis Types

| Type | Features |
|---|---|
| Allergic | Bilateral; itching; papillary reaction; watery/ropy discharge; chemosis |
| Viral | Watery discharge; follicular reaction; pre-auricular lymphadenopathy |
| Bacterial | Mucopurulent discharge; papillary reaction; morning crusting |
| Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (GPC) | Associated with contact lens wear; giant papillae (>1 mm) on upper tarsal conjunctiva |
| Ophthalmia Neonatorum | Neonatal conjunctivitis; within 30 days of birth; gonorrheal (day 1–5), chlamydial (days 5–14) |

---

### Eyelid Anomalies

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Ptosis | Drooping of upper eyelid; can be neurogenic (CN III palsy, Horner's) or myogenic |
| Hordeolum | Acute staphylococcal infection; internal (meibomian gland) or external (Zeis/Moll gland) |
| Chalazion | Chronic lipogranuloma of meibomian gland; painless, firm nodule |
| Blepharitis | Chronic inflammation of eyelid margins; anterior (staphylococcal, seborrheic) or posterior (meibomian gland dysfunction) |
| Lagophthalmos | Inability to fully close the eyelid; exposes cornea to drying and abrasion |
| Ectropion | Outward turning of eyelid margin |
| Entropion | Inward turning of eyelid margin; lashes may contact globe |

### Eyelash Anomalies

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Trichiasis | Misdirected lashes growing inward; rubs against cornea |
| Distichiasis | Accessory row of lashes from meibomian gland orifices |
| Poliosis | Localized depigmentation of lashes; associated with Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada, sympathetic ophthalmia |

---

### Iris Anomalies

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Aniridia | Congenital absence or rudimentary iris; associated with Wilms' tumor (PAX6 gene) |
| Iris coloboma | Keyhole-shaped defect, usually inferonasal; failure of fetal fissure closure |
| Heterochromia | Difference in iris color between two eyes (binocular) or within one iris (sectoral) |
| Iris nevus | Flat, pigmented lesion on iris; benign but monitor for growth |

---

### Scleral Anomalies

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Staphyloma | Ectatic bulging of sclera lined by uveal tissue; common in high myopia |
| Episcleritis | Inflammation of episcleral tissue; sectoral or diffuse; self-limiting |
| Scleritis | Deeper inflammation of sclera; painful; associated with autoimmune disease |

---

### Palpation Test for IOP

Gross assessment of intraocular pressure; compares one eye to the other — NOT a replacement for tonometry.

**Procedure:**
1. Instruct patient to look down and close both eyes
2. Place forefingers of each hand on the upper scleral portion under the orbital ridge
3. Alternately apply gentle downward pressure with each finger
4. Examiner closes own eyes or looks away to avoid visual bias on tactile judgment
5. Record: hard / firm / soft

## 6. Objective Refraction — Retinoscopy

### Definition

Objective method of determining the refractive state without relying on patient responses. Also called: skiascopy, keratoscopy, shadow test, skiametry, pupilloscopy.

**Principle (Foucault's method):** The refractive state is determined by locating the eye's conjugate foci in space with a light source at a known point. The fundus reflex appears to be located in the plane of the patient's pupil.

---

### Types of Retinoscopes

| Type | Light Source | Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| Spot retinoscope | Round spot of light | Simple |
| Streak retinoscope | Linear filament (streak) | Preferred for astigmatism; can confirm motion by rotating streak |

**Important:** The streak orientation is always **perpendicular to the meridian being scoped**.

---

### Types of Retinoscopy

| Type | Fixation Distance | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Static | 6 metres (infinity) | Determines refractive state at distance |
| Dynamic | Near (40 cm or working distance) | Determines accommodative lag at near |

---

### Retinoscopic Reflexes

| Reflex | Movement | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| With-motion | Reflex moves in SAME direction as streak | Focal point behind examiner (hyperopia / low myopia) | Add PLUS lens |
| Against-motion | Reflex moves in OPPOSITE direction | Focal point between patient and examiner (myopia > working distance) | Add MINUS lens |
| Neutral | Pupil lights up completely; no movement | Conjugate point = examiner's eye plane | NEUTRALIZED — subtract working distance |

**Strategy:** Always convert against-motion to with-motion first, then approach neutrality from the with-motion direction.

---

### Working Distance

| Working Distance | Working Distance Lens to Subtract |
|---|---|
| 50 cm | +2.00 D |
| 67 cm | +1.50 D |
| 1 metre | +1.00 D |

```
Net finding = Gross finding − Working Distance Lens
```

Example: Gross = +2.00 D, WD = 50 cm (+2.00 D) → Net = **Plano**

---

### Procedure: Static Retinoscopy — Spherical Method

1. Patient fixates a 6-metre target with non-tested eye
2. Examiner works at chosen working distance (usually 50 cm)
3. Shine streak across pupil; observe reflex motion
4. If against-motion: add minus sphere until with-motion
5. Add plus in +0.25 D steps until pupil lights uniformly (neutral reflex)
6. Record gross sphere power at neutrality
7. Repeat across perpendicular meridian if astigmatism suspected
8. Compute net finding by subtracting WD lens from gross
9. Measure VA with net retinoscopy finding in place

---

### Sphero-Cylinder Method

1. Neutralize the **least myopic (most hyperopic) meridian first** using sphere
2. Neutralize the second meridian using minus cylinder added to the sphere
3. The streak is oriented perpendicular to the meridian being scoped

**Worked example:**
- At 180°: with-motion neutralized with +3.00 D
- At 90°: against-motion → add −0.75 cyl x 90 until neutral
- Gross: +3.00 sph / −0.75 cyl x 90
- Net (WD = 50 cm, −2.00 D): **+1.00 sph / −0.75 cyl x 90**

---

### Astigmatism — Special Phenomena (Streak Retinoscopy)

| Phenomenon | Description |
|---|---|
| Break | Reflex not continuous with the streak on the patient's face |
| Thickness | Reflex thickness varies as streak rotates 360° |
| Skew | Reflex moves in a different direction than the streak (not aligned with principal meridian) |
| Scissors | Reflex appears to split in opposite directions — indicates **irregular astigmatism** |

**No skew phenomenon** occurs with purely spherical refractive error.

---

### Accuracy Errors in Retinoscopy

- Incorrect working distance (most common)
- Scoping off the patient's visual axis
- Failure of patient to fixate the distance target (accommodation interferes)
- Failure to obtain a reversal (confirmation of neutrality)
- Failure to locate the principal meridians correctly
- Missing scissors motion (indicates irregular astigmatism)

---

### Dynamic Retinoscopy Methods

Used at near, typically at 40 cm (unless otherwise stated). **No working distance correction applied.**

#### MEM (Monocular Estimate Method) — Harold Haynes

Measures the accommodative lag at near working distance. Pre-cut plus lenses (in 0.25 D increments) are briefly held before the eye; the power that eliminates the reflex movement indicates the lag.

- Normal lag: **+0.50 D to +0.75 D** (the eye under-accommodates slightly at near)

#### Nott Method

Target held at working distance; examiner moves back or forward until neutral reflex. No lens added.

#### OEP Method — Skeffington (Test #5)

Patient fixates a 20/40 letter or larger at working distance (typically 40 cm). Lag and lead of accommodation are noted with the habitual correction in place.

#### Sheard's Method

Examiner notes the retinoscopic reflex at two different working distances.

#### Tait's Method

Working distance: 13 inches or 16 inches (33 cm).
- Formula: **PEX = En − Ed**; **AC = TC − PEX**
- TC at 13" = 18Δ; TC at 16" = 15Δ

#### OEP Dynamic Retinoscopy Net Calculations

```
Net #4 = Gross #4 − Working Distance (WD in dioptres)
Net #5 = Gross #5 − Lag
 (Modified if AA < 5.00D: Modified Lag = Lag × AA/5)
Net #6 = Gross #6 − Lag
Reading Add = Net #5 − Net #4
```

## 8. Subjective Refraction

Technique of comparing lenses using changes in vision to arrive at the dioptric lens combination that results in maximum visual acuity. Uses the net retinoscopy finding as the starting point.

### 4 Basic Steps

1. Find the best sphere (fogging + defogging)
2. Determine the cylindrical component (axis and power)
3. Sphere refinement (Duochrome / bichrome test)
4. Binocular balancing

### Methods of Relaxing Accommodation

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| Fogging | Most common non-cycloplegic method; add plus to blur, relaxing accommodation |
| Cycloplegia | Pharmacologic paralysis of ciliary muscle; dilated pupil |
| Cyclodamia | Non-cycloplegic fogging technique |

---

### Step 1: Fogging Technique

Add enough plus to reduce VA to approximately **20/60 to 20/80**, relaxing accommodation. The patient sees blurred but not completely dark.

**Procedure:**
1. Occlude left eye; present Snellen chart
2. Ask patient to fixate the largest letter (E)
3. Add plus in +0.25 D steps until VA reduces to ~20/60 to 20/80
4. This is the fog — accommodation is now relaxed
5. Begin defogging

---

### Step 2: Defogging — Best Sphere

Reduce the fogging lens in −0.25 D steps (decrease plus for hyperopia; increase minus for myopia) until the patient achieves best VA without going past maximum plus / minimum minus. This is the MPMVA — maximum plus / minimum minus for best visual acuity.

---

### Step 3a: Cylinder Axis — Two Methods

| Method | When to Use | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Rotation Method | Cylinder power **above 0.75 D** | Rotate axis clockwise then counterclockwise until blur; average of two blur positions = refined axis |
| Crossed Cylinder Method | Cylinder power **below 0.75 D** | Handle of cross-cylinder placed parallel to tentative axis; flip; rotate BOTH cross-cylinder and axis toward RED DOT until equality |

---

### Step 3b: Cylinder Power — Cross-Cylinder Method

The red dot of the cross-cylinder is placed parallel to the refined axis.

| Patient Prefers | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Position with RED dot parallel to axis | Cylinder is too WEAK | INCREASE minus cylinder power |
| Position with WHITE dot parallel to axis | Cylinder is too STRONG | DECREASE minus cylinder power |
| Both positions equal | Cylinder correct | No change |

Memory: **Red dot = add cylinder** (red = stop = needs MORE). **White dot = reduce cylinder**.

**Cylinder power interpretation using the clock-face target (Maddox clock test):**

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Original black line stays black after adding minus cyl | Cylinder correction too WEAK — add more |
| Original blurred line becomes blackest with minus cyl | Cylinder correction too STRONG — reduce |
| Blurred line does NOT darken with adding minus | IMPROPER FOG — re-fog and restart |
| Increasing minus produces no reversal; original black line returns | IRREGULAR astigmatism — use weakest cylinder that best equalizes |

---

### Step 3c: Sphere Refinement — Duochrome / Bichrome Test

Based on chromatic aberration: the eye focuses different wavelengths at different depths.

- **Green** (short wavelength) focuses **in front of** the retina
- **Red** (long wavelength) focuses **behind** the retina
- **Yellow** focuses exactly **on** the retina in emmetropia

| Patient Reports | Hyperope Action | Myope Action |
|---|---|---|
| GREEN side clearer | INCREASE plus (under-corrected) | DECREASE minus (over-corrected) |
| RED side clearer | DECREASE plus (over-corrected) | INCREASE minus (under-corrected) |
| Both equal | Endpoint (correct) | Endpoint (correct) |

**Golden rule:** If no point of equality is reached, ALWAYS leave **green clearer** (= more plus / less minus). Leaving green clearer protects against over-minusing and latent hyperopia.

---

### Step 4: Binocular Balancing

Purpose: balance the state of accommodation between the two eyes after monocular refraction. Only done when both eyes have similar VA (within 1 line of each other).

| Technique | How It Works | Best Use |
|---|---|---|
| Turville Infinity Balance (TIB) | Chart at 6 m reflected in a mirror; metal septum bisects so OD sees L, OS sees F; peripheral fusion maintained | Also detects heterophoria simultaneously |
| Prism Dissociation | 3Δ BD on OD, 3Δ BU on OS; two vertically displaced targets | Most controlled method |
| Alternate Occlusion | Cover each eye alternately; compare clarity | Simplest but least reliable |
| Bichrome Method | Red/green filter for binocular equalization | Quick check |

**Prism Dissociation — Procedure:**
1. Add +0.50 to +0.75 D to sphere of BOTH eyes
2. Target: 20/30 row (one line below best monocular VA)
3. 3Δ BD on OD; 3Δ BU on OS
4. Ask: are both targets equally clear?
5. If unequal: add +0.25 D to the BETTER eye in steps until equal
6. If +0.25 D reverses preference: allow dominant eye to retain better vision
7. Once equal: reduce fog to best VA

**TIB Heterophoria Indicators:**

| Pattern Seen | Phoria |
|---|---|
| L \| F (aligned) | Orthophoria |
| L \|\| F (F displaced further right) | Esophoria (uncrossed diplopia) |
| F L (F to the left of L) | Exophoria (crossed diplopia) |
| L displaced up from F | Right hypophoria / Left hyperphoria |
| L displaced down from F | Left hypophoria / Right hyperphoria |
| L tilted inward at top | Right incyclophoria |
| L tilted outward at top | Right excyclophoria |

## 9. Slit-Lamp Biomicroscopy

### Definition and Clinical Use

Clinical procedure used to examine the lids, anterior segment, and posterior segment of the eye. Called a "slit lamp" because the illumination system produces a tall, narrow slit of light — an optic section.

**Clinical uses:**
- Diagnostic and therapeutic visualization with stereoscopic view
- Contact lens fitting — evaluating fitting relationships with cornea, conjunctiva, and lids
- Foreign body removal from cornea and conjunctiva
- Measuring corneal thickness, anterior chamber depth, and IOP

---

### Two Instrument Types

| Type | Light Source Location |
|---|---|
| Zeiss slit lamp | **Base** of the instrument |
| Haag Streit | **Top** of the instrument |

Memory: **Zeiss = Base. Haag Streit = Top.** (Very frequently swapped in MCQs.)

---

### Basic Components

**1. Illumination Arm** — houses the illumination system (0–90° from straight ahead).
- Slit control: adjusts slit width and height/length
- Click stop: when engaged, slit beam focus coincides with viewing system focus
- Filters: cobalt blue, red-free (green), neutral density, diffusing

| Filter | Appearance | Clinical Use |
|---|---|---|
| Cobalt blue | Blue light | Fluorescein staining; contact lens fitting |
| Red-free (green) | Red objects appear darker | Blood vessels, limbal arcades, neovascularization |
| Neutral density | Reduces intensity | General examination |
| Diffusing | Wide diffuse beam | Low-mag overview of eye and adnexa |

**2. Microscope Arm** — houses the viewing system (normally straight ahead).
- Oculars: adjustable for examiner's refractive error and PD
- Magnification lever/changer: stepwise or continuous (zoom) system

**3. Slit-Lamp Position Control**
- Joystick / Elevation knob: controls horizontal movement and height

---

### Six Illumination Techniques — Mnemonic: DR IS F

(Diffuse, Retro, Indirect, Specular, Filtered — plus Direct as the core technique)

| Technique | Setup | What to Observe |
|---|---|---|
| Diffuse | 30–45°, wide open slit, diffusing filter, low-medium magnification | General view of anterior eye and palpebral conjunctiva; contact lens centration and movement |
| Direct (Optic Section) | 30–45°, very narrow focused slit | Corneal curvature variation, corneal thickness, depth of foreign body |
| Direct (Parallelepiped) | 30–45°, wider focused slit (~3 mm) | Corneal stroma, epithelial breakdown, lens fit, lens surface, endothelium |
| Indirect | 30–45°, moderate beam, slit offset beside area of interest | Corneal microcysts, vacuoles, epithelial anomalies |
| Retro-illumination | Light reflected off iris or fundus; microscope focused on cornea | Corneal neovascularization, edema, microcysts, vacuoles, infiltrates; contact lens surface deposits |
| Specular Reflection | **60° angle**, parallelepiped, move illumination arm until bright reflex appears (angle in = angle out) | Individual endothelial cells (polymegethism, guttata), tear layer stability and lipid layer |
| Filtered (Cobalt Blue) | Cobalt blue + Wratten filter (#12, yellow) | Corneal staining patterns; RGP contact lens fitting |

**Important:** Specular reflection is the ONLY illumination technique that requires a specific 60° angle. Van Herick also uses 60° but for a different purpose.

---

### Slit-Lamp Examination Routine

Examination order: **Lids → Lashes → Conjunctiva → Limbus → Cornea → Tears → Aqueous → Iris → Crystalline Lens**

1. Set up slit lamp: focus eyepieces, adjust headrest, position fixation target
2. Decrease room illumination
3. Start with DIFFUSE illumination at LOW magnification
4. Systematically examine all anterior structures
5. Switch to other illumination techniques as needed
6. Van Herick at temporal limbus: 60° angle, optic section, assess anterior chamber depth

---

### Van Herick Technique

Estimates depth of the anterior chamber to screen for angle closure risk.

**Setup:** Slit lamp at **60°**, narrow the beam to an optic section, patient looks straight ahead, focus beam sharply on cornea at the temporal limbus. Compare the width of the dark "shadow" on the iris (the optically empty aqueous) to the width of the corneal optic section.

| Grade | Shadow : Cornea Ratio | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 4 | ≥ 1:1 | Open anterior chamber angle — safe |
| Grade 3 | Between 1:2 and 1:4 | Open angle — monitor |
| Grade 2 | Exactly 1:4 | Narrow angle — caution |
| Grade 1 | < 1:4 | Susceptible to angle closure — high risk |
| Grade 0 | No shadow; cornea "sits" on iris | Angle is CLOSED — active angle-closure attack |

**Memory:** Smaller grade = more danger. Grade 0 = attack. Grade 1 = risk only (not attack).

**The shadow represents the optically empty aqueous in the anterior chamber, NOT the cornea itself.**

## 10. Ophthalmoscopy

### Direct vs Indirect Ophthalmoscopy

| Feature | Direct | Indirect |
|---|---|---|
| Image type | Virtual and **ERECT** | Real and **INVERTED** |
| Magnification | ~15× | 2–5× (5× with 12D; less with higher power) |
| Field of view | 10° (2 disc diameters) | 37° with 20D (8 disc diameters) |
| Area visible | Not beyond equator | Between equator and ora serrata visible |
| Maximum resolving power | 70 µm | 20 µm |
| Working distance | 1–2 cm (very close) | Arm's length |
| Stereopsis | None (monocular) | Excellent (binocular) |
| Illumination | Poorer | Better |
| Fundus image | Fixed, limited | Wide, peripheral access |

**Condensing lens for indirect:** Higher power = lower magnification + larger field of view. Most commonly used: **+14D** and **+20D**.

---

### Procedure: Direct Ophthalmoscopy

1. Reduce room illumination
2. Set lens wheel to 0 D (compensate for examiner's refractive error)
3. For right eye: examiner uses RIGHT eye, holds in RIGHT hand; approach from temporal side at ~15° from line of sight
4. Begin at arm's length; look for red reflex
5. Move in to 1–2 cm; focus on optic disc
6. Evaluate: disc margin, color, cup-to-disc ratio
7. Follow four main vascular arcades
8. Examine macula last (bright light is uncomfortable); ask patient to look directly at the light

---

### Opacity Localization by Parallax (Direct Ophthalmoscopy)

Move the ophthalmoscope from side to side:

| Opacity Movement (vs. ophthalmoscope) | Location |
|---|---|
| Opposite direction | Anterior (cornea, anterior chamber, anterior lens) |
| Little or no movement | Near the nuclear lens |
| Same direction | Posterior lens or vitreous |
| Vitreous: floats back to original position | Vitreous |

When the PATIENT moves the eye: anterior opacities move in the SAME direction; posterior opacities move in the OPPOSITE direction.

---

### Normal Fundus Appearance

| Structure | Normal Appearance |
|---|---|
| Optic disc | Round to oval; 1.5–1.7 mm diameter; red-yellow, nasal side more pronounced; sharp margins |
| Vessels emerge from | Within the physiologic cup/excavation |
| Retinal periphery | Lighter than central area; occasional pigmentation irregularities |
| Fovea | Appears darker than surrounding retina |
| Macula | Yellow color visible only in red-free illumination |
| Arteries | Light red, straight, white reflex stripes |
| Veins | Dark red, tortuous, pulsating |
| Artery : Vein caliber | 2:3 (veins larger) |
| Choroidal vessels | White/pink/yellow-white; very tortuous; numerous anastomoses |

### Ophthalmoscope Accessories

**Light apertures:**
- Micro-spot (small): undilated, well-lit room
- Medium aperture: dark room, non-dilated pupil (most commonly used)
- Large aperture: dilated pupil, general examination
- Half-light: partially obstructed pupil (e.g., cataract)
- Slit beam: examines contour abnormalities of cornea, lens, retina
- Grid: rough approximation of relative distance between retinal lesions

**Ophthalmoscope filters:**
- Cobalt blue (violet): fluorescein staining, contact lens evaluation
- Red-free (yellowish-green): enhances contrast with red; best for blood vessels
- Yellow filter: decreases light intensity; provides comfort for patient's retina

### Comprehensive Glaucoma Examination — "TOP GP"

| Test | Measures | Instrument |
|---|---|---|
| **T**onometry | Intraocular pressure (normal: 12–22 mmHg) | Contact / non-contact tonometer |
| **O**phthalmoscopy | Shape and color of optic nerve | Dilated fundus exam |
| **P**erimetry | Complete visual field map | Automated perimeter |
| **G**onioscopy | Angle where iris meets cornea (open vs. closed) | Gonioscopic contact lens |
| **P**achymetry | Central corneal thickness (CCT) | Pachymeter |

**Gonioscopy:** A hand-held contact lens with a mirror placed on the numbed eye. Shows whether the angle is open (open-angle / chronic glaucoma) or closed (angle-closure / acute glaucoma). **Required to differentiate open-angle from angle-closure — cannot determine this with tonometry alone.**

**Pachymetry:** Thin CCT is a strong predictor of glaucoma development in patients with borderline IOP. Thin CCT can cause **underestimation** of IOP; thick CCT can **falsely inflate** tonometry readings.

**Normal IOP:** 12–22 mmHg. Most glaucoma cases exceed 20 mmHg, but normal tension glaucoma exists within the normal range.

**Imaging for glaucoma:**
- Scanning Laser Tomography (HRT3) — evaluates optic nerve head
- Laser Polarimetry (GDX) — measures retinal nerve fiber layer thickness
- Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) — both time-domain and spectral-domain

## 11. Keratometry

### Definition

Clinical technique used to measure the curvature of the anterior surface of the cornea. Determines power, toricity, and integrity of the cornea. Uses the principle of a convex mirror and a doubling principle.

### Information Provided

- Radius of curvature of the anterior corneal surface
- Direction of the eye's principal meridians (with-the-rule or against-the-rule astigmatism)
- Axis and degree of corneal astigmatism
- Any corneal distortion

### Optical Principle

The relationship between the size of an object and the size of its image reflected from a convex mirror. The doubling principle allows the examiner to measure the radius of the cornea without the image moving during the measurement.

### Astigmatism Direction

- **With-the-rule (WTR):** Weakest corneal meridian at or near 180° (vertical meridian has more power). The minus axis of the correcting cylinder is at 180°.
- **Against-the-rule (ATR):** Weakest corneal meridian at or near 90°. The minus axis of the correcting cylinder is at 90°.

**Sample recording:**
```
R: 42.00 at 180 / 43.00 at 90 → Corneal astigmatism: -1.00 cyl x 180
L: 42.25 at 170 / 43.50 at 80 → Corneal astigmatism: -1.25 cyl x 170
```

### Javal's Rule

Estimates total refractive astigmatism from corneal astigmatism:

```
At = p(Ac) + k
```

Where:
- **At** = total (refractive) astigmatism
- **Ac** = corneal astigmatism
- **p** = 1.25 (the cornea contributes approximately 1.25× its measured power to the total)
- **k** = −0.50 cyl × 90 (physiologic ATR astigmatism from the posterior corneal surface)

**Simplified:** `At = 1.25(Ac) + (−0.50 cyl × 90)`

### Sources of Keratometry Error

- Improper calibration
- Faulty patient positioning
- Lack of proper fixation by patient
- Reduced examiner visual acuity
- Accommodative fluctuation by examiner
- Localized corneal distortions, excessive tear fluid, abnormal lid position
- Improper focusing of the corneal image

## 12. Phorometric Test — OEP 21 Steps

### Two Visual Patterns

| Pattern | Definition | Buffer Area |
|---|---|---|
| Accommodative Pattern | Ability to see clearly at varying distances | **Hyperopia** buffers accommodation |
| Convergence Pattern | Mechanism for maintaining single vision | **Exophoria** buffers convergence |

The phorometric test investigates the relationship between these two patterns using probe bodies (lenses and prisms).

### Probe Bodies — 4 Basic Changes

| Probe Body | Pattern Affected | Induced Change | Perceived Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Convex lens (+) | Inhibits accommodation | Object appears to move AWAY | Target appears BIGGER |
| Concave lens (−) | Stimulates accommodation | Object appears to move CLOSER | Target appears SMALLER |
| Base-In prism | Inhibits convergence | Object appears to move AWAY | Target appears BIGGER |
| Base-Out prism | Stimulates convergence | Object appears to move CLOSER | Target appears SMALLER |

**Memory shortcut:** Plus lens and BI prism both **INHIBIT** (object moves away, bigger). Minus lens and BO prism both **STIMULATE** (object moves closer, smaller).

---

### Phoria Measurement Rules

- **Lateral phoria:** use a **VERTICAL dissociating prism**
- **Vertical phoria:** use a **HORIZONTAL dissociating prism**
- Phorias measured at 20 ft (6 m) and 16 inches (40 cm)
- At 16 inches: stimulus to accommodation = 2.50 D; stimulus to convergence = 15 prism dioptres (if PD ~64 mm)

**Von Graefe method:** Dissociating prism placed in front of one eye; measuring prism in front of the other eye.

**Standard prism setup (for tests #3, 8, 12, 13A, 13B, 15A, 15B, 18):** OD: 12–15Δ BI; OS: 6Δ BU

### Board Traps — The Most Tested Errors

**Case History**
- HIGH blood sugar = MORE MYOPIC (not more hyperopic). The lens swells, shifting the focal point anterior.
- Asthenopia = eyestrain. Amblyopia = reduced VA. Not interchangeable.
- Not all headaches are refractive. Location and triggers matter. Occipital headaches may be convergence insufficiency or systemic, not astigmatism.

**Visual Acuity**
- Snellen acuity is minimum SEPARABLE (resolution), not minimum visible.
- Scoring stop rule: more than HALF the letters on a row missed — not the first row with any missed letter.
- Easiest letter = L. Hardest letter = B.
- Pinhole that does NOT improve VA suggests pathology, not refractive error.

**Color Vision**
- Ishihara tests ONLY red-green deficiencies. Blue (tritan) defects require HRR.
- Plate 1 of Ishihara is a DEMONSTRATION plate — failure may indicate very low VA or malingering, not color deficiency.

**Retinoscopy**
- Scissors motion = IRREGULAR astigmatism. Cannot be corrected with a standard cylinder.
- Streak orientation is PERPENDICULAR to the meridian being tested (not parallel).
- Dynamic retinoscopy does NOT subtract a working distance lens.
- Against-motion does not always mean high myopia — the patient may be over-minused or highly accommodating.

**Subjective Refraction**
- RED dot on cross-cylinder = INCREASE cylinder. WHITE = DECREASE. Commonly reversed in MCQs.
- Blurred line does NOT darken with adding minus cylinder = IMPROPER FOG, not irregular astigmatism. Re-fog first.
- Rotation method for axis: ABOVE 0.75 D. Cross-cylinder method: BELOW 0.75 D.
- If duochrome equality cannot be reached, leave GREEN clearer — never red. Red clearer = over-minused.
- Dominant eye is allowed to retain better vision if +0.25 D reversal occurs in binocular balancing.
- Alternate occlusion is the simplest but LEAST reliable balancing method.

**Slit-Lamp**
- Zeiss = light at BASE. Haag Streit = light at TOP. Frequently swapped in MCQs.
- Specular reflection requires EXACTLY 60°. Van Herick also uses 60° but for a different purpose.
- Direct (optic section) = very narrow slit. Direct (parallelepiped) = ~3 mm wide. Not the same setup.
- Retro-illumination: light reflected off IRIS or FUNDUS; microscope focuses on CORNEA, not iris.
- Click stop: when engaged, slit beam and viewing system are co-focused.

**Van Herick**
- Grade 1 = susceptible to closure, NOT an active attack. Grade 0 = active angle-closure attack.
- The shadow represents optically empty aqueous, NOT the cornea.

**Ophthalmoscopy**
- Direct gives a VIRTUAL, ERECT image. Indirect gives a REAL, INVERTED image. Most commonly swapped MCQ answers.
- Indirect sees equator to ora serrata. Direct does NOT.
- IOP within 12–22 mmHg does NOT rule out glaucoma. Normal tension glaucoma exists.
- Thin CCT = underestimated IOP. Thick CCT = falsely elevated IOP.
- Gonioscopy is required to distinguish open-angle from angle-closure glaucoma. Tonometry alone cannot do this.
- Higher condensing lens power in indirect ophthalmoscopy = LOWER magnification + LARGER field.

**Phorometric Tests (21 Steps)**
- #14A = DISSOCIATED cross-cylinder (prisms separate the images). #14B = FUSED (no dissociating prisms).
- PRC/NRC (#16A/#17A) endpoints = BLUR. PFR/NFR (#16B/#17B) endpoints = BREAK/RECOVERY.
- Buffer areas: Hyperopia buffers accommodation. Exophoria buffers convergence. Different patterns.
- Test #9 requires an auxiliary +0.25 D lens. Tests #10 and #11 do not.
- Vertical phoria uses a HORIZONTAL dissociating prism. Lateral phoria uses a VERTICAL dissociating prism.
