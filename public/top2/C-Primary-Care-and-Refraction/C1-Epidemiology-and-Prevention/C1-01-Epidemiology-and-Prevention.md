## The Simple Version

Public health is care aimed at **populations**, not single patients. Where the rest of your training treats the person in the chair, this note zooms out to the whole community: who goes blind, why, how common it is, and how we prevent it.

Three ideas carry most of the marks:
1. **The vocabulary of epidemiology** (epidemic vs pandemic vs endemic; incidence vs prevalence; morbidity vs mortality).
2. **The three levels of prevention** (primary, secondary, tertiary), which map onto three levels of eye care.
3. **The numbers** (global and Philippine blindness statistics and their leading causes).

**Teaching angle:** the single most repeated fact in this topic is that the two leading causes of vision impairment worldwide are **uncorrected refractive error and cataract**, and that **over 80% of vision impairment is avoidable** (preventable or curable). If you remember nothing else, remember that.

---

## But Why?

**Why does optometry belong in public health at all?**
Because the biggest cause of avoidable blindness is **uncorrected refractive error**, and correcting refractive error is exactly what optometrists do. A pair of glasses is a public-health intervention. That is why the State (RA 8050) calls optometry part of primary health care.

**Why is "avoidable" the key word, not "blind"?**
Because over 80% of vision impairment can be prevented or cured. The tragedy is not that these conditions exist but that they go untreated: only about a third of refractive-error patients and a sixth of cataract patients get the care they need. The public-health job is closing that gap.

**Why distinguish incidence from prevalence?**
Incidence counts **new** cases over a period (the flow); prevalence counts **all existing** cases at a point (the pool). A chronic disease like glaucoma has low incidence but high prevalence because cases accumulate. Mixing them up changes the meaning of any statistic.

**Why three levels of prevention?**
Because you can intervene before, during, or after a disease starts. **Primary** stops it beginning, **secondary** catches it early and reverses it, **tertiary** limits the damage once it is established. Naming the level tells you what action is appropriate.

## The Details

### Core Definitions

- **Public health (Winslow):** the science and art of **preventing disease, prolonging life, and promoting health** through organized community effort.
- **Public health optometry:** using the full scope of optometry to prevent disease, prolong life, and promote health for **groups of people at the community level**.
- **Health (WHO):** a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease.
- **Visual hygiene:** the science and art of conserving and promoting healthful, comfortable vision.
- **Hygiene:** the science of preserving health; the rules designed to promote it.
- **Visual sanitation:** the principles for understanding the causes and sources of ocular infection and disease.
- **Sanitation:** sanitary measures; cleanliness, freedom from dirt and germs.

**Three goals of public health (Winslow follow-on):** reduce discomfort, disability, and disease in society; promote quality of life; attain the highest level of physical, mental, and social well-being with available resources.

**Emphasis of public health:** prevention of disease, the health of *groups* (not individuals), control of communicable infection, education in personal hygiene, and organizing services for early diagnosis and preventive treatment.

### Epidemiology Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Epidemic** | More cases than expected in a region/group over a time period (sudden outbreak) |
| **Pandemic** | An epidemic gone very widespread, across regions/continents (e.g., COVID-19) |
| **Endemic** | Constantly present in a community at low frequency, peculiar to a locality |
| **Incidence** | Number of **new** cases over a period (the flow) |
| **Prevalence** | Number of **all existing** cases at a point (the pool) |
| **Morbidity** | State of being diseased; incidence/prevalence of disease in a population |
| **Mortality** | Number of deaths in a population |

### Levels of Prevention (and matching optometric care)

| Level | General meaning | Optometric example |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Prevent onset before disease begins | Health education, visual hygiene, screening, accident/injury prevention |
| **Secondary** | Detect early and reverse ongoing deterioration | Early detection and correction (glasses, treating amblyopia) to restore performance |
| **Tertiary** | Limit disability once disease is established | Low-vision rehabilitation; maintain remaining function |

### Three Levels of Eye Care

- **Primary eye care:** community level. Promote eye hygiene, measure and record VA, refer subnormal vision and blindness, detect disease early, give simple treatment, refer severe cases, join immunization and blindness-prevention drives.
- **Secondary eye care:** hospitals without large technical facilities; ophthalmologists; promote prevention and train primary-level personnel.
- **Tertiary eye care:** the full complex of medical and surgical care by ophthalmology specialists with advanced technology; institutes of ophthalmology; train personnel at other levels.

### Organizations

- **WHO:** UN specialized agency for health; established **April 7, 1948**; governed by **192 member states** through the **World Health Assembly** (approves programme and the biennial budget). Goal: highest possible level of health for all.
- **DOH:** principal health agency of the Philippines; both stakeholder and regulator. Structure: ~17 central offices, 16 Centers for Health Development (regional), 70 hospitals, and **4 attached agencies**: **PhilHealth** (national health insurance), Dangerous Drugs Board, Philippine Institute of Traditional and Alternative Health Care, and Philippine National AIDS Council.
- **PhilHealth:** adequate and affordable social health insurance for all Filipinos.
- **SSS:** protects members against socially recognized hazards.

### Global Burden (WHO)

- **2.2 billion** people have a vision impairment; at least **1 billion** of these was preventable or is still unaddressed.
- **Leading causes worldwide: uncorrected refractive error and cataract.**
- **Over 80% of all vision impairment is avoidable** (preventable or curable).
- Access gap: only ~**36%** of refractive-error and ~**17%** of cataract patients get appropriate care.
- Most affected are people **over 50**; at-risk groups are the **over-50s** and **children under 15**.
- By 2050, blindness could reach ~115 million (from 38.5 million in 2020) due to population growth and aging.
- **Annual global cost of lost productivity: ~US$411 billion.**
- Vision disorders are the **second most prevalent chronic health problem** in the population.

**Single largest unaddressed cause:** **presbyopia (826 million)**, even though presbyopia is not what people first name. Of the ≥1 billion with moderate-to-severe impairment, the rank by count is: unaddressed presbyopia 826M → uncorrected refractive error 123.7M → un-operated cataract 65.2M → glaucoma 6.9M → corneal opacity 4.2M → diabetic retinopathy 3M → trachoma 2M.

**Other global counts (WHO 2019):** myopia ~2.6 billion (2020); glaucoma 76 million (40–80 yrs); AMD 195.6 million; diabetic retinopathy 146 million.

### WHO 2019 — Which Conditions Cause Vision Impairment

| Do NOT typically cause VI | CAN cause VI (including blindness) |
|---|---|
| Blepharitis | Age-related macular degeneration |
| Chalazion / hordeolum (stye) | Cataract |
| Conjunctivitis | Corneal opacity |
| Dry eye | Diabetic retinopathy |
| Pterygium / pinguecula | Glaucoma |
| Subconjunctival hemorrhage | Trachoma, Refractive error |

### Philippine Burden (DOH 2017 / PERI-DOH)

**Causes of bilateral BLINDNESS (332,150 persons):**
- **Cataract ~33% (109,609)** — the leading cause
- **Errors of refraction ~25% (83,037)**
- **Glaucoma ~14% (46,501)**

**Causes of bilateral LOW VISION (2,179,733 persons) — order FLIPS:**
- **Errors of refraction ~43% (937,285)** — now the leading cause
- **Cataract ~34% (741,109)**
- Rest: glaucoma and other eye diseases

> **Key distinction:** for *blindness* cataract leads; for *low vision* refractive error leads. Same country, opposite top cause. This swap is a favorite board item.

Major causes of blindness (global ranking echoed locally): un-operated cataract ~35%, uncorrected refractive error ~21%, glaucoma ~8%.

**2018 PERI-DOH Philippine Eye Disease Study:** overall VI prevalence (presenting VA worse than 6/18 in the better eye) = **1.98%**. Major causes: cataract 1.06% (cataract needing surgery, VA ≤6/60: ~333,639), uncorrected EOR 0.38%, glaucoma 0.27%, maculopathy 0.2%. Among those *without* VI: 3.26% still have EOR, 0.69% glaucoma, 0.59% diabetic retinopathy.

**Children:** 1 in 10 school children have an eye problem; ~25% of those have **amblyopia**, the rest mostly refractive error. Amblyopia prevalence ~1 to 3%; EOR ~3 to 15%.

**Prevention program:** the DOH **Community Eye Health Program (CEHP)** integrates eye care into local public health through a service delivery network (barangay health stations → rural health units → tertiary hospitals), focused on avoidable blindness (cataract, EOR, childhood blindness).

## Memory Anchors

- **"Prevent, prolong, promote"** = the three verbs of public health (Winslow).
- **EPIC sizes: Endemic < Epidemic < Pandemic** (local always present → regional outbreak → global).
- **Incidence = new (flow); Prevalence = all (pool).**
- **Prevention 1-2-3: prevent onset → catch early → limit damage.**
- **The headline number: 80%+ of vision impairment is avoidable; top causes = refractive error + cataract.**
- **Philippines blindness order: Cataract > Refractive error > Glaucoma.**
- **Philippines low vision order: Refractive error > Cataract** (the flip). Think "low vision = light correction problem, so EOR leads."
- **WHO born 1948; World Health Day is April 7.**

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** prevalence and incidence are interchangeable. Wrong. Incidence is **new** cases over time; prevalence is **all** existing cases at a point.
- **Trap:** the leading cause of global vision impairment is glaucoma. Wrong. It is **uncorrected refractive error and cataract**.
- **Trap:** most vision impairment is untreatable. Wrong. **Over 80% is avoidable** (preventable or curable).
- **Trap:** secondary prevention means surgery for advanced disease. Wrong. Secondary = **early detection and reversal**; managing established disability is tertiary.
- **Trap:** an endemic disease is a sudden outbreak. Wrong. Endemic is **constantly present at low frequency**; the sudden outbreak is an epidemic.
- **Trap:** the leading cause of blindness in the Philippines is refractive error. Close, but **cataract** is first; refractive error is second.
- **Trap:** refractive error is the top cause of *both* blindness and low vision in the Philippines. Wrong. Blindness = **cataract** first; only **low vision** has refractive error first.
- **Trap:** dry eye, pterygium, or conjunctivitis are leading causes of vision impairment. Wrong. WHO lists these as conditions that do **not** typically cause VI.
- **Trap:** the single biggest cause of global vision impairment by headcount is refractive error. By count it is actually **unaddressed presbyopia (826M)**; refractive error and cataract are the leading *named* causes of impairment and blindness.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A disease is constantly present in a community at a low, steady frequency. This pattern is:

- A. Epidemic
- B. Pandemic
- C. Endemic
- D. Sporadic

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Endemic.** Constant low-level presence in a locality. An epidemic is an outbreak above expected levels; a pandemic is an epidemic spread across regions.
</details>

**2.** The two leading causes of vision impairment worldwide are:

- A. Glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy
- B. Uncorrected refractive error and cataract
- C. Trachoma and corneal opacity
- D. AMD and glaucoma

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Uncorrected refractive error and cataract.** Both are largely avoidable, which is why over 80% of vision impairment is preventable or curable.
</details>

**3.** Conducting community vision screening and health education to stop disease before it starts is which level of prevention?

- A. Primary
- B. Secondary
- C. Tertiary
- D. Quaternary

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**A. Primary.** Primary prevention acts before onset. Secondary is early detection and reversal; tertiary limits established disability (e.g., low-vision rehab).
</details>

**4.** In the Philippines, the leading cause of bilateral blindness is:

- A. Glaucoma
- B. Refractive error
- C. Cataract
- D. Diabetic retinopathy

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Cataract** (about a third of cases), followed by errors of refraction, then glaucoma.
</details>

**5.** A statistic counts only the new cases of glaucoma diagnosed in a region during one year. This measures:

- A. Prevalence
- B. Incidence
- C. Mortality
- D. Endemicity

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Incidence.** New cases over a defined period. Prevalence would count all existing cases at a point in time.
</details>

**6.** In the Philippines, the leading cause of bilateral **low vision** (as opposed to blindness) is:

- A. Cataract
- B. Errors of refraction
- C. Glaucoma
- D. Diabetic retinopathy

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Errors of refraction** (~43%), with cataract second (~34%). The order flips from blindness, where cataract leads.
</details>

**7.** Which of the following does WHO list as a condition that does **not** typically cause vision impairment?

- A. Trachoma
- B. Corneal opacity
- C. Pterygium
- D. Diabetic retinopathy

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Pterygium.** Along with blepharitis, chalazion/stye, conjunctivitis, dry eye, and subconjunctival hemorrhage. The other three are on the list of conditions that *can* cause VI.
</details>
