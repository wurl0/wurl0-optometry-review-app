## The Simple Version

Vision therapy trains the binocular visual system the same way physical therapy trains a recovering muscle — not by compensating for weakness, but by rebuilding actual capacity. Prism moves the target; VT moves the eye. The clinical workflow has three steps: identify which system is broken (accommodation, vergence, or sensory fusion), confirm how severely it is broken through a targeted battery of tests, then choose the modality that addresses that specific deficit.

Before prescribing VT, you need to know which muscle or system is causing the problem. For horizontal phorias, the test pattern tells you the type. For vertical deviations and paretic muscles, you use the Parks 3-step test to narrow the involved muscle from a field of eight possibilities to one.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "The patient has convergence insufficiency. You give prism. Six months later, symptoms return when the prism is removed. Why?" (Prism compensated the demand; it never trained the vergence system. The answer is VT, not more prism.)

---

## But Why?

**Why does VT produce durable improvement when prism does not?**
Prism shifts the perceived target so the eyes need less convergence to fuse it — this removes symptoms but leaves the vergence system unchanged. VT repeatedly stimulates the vergence system near and slightly beyond its current limit. Each successful fusion attempt at a higher demand level reinforces the neural drive, gradually pushing the blur and break points outward. The vergence system adapts the same way any trained skill adapts — through repetition under increasing demand.

**Why does the Parks 3-step test work?**
Six of the eight cyclovertical muscles each have a field of action where they are the primary vertical mover. When a muscle is paretic, the vertical deviation is largest in its field of action. Each step of the Parks test eliminates half the remaining candidates by identifying which eye, which gaze direction, and which head tilt produces the greatest deviation. Three binary decisions → one muscle.

**Why is the Brock string both a diagnostic and a therapeutic tool?**
The Brock string works by creating physiological diplopia — if the patient fixates one bead, the other beads appear double, and the string forms an X at the fixated bead. This requires that both eyes' inputs reach consciousness. A patient who suppresses will not see the doubled strings. Demonstrating this to the patient is the diagnosis of suppression; practicing until they consistently see the X is the beginning of treating it. The same stimulus serves both purposes.

**Why is patching more effective than atropine for most amblyopia?**
Patching creates complete occlusion of the dominant eye — the brain has no choice but to process the amblyopic eye's input. Atropine blurs the dominant eye for near only; it still provides clear distance vision, so the suppression mechanism is only partially challenged. For moderate amblyopia, the PEDIG trials showed comparable outcomes, but for deeper amblyopia, patching offers stronger and more consistent occlusion.

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Root / Origin | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| Orthoptics | Greek *orthos* (straight) + *ops* (eye) | Diagnosis and non-surgical treatment of BV anomalies; literally "straight eye training" |
| Vision therapy (VT) | English compound | Broader term; includes accommodation, sensory fusion, and higher perceptual training — not just convergence |
| Pleoptics | Greek *pleion* (more) + *ops* | Monocular technique for eccentric fixation; uses intense light to dazzle peripheral retina and rehabilitate foveal fixation |
| Eccentric fixation | Latin *ex-centrum* (off-center) | Fixation in the amblyopic eye at a retinal point other than the fovea; marker of deep amblyopia |
| Physiological diplopia | Greek *diploos* (double) | Normal doubling of non-fixated objects; expected finding when fusion is intact; absent when suppression is present |
| Bielschowsky head tilt test | Named: Alfred Bielschowsky (1900s) | Step 3 of Parks test; tilting the head increases vertical fusion demand on cyclovertical muscles of the ipsilateral eye |
| Synoptophore / Amblyoscope | Greek *synoptos* (seen together) | Instrument for measuring objective and subjective angle of deviation and training at all three Grades of BV |
| CAM vision stimulator | Cambridge (Univ.) Amblyopia Model | Rotating high-contrast gratings; spatially stimulates amblyopic eye monocularly; 7 min/session |
| Dichoptic training | Greek *dikha* (separately) + *optikos* | Presents different images to each eye simultaneously; forces cortical integration; modern active amblyopia therapy |

> Lookalike trap: VT and orthoptics overlap but are not identical. Orthoptics (traditional term) focuses on motor alignment and convergence. VT (OEP-influenced) includes accommodation, sensory fusion, and perceptual training. In Philippine board context, the terms are used interchangeably for this section.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

#### Vergence Training

The vergence system has a homeostatic zone called the **zone of clear single binocular vision (ZCSBV)** — bounded by the blur point on one side (where accommodation breaks down) and the break point on the other (where fusion breaks down). VT expands this zone by repeatedly driving the vergence system to its limit and requiring successful fusion.

Mechanism: sustained near vergence effort → increased motor fusion demand → neural adaptation in the vergence controller → raised blur and break thresholds → wider ZCSBV.

Convergence exercises build **positive fusional vergence (PFC)**; divergence exercises build **negative fusional vergence (NFC)**. The target is always the condition's compensating reserve — CI patients need more PFC; FVD patients need both.

#### Accommodation Training

Accommodative flexibility training (lens flippers) exploits the accommodative/vergence linkage. Every flip from minus to plus triggers relaxation of accommodation, which drives a vergence response. Training this repeatedly under time pressure improves the speed of the ciliary muscle response and the accuracy of the AC/A-driven vergence following it.

#### Sensory / Antisuppression Training

Suppression is an active cortical inhibition. It cannot be removed by demand alone — the suppressed eye's signal must first be made strong enough to compete with the dominant eye. Antisuppression training uses red-green filters, polaroids, or Bernell instruments to present stimuli that can only be seen binocularly, forcing the brain to use both eyes. Progression: peripheral stimulation first (where suppression is weaker) → central stimulation (foveal area, where suppression is strongest).

### Parks 3-Step Test (for Vertical Deviations)

Used to identify a single paretic cyclovertical muscle when a vertical deviation is present.

```
STEP 1 — Which eye is hypertropic?
  Right hypertopia → paretic muscle is in ONE of these 4:
    Right depressors: RSO, RIR
    Left elevators: LIO, LSR

  Left hypertopia → paretic muscle is in ONE of these 4:
    Left depressors: LSO, LIR
    Right elevators: RIO, RSR

STEP 2 — In which gaze direction is the vertical deviation largest?
  Right gaze (adducted OS, abducted OD):
    • Right hyper + larger in RIGHT gaze → RSR or LIR (SR acts in abduction; IR acts in adduction)
    • Narrows to 2 muscles

  Left gaze (adducted OD, abducted OS):
    • Right hyper + larger in LEFT gaze → RIR or LSR
    • Narrows to 2 muscles

STEP 3 — Bielschowsky Head Tilt Test
  Tilt head toward the HIGHER eye:
    • If deviation INCREASES → ipsilateral intorter is paretic (SO of higher eye)
    • If deviation DECREASES → contralateral extorter is paretic (SR of lower eye)

  Logic: Head tilt triggers compensatory intorsion in ipsilateral eye. If SO (primary intortor)
  is paretic, the SR (extortor + elevator) compensates alone → more elevation → deviation worsens.
  If SR is paretic, head tilt unmasks SR weakness directly.

MEMORY: H-G-T (Higher eye, Gaze direction, Tilt direction)
  Each step cuts candidates in half: 8 → 4 → 2 → 1
```

### VT Modality Reference

#### Vergence Training Techniques

| Technique | Target | Mechanism | Home or Office |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pencil push-up** | CI | Patient fixates pencil tip, brings it slowly toward nose until diplopia; holds, then recedes; 20 reps | Home |
| **Brock string** | CI + suppression | 3 beads on 5-foot string; fixate each bead sequentially; normal response = X pattern at bead; suppression = one string disappears | Home and office |
| **Prism flippers** | FVD, CI, CE | Alternate base-in and base-out prism flippers; patient clears target with each flip; builds both PFC and NFC | Office and home |
| **Aperture rule trainer** | CI, CE | Convergence or divergence through apertures; demands increase by moving aperture position | Office |
| **Vectograms / tranaglyphs** | CI, suppression | Polarized or red-green targets; can be set to different vergence demands; antisuppression rings built in | Office |
| **Step vergence (prism bar)** | FVD | Patient fuses progressively larger prism increments at distance and near; trains fusional range systematically | Office |

#### Accommodation Training Techniques

| Technique | Target | Mechanism | Home or Office |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lens flippers ±2.00D** | Accommodative infacility | Alternate plus and minus lenses; patient clears each side before flipping; target 12 cycles/minute for adults | Home and office |
| **Hart chart** | Accommodation + saccades | Small letter chart (near) + large letter chart (distance); patient reads alternately while maintaining clear focus | Home |
| **Monocular push-up** | AI (reduced AA) | Single-eye push-up to near point; builds monocular AA | Home |

#### Antisuppression and Sensory Training

| Technique | Target | What patient does |
|---|---|---|
| **Red-green / GTVT chart** | Suppression | Under anaglyph glasses, certain letters/lines visible to one eye only; patient reports what they see; increases awareness of suppression |
| **Bernell red light stimulation** | Amblyopia | Monocular exposure to red light; stimulates retinal function; check VA improvement after exposure |
| **Synoptophore grades** | Sensory fusion | Progress from Grade I (SMP) → Grade II (flat fusion) → Grade III (stereopsis) using matched slides; builds up BV grades systematically |
| **Brock string** | Suppression | As above; physiological diplopia training is antisuppression in function |

#### Amblyopia-Specific Techniques

| Technique | Mechanism | Key Facts |
|---|---|---|
| **Patching (occlusion)** | Occludes dominant eye; brain forced to use amblyopic eye | Part-time (2-6 hrs/day) = as effective as full-time for moderate amblyopia (PEDIG trials); compliance is the main barrier |
| **Atropine penalization** | 1% atropine in dominant eye blurs near vision; amblyopic eye is clearer for near | No patch; avoids compliance battle; risk = photophobia in dominant eye; comparable to patching for moderate amblyopia |
| **CAM vision stimulator** | Rotating high-contrast gratings stimulate spatial frequency channels in amblyopic visual cortex | Done MONOCULARLY (non-amblyopic eye occluded); 7 min/session, once/week, 10-20 sessions; check VA before and after each session |
| **Pleoptics** | Intense light dazzles peripheral retina; fovea functions better in relative isolation after dazzle | For eccentric fixation specifically; largely replaced by patching; expensive equipment; inferior VA gains vs conventional patching |
| **Perceptual learning / dichoptic training** | Repeated practice of specific visual tasks improves neural efficiency; dichoptic forces cortical integration | Modern; home-based versions using video games or apps; promising for older patients and adults |
| **Near activities (paper-based)** | Reading, writing, puzzles under minimal occlusion (1 hr/day) | Von Noorden: minimal occlusion + near activities effective for older children |
| **Pegboard stimulator** | Eye-hand coordination + antisuppression | Child places golf tees in rotating pegboard under patch or binocularly; speed increases as skill improves |

### Comparisons That Matter

**VT vs Prism — Key Distinction**

| | Prism | VT |
|---|---|---|
| What it changes | The stimulus location | The vergence capacity |
| Effect on fusional range | None | Blur and break points shift outward |
| Durability | Temporary; symptoms return when removed | Durable when therapy is completed |
| Best candidate | Elderly, presbyopes, low compliance | Children and motivated adults |
| When to use | Phoria fails Sheard's/Percival's + poor VT candidate | Phoria fails Sheard's/Percival's + patient can comply |

**Suppression vs Amblyopia — Don't Conflate**

| | Suppression | Amblyopia |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Active cortical inhibition (facultative or obligatory) | End-result reduced VA from obligatory suppression |
| VA reduction | Only if obligatory | Yes — defining feature |
| Treatment | Antisuppression VT | Optical correction + patching/penalization + VT |
| Can exist without the other | Yes (facultative suppression = no VA loss) | No — amblyopia requires prior obligatory suppression |

**Patching vs Atropine (PEDIG Comparison)**

| | Full-time patching | Part-time patching | Atropine |
|---|---|---|---|
| Moderate amblyopia (20/40–20/100) | Effective | Equally effective | Comparable |
| Deep amblyopia (>20/100) | Preferred | — | Less effective |
| Compliance | Hardest | Easier | Easiest |
| Side effect | Occlusion amblyopia risk if over-patched | Less risk | Photophobia in dominant eye |

### Memory Anchors

**Parks 3-step mnemonic — HGT (High, Gaze, Tilt)**
- **H**igh eye → which eye is hypertropic? (picks 4 muscles)
- **G**aze → which gaze makes it worse? (picks 2 muscles)
- **T**ilt → Bielschowsky head tilt toward higher eye (picks 1 muscle)
- Each step halves the candidates: 8 → 4 → 2 → 1

**VT modality by condition:**
- CI → Brock string + pencil push-up + aperture rule + prism flippers
- CE → NRA exercise + lens flippers (plus) + divergence training
- FVD → step vergence (both BI and BO) + vectograms
- AI → push-up training + Hart chart
- Infacility → lens flippers (±2.00D) to 12 cpm
- Suppression → antisuppression charts → Brock string → vectogram → synoptophore grades
- Amblyopia → patch or atropine → CAM or near activities → active VT if plateau

**Pleoptics = eccentric fixation (not standard amblyopia)**
Pleoptics ≠ patching. Pleoptics is for patients who cannot use their fovea because it is suppressed by a non-foveal fixation reflex. For standard strabismic or anisometropic amblyopia, patching is the first line.

**CAM is monocular — common exam trap**
The non-amblyopic eye is OCCLUDED during CAM. The amblyopic eye alone is stimulated. Confusing CAM with binocular therapies is a common wrong answer.

**Brock string normal response = X pattern**
If the patient sees only one string line (no X at the bead), one eye's signal is suppressed. This is both the test positive for suppression AND the target to overcome in therapy.

### Parks 3-Step Decision Tree (Right Hypertopia example)

```
Right hyper → 4 candidates: RSO, RIR, LIO, LSR
│
├── Larger in RIGHT gaze → RSO or LIR
│   ├── Head tilt RIGHT (toward higher eye = OD) → INCREASES → RSO paretic ✓
│   └── Head tilt RIGHT → DECREASES → LIR paretic ✓
│
└── Larger in LEFT gaze → RIR or LSR
    ├── Head tilt RIGHT → INCREASES → RIR paretic (IR acts in adduction = left gaze field)
    └── Head tilt RIGHT → DECREASES → LSR paretic
```

## Board Traps

1. **"VT will fix it — no need for glasses first."**
   Wrong. Glasses come first, always. An uncorrected hyperope's AC/A-driven esophoria must be corrected optically before the true muscle demand is known. VT on top of a wrong prescription trains against the wrong baseline.

2. **"Pleoptics is for all amblyopia."**
   Pleoptics is specifically for eccentric fixation. For standard amblyopia with central fixation, patching is the first line. Pleoptics requires expensive equipment and shows inferior VA gains compared to patching for most cases.

3. **"CAM is done with both eyes open."**
   The NON-amblyopic eye is OCCLUDED during CAM. It is a monocular procedure. "Both eyes open" is the trap answer.

4. **"If patching improves VA, stop immediately."**
   Premature stopping causes regression. Patching continues until VA either equalizes between the two eyes or shows no further improvement over two consecutive cycles. Stopping too early is one of the most common reasons amblyopia recurs.

5. **"Brock string is only diagnostic."**
   It is both diagnostic and therapeutic. A patient who cannot see the physiological diplopia X pattern is demonstrating suppression. Regular Brock string practice trains awareness of that binocular input — it is antisuppression VT.

6. **"Sheard's and Percival's criteria determine when to prescribe VT."**
   They determine when to prescribe PRISM. VT is indicated when the patient is a good candidate (age, motivation, compliance) and prism would only compensate without building capacity. See B5-01 for the criteria.

7. **"Parks 3-step test works for horizontal deviations."**
   Parks 3-step is for VERTICAL deviations only. It identifies paretic cyclovertical muscles. For horizontal phoria types (CI, CE, etc.), classification comes from the pattern of distance vs near deviation and the AC/A ratio.

## Practice Questions

**Q1.** A 10-year-old presents with blurred near vision, frequent headaches, and NPC of 14cm (break). Near PFC blur is 8Δ. Sheard's criterion is NOT met. Full refractive correction has been given. The most appropriate next management step is:

A. Add base-out prism to spectacles
B. Prescribe base-in prism at near
C. Begin vision therapy with convergence exercises
D. Refer for strabismus surgery

<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**C — Vision therapy with convergence exercises**

The findings indicate convergence insufficiency (NPC > 10cm, reduced near PFC). Full correction was already given (Rung 1). The child is 10 years old — a good VT candidate. Sheard's failure in a child with CI is the prime indication for VT. Base-out prism at near (B) would worsen convergence demand. BI prism (A) compensates but does not train. Surgery (D) is for tropias, not phoria anomalies.
</details>

---

**Q2.** A patient has right hypertropia. The deviation is largest in left gaze. On Bielschowsky head tilt to the RIGHT, the deviation INCREASES. Which muscle is paretic?

A. Right Superior Oblique
B. Right Inferior Rectus
C. Left Superior Rectus
D. Left Inferior Oblique

<details><summary>Answer</summary>
**B — Right Inferior Rectus**

Parks 3-step: Right hyper → 4 candidates (RSO, RIR, LIO, LSR). Larger in LEFT gaze → narrows to RIR or LSR (the muscles with field of action in left gaze for the right hyper group — IR acts in adduction which is left gaze for OD). Head tilt to RIGHT (toward higher eye, OD) INCREASES the deviation → the ipsilateral intortor (RSO) cannot compensate, but wait — if RSO was paretic, tilt right would increase because SO fails; but we already narrowed to RIR or LSR. Tilt RIGHT increases → RIR is paretic (tilt toward higher eye + increase = the depressor of the higher eye is at fault). LSR would show decrease on right tilt.
</details>

## Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue → rule → trap`.

**JCC axis/power** — cue: which dot position the patient prefers.
- Prefers **P1** (red dots ∥ tentative axis) → cyl under-powered → **add minus cyl**. Prefers **P2** (white dots ∥ axis) → **reduce minus**.
- Trap: adding cylinder when the patient actually preferred P2.

**Duochrome** — cue: red side vs green side clearer.
- **Green clearer** → over-minused → add plus / reduce minus. **Red clearer** → add minus. (RAM-GAP: Red Add Minus, Green Add Plus.)
- Trap: over-minusing a patient who says green is clearer.

**Cover test phoria vs tropia** — cue: movement on cover vs without cover.
- Moves **only when covered** (recovers on uncover) = phoria. Moves **without cover** = tropia.
- Trap: calling a phoria a tropia because the eye moved — a phoria only moves under cover.

**Prism for phoria** — cue: exo / eso / hyper.
- Exophoria → **BI**, esophoria → **BO**, hyperphoria → **BD before the higher eye**.
- Trap: base-out for exo (it's base-in).

**Retinoscopy reflex** — cue: with vs against motion.
- **With** motion → add plus (under-plussed/hyperopic). **Against** → add minus. Neutral = endpoint (+ working distance).
- Trap: forgetting to subtract the working-distance lens at the end.
