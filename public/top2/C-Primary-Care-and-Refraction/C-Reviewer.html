C1 — Epidemiology and Prevention (Public Health Optometry)

The Simple Version

Public health is care aimed at populations, not single patients. Where the rest of your training treats the person in the chair, this note zooms out to the whole community: who goes blind, why, how common it is, and how we prevent it.

Three ideas carry most of the marks:

  1. The vocabulary of epidemiology (epidemic vs pandemic vs endemic; incidence vs prevalence; morbidity vs mortality).
  2. The three levels of prevention (primary, secondary, tertiary), which map onto three levels of eye care.
  3. The numbers (global and Philippine blindness statistics and their leading causes).

Teaching angle: the single most repeated fact in this topic is that the two leading causes of vision impairment worldwide are uncorrected refractive error and cataract, and that over 80% of vision impairment is avoidable (preventable or curable). If you remember nothing else, remember that.

But Why?

Why does optometry belong in public health at all?

Because the biggest cause of avoidable blindness is uncorrected refractive error, and correcting refractive error is exactly what optometrists do. A pair of glasses is a public-health intervention. That is why the State (RA 8050) calls optometry part of primary health care.

Why is "avoidable" the key word, not "blind"?

Because over 80% of vision impairment can be prevented or cured. The tragedy is not that these conditions exist but that they go untreated: only about a third of refractive-error patients and a sixth of cataract patients get the care they need. The public-health job is closing that gap.

Why distinguish incidence from prevalence?

Incidence counts new cases over a period (the flow); prevalence counts all existing cases at a point (the pool). A chronic disease like glaucoma has low incidence but high prevalence because cases accumulate. Mixing them up changes the meaning of any statistic.

Why three levels of prevention?

Because you can intervene before, during, or after a disease starts. Primary stops it beginning, secondary catches it early and reverses it, tertiary limits the damage once it is established. Naming the level tells you what action is appropriate.

The Details

Core Definitions

  • Public health (Winslow): the science and art of preventing disease, prolonging life, and promoting health through organized community effort.
  • Public health optometry: using the full scope of optometry to prevent disease, prolong life, and promote health for groups of people at the community level.
  • Health (WHO): a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease.
  • Visual hygiene: the science and art of conserving and promoting healthful, comfortable vision.
  • Hygiene: the science of preserving health; the rules designed to promote it.
  • Visual sanitation: the principles for understanding the causes and sources of ocular infection and disease.
  • Sanitation: sanitary measures; cleanliness, freedom from dirt and germs.

Three goals of public health (Winslow follow-on): reduce discomfort, disability, and disease in society; promote quality of life; attain the highest level of physical, mental, and social well-being with available resources.

Emphasis of public health: prevention of disease, the health of groups (not individuals), control of communicable infection, education in personal hygiene, and organizing services for early diagnosis and preventive treatment.

Three Levels of Eye Care

  • Primary eye care: community level. Promote eye hygiene, measure and record VA, refer subnormal vision and blindness, detect disease early, give simple treatment, refer severe cases, join immunization and blindness-prevention drives.
  • Secondary eye care: hospitals without large technical facilities; ophthalmologists; promote prevention and train primary-level personnel.
  • Tertiary eye care: the full complex of medical and surgical care by ophthalmology specialists with advanced technology; institutes of ophthalmology; train personnel at other levels.

Organizations

  • WHO: UN specialized agency for health; established April 7, 1948; governed by 192 member states through the World Health Assembly (approves programme and the biennial budget). Goal: highest possible level of health for all.
  • DOH: principal health agency of the Philippines; both stakeholder and regulator. Structure: ~17 central offices, 16 Centers for Health Development (regional), 70 hospitals, and 4 attached agencies: PhilHealth (national health insurance), Dangerous Drugs Board, Philippine Institute of Traditional and Alternative Health Care, and Philippine National AIDS Council.
  • PhilHealth: adequate and affordable social health insurance for all Filipinos.
  • SSS: protects members against socially recognized hazards.

Global Burden (WHO)

  • 2.2 billion people have a vision impairment; at least 1 billion of these was preventable or is still unaddressed.
  • Leading causes worldwide: uncorrected refractive error and cataract.
  • Over 80% of all vision impairment is avoidable (preventable or curable).
  • Access gap: only ~36% of refractive-error and ~17% of cataract patients get appropriate care.
  • Most affected are people over 50; at-risk groups are the over-50s and children under 15.
  • By 2050, blindness could reach ~115 million (from 38.5 million in 2020) due to population growth and aging.
  • Annual global cost of lost productivity: ~US$411 billion.
  • Vision disorders are the second most prevalent chronic health problem in the population.

Single largest unaddressed cause: presbyopia (826 million), even though presbyopia is not what people first name. Of the ≥1 billion with moderate-to-severe impairment, the rank by count is: unaddressed presbyopia 826M → uncorrected refractive error 123.7M → un-operated cataract 65.2M → glaucoma 6.9M → corneal opacity 4.2M → diabetic retinopathy 3M → trachoma 2M.

Other global counts (WHO 2019): myopia ~2.6 billion (2020); glaucoma 76 million (40–80 yrs); AMD 195.6 million; diabetic retinopathy 146 million.

Practice Questions

1. A disease is constantly present in a community at a low, steady frequency. This pattern is:

  • A. Epidemic
  • B. Pandemic
  • C. Endemic
  • D. Sporadic
Answer

C. Endemic. Constant low-level presence in a locality. An epidemic is an outbreak above expected levels; a pandemic is an epidemic spread across regions.

2. The two leading causes of vision impairment worldwide are:

  • A. Glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy
  • B. Uncorrected refractive error and cataract
  • C. Trachoma and corneal opacity
  • D. AMD and glaucoma
Answer

B. Uncorrected refractive error and cataract. Both are largely avoidable, which is why over 80% of vision impairment is preventable or curable.

3. Conducting community vision screening and health education to stop disease before it starts is which level of prevention?

  • A. Primary
  • B. Secondary
  • C. Tertiary
  • D. Quaternary
Answer

A. Primary. Primary prevention acts before onset. Secondary is early detection and reversal; tertiary limits established disability (e.g., low-vision rehab).

4. In the Philippines, the leading cause of bilateral blindness is:

  • A. Glaucoma
  • B. Refractive error
  • C. Cataract
  • D. Diabetic retinopathy
Answer

C. Cataract (about a third of cases), followed by errors of refraction, then glaucoma.

5. A statistic counts only the new cases of glaucoma diagnosed in a region during one year. This measures:

  • A. Prevalence
  • B. Incidence
  • C. Mortality
  • D. Endemicity
Answer

B. Incidence. New cases over a defined period. Prevalence would count all existing cases at a point in time.

6. In the Philippines, the leading cause of bilateral low vision (as opposed to blindness) is:

  • A. Cataract
  • B. Errors of refraction
  • C. Glaucoma
  • D. Diabetic retinopathy
Answer

B. Errors of refraction (~43%), with cataract second (~34%). The order flips from blindness, where cataract leads.

7. Which of the following does WHO list as a condition that does not typically cause vision impairment?

  • A. Trachoma
  • B. Corneal opacity
  • C. Pterygium
  • D. Diabetic retinopathy
Answer

C. Pterygium. Along with blepharitis, chalazion/stye, conjunctivitis, dry eye, and subconjunctival hemorrhage. The other three are on the list of conditions that can cause VI.

C2-01 — Ametropia and Refractive Errors

The Simple Version

Ametropia means the eye does not focus parallel rays exactly on the retina with accommodation relaxed. That one-sentence definition covers every refractive error: myopia, hyperopia, and astigmatism all share this root. The cause is always one of four variables — axial length, corneal power, lens power, or anterior chamber depth — being wrong for each other. Presbyopia is different: it is not an error of focus but a loss of the focusing range itself, caused by progressive stiffening of the crystalline lens. Understanding each condition means knowing which variable is off, in which direction, and how much the correction has to shift the far point back to infinity.

Teaching angle: Ask a class — "If I surgically make the cornea flatter on a myope, will their prescription go up or down, and why?" This question forces the student to connect corneal curvature to power to far point to correction — which is the entire logic chain of C2.

Cross-reference: The optical physics behind ametropia (Gullstrand's schematic eye, axial length, n-to-power rule, far point concept) are fully built in D1-01, Gullstrand section. Read that note first if the physical optics feel unclear. C2 builds the clinical layer on top of that foundation.

But Why?

Why do hyperopes get headaches when reading but myopes rarely do?

Because hyperopes must accommodate just to see distance clearly.

Why must hyperopes accommodate at distance?

Because their far point is virtual — located behind the eye — so even parallel rays from infinity require extra converging power to land on the retina.

Why does that matter for headaches?

Because the ciliary muscle is contracting continuously: at distance to see clearly, and then contracting further at near for the additional demand. The muscle never fully rests.

Why does the ciliary muscle have to work continuously in hyperopia?

Because uncorrected hyperopia means the eye's resting power is insufficient. The eye compensates by using accommodation as a permanent supplement instead of reserving it for near only.

Why does myopia not do the same thing?

Because a myope's far point is in front of the eye. At near, objects fall naturally closer to the far point. A low myope often needs less accommodation at near than an emmetrope does — the refractive error accidentally assists near vision.

Lookalike trap: Hyperopia vs Hypermetropia — identical condition, different names by convention. Boards use hyperopia; some older sources say hypermetropia. Do not treat them as different things.

Lookalike trap: Myopia vs Pseudomyopia — real myopia is structural (too-long eye); pseudomyopia is functional (ciliary spasm mimics myopia). Pseudomyopia resolves under cycloplegia. Real myopia does not.

How It Works — The Mechanism

The two-variable system: Every refractive state is the result of a mismatch between the eye's POWER and its LENGTH.

EMMETROPIA: POWER matches LENGTH → F2 lands exactly on retina
MYOPIA: POWER too strong → F2 in front of retina
 OR LENGTH too long → retina is past F2
HYPEROPIA: POWER too weak → F2 would be behind retina
 OR LENGTH too short → retina is before F2
ASTIGMATISM: POWER unequal in meridians → two line foci, not one point

Classification by mechanism:

  • Axial ametropia — axial length is the variable; refractive media are normal
  • Refractive ametropia — axial length is normal; power of the media is wrong
  • Curvature type: corneal or lenticular radius is abnormal
  • Index type: index of refraction of a medium is abnormal

Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

Hofstetter's Formulas — Amplitude of Accommodation by Age

Minimum AOA = 15 − 0.25 × age
Average AOA = 18.5 − 0.30 × age
Maximum AOA = 25 − 0.40 × age

(AOA in diopters; age in years)

What it states in plain language: A person's ability to focus at near (accommodation amplitude) decreases predictably and linearly with age. Hofstetter gives three reference curves — minimum expected, average, and maximum — so a clinician can compare a patient's measured amplitude to what is normal for their age.

What each part means:

  • 15, 18.5, 25 — the y-intercepts; theoretical amplitude at age 0. These are not biologically real but are the mathematical starting points fitted to population data.
  • 0.25, 0.30, 0.40 — the slopes; how many diopters of amplitude are lost per year of age. The minimum curve loses the least per year; the maximum curve loses the fastest per year.
  • Age — the independent variable. Accommodation loss is approximately linear over the clinical range (ages 10 to 70).
  • The three formulas describe the distribution: minimum = worst 10th percentile for age; average = 50th percentile; maximum = 90th percentile.

Where it comes from: Henry Hofstetter (1950) revised Donders' original lookup table by fitting a continuous linear regression to population data across ages. The advantage over Donders' table is that you can calculate an expected value for any exact age rather than looking up the nearest listed age. The linear relationship holds because lens stiffening progresses at a roughly constant rate through the clinical range.

The analogy that makes it click: Think of a rubber band that is slowly losing its elasticity with age. At age 10, it stretches far (12–19D). By age 45, it barely stretches (3–4D). By age 75, it barely moves (0–0.5D). The minimum formula is the rubber band that started out weakest; the maximum is the one that started strongest. All three lose elasticity at slightly different rates, but they all lose it.

Why this form and not another: The linear form works because presbyopia is not an on/off event — it is a gradual process. Using a curve or exponential would be harder to apply clinically and is not justified by the population data in the clinically relevant age range. The three-formula system captures the biologic variability without requiring case-by-case measurement of every healthy patient.

What it does NOT explain:

  • Does not account for medications (atropine, cycloplegics, miotics artificially alter AOA)
  • Does not account for systemic disease (diabetes, myasthenia gravis, Graves' disease)
  • Does not account for refractive error (a myope's effective near point is different from an emmetrope's)
  • The Hofstetter formula gives expected amplitude, not the actual clinical add power needed

Cross-subject connections:

  • C6 (Management of Visual Disorders): The presbyopia ADD is calculated from the AOA using the middle-third rule: Add = WD − ½ AOA. The Hofstetter formula gives you the AOA needed for that calculation.
  • B5 (BV + Fusion): AC/A ratio management depends on knowing how much accommodation the patient is using. Hofstetter tells you the expected amplitude — patients using more than their expected AOA for their age are compensating, which affects the convergence demand analysis.

AC/A Ratio — Accommodative Convergence per Accommodation

AC/A = change in convergence (prism diopters) / change in accommodation (diopters)

Normal: 3:1 to 5:1 (i.e., 3–5Δ of convergence per 1D of accommodation)

What it states in plain language: For every diopter of accommodation the eyes produce, a fixed amount of convergence is generated automatically. This ratio describes how tightly convergence is coupled to accommodation in a given patient.

What each part means:

  • AC (accommodative convergence) — the convergence that automatically occurs when the ciliary muscle contracts; measured in prism diopters
  • A (accommodation) — the change in lens power; measured in diopters
  • Ratio — a fixed physiological constant for each patient; it does not change with the amount of stimulus, only with age (decreases slightly as presbyopia develops)

Where it comes from: The AC/A ratio reflects the neurological coupling between the accommodation and vergence systems. Both are driven by blur and proximity signals that travel through the same neural channels. Maddox (1893) first described the relationship; Donders and later Alpern quantified it clinically.

Why this matters: A high AC/A (>5:1) means excessive convergence relative to accommodation — the patient over-converges when focusing at near, producing esophoria or esotropia at near. A low AC/A (<3:1) means too little convergence relative to accommodation — the patient under-converges, producing exophoria at near.

Cross-subject connections:

  • B5 (BV + Fusion): AC/A is used in the OEP/SOAP method. Morgan's norms use the AC/A to predict expected phoria shifts from distance to near. Sheard's criterion depends on knowing the vergence demand, which is set by the AC/A.
  • C6 (Management): Treating accommodative esotropia in children — the plus lens add reduces accommodation, which via the high AC/A reduces convergence, which reduces the esotropia. The AC/A ratio is the mechanism that makes plus lens treatment work.

Retinoscopy Working Distance Correction

Net retinoscopy finding = Gross finding − (1 / working distance in meters)

Working distance 67 cm (0.67 m) → subtract 1.50D
Working distance 50 cm (0.50 m) → subtract 2.00D
Working distance 40 cm (0.40 m) → subtract 2.50D

What it states in plain language: Because the examiner is not at optical infinity when performing retinoscopy, the gross finding includes a built-in amount of myopia equal to the vergence at the working distance. Subtracting the working distance correction converts the gross finding to the actual refractive error.

What each part means:

  • Gross finding — the lens power that produces neutral motion at the working distance; this neutralizes the patient's retina with the examiner's peephole as the target
  • Working distance — distance from the examiner's peephole to the patient's eye, in meters
  • 1/WD — the vergence at the working distance; this is the "extra" myopia the gross finding contains
  • Net finding — the actual refractive error, equivalent to what would be found if the examiner were at optical infinity

Where it comes from: The examiner is not at infinity. Light returning from the patient's retina is diverging at 1/WD diopters when it reaches the examiner's peephole. Neutral motion occurs when the examiner's eye is conjugate with the patient's retina, which happens when the gross power placed in front of the patient's eye makes the patient's far point equal to the working distance.

The analogy: The working distance correction is like zeroing a scale before weighing. The gross finding includes the instrument's position as part of the measurement. The correction removes the positional offset so you read the actual error.

Cross-subject connections:

  • C4 (Objective Examination): The working distance correction is applied at every retinoscopy encounter. 67 cm is the standard working distance (room-sized lanes); 50 cm is used in smaller exam rooms.
  • D1-01 (Geometric Optics): The correction is just a vergence calculation: vergence at 0.67m = 1/0.67 = 1.50D. The same D = 1/f formula underpins both.

Practice Questions

1. A 7-year-old is referred for an eye examination. The parents report that the child squints to see the blackboard but reads comfortably at near. Retinoscopy reveals AGAINST motion in all meridians at 67 cm working distance. Which lens is most likely to neutralize the reflex?

  • A. +1.50 DS
  • B. +3.00 DS
  • C. −1.50 DS
  • D. −3.00 DS
Answer

D. −3.00 DS. Against motion at 67cm (1.50D WD) means the patient is myopic by more than 1.50D. If the gross finding is −1.50D (the value that produces neutral), the net finding = −1.50 + (−1.50) = −3.00D. Against motion requires MINUS lenses for neutralization. A and B are wrong because plus lenses are for with motion. C is the gross finding, not the net finding.

2. A 45-year-old emmetrope complains that she needs to hold newspapers further away to read comfortably. Using Hofstetter's average formula, what is her expected accommodative amplitude?

  • A. 3.75 D
  • B. 5.00 D
  • C. 5.00 D (minimum)
  • D. 4.00 D
Answer

B. 5.00 D (average). Average AOA = 18.5 − 0.30 × 45 = 18.5 − 13.5 = 5.00 D. This is the average expected amplitude. Minimum = 15 − 0.25 × 45 = 15 − 11.25 = 3.75D. Maximum = 25 − 0.40 × 45 = 25 − 18 = 7.00D. The question asks for the average Hofstetter formula result, so the answer is 5.00D.

C3-01 — Preliminary Examination

The Simple Version

The preliminary examination is everything done before you pick up a retinoscope. It gives you a snapshot of the patient's functional vision status: how clearly they see, how their pupils behave, how their eyes move and align, and how close they can focus. Each test answers a specific clinical question. When you understand the question each test is answering, you stop memorizing procedures and start reasoning through what any abnormal finding means. A case history is not small talk — it is a diagnostic tool. The first test is observation. Everything follows from there.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "If a patient walks in and you could do only one preliminary test before the refraction, which would it be and why?" The argument forces them to think about what information is irreplaceable and what builds on what. Visual acuity is the answer — it is the baseline against which every other finding is interpreted.

But Why?

Why is the case history done first, before any tests?

Because it directs which tests are necessary.

Why does the chief complaint determine the test sequence?

Because a patient with diplopia needs motility and cover testing prioritized. A patient with blur needs VA and refraction first. Without the history, you run every test without knowing which finding matters.

Why does the history have to proceed from general to specific?

Because asking specific questions first introduces bias. A patient asked "is your headache in your forehead?" will say yes even if it is not. Open-ended first, then focused.

Why is the preliminary examination done before the refraction?

Because several preliminary findings directly modify the refraction approach. A reduced NPC tells you to add convergence testing to the BV analysis. A positive Marcus Gunn tells you to investigate the optic nerve before attributing poor VA to refractive error. Pupil anomalies change your pharmacology decisions. The preliminary examination is the map that tells you where the refraction will lead.

Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

Near PD Calculation — Formula 2 (Simplified)

NPD = (DPD × WD) / (WD + 27)

DPD = far (distance) PD in mm
WD = near working distance in mm
27 = constant (13.5mm behind cornea to center of rotation + 13.5mm spectacle plane)

What it states in plain language: As the eyes converge for near, they physically move closer together. The near PD is always smaller than the distance PD. This formula calculates how much the PD decreases based on the working distance.

What each part means:

  • DPD — the measured distance PD; the baseline measurement when the eyes are parallel
  • WD — the patient's intended reading distance in mm; closer working distance = greater convergence = smaller near PD
  • 27 — accounts for the location of the eyes' center of rotation relative to the spectacle plane; standard anatomical constant (13.5mm × 2)
  • The formula says: the near PD is proportional to the working distance, scaled by the total distance from the lens to the eye's rotation point

The analogy: Think of two spotlights on ceiling tracks. When they both point straight ahead (infinity), they are maximally separated (distance PD). When you angle them to shine at a point 40cm below on the floor, they tilt inward — the beams are now closer at the floor (near PD). The tighter the target, the more they cross inward.

Cross-subject connections:

  • D2-01 (Dispensing): The near PD is critical for bifocal and near lens decentration. Wrong near PD creates unwanted prism at the reading position.
  • B5 (BV): Convergence demand is set by the working distance relative to the PD. This formula is the basis for calculating how many prism diopters of convergence the patient must maintain at near.

Amplitude of Convergence

Amp. of Conv. (Δ) = (NPD in cm × 10) / (NPC in cm + 3)

NPD = near pupillary distance in cm
NPC = near point of convergence distance from bridge of nose, in cm
3 = distance from bridge of nose to the spectacle plane (constant)

What it states in plain language: The amplitude of convergence is the maximum amount of convergence available to the patient, expressed in prism diopters.

Why the "+3": The NPC is measured from the bridge of the nose. The spectacle plane is approximately 13mm in front of the center of rotation. The "+3" converts NPC from the bridge-of-nose reference to the spectacle plane reference.

Clinical note: Normal amplitude of convergence varies but a rough clinical norm is 30–60 prism diopters for a young adult. The actual demand at a given working distance can be calculated separately.

Hirschberg Method — Corneal Reflex to Prism Diopters

Corneal reflex displacement → Approximate deviation:
 Slightly nasal (0.5mm) = physiologic (+angle kappa, 5°, ~10Δ)
 At pupillary margin = 15° = 30 prism diopters
 Between pupil and limbus = 30° = 60 prism diopters
 At limbus = 45° = 90 prism diopters

What it states in plain language: Each millimeter the corneal light reflex is displaced from its normal nasal position represents approximately 15 prism diopters of deviation (or equivalently, 7 degrees).

Interpretation direction:

  • Reflex displaced NASALLY beyond normal → ESO deviation (eye deviated inward)
  • Reflex displaced TEMPORALLY → EXO deviation (eye deviated outward)
  • Reflex displaced UP → HYPO deviation; displaced DOWN → HYPER deviation

Cross-subject connections:

  • F2 (Pediatric): Hirschberg is the go-to alignment test for young children who cannot cooperate with cover testing.
  • D1 (Prisms): The 1mm = 7° = ~15Δ relationship follows from the geometry of the corneal reflex — the same principle as the prismatic deviation formula.

Practice Questions

1. During a preliminary examination, the examiner covers the patient's right eye. The uncovered left eye is observed to move inward (nasally). Upon removing the occluder from the right eye, the left eye moves back outward. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  • A. Left esotropia
  • B. Left exophoria
  • C. Left esophoria
  • D. Right exotropia
Answer

B. Left exophoria. The uncovered left eye moved INWARD (nasal) when the right was covered. This means the left eye wanted to drift outward (exo direction) when fusion was released by covering. When the cover was removed, the left eye moved back out to its normal position — consistent with a PHORIA (latent deviation controlled by fusion). The inward movement of the left eye when uncovered means the left eye was EXO under cover. Since it corrected on uncovering and there was no movement on the unilateral cover test, this is a phoria, not a tropia.

2. A patient's VA is OD 20/60, OS 20/20. Pinhole placed over the right eye improves VA to 20/30 but not to 20/20. The most likely explanation is:

  • A. The right eye has a purely organic pathology
  • B. The right eye has an uncorrected refractive error only
  • C. The right eye has both an uncorrected refractive error AND an organic component
  • D. The pinhole result is invalid — a 20/60 eye should always reach 20/20 through pinhole
Answer

C. The right eye has both an uncorrected refractive error AND an organic component. Pinhole improvement indicates a refractive component (since aberrations were reduced and VA improved from 20/60 to 20/30). However, 20/20 was not reached, indicating the improvement is incomplete — an organic component (amblyopia, early cataract, macular pathology) is also limiting the final VA. Pure refractive error alone would be expected to reach 20/20 (or close to it) through pinhole. A = wrong because some improvement occurred. B = wrong because the improvement was incomplete.

The Simple Version

The objective examination is everything done without asking the patient for verbal feedback — you observe, measure, and record. It has four core tools: the slit lamp (biomicroscope), the ophthalmoscope, the keratometer, and the retinoscope. Each answers a different question. The slit lamp shows you the anterior segment in layered detail. The ophthalmoscope shows you the posterior segment and media. The keratometer measures corneal curvature and tells you how much of the patient's astigmatism comes from the cornea. The retinoscope gives you the refractive error without asking the patient a single question. A clinician who masters all four can complete a full workup on a patient who cannot communicate at all.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "If you had to choose one objective test for a newborn with suspected cataracts, which would you use and why?" The answer forces them to match the tool to the clinical question: ophthalmoscopy for media opacity, retinoscopy for refraction, slit lamp for anterior segment detail.

Cross-reference: Retinoscopy principles (movements, working distance, gross vs net) are fully covered in C2-01. This note covers the slit lamp, ophthalmoscopy, and keratometry in full, and cross-references C2-01 for retinoscopy.

But Why?

Why does the slit lamp use a narrow beam of light instead of a broad one?

Because a narrow slit creates an optical cross-section of the tissue, like slicing through it. You see each layer separately — epithelium, stroma, endothelium — rather than a blurred total thickness.

Why does indirect ophthalmoscopy give a wider field but lower magnification than direct?

Because the condensing lens forms an aerial real image of the fundus in the space between the lens and the examiner. The magnification depends on the ratio of the lens power to the eye's power. Higher lens power = shorter focal length = image formed closer = smaller, wider-field image. The binocular design adds stereo depth, which direct cannot provide.

Why does the keratometer treat the cornea as a convex mirror?

Because the tear film over the anterior cornea is a reflective surface. The mire image size depends on the radius of curvature of that mirror — the same physics as any convex mirror. A steeper cornea (smaller radius) produces a smaller mire image; a flatter cornea produces a larger one. Measuring image size gives you radius.

Why does Javal's rule include a fixed −0.50 DC × 90° term?

Because the posterior corneal surface and tilted crystalline lens together contribute a small, predictable amount of against-the-rule astigmatism (approximately −0.50D at 90°) to every eye. This internal astigmatism is not measured by the keratometer, which reads only the anterior cornea. Javal's rule adds this constant correction to convert corneal astigmatism to total refractive astigmatism.

How It Works — The Mechanism

Slit lamp: how the optical section works

The slit lamp is a biomicroscope with a coupled illumination system. The illumination arm and the microscope arm share a common focal point. When the beam is narrow (optic section), it passes through the tissue like a plane of light — each layer scatters slightly differently, allowing the examiner to distinguish the epithelium, stroma, and endothelium of the cornea as visually distinct zones. The illumination arm can be decoupled from the microscope arm for indirect and retro-illumination techniques.

Ophthalmoscope: how the image forms

  • Direct: The instrument illuminates the retina through the pupil. The examiner looks through a peephole and a series of correcting lenses. The retina acts as a self-luminous object. The image formed is virtual and erect — as if looking at the fundus through a +15D magnifying glass. No aerial image exists between the patient and the examiner.
  • Indirect: The condensing lens held in front of the patient's eye takes the diverging light from the illuminated retina and forms a real, inverted, aerial image of the fundus in space between the lens and the examiner. The examiner then looks at this aerial image with the headborne binocular ophthalmoscope. The image is real and inverted because the condensing lens is acting like the objective of a microscope.

Keratometer: how the corneal radius is measured

The tear film over the anterior cornea acts as a convex mirror. When the keratometer projects a mire (target image) at a known distance and size, it measures the size of the reflected virtual image in the corneal mirror. Since the size of the virtual image in a convex mirror depends on the radius of curvature, measuring image size gives radius:

f = r/2 (for mirrors: focal length = half the radius)
For corneal power: D = (n − 1) / r = 0.3375 / r (using reduced corneal index 1.3375)
r in meters → D in diopters

The keratometer then converts the radius to corneal power in diopters. The instrument requires a doubling device (prism or mirror) to stabilize the mire image during measurement.

Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

Javal's Rule — Corneal to Total Astigmatism

At = 1.25 (Ac) + (−0.50 Cyl × 90°)

At = total (refractive) astigmatism
Ac = corneal astigmatism (from keratometry)
1.25 = empirical scaling factor
−0.50 Cyl × 90° = constant internal astigmatism contribution (ATR direction)

What it states in plain language: The total astigmatism of the eye is approximately 1.25 times the corneal astigmatism, minus a fixed 0.50D against-the-rule correction. The correction accounts for the astigmatism that comes from internal structures (back of the cornea, tilted lens) that the keratometer cannot see.

What each part means:

  • At — the total refractive astigmatism you would measure by retinoscopy or subjective refraction. This is what goes into the actual spectacle Rx.
  • Ac — corneal astigmatism only, measured by keratometry. The keratometer reads only the anterior corneal surface.
  • 1.25 — empirically derived scaling factor. The cornea typically contributes approximately 80% of the total astigmatism, but it is scaled up slightly because the keratometer reads only one surface and uses a simplified index.
  • −0.50 Cyl × 90° — the internal astigmatism constant. Internal structures (posterior corneal surface, lens tilt) add approximately 0.50D of ATR astigmatism consistently across the population.

Where it comes from: Emile Javal and Schiotz (19th century) observed that in most eyes, the total refractive astigmatism measured subjectively was predictably different from the corneal astigmatism measured by keratometry. They derived an empirical formula to bridge the two. The correction factor (−0.50 × 90°) represents the statistical average of internal astigmatism across large populations.

The analogy: Javal's rule is like estimating the total weight of a package from the weight of the box alone, knowing that bubble wrap always adds approximately 0.5kg. You weigh the box (Ac), multiply by your scale factor (1.25), and subtract 0.5kg (internal component). You never weigh the bubble wrap directly — but you know its average contribution.

Why this form and not another: If you used Ac directly without the internal correction, you would consistently over-correct the astigmatism in WTR eyes and under-correct ATR eyes. The −0.50 Cyl × 90° term is what makes the formula accurate for both WTR and ATR astigmatism.

What it does NOT explain:

  • Not valid for keratoconus or irregular corneas (the relationship between corneal and internal astigmatism breaks down)
  • Not valid when the crystalline lens has significant lenticular astigmatism (e.g., nuclear sclerosis with lens tilt)
  • Empirical estimate only — specific patients may deviate significantly from the predicted At

Cross-subject connections:

  • C2-01 (Astigmatism): Javal's rule predicts the starting cylinder power for the subjective refraction. Knowing Ac from keratometry means you enter the refraction with a predicted Rx rather than starting blind.
  • D3-01 (Contact Lenses): In soft CL fitting, residual astigmatism = At − Ac. Javal's rule gives a quick estimate of how much astigmatism remains after placing a spherical soft lens (which masks Ac but not the internal component).

Van Herick Anterior Chamber Angle Grading

Slit beam at 60° to the cornea; compare SHADOW width (AC depth) to OPTIC SECTION width (corneal thickness)

Grade 4: Shadow ≥ 1/2 corneal thickness → Ratio 1:1 → Open angle → Safe
Grade 3: Shadow = 1/4 to 1/2 corneal thickness → Ratio 1:2 → Open angle → Safe
Grade 2: Shadow = 1/4 corneal thickness → Ratio 1:4 → Narrow angle → Caution
Grade 1: Shadow < 1/4 corneal thickness → < 1:4 → At risk for angle closure
Grade 0: No shadow (cornea "sits" on iris) → Angle closed → Angle closure attack

What it states in plain language: The Van Herick technique compares the depth of the anterior chamber angle at the limbus to the thickness of the cornea, using the shadow formed between the corneal and iris reflex beams. A deeper AC angle means a lower risk of angle-closure glaucoma.

Clinical use:

  • Grades 0–1: dilating eye drops are CONTRAINDICATED (risk of precipitating angle closure)
  • Grade 2: dilate with caution and monitor
  • Grades 3–4: dilating drops are generally safe

  • E4 (Emergencies): Acute angle closure glaucoma presents as Grade 0 on Van Herick — halos, pain, elevated IOP, shallow AC.

Practice Questions

1. A 55-year-old patient has keratometry readings of OD: 43.50 @ 180 / 45.50 @ 90. Using Javal's rule, what is the expected total (refractive) astigmatism?

  • A. −2.00 Cyl × 180°
  • B. −2.00 Cyl × 90°
  • C. −2.00 Cyl × 180° combined with internal correction
  • D. −2.00 Cyl × 180° (Ac = 2.00D; At ≈ −2.00D WTR)
Answer

Working:

Ac = 45.50 − 43.50 = 2.00D. Weakest meridian is at 180° → Ac = −2.00 Cyl × 180° (WTR).

At = 1.25(−2.00 × 180) + (−0.50 × 90) = (−2.50 × 180) + (−0.50 × 90) = This cross-cylinder combination: resolves to −2.00 Cyl × 180° approximately.

Working through the cross-cylinder: −2.50 Cyl × 180 = −2.50 Cyl × 180; and −0.50 × 90 partially cancels the WTR component. The net total astigmatism is approximately −2.00 Cyl × 180°.

D is correct. The internal astigmatism (−0.50 × 90°) partially cancels the WTR corneal component in Javal's rule, reducing the predicted total slightly from 2.50D to ~2.00D.

2. During slit lamp examination of a patient being evaluated for dilation, the Van Herick shadow width appears equal to about one-quarter of the corneal optic section width. Which of the following actions is most appropriate?

  • A. Proceed with dilation — the angle is wide open
  • B. Dilate with caution and monitor for angle closure
  • C. Do not dilate — the angle is closed
  • D. Measure IOP before deciding, then dilate freely if normal
Answer

B. Dilate with caution and monitor for angle closure. A shadow width equal to 1/4 of the corneal optic section width corresponds to Van Herick Grade 2 (ratio 1:4). This indicates a narrow anterior chamber angle — not closed, but at risk. Dilation is not absolutely contraindicated but must be done with caution, the patient should be monitored, and the IOP checked after dilation. Grade 0–1 = absolute contraindication. Grades 3–4 = safe to dilate. Grade 2 = proceed with caution.

C5-01 — Subjective Examination

The Simple Version

Subjective refraction is how you find a prescription by asking the patient "which is clearer?" You can't see what the patient sees, so you use lenses to move the image around the retina, then let the patient report back. The whole method rests on one trick: fog the eye first by adding enough plus power so accommodation shuts down, then slowly reduce that plus until you reach the clearest lens possible.

After getting the best monocular lens for each eye, you balance the two eyes so neither is working harder accommodatively than the other. Then, in the OEP system, the result of that subjective refraction (Test #7) feeds directly into 14 more tests that map the full accommodation-convergence relationship.

Teaching angle: Ask the class: if you gave a patient the sharpest possible prescription right now, why might they still get headaches at near? (Because accommodation was engaged during refraction, pulling the endpoint toward too much minus. Fogging prevents this.)

But Why?

Why fog the eye before starting?

Because accommodation is an involuntary reflex to blur. If the retinal image is blurred, the ciliary muscle contracts to clear it. If accommodation is active, your refraction will find a lens that corrects blur plus the error introduced by the contracting ciliary muscle. The prescription ends up over-minused.

Why does plus power relax accommodation?

Plus moves the focal point in front of the retina, making the eye artificially myopic. There is no blur behind the retina, so no signal to accommodate. The ciliary muscle relaxes.

Why does the astigmatic dial work at all?

An uncorrected astigmatic eye has two principal meridians that focus at different distances. A fan chart line running parallel to the steeper meridian (power meridian) forms a sharp line on the retina while the perpendicular line stays blurred. The darkest line you see is pointing toward the power meridian.

Why must the eye be slightly under-fogged for the JCC test?

The JCC is a comparison test. Position 1 and Position 2 both have to be visible. If the eye is over-fogged, both positions look equally blurred and the patient cannot give a meaningful preference. The underfog keeps the contrast just detectable enough to compare.

Why balance binocularly after monocular refraction?

Monocular refraction finds the best lens for each eye independently, but the eyes don't work independently. After the occluder comes off, binocular vision can shift the accommodative state of both eyes toward whichever eye was left with more residual accommodation. Binocular balance removes that asymmetry before the final prescription is set.

How It Works — The Mechanism

The trigger: You need the patient's static refractive error — the refractive state with zero accommodation.

Monocular sequence:

  1. Occlude the non-test eye. Present the Snellen chart.
  2. If the patient can read the top letter, add plus until that letter blurs. If the patient cannot read it at all, add minus until they reach 20/30, then add +0.50 to +0.75D to re-fog to 20/30–20/40.
  3. Present the astigmatic dial (fan chart). The darkest line = power meridian. Axis = 90° from the darkest line.
  4. Add minus cylinder with axis perpendicular to the darkest line, in 0.25D steps. For every −0.50D cylinder added, add +0.25D sphere to keep the interval of Sturm centered on the retina. Stop when all lines appear equally dark.
  5. Confirm cylinder axis: JCC with handle parallel to the tentative axis. Flip. Rotate toward the red dot if patient prefers Position 1, toward the white dot if they prefer Position 2. Stop at equality.
  6. Refine cylinder power: JCC with red or white dot parallel to the axis. If patient prefers Position 1 (red dot || axis), add minus. If patient prefers Position 2, reduce minus. Stop at equality.
  7. Defog: reduce plus (or add minus) in 0.25D steps until best VA is reached.
  8. Sphere refinement: duochrome test. Equal = done. Green clearer = reduce minus or add plus. Red clearer = add minus or reduce plus.
  9. Record: monocular best VA + MPMVA.

Key players:

  • Ciliary muscle: contracts during accommodation; fogging inactivates it
  • Astigmatic dial (fan chart): externalizes the interval of Sturm so it is visible to the patient
  • JCC: flips between deliberate over- and under-correction states so the patient can locate the neutral point
  • Duochrome target: uses the eye's chromatic aberration as a built-in reference signal

Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

Theory: Chromatic Aberration — the mechanism behind the Duochrome test

Shorter wavelength (green, ~550 nm) → refracted more → focuses closer to lens
Longer wavelength (red, ~650 nm) → refracted less → focuses farther from lens
Gap between red and green focal points ≈ 0.50D in the human eye

What it states in plain language: Light of different colors bends by different amounts when it passes through a refractive medium. The cornea and lens behave the same way, so red and green light focus at different depths inside the eye. The duochrome test puts a known focal-point gap to clinical use.

What each part means:

  • Green (shorter wavelength, ~550 nm): bends more, focuses in front of the retina in an emmetropic eye
  • Red (longer wavelength, ~650 nm): bends less, focuses behind the retina in an emmetropic eye
  • The gap between these two focal points is approximately 0.50D
  • When the prescribed lens is correct, the retina sits between the two focal points, so both colors appear equally sharp

Where it comes from: Newton described chromatic dispersion in 1672. In clinical refraction, the duochrome test was designed to detect whether a sphere prescription is above or below the neutral point by exploiting the fixed 0.50D red-green focal gap. If you know which color focuses where, you can tell which side of the retina the focal point has fallen on.

The analogy: Think of red and green photons as two runners going through the same race course (the cornea and lens). The lighter runner (green) brakes earlier and stops closer to the start. The heavier runner (red) carries more momentum and travels farther before stopping. You cannot move the runners, but you can move the finish line (the retina, via lenses) until both runners stop at the same place. That is the sphere endpoint.

Why this form and not another: If red and green focused at the same point, there would be no differential signal and the test would be useless. A fixed 0.50D gap is clinically useful: large enough to detect with acuity-based comparison, small enough that the endpoint does not introduce its own error. A larger gap would force a coarser correction step.

What it does NOT explain:

  • Patients with red-green color vision deficiency cannot distinguish the two channels. The test is unreliable in this population.
  • The test assumes the eye is near the neutral point. A heavily fogged eye will show red clearer regardless of sphere power — fogging pulls the focal point in front of the retina, so the red channel wins by default. The test is only valid after fog is reduced to the working level.

Cross-subject connections:

  • Physiological Optics (B4): Color perception relies on trichromatic channel separation. The duochrome test uses only two channels — red and green — which is why the test fails in red-green color blindness (protanopia, deuteranopia).
  • Theoretical Optics (D1): Achromatic doublet lens design minimizes this same chromatic aberration that the duochrome test quantifies. In optics, it is corrected; in refraction, it is used as a tool.

Device / Rule: Jackson Cross-Cylinder (JCC)

JCC (±0.25D version):
 +0.25 cyl × 90 crossed with −0.25 cyl × 180
Optical equivalents:
 = +0.25S −0.50 cyl × 180
 = −0.25S +0.50 cyl × 90

What it states in plain language: The JCC is a lens with equal plus cylinder in one axis and equal minus cylinder in the perpendicular axis. Flipping it swaps which axis gets the plus and which gets the minus. This creates two comparison states — a deliberate overcorrection and a deliberate undercorrection — with the sphere equivalent held constant. The patient compares the two and reports which is clearer.

What each part means:

  • Red dots mark the minus cylinder axis of the JCC lens
  • White dots mark the plus cylinder axis of the JCC lens
  • Position 1 (red dot parallel to tentative axis): JCC minus cylinder is aligned with the patient's tentative axis = overcorrection + added fog
  • Position 2 (white dot parallel to tentative axis): JCC plus cylinder is aligned with the patient's tentative axis = undercorrection + less fog
  • Equality between positions = the tentative cylinder power or axis is correct

Where it comes from: Edward Jackson designed the lens in 1907 to replace the method of repeatedly changing cylinders in a trial frame to find the optimum. His insight was that a single flip of a pre-built compound lens creates the two comparison states needed, without disturbing the rest of the prescription.

The analogy: Imagine you are trying to find the exact position of a balance point on a plank. You shift 3 cm to the left, then 3 cm to the right. If the plank tips equally in both directions, you are already on the balance point. If it tips more to the right when you shift right, you need to move the balance point rightward. The JCC applies this logic to cylinder power and axis — a small over/under flip around the current setting until the two responses are equal.

Why this form and not another: The equal-and-opposite powers (e.g., both sides ±0.25D) are intentional. If the plus and minus components were unequal, flipping the lens would also shift the mean spherical equivalent, adding a confounding variable. The equal design keeps the spherical equivalent constant across both positions so that only the cylinder is being compared.

What it does NOT explain:

  • JCC is unreliable when VA is worse than 20/30. At that acuity level, the patient cannot perceive the contrast difference between the two positions.
  • Irregular astigmatism cannot be corrected by any cylindrical lens, so JCC findings in that population are meaningless.
  • Uncooperative or indecisive patients will produce inconsistent results.

Cross-subject connections:

  • Contact Lenses (D3): Toric contact lens axis refinement uses the same over-rotate / under-rotate comparison logic as the JCC axis check. The principle is identical; only the instrument differs.
  • Binocular Vision (B5): Accommodative convergence responds to the sphere equivalent of whatever lens is in front of the eye. Because the JCC flips change the sphere equivalent by 0.50D between positions, a patient with high AC/A may show phoria changes during the test — the JCC can inadvertently probe binocular function.

OEP 21 Steps — Step-by-Step Procedures

Two general techniques run through the entire 21-step sequence. Every phoria test uses Von Graefe. Every vergence test uses a prism ramp. Everything else is a variation of adding or removing lenses until a specific endpoint appears.

Von Graefe method (used in: #3, #8, #12-phoria, #13A, #13B, #15A, #15B, #18-phoria)

  1. Place the dissociating prism (OD: 12–15Δ BI) and the measuring prism (OS: 6Δ BU) in the phoropter
  2. Adjust to the correct PD (far or near depending on the test)
  3. Patient fixates the smallest readable letter at the test distance
  4. Patient sees two targets: one displaced laterally (OD, through BI) and one displaced vertically (OS, through BU), creating an upper and lower image
  5. The examiner slowly reduces the BU measuring prism while the patient watches both images
  6. Endpoint: patient reports the two images are lined up one directly above the other (vertical alignment) — no lateral offset between them
  7. Record the prism value at alignment; direction of displacement before alignment tells you the phoria type (exo vs. eso)

For vertical phoria (#12, #18): the dissociating and measuring prisms are both horizontal (BU and BD), and the endpoint is horizontal alignment instead.

Prism ramp / vergence build (used in: #9, #10, #11, #16A, #16B, #17A, #17B, #18-duction)

  1. Set lenses per the test requirement; both eyes ready to turn prism in the test direction (BO or BI or BU/BD)
  2. Slowly increase the prism binocularly in small steps while the patient fixates the smallest readable letter
  3. Blur endpoint: patient first reports that the target has blurred but is still single — record this value
  4. Break endpoint: patient first reports diplopia (double vision) — record this value
  5. To find recovery: slowly reduce the prism back toward zero while patient watches the two targets
  6. Recovery endpoint: patient first reports the two images merging back into one — record this value
  7. Not all tests have all three endpoints — see individual steps below

#1 — Case History

(Covered in C3-01. No phoropter or lenses.)

Patient reports chief complaint, history of present illness, ocular history, family history. The "4 WEED components" govern documentation (Why here, Existing conditions, Environmental factors, Duration/Description).

#2 — Entering Visual Acuity

(Covered in C3-01. No phoropter or lenses.)

VA measured without any correction in place (entering, unaided). Pinhole check if entering VA is reduced. Records: OD, OS, OU; distance and near if relevant.

#3 — Habitual Lateral Phoria at Far

Setup: Habitual Rx in phoropter | Far PD | OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU | Target: smallest readable at 20ft | Illumination: 20 ft candle

Procedure (Von Graefe):

  1. Load habitual correction and set far PD
  2. Place OD: 12–15Δ BI (dissociates fusion laterally) and OS: 6Δ BU (separates images vertically)
  3. Patient fixates smallest readable letter at 20ft; reports seeing two targets at different heights
  4. Slowly reduce the BU measuring prism while patient watches both images
  5. Endpoint: patient reports the two images are vertically aligned (one directly above the other, no lateral offset)
  6. Record the phoria direction: if image was displaced nasally before alignment → esophoria; temporally → exophoria

Significance: Baseline lateral muscular imbalance at far, taken before any subjective refraction is done. This is the "as the patient lives" phoria.

#4 — Static Retinoscopy at Near (Gross)

Setup: Habitual correction | Working distance = 20" | No prisms | Target: near point card | Illumination: standard

Procedure:

  1. Load habitual correction into phoropter; set near PD
  2. Position the retinoscope at 20" from the patient's eyes
  3. Patient fixates on a near target placed at 20" (same plane as retinoscope)
  4. Perform standard retinoscopy — neutralize the reflex by adding or reducing lenses
  5. No working distance lens subtracted (unlike distance retinoscopy) — the 20" distance is the test condition itself
  6. Endpoint: neutral reflex (shadow moves with the beam until neutrality)
  7. Record Gross #4 finding; this is the starting lens for tests #5 and #6

Significance: Provides the static retinoscopy reference at near. Gross #4 minus the working distance dioptric equivalent = Net #4, used in the Reading Add calculation.

#5 — Dynamic Retinoscopy at 20"

Setup: Result of #4 (Gross) in phoropter | Working distance = 20" | No prisms | Target: small letters or pictures at 20" | Binocular

Procedure:

  1. Load Gross #4 lenses into phoropter
  2. Position at 20" from the patient; patient fixates on letters or pictures at the plane of the retinoscope (patient looks, reads, and interacts with the near target — not passive fixation)
  3. Initial reflex: assumed to be with motion (accommodation is active for near target)
  4. Add plus in 0.25D steps until an against-movement reflex is obtained
  5. Then reduce plus (toward minus) in 0.25D steps until the first neutrality (HNP — Highest Neutral Point) is established
  6. Record Gross #5 (the highest plus at which the neutral point first appears)
  7. Note the near phoria at this stage — used to determine the lag value from the OEP table

Significance: The HNP here indicates the limit of positive fusional convergence available at 20". Gross #5 minus lag (from OEP table based on #15A phoria and AA) = Net #5. Reading Add = Net #5 minus Net #4.

Lag values (from OEP table): 1 XOP = 0.12D; 2 XOP = 0.25D; 3 XOP = 0.37D; 4 XOP = 0.50D; up to 12+ XOP progressively larger lag values.

#6 — Dynamic Retinoscopy at 40"

Setup: Result of #4 (Gross) | Working distance = 40" | No prisms | Target: small letters at 40"

Procedure:

  1. Load Gross #4 lenses; move examiner position to 40" from the patient
  2. Patient fixates on a near target at 40" — accommodation is still active but at lower demand than #5
  3. Perform retinoscopy; initial reflex is neutral (accommodation is less active at this distance)
  4. HNP at this distance = the neutral point in the with-to-against transition range
  5. Record Gross #6
  6. Net #6 = Gross #6 minus lag (0.25D if AA ≥ 5.00D)

Significance: Cross-check for the near correction. The higher working distance puts less demand on accommodation, so the neutral range shifts.

#7 — Monocular Subjective Refraction (H/E)

Setup: Best sphere | Far PD | No prisms | Target: Snellen at 20ft

(Full procedure is in the Monocular Subjective Refraction section above. Summary below.)

  1. Occlude one eye
  2. Establish ideal fog (+0.50 to +0.75D above best sphere; target 20/30–20/40)
  3. Astigmatic dial: find power meridian (darkest line), axis (90° from it), add minus cyl
  4. Add +0.25D sphere per −0.50D cyl added; equalize lines
  5. JCC axis check (handle || axis); JCC power check (red/white dot || axis)
  6. Defog to best VA; duochrome sphere refinement
  7. Endpoint: MPMVA — most plus / least minus at best VA
  8. Repeat for the other eye

Significance: This is the pivot of the entire 21-step sequence. The result (Test #7) is the lens in place for tests #8 through #18, and the lens basis for #19–#21 in non-presbyopes.

#8 — Induced Lateral Phoria at Far

Setup: Test #7 Rx | Far PD | OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU | Target: smallest readable at 20ft

Procedure: Identical to #3 (Von Graefe method), but with Test #7 lenses in place instead of habitual correction.

  1. Load Test #7 result into phoropter; set far PD
  2. Place OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU
  3. Patient fixates smallest readable at 20ft
  4. Reduce BU prism until vertical alignment
  5. Record phoria value

Significance: Shows how the subjective refraction changes the phoria. Compare to #3: if the phoria improves (less exo or moves toward ortho), the correction is helping convergence; if it worsens, investigate the near correction further.

#9 — True Adduction (Advergence) at Far

Setup: Test #7 + auxiliary +0.25S | Far PD | OU ready to turn BO | Target: smallest readable at 20ft

Procedure (prism ramp — BO direction, stops at blur):

  1. Load Test #7 + +0.25D auxiliary sphere; set far PD
  2. Place prisms OU ready to turn BO; start at zero
  3. Slowly increase BO prism binocularly while patient fixates
  4. Endpoint: blur only — stop when patient first reports the target has blurred (do NOT continue to break)
  5. The blur point equals the same level of blur produced by the +0.25D auxiliary sphere alone — this is the reference

The +0.25D auxiliary sphere is added so that when the BO prism causes accommodation to be recruited (which blurs the +0.25S image), the examiner knows the patient has reached the limit of their fusional convergence reserve. The test stops at blur, not break. This makes #9 distinct from #10, which also uses BO prisms but continues to break and recovery.

Significance: Blur point indicates the limit of available fusional convergence in reserve at far — the point at which accommodation would need to be recruited to maintain clarity. Compare to #10 (full BO range).

#10 — Convergence at Far

Setup: Test #7 | Far PD | OU ready to turn BO | Target: smallest readable at 20ft

Procedure (prism ramp — BO direction):

  1. Load Test #7; set far PD; both eyes ready to turn BO
  2. Slowly increase BO prism binocularly while patient fixates
  3. First blur: record (stimulus to accommodation via convergence demand)
  4. Continue until diplopia: record break
  5. Reduce prism until single: record recovery

Significance: BO vergence stimulates convergence (as if object moves closer). Blur point = fusional convergence reserve before accommodation is recruited. Break = total positive convergence reserve at far. Recovery = limit of the fusional field.

#11 — Abduction (Abvergence) at Far

Setup: Test #7 | Far PD | OU ready to turn BI | Target: smallest readable at 20ft

Procedure (prism ramp — BI direction):

  1. Load Test #7; set far PD; both eyes ready to turn BI
  2. Slowly increase BI prism binocularly
  3. Break (diplopia): record
  4. Reduce prism until single: record recovery

No blur endpoint for #11 — BI prisms inhibit convergence and relax accommodation; there is no accommodative demand to blur the image before the break.

Significance: Negative fusional convergence (divergence) reserve at far. Breakpoint = how much convergence can be relaxed at distance before fusion fails.

#12 — Vertical Phoria and Duction at Far

Setup: Test #7 | Far PD | Prisms ready to turn BU and BD | Target: smallest readable at 20ft

Two parts: phoria measurement, then duction measurement.

Vertical phoria (Von Graefe variant):

  1. Place horizontal dissociating prism (BI or BO) before one eye and measuring prism (BU or BD) before the other
  2. Patient fixates target; sees two images horizontally separated
  3. Adjust measuring prism until images are horizontally aligned (one beside the other, no vertical offset)
  4. Record: right/left hyperphoria, or orthophoria

Vertical duction:

  1. Same setup; prisms ready to turn BU and BD binocularly
  2. Slowly build BU then BD prism
  3. Record break and recovery in each direction

Significance: Vertical imbalance at far. Duction determines the reserve used to compensate any existing vertical imbalance. Normal vertical phoria is typically orthophoria or 1Δ or less.

#13A — Habitual Lateral Phoria at Near

Setup: Habitual near correction | Near PD | OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU | Target: smallest readable at 16" | Illumination: 20 ft candle

Procedure: Von Graefe at near. Steps identical to #3 but at 16" with near PD and habitual near Rx.

  1. Load habitual near correction; set near PD
  2. Place OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU
  3. Patient fixates smallest readable at 16"
  4. Reduce BU prism until vertical alignment

Significance: Lateral muscular imbalance at near using habitual correction — the "as the patient reads" phoria, before any OEP near correction is applied. Near equivalent of #3.

#13B — Induced Lateral Phoria at Near

Setup: Gross #14A result | Near PD | OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU | Target: smallest readable at 16"

Procedure: Von Graefe at near — steps identical to #13A, but Gross #14A lenses are in place.

Significance: Phoria at near after the cross-cylinder near correction (#14A) is applied. Shows how the near addition changes the phoria. Near equivalent of #8. Compare to #13A to assess the effect of the near correction on lateral balance.

#14A — Dissociated Cross-Cylinder Test

Setup: Test #7 | Near PD | OD: 3Δ BD | OS: 3Δ BU | Auxiliary: cross-cyl (−0.50D, red dot at 90°) | Target: cross-grid at 16" | Illumination: dim

Procedure:

  1. Load Test #7 lenses; set near PD
  2. Place OD: 3Δ BD and OS: 3Δ BU (vertical prisms dissociate the eyes — patient sees two separate cross-grids)
  3. Place a −0.50D cross-cylinder with red dot at 90° before both eyes
  4. The patient views the cross-grid target and reports which set of lines (horizontal or vertical) appears darker/bolder
  5. Add plus sphere in 0.25D steps to both eyes until the horizontal and vertical lines of the cross-grid appear equally dark
  6. Endpoint: equality at the highest plus — record Gross #14A

Significance: Measures the lag of accommodation under dissociated conditions. The amount of plus needed to equalize the lines = how much the eye is lagging behind the accommodative stimulus. Dissociated = prisms separate the eyes, removing binocular fusion as a variable.

#14B — Binocular Cross-Cylinder Test

Setup: Gross #14A result | Near PD | No prisms | Auxiliary: cross-cyl (−0.50D, red dot at 90°) | Target: cross-grid at 16" | Illumination: dim

Procedure:

  1. Load Gross #14A lenses; set near PD; no prisms this time
  2. Place −0.50D cross-cylinder (red dot at 90°) before both eyes
  3. Patient binocularly views the cross-grid
  4. Add plus in 0.25D steps until horizontal and vertical lines appear equally dark
  5. Endpoint: equality at the highest plus — record Gross #14B

Significance: Lag of accommodation under fused (binocular) conditions. Because there are no prisms, normal binocular fusion is maintained. Compare to #14A: if the lag is larger under fusion, accommodation is being driven by convergence demand more than by clarity alone.

#15A — Induced Phoria Through Unfused Cross-Cylinder

Setup: Gross #14A result | Near PD | OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU | Target: reduced Snellen at 16"

Procedure: Von Graefe at near using Gross #14A lenses.

  1. Load Gross #14A lenses; set near PD
  2. Place OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU
  3. Patient fixates reduced Snellen at 16"
  4. Reduce BU prism until vertical alignment
  5. Record phoria

Significance: Shows the influence of the #14A near correction on lateral phoria at near. The result helps determine whether the near addition is appropriate for the patient's binocular balance.

#15B — Induced Phoria Through Fused Cross-Cylinder

Setup: Gross #14B result | Near PD | OD: 12–15Δ BI | OS: 6Δ BU | Target: reduced Snellen at 16"

Procedure: Von Graefe at near using Gross #14B lenses. Steps identical to #15A, but #14B lenses are in place.

Significance: Shows the influence of the #14B near correction (fused lag) on lateral phoria. Cross-check for #15A.

#16A — Positive Relative Convergence (PRC)

Setup: Non-presbyope: Test #7 | Myope: habitual minus | Presbyope: 14B Net | Near PD | OU ready to turn BO | Target: smallest readable at 16"

Procedure (prism ramp — BO):

  1. Load appropriate lenses (Test #7 for non-presbyope); set near PD
  2. Place prisms OU ready to turn BO; start at zero
  3. Slowly increase BO prism binocularly
  4. Endpoint: blur only (the point at which the target first blurs; do NOT continue to break)
  5. Record blur value as PRC

Significance: The blur point indicates how much positive fusional innervation is in reserve at 40cm. This is the convergence reserve without pushing to the breaking point — the "usable" reserve.

#16B — Positive Fusional Reserve (PFR)

Setup: Same as #16A

Procedure (prism ramp — BO, continued past blur to break and recovery):

  1. Same setup as #16A; same lens and prism direction (BO)
  2. Continue increasing BO prism past the blur point until diplopia (break)
  3. Record break value
  4. Reduce prism until single vision recovers: record recovery value

Significance: Break = total positive convergence reserve at near including accommodative convergence. Recovery = limit of the fusional field. A large blur-to-break gap suggests the patient is using accommodation-driven convergence heavily.

#17A — Negative Relative Convergence (NRC)

Setup: Same lenses as #16A | Near PD | OU ready to turn BI | Target: smallest readable at 16"

Procedure (prism ramp — BI):

  1. Load same lenses as #16A; set near PD
  2. Place prisms OU ready to turn BI; start at zero
  3. Slowly increase BI prism binocularly
  4. Endpoint: blur only (first blur, do not go to break)
  5. Record blur value as NRC

Significance: Measures how much convergence can be relaxed before the image blurs — the negative fusional reserve without pushing to diplopia.

#17B — Negative Fusional Reserve (NFR)

Setup: Same as #17A

Procedure (prism ramp — BI, past blur to break and recovery):

  1. Same setup as #17A; continue past blur to break
  2. Record break value (diplopia)
  3. Reduce BI prism until single: record recovery

Significance: Break = how much accommodative convergence can be inhibited by relaxing accommodation at near. Recovery = limit of the negative fusional field.

#18 — Vertical Phoria and Duction at Near

Setup: Test #7 | Near PD | Prisms ready to turn BU and BD | Target: smallest readable at 16"

Procedure: Same two-part structure as #12, but at 16" with near PD.

Vertical phoria:

  1. Load Test #7; near PD; place horizontal dissociating prism + BU/BD measuring prism
  2. Patient fixates target; reduce measuring prism until horizontal alignment
  3. Record vertical phoria value

Vertical duction:

  1. Prisms ready to turn BU and BD binocularly
  2. Slowly build BU prism until break; reduce until recovery
  3. Repeat for BD direction
  4. Record break and recovery for each direction

Significance: Vertical imbalance at near. Near equivalent of #12. Duction determines the reserve used at near to compensate any vertical imbalance.

#19 — Amplitude of Accommodation (AA)

Setup: Test #7 (non-presbyope: minus added) | Near PD | No prisms | Target:.62 Em Type at 16"

Procedure:

Non-presbyope (push-up method with lenses):

  1. Load Test #7 lenses; set near PD
  2. Patient fixates.62 Em Type print at 16"
  3. Add minus lenses in 0.25D steps until the patient reports the print is difficult to read
  4. Endpoint: print difficult to read (first sustained blur)
  5. Record the total minus added = amplitude of accommodation in diopters (in addition to the 2.50D base from the 16" distance)

Presbyope (push-down — plus lenses):

  1. Load Test #7 lenses; set near PD
  2. Start with enough plus that the print is unreadable
  3. Reduce plus (or add minus) in 0.25D steps until the patient first reads the.62 Em Type
  4. Endpoint: first readable
  5. Record the lens at first readable

Significance: Determines total amplitude of accommodation. Expected values follow Hofstetter's formula: minimum AA = 15 − 0.25(age); average = 18.5 − 0.30(age).

#20 — Positive Relative Accommodation (PRA)

Setup: Non-presbyope: Test #7 | Presbyope: 14B Net | Near PD | No prisms | Target: smallest readable reduced Snellen at 16" (20/20)

Procedure:

  1. Load appropriate lenses; set near PD
  2. Patient fixates 20/20 reduced Snellen at 16"
  3. Add minus lenses in 0.25D steps binocularly
  4. Endpoint: patient cannot read the 20/20 line (first sustained blur)
  5. Record total minus added = PRA value (expected normal: approximately −2.37D)

Significance: Measures the extent to which accommodation can be stimulated while fixation is maintained at 16". Higher PRA = more accommodative reserve available for near work.

#21 — Negative Relative Accommodation (NRA)

Setup: Non-presbyope: Test #7 | Presbyope: 14B Net | Near PD | No prisms | Target: smallest readable reduced Snellen at 16" (20/20)

Procedure:

  1. Load appropriate lenses; set near PD
  2. Patient fixates 20/20 reduced Snellen at 16"
  3. Add plus lenses in 0.25D steps binocularly
  4. Endpoint: patient cannot read the 20/20 line (first sustained blur)
  5. Record total plus added = NRA value (expected normal: approximately +2.00D)

Significance: Measures the extent to which accommodation can be inhibited while fixation is maintained at 16". Together with PRA, defines the range of relative accommodation. NRA + PRA should center roughly on the resting point of accommodation.

OEP Clinical Interpretation Guide

The 21 steps are not just a checklist. Each result answers a question in a clinical story, and the questions build on each other. The picture gets sharper as you go — the phoria at #3 plants a hypothesis, the vergences at #9–#12 test it, the near phorias at #13A and #13A confirm or overturn it, and the accommodation tests at #19–#21 explain whether the binocular finding is optically driven or muscular.

Here is what to watch at each stage and how to connect the dots.

Stage 1 — Before correction (#1–#3): Baseline picture

At #3 (habitual phoria at far), you see the patient as they actually live. This is your starting reference.

What to note:

  • Is the far phoria orthophoric, exo, or eso? Note the amount.
  • Any vertical component? Even 1–2Δ of vertical imbalance produces disproportionate symptoms — headaches, tilted print, monocular blur. Flag this immediately.
  • A large eso at far before any correction suggests the habitual Rx may be over-minused or significantly under-plussed — the eye is already straining to maintain distance fixation.

Normal reference: approximately 1Δ exo at far (ortho is also normal)

Stage 2 — Objective near picture (#4–#6): What the retinoscope says

The lag of accommodation from the dynamic retinoscopy sequence (#5 vs #4) tells you how well accommodation is doing the job of keeping up with near demand.

Normal lag: +0.50 to +0.75D

Stage 3 — The subjective refraction (#7): The anchor

Test #7 is the reference lens for everything that follows. An error here cascades.

What to compare:

C6-01 — Management of Visual Disorders

The Simple Version

Management of visual disorders in optometry follows a fixed priority: fix the optics first, then address the muscle problem, then train the system, then refer for surgery if none of that works. This order matters because most binocular disorders are at least partly driven by uncorrected refractive error, and prism or therapy on top of a wrong prescription is treating the wrong thing.

The three main areas here are phorias (latent deviations), tropias (manifest deviations), and amblyopia (reduced VA not explainable by optics alone). Each has its own management ladder, but all three start at the same first rung: correct the refractive error.

Teaching angle: Ask: "A child has esotropia. The parent wants surgery. What do you do first?" (Full cycloplegic refraction, because if the deviation is accommodative, the right glasses may straighten the eyes completely — and surgery on an uncorrected hyperope is treating the symptom, not the cause.)

But Why?

Why correct the refractive error before anything else?

Uncorrected hyperopia drives accommodation, and accommodation drives convergence via the AC/A ratio. An eye that is over-accommodating will over-converge. Giving glasses removes the accommodation demand, which removes the convergence excess. Without that step, the full deviation cannot be assessed, and any prism or VT prescribed will be based on an inflated angle.

Why does occlusion therapy work for amblyopia?

The amblyopic eye is not blind — its retina and visual pathway are intact. The problem is cortical: the brain has learned to suppress the amblyopic eye's input because it is blurrier than the fellow eye. Covering the good eye forces the brain to use the suppressed eye, re-opening the cortical representation of that eye through use-dependent plasticity.

Why does atropine penalization work?

Atropine paralyzes accommodation in the good eye, making it blurry for near tasks. The amblyopic eye then provides clearer near vision and the brain is nudged toward using it. The mechanism is the same as patching (force use of the amblyopic eye) but without a patch — useful for children who remove patches or have patching-induced contact dermatitis.

Why does simply correcting the refractive error sometimes resolve amblyopia on its own?

In anisometropic amblyopia, the brain suppresses the eye with the worse image — not because of strabismus, but because of chronic image disparity. Providing the correct lens equalizes the retinal image quality. The brain no longer has a reason to suppress one eye, and cortical plasticity allows some recovery, especially during the sensitive period.

Why is patching alone insufficient for amblyopia?

Patching improves monocular VA by forcing use of the amblyopic eye, but it does not rewire the binocular cortical circuits that process binocularity and fusion. If the underlying strabismus or anisometropia is not addressed, the amblyopia returns when the patch is removed. VT combined with patching addresses both the monocular acuity deficit and the binocular dysfunction.

How It Works — The Decision Framework

The management of any visual disorder follows the same four-rung ladder. The rungs must be climbed in order.

Rung 1: Correct the refractive error

  • Always first
  • Clears the retinal image
  • Removes accommodative demand (reduces AC/A-driven convergence in CE and accommodative esotropia)
  • In anisometropic amblyopia, may resolve the amblyopia partially or fully without patching
  • Prism or VT prescribed before the refractive error is corrected is based on the wrong angle

Rung 2: Prism prescription

  • Used when Sheard's or Percival's criterion is not met after optical correction
  • Compensating prism: reduces the demand on fusional vergence without training it
  • Direction: base opposite to the deviation tendency (exo → BI; eso → BO; hyperphoria → BD before the higher eye)
  • Limitation: does not strengthen the vergence system; vergence ranges remain unchanged; symptoms may return if prism is discontinued

Rung 3: Vision therapy (VT)

  • Trains the vergence system to expand the zone of clear single binocular vision (ZCSBV)
  • Indicated for CI, FVD, accommodative insufficiency, and some CE cases
  • More durable than prism because it builds actual vergence capacity
  • For amblyopia: active therapy (patching, penalization, CAM, OVT, perceptual learning)
  • Combines sensory and motor activities; addresses both monocular and binocular function

Rung 4: Surgical referral

  • For large-angle constant tropias where optical and VT management is insufficient
  • Accommodative esotropia: surgery is avoided if the full hyperopic Rx resolves the angle
  • Large exotropia: surgery is the primary treatment when deviation is constant and large
  • Always refer to ophthalmology

Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

Rule: Sheard's Criterion

Compensating fusional vergence recovery ≥ 2 × phoria
If criterion is NOT met: patient is symptomatic

What it states in plain language: The vergence reserve available to compensate for a phoria must be at least twice the size of the phoria. If your phoria uses more than half your available reserve, you will develop symptoms.

What each part means:

  • Compensating fusional vergence = the vergence in the direction OPPOSITE to the deviation
  • For exophoria: the compensating vergence is the BI (negative) fusional vergence (how far BI you can push before diplopia)
  • For esophoria: the compensating vergence is the BO (positive) fusional vergence
  • Recovery value (not break) is used as the measure of functional reserve — the point at which the patient can re-fuse, not the point at which they lose fusion
  • Phoria = the size of the latent deviation (in Δ)

Where it comes from: Charles Sheard (1920) observed that many symptomatic patients had normal phoria magnitudes but insufficient vergence reserves. He proposed the 2:1 ratio as a clinical threshold, reasoning that any demand requiring more than half the total reserve would be sustained at the margin, creating fatigue and symptoms.

The analogy: Think of the phoria as a car constantly pulling left, and the fusional vergence as the force you apply to the steering wheel to keep it straight. If the pull is strong enough that you're already using more than half your steering strength just to maintain the lane, you'll fatigue quickly. If the pull only needs a quarter of your strength, you can sustain it comfortably all day.

Why this form and not another: The 2:1 ratio sets a practical comfort margin. A ratio of 1:1 would mean the patient is always using 100% of reserve — technically possible but functionally exhausting. The 2:1 ratio leaves half the reserve as a safety buffer for visual tasks that increase demand (fatigue, illness, stress).

What it does NOT explain:

  • Sheard's criterion does not apply to vertical phorias, where even small amounts cause significant symptoms
  • It does not account for asymmetric vergence ranges (a patient with a large blur-to-break gap may have an unusual profile)
  • When the ZCSBV is small and asymmetric, Percival's criterion may be more informative

Cross-subject connections:

  • Binocular Vision (B5): Sheard's criterion is used alongside Morgan's norms to identify binocular vision disorders from OEP data
  • VT (C7): The goal of VT for vergence disorders is to expand the recovery value until Sheard's is met and sustained

Rule: Percival's Criterion

The demand point (phoria) must fall within the middle third of the zone of clear single binocular vision (ZCSBV)
Middle third = between 1/3 BO and 1/3 BI of the total fusional vergence range

What it states in plain language: The patient's resting eye position (phoria) must sit in the comfortable center zone of their available vergence range. If it falls in the outer one-third of either the BO or BI side, they will be symptomatic.

Where it comes from: Percival (1892) proposed that comfort is greatest when the visual demand falls in the middle of the available range — neither near the BO limit nor the BI limit. It is geometric rather than ratio-based (unlike Sheard's).

When to use Percival's over Sheard's: Percival's is more useful when the ZCSBV is small or asymmetric — common in FVD patients where both BO and BI ranges are reduced. In these cases, a normal-sized phoria can still violate Percival's because the entire range is compressed.

If demand is in the outer BI third: prescribe BO prism = (1/3 × BI break) − (2/3 × BO break)

  • NPC: how close the object gets before one eye breaks away (in cm, measured from the spectacle plane)
  • +3: accounts for the distance between the spectacle plane and the center of rotation (~27 mm ≈ 3 cm); ensures the denominator represents the actual working distance from the ocular center of rotation, not the spectacle plane

Practice Questions

1. A 7-year-old presents with 20/20 OD and 20/60 OS. Examination shows 4 Δ esotropia OU with +3.50D hyperopia. Cycloplegic refraction confirms +3.50D OU. What is the most appropriate first management step?

  • A. Patch the right eye for 6 hours/day
  • B. Prescribe full cycloplegic correction (+3.50D) and reassess
  • C. Refer immediately for strabismus surgery
  • D. Prescribe BO prism to compensate for the esotropia
Answer

B. Prescribe full cycloplegic correction (+3.50D) and reassess.

This is a classic accommodative esotropia presentation: esotropia with significant hyperopia. The uncorrected hyperopia drives accommodation, which drives convergence via the AC/A ratio, producing the esotropia. The first step is full hyperopic correction — the glasses may resolve the deviation completely. Patching (A) is premature before the refractive error is addressed; the amblyopia in OS may partially or fully resolve with correction alone. Surgery (C) is premature; surgery on an uncorrected accommodative esotropia treats the symptom without removing the cause. Prism (D) would compensate the deviation without removing the accommodative drive; the esotropia would persist when the prism is removed.

2. A patient has 8 Δ exophoria at near. BI recovery at near (#17B) is 10 Δ. The examiner wants to prescribe compensating prism. How much prism is needed according to Sheard's criterion, and in which direction?

  • A. 2 Δ BI
  • B. 2 Δ BO
  • C. 4 Δ BI
  • D. No prism needed; criterion is met
Answer

A. 2 Δ BI.

Sheard's criterion: BI recovery (10 Δ) must be ≥ 2 × phoria (2 × 8 = 16 Δ). It is not met (10 < 16). Prism needed = (2 × 8 − 10) ÷ 3 = (16 − 10) ÷ 3 = 6 ÷ 3 = 2 Δ. Direction: exophoria → BI prism (base-in compensates for the outward deviation). B is wrong direction. C over-estimates the amount. D is incorrect because the criterion is clearly not met.

C7-01 — Vision Therapy and Orthoptics

The Simple Version

Vision therapy trains the binocular visual system the same way physical therapy trains a recovering muscle — not by compensating for weakness, but by rebuilding actual capacity. Prism moves the target; VT moves the eye. The clinical workflow has three steps: identify which system is broken (accommodation, vergence, or sensory fusion), confirm how severely it is broken through a targeted battery of tests, then choose the modality that addresses that specific deficit.

Before prescribing VT, you need to know which muscle or system is causing the problem. For horizontal phorias, the test pattern tells you the type. For vertical deviations and paretic muscles, you use the Parks 3-step test to narrow the involved muscle from a field of eight possibilities to one.

Teaching angle: Ask: "The patient has convergence insufficiency. You give prism. Six months later, symptoms return when the prism is removed. Why?" (Prism compensated the demand; it never trained the vergence system. The answer is VT, not more prism.)

But Why?

Why does VT produce durable improvement when prism does not?

Prism shifts the perceived target so the eyes need less convergence to fuse it — this removes symptoms but leaves the vergence system unchanged. VT repeatedly stimulates the vergence system near and slightly beyond its current limit. Each successful fusion attempt at a higher demand level reinforces the neural drive, gradually pushing the blur and break points outward. The vergence system adapts the same way any trained skill adapts — through repetition under increasing demand.

Why does the Parks 3-step test work?

Six of the eight cyclovertical muscles each have a field of action where they are the primary vertical mover. When a muscle is paretic, the vertical deviation is largest in its field of action. Each step of the Parks test eliminates half the remaining candidates by identifying which eye, which gaze direction, and which head tilt produces the greatest deviation. Three binary decisions → one muscle.

Why is the Brock string both a diagnostic and a therapeutic tool?

The Brock string works by creating physiological diplopia — if the patient fixates one bead, the other beads appear double, and the string forms an X at the fixated bead. This requires that both eyes' inputs reach consciousness. A patient who suppresses will not see the doubled strings. Demonstrating this to the patient is the diagnosis of suppression; practicing until they consistently see the X is the beginning of treating it. The same stimulus serves both purposes.

Why is patching more effective than atropine for most amblyopia?

Patching creates complete occlusion of the dominant eye — the brain has no choice but to process the amblyopic eye's input. Atropine blurs the dominant eye for near only; it still provides clear distance vision, so the suppression mechanism is only partially challenged. For moderate amblyopia, the PEDIG trials showed comparable outcomes, but for deeper amblyopia, patching offers stronger and more consistent occlusion.

How It Works — The Mechanism

Vergence Training

The vergence system has a homeostatic zone called the zone of clear single binocular vision (ZCSBV) — bounded by the blur point on one side (where accommodation breaks down) and the break point on the other (where fusion breaks down). VT expands this zone by repeatedly driving the vergence system to its limit and requiring successful fusion.

Mechanism: sustained near vergence effort→increased motor fusion demand→neural adaptation in the vergence controller→raised blur and break thresholds→wider ZCSBV.

Convergence exercises build positive fusional vergence (PFC); divergence exercises build negative fusional vergence (NFC). The target is always the condition's compensating reserve — CI patients need more PFC; FVD patients need both.

Accommodation Training

Accommodative flexibility training (lens flippers) exploits the accommodative/vergence linkage. Every flip from minus to plus triggers relaxation of accommodation, which drives a vergence response. Training this repeatedly under time pressure improves the speed of the ciliary muscle response and the accuracy of the AC/A-driven vergence following it.

Sensory / Antisuppression Training

Suppression is an active cortical inhibition. It cannot be removed by demand alone — the suppressed eye's signal must first be made strong enough to compete with the dominant eye. Antisuppression training uses red-green filters, polaroids, or Bernell instruments to present stimuli that can only be seen binocularly, forcing the brain to use both eyes. Progression: peripheral stimulation first (where suppression is weaker) → central stimulation (foveal area, where suppression is strongest).

Practice Questions

Q1. A 10-year-old presents with blurred near vision, frequent headaches, and NPC of 14cm (break). Near PFC blur is 8Δ. Sheard's criterion is NOT met. Full refractive correction has been given. The most appropriate next management step is:

A. Add base-out prism to spectacles B. Prescribe base-in prism at near C. Begin vision therapy with convergence exercises D. Refer for strabismus surgery

Answer

C — Vision therapy with convergence exercises

The findings indicate convergence insufficiency (NPC > 10cm, reduced near PFC). Full correction was already given (Rung 1). The child is 10 years old — a good VT candidate. Sheard's failure in a child with CI is the prime indication for VT. Base-out prism at near (B) would worsen convergence demand. BI prism (A) compensates but does not train. Surgery (D) is for tropias, not phoria anomalies.

Q2. A patient has right hypertropia. The deviation is largest in left gaze. On Bielschowsky head tilt to the RIGHT, the deviation INCREASES. Which muscle is paretic?

A. Right Superior Oblique B. Right Inferior Rectus C. Left Superior Rectus D. Left Inferior Oblique

Answer

B — Right Inferior Rectus

Parks 3-step: Right hyper → 4 candidates (RSO, RIR, LIO, LSR). Larger in LEFT gaze → narrows to RIR or LSR (the muscles with field of action in left gaze for the right hyper group — IR acts in adduction which is left gaze for OD). Head tilt to RIGHT (toward higher eye, OD) INCREASES the deviation → the ipsilateral intortor (RSO) cannot compensate, but wait — if RSO was paretic, tilt right would increase because SO fails; but we already narrowed to RIR or LSR. Tilt RIGHT increases → RIR is paretic (tilt toward higher eye + increase = the depressor of the higher eye is at fault). LSR would show decrease on right tilt.

Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue→rule→trap`.

JCC axis/power — cue: which dot position the patient prefers.

  • Prefers P1 (red dots ∥ tentative axis) → cyl under-powered → add minus cyl. Prefers P2 (white dots ∥ axis) → reduce minus.
  • Trap: adding cylinder when the patient actually preferred P2.

Duochrome — cue: red side vs green side clearer.

  • Green clearer → over-minused → add plus / reduce minus. Red clearer → add minus. (RAM-GAP: Red Add Minus, Green Add Plus.)
  • Trap: over-minusing a patient who says green is clearer.

Cover test phoria vs tropia — cue: movement on cover vs without cover.

  • Moves only when covered (recovers on uncover) = phoria. Moves without cover = tropia.
  • Trap: calling a phoria a tropia because the eye moved — a phoria only moves under cover.

Prism for phoria — cue: exo / eso / hyper.

  • Exophoria → BI, esophoria → BO, hyperphoria → BD before the higher eye.
  • Trap: base-out for exo (it's base-in).

Retinoscopy reflex — cue: with vs against motion.

  • With motion → add plus (under-plussed/hyperopic). Against → add minus. Neutral = endpoint (+ working distance).
  • Trap: forgetting to subtract the working-distance lens at the end.
