## C4-01 — Objective Examination
*Personal study notes | TOS: C.4 | 150 items (Subject C)*

## The Simple Version

The objective examination is everything done without asking the patient for verbal feedback — you observe, measure, and record. It has four core tools: the slit lamp (biomicroscope), the ophthalmoscope, the keratometer, and the retinoscope. Each answers a different question. The slit lamp shows you the anterior segment in layered detail. The ophthalmoscope shows you the posterior segment and media. The keratometer measures corneal curvature and tells you how much of the patient's astigmatism comes from the cornea. The retinoscope gives you the refractive error without asking the patient a single question. A clinician who masters all four can complete a full workup on a patient who cannot communicate at all.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "If you had to choose one objective test for a newborn with suspected cataracts, which would you use and why?" The answer forces them to match the tool to the clinical question: ophthalmoscopy for media opacity, retinoscopy for refraction, slit lamp for anterior segment detail.

> **Cross-reference:** Retinoscopy principles (movements, working distance, gross vs net) are fully covered in **C2-01**. This note covers the slit lamp, ophthalmoscopy, and keratometry in full, and cross-references C2-01 for retinoscopy.

## But Why?

**Why does the slit lamp use a narrow beam of light instead of a broad one?**
Because a narrow slit creates an optical cross-section of the tissue, like slicing through it. You see each layer separately — epithelium, stroma, endothelium — rather than a blurred total thickness.

**Why does indirect ophthalmoscopy give a wider field but lower magnification than direct?**
Because the condensing lens forms an aerial real image of the fundus in the space between the lens and the examiner. The magnification depends on the ratio of the lens power to the eye's power. Higher lens power = shorter focal length = image formed closer = smaller, wider-field image. The binocular design adds stereo depth, which direct cannot provide.

**Why does the keratometer treat the cornea as a convex mirror?**
Because the tear film over the anterior cornea is a reflective surface. The mire image size depends on the radius of curvature of that mirror — the same physics as any convex mirror. A steeper cornea (smaller radius) produces a smaller mire image; a flatter cornea produces a larger one. Measuring image size gives you radius.

**Why does Javal's rule include a fixed −0.50 DC × 90° term?**
Because the posterior corneal surface and tilted crystalline lens together contribute a small, predictable amount of against-the-rule astigmatism (approximately −0.50D at 90°) to every eye. This internal astigmatism is not measured by the keratometer, which reads only the anterior cornea. Javal's rule adds this constant correction to convert corneal astigmatism to total refractive astigmatism.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**Slit lamp: how the optical section works**

The slit lamp is a biomicroscope with a coupled illumination system. The illumination arm and the microscope arm share a common focal point. When the beam is narrow (optic section), it passes through the tissue like a plane of light — each layer scatters slightly differently, allowing the examiner to distinguish the epithelium, stroma, and endothelium of the cornea as visually distinct zones. The illumination arm can be decoupled from the microscope arm for indirect and retro-illumination techniques.

**Ophthalmoscope: how the image forms**

- **Direct:** The instrument illuminates the retina through the pupil. The examiner looks through a peephole and a series of correcting lenses. The retina acts as a self-luminous object. The image formed is virtual and erect — as if looking at the fundus through a +15D magnifying glass. No aerial image exists between the patient and the examiner.

- **Indirect:** The condensing lens held in front of the patient's eye takes the diverging light from the illuminated retina and forms a real, inverted, aerial image of the fundus in space between the lens and the examiner. The examiner then looks at this aerial image with the headborne binocular ophthalmoscope. The image is real and inverted because the condensing lens is acting like the objective of a microscope.

**Keratometer: how the corneal radius is measured**

The tear film over the anterior cornea acts as a convex mirror. When the keratometer projects a mire (target image) at a known distance and size, it measures the size of the reflected virtual image in the corneal mirror. Since the size of the virtual image in a convex mirror depends on the radius of curvature, measuring image size gives radius:

```
f = r/2   (for mirrors: focal length = half the radius)
For corneal power: D = (n − 1) / r = 0.3375 / r (using reduced corneal index 1.3375)
r in meters → D in diopters
```

The keratometer then converts the radius to corneal power in diopters. The instrument requires a doubling device (prism or mirror) to stabilize the mire image during measurement.

### Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

---

**Javal's Rule — Corneal to Total Astigmatism**

```
At = 1.25 (Ac) + (−0.50 Cyl × 90°)

At = total (refractive) astigmatism
Ac = corneal astigmatism (from keratometry)
1.25 = empirical scaling factor
−0.50 Cyl × 90° = constant internal astigmatism contribution (ATR direction)
```

**What it states in plain language:**
The total astigmatism of the eye is approximately 1.25 times the corneal astigmatism, minus a fixed 0.50D against-the-rule correction. The correction accounts for the astigmatism that comes from internal structures (back of the cornea, tilted lens) that the keratometer cannot see.

**What each part means:**
- **At** — the total refractive astigmatism you would measure by retinoscopy or subjective refraction. This is what goes into the actual spectacle Rx.
- **Ac** — corneal astigmatism only, measured by keratometry. The keratometer reads only the anterior corneal surface.
- **1.25** — empirically derived scaling factor. The cornea typically contributes approximately 80% of the total astigmatism, but it is scaled up slightly because the keratometer reads only one surface and uses a simplified index.
- **−0.50 Cyl × 90°** — the internal astigmatism constant. Internal structures (posterior corneal surface, lens tilt) add approximately 0.50D of ATR astigmatism consistently across the population.

**Where it comes from:**
Emile Javal and Schiotz (19th century) observed that in most eyes, the total refractive astigmatism measured subjectively was predictably different from the corneal astigmatism measured by keratometry. They derived an empirical formula to bridge the two. The correction factor (−0.50 × 90°) represents the statistical average of internal astigmatism across large populations.

**The analogy:**
Javal's rule is like estimating the total weight of a package from the weight of the box alone, knowing that bubble wrap always adds approximately 0.5kg. You weigh the box (Ac), multiply by your scale factor (1.25), and subtract 0.5kg (internal component). You never weigh the bubble wrap directly — but you know its average contribution.

**Why this form and not another:**
If you used Ac directly without the internal correction, you would consistently over-correct the astigmatism in WTR eyes and under-correct ATR eyes. The −0.50 Cyl × 90° term is what makes the formula accurate for both WTR and ATR astigmatism.

**What it does NOT explain:**
- Not valid for keratoconus or irregular corneas (the relationship between corneal and internal astigmatism breaks down)
- Not valid when the crystalline lens has significant lenticular astigmatism (e.g., nuclear sclerosis with lens tilt)
- Empirical estimate only — specific patients may deviate significantly from the predicted At

**Cross-subject connections:**
- **C2-01 (Astigmatism):** Javal's rule predicts the starting cylinder power for the subjective refraction. Knowing Ac from keratometry means you enter the refraction with a predicted Rx rather than starting blind.
- **D3-01 (Contact Lenses):** In soft CL fitting, residual astigmatism = At − Ac. Javal's rule gives a quick estimate of how much astigmatism remains after placing a spherical soft lens (which masks Ac but not the internal component).

**Reading the Result:**

| Relationship of At vs Ac | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| At ≈ 1.25(Ac) | Normal — internal astigmatism follows the population average |
| At significantly greater than 1.25(Ac) | High internal (lenticular) astigmatism — may be from lens tilt or sclerosis |
| At much less than 1.25(Ac) | Internal astigmatism is compensating the corneal component — residual very low |
| Ac present but At = 0 | Corneal and internal astigmatism perfectly cancelling — unusual; verify |

**Van Herick Anterior Chamber Angle Grading**

```
Slit beam at 60° to the cornea; compare SHADOW width (AC depth) to OPTIC SECTION width (corneal thickness)

Grade 4: Shadow ≥ 1/2 corneal thickness       → Ratio 1:1 → Open angle → Safe
Grade 3: Shadow = 1/4 to 1/2 corneal thickness → Ratio 1:2 → Open angle → Safe
Grade 2: Shadow = 1/4 corneal thickness        → Ratio 1:4 → Narrow angle → Caution
Grade 1: Shadow < 1/4 corneal thickness        → < 1:4   → At risk for angle closure
Grade 0: No shadow (cornea "sits" on iris)      → Angle closed → Angle closure attack
```

**What it states in plain language:**
The Van Herick technique compares the depth of the anterior chamber angle at the limbus to the thickness of the cornea, using the shadow formed between the corneal and iris reflex beams. A deeper AC angle means a lower risk of angle-closure glaucoma.

**Clinical use:**
- Grades 0–1: dilating eye drops are CONTRAINDICATED (risk of precipitating angle closure)
- Grade 2: dilate with caution and monitor
- Grades 3–4: dilating drops are generally safe

**Cross-subject connections:**
- **G3 (DPA):** Every DPA examination requires an anterior chamber angle assessment. Van Herick Grade 0–1 = contraindication to dilation.
- **E4 (Emergencies):** Acute angle closure glaucoma presents as Grade 0 on Van Herick — halos, pain, elevated IOP, shallow AC.

**Keratometry Procedure**

```
1. Focus eyepiece
2. Adjust instrument to patient (height, chin rest)
3. Center light on patient's cornea
4. Align instrument to patient's visual axis
5. Instruct patient to look at center of mire
6. Identify the PRIMARY MERIDIAN (meridian closest to 180°; read by plus mire signs)
7. Read the primary meridian power
8. Rotate 90° to SECONDARY MERIDIAN (read by minus mire signs)
9. Read the secondary meridian power
10. Record: "Power at meridian / Power at meridian"
    Example: R: 42.00 @ 180 / 43.00 @ 90

INTERPRETATION:
  WTR corneal astigmatism: weakest meridian at or near 180° (axis of minus correcting cylinder at 180°)
  ATR corneal astigmatism: weakest meridian at or near 90° (axis of minus correcting cylinder at 90°)
  Oblique: principal meridians between 30–60° or 120–150°

CALCULATE CORNEAL ASTIGMATISM:
  Ac = Difference between two K readings
  Example: 43.00 − 42.00 = 1.00D at 180° (WTR)

APPLY JAVAL'S RULE TO PREDICT TOTAL ASTIGMATISM:
  At = 1.25(Ac) + (−0.50 Cyl × 90°)
```

**Opacity Parallax in Direct Ophthalmoscopy**

```
Move ophthalmoscope LEFT, observe opacity movement:

Opacity moves OPPOSITE to scope   → Anterior opacity (cornea, anterior lens)
  → The farther anterior, the greater the extent of apparent motion

No apparent movement              → Opacity at or near lens nucleus

Opacity moves SAME direction      → Posterior opacity (posterior lens or vitreous)
  → Vitreous opacity: floats back to original position when scope stops

Instruct patient to move eye:
  Anterior opacity   → moves in SAME direction as eye
  Posterior opacity  → moves in OPPOSITE direction to eye
```

### The Biomicroscope — Systems, Optics, and Settings

Most notes teach what the slit lamp *shows* you. This section teaches how the instrument itself is built and how you drive it, because that is exactly what procedure-style drills test and it rarely appears in finding-based reviewers.

**Three systems make up the slit lamp:**
- **Illumination system** — the light source, condenser, slit aperture, and filters. This is what you tilt to create the beam.
- **Observation (viewing) system** — the microscope: eyepieces, magnification changer, and objective. This is what you look through.
- **Mechanical system** — the joystick, the base that slides on the table, the chin/forehead rest, and the patient-positioning frame. The light source is part of the *illumination* system, not the mechanical one.

**Two illumination brands, sorted by where the lamp sits:**
```
ILLUMINATION SOURCE LOCATION
  Haag-Streit ──── light comes from the TOP (bulb above, mirror sends beam down)
  Zeiss ────────── light comes from the BASE (bulb below)
```

**Two viewing (microscope) designs, sorted by how you change power:**
```
MAGNIFICATION CHANGER
  Greenough (converging tubes) ── magnification changed by a drum/lever (continuous feel)
  Galilean (telescopic) ───────── magnification changed by flipping fixed telescopes in/out (stepped)
```
(Board framing keys the "lever to change magnification" to the Greenough system; the Galilean changes power in discrete flip steps. Know both — the drill just wants the Greenough/Galilean split.)

**The magnifier optics — two lenses set the range:**
```
Eyepiece (ocular) lens ──── +10 D
Objective lens ─────────── +22 D
Rule: high magnification → SMALLER (decreased) field of view, and less depth of focus
```

**Suggested working magnification, by what you are looking at:**
```
STRUCTURE                     MAGNIFICATION
  Ocular adnexa ───────────── 6x   (low power, wide survey)
  Lids and lashes ─────────── 6x–10x
  Cornea ──────────────────── 16x
  Van Herick angle estimate ─ 16x   (illumination arm at 60°, gray/diffuser filter OFF)
  Iris ────────────────────── 16x
  Anterior chamber (cells/flare) 25x–40x (room fully DARK)
```

**Alignment and scanning technique — the steps a drill will quiz:**
- Set the **illumination arm to 0°** (coaxial with the microscope) before focusing when you use the focusing rod/bar.
- **Scan left to right**, and view the **lids and lashes in sections** — not in one quick sweep (you miss the lid margin, meibomian orifices, and lash bases otherwise).
- **Dim** the overhead room light — do **not** switch it fully off for the whole exam (you still need some light to position the patient and instrument); the room goes fully dark only for the anterior-chamber cell/flare check.
- To read the **cornea**, **remove the filter** so you have full white light on the tissue.
- To **evert the upper lid**, create the crease **above the tarsal plate** (press at the superior tarsal border while the patient looks down).

**Anterior chamber — cells vs flare (the Tyndall pair):**
```
Beam in a dark room, high mag, small bright square in the AC:
  FLARE ─── haze/"headlight-in-fog" ─── PROTEIN leaked into aqueous
  CELLS ── discrete floating dots ───── inflammatory white blood cells
```
Van Herick estimates the *angle*; the cell/flare check estimates *active inflammation*. If the Van Herick beam is set at **less than 60°**, it **underestimates** the true angle.

### Memory Anchors

**Ophthalmoscopy image characteristics:**
- **DIVE** for Direct: **D**irect = **I**mage **V**irtual and **E**rect (upright)
- Indirect = inverted real (opposite of direct on both counts)

**Magnification vs field of view in indirect:**
- "Higher lens power = Wider field, Weaker magnification" — think of a fisheye camera lens: wider angle = smaller objects

**Slit lamp illumination angle rules:**
- Most techniques: **30–45°** between illumination and microscope
- Specular reflection and Van Herick: **60°** (angle of incidence = angle of reflection)

**Which brand's lamp is where — "HAAG hangs high":**
- **Haag**-Streit = light from the **top** (hangs high); **Zeiss** = light from the **base** (down at zero/base).

**Slit lamp lens powers — "10 in, 22 out":**
- Eye**p**iece = +**10** D (the one you peer through); objective = +**22** D (the one facing the patient).

**Magnification ladder — "6 outside, 16 middle, 40 deepest":**
- **6x** for the outside (adnexa, lids) → **16x** for the middle layers (cornea, iris, Van Herick) → **25–40x** for the deepest, dimmest job (anterior chamber cells and flare).

**Flare vs cells — "Flare = Fluid protein, Cells = inflammatory Cells":**
- **F**lare = **F**og of leaked **protein**; cells = discrete floating white blood cells. Both read in a fully dark room at high mag.

**Javal's rule shorthand:**
```
At = 1.25(Ac) − 0.50 × 90
```
Think: "Multiply by 1.25, then subtract a quarter diopter of ATR internally."

**WTR vs ATR memory:**
- **W**ith-the-rule = **W**alls have it (most young eyes): steep vertical = cyl axis horizontal (180°)
- **A**gainst-the-rule = **A**ged: steep horizontal = cyl axis vertical (90°)

**Normal fundus values:**
- Disc = 1.5–1.7mm, round/oval, sharp margins, red-yellow, cup at center
- Veins darker, more tortuous, pulsating; arteries lighter, straighter, reflex stripe
- V:A ratio = 3:2 (veins are the larger number)
- Fovea appears darker than surrounding retina

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1: "Direct ophthalmoscopy gives an inverted image."**
  Wrong. DIRECT gives an ERECT (upright) VIRTUAL image. INDIRECT gives an INVERTED REAL image. This is one of the most commonly tested distinctions.

- **Trap 2: "A stronger condensing lens in indirect gives better magnification."**
  Wrong. Higher power condensing lens = LESS magnification but WIDER field of view. The image is formed closer to the lens, making it smaller. +14D gives higher magnification and narrower field than +20D.

- **Trap 3: "Van Herick Grade 0 means no anterior chamber."**
  Correct interpretation — Grade 0 is when the cornea appears to "sit" on the iris with no shadow visible. This means angle closure. Dilating drops in this situation can trigger acute angle-closure glaucoma.

- **Trap 4: "Javal's rule only applies to WTR astigmatism."**
  Wrong. Javal's rule applies to both WTR and ATR corneal astigmatism. The formula accounts for the direction through the −0.50 Cyl × 90° constant. For WTR Ac, the internal component partially cancels; for ATR Ac, it adds.

- **Trap 5: "The slit lamp cobalt blue filter is used for blood vessel examination."**
  Wrong. The RED-FREE filter is used for blood vessels (makes them appear very dark). The COBALT BLUE filter is used with fluorescein dye to show corneal staining patterns.

- **Trap 6: "In opacity parallax, an anterior opacity moves in the same direction as the ophthalmoscope."**
  Wrong. An ANTERIOR opacity moves in the OPPOSITE direction to the ophthalmoscope (parallax against). A posterior opacity moves in the SAME direction. The confusion happens because the relationship reverses when the patient moves their eye vs when the ophthalmoscope moves.

- **Trap 7: "The keratometer measures total corneal power."**
  Wrong. The keratometer measures ANTERIOR corneal surface power only, using a simplified refractive index (1.3375 or 1.336 depending on instrument design). It does not measure the posterior corneal surface. This is why Javal's rule is needed to estimate total astigmatism.

- **Trap 8: "The light source is part of the slit lamp's mechanical system."**
  Wrong. The light source belongs to the ILLUMINATION system. The mechanical system is the joystick, the sliding base/table, and the patient-positioning frame (chin and forehead rest). This is a classic "which one is NOT part of the mechanical system" trap — the answer is the light source.

- **Trap 9: "Higher magnification gives you a bigger field of view."**
  Wrong. Higher magnification always gives a SMALLER (decreased) field of view and less depth of focus. You trade width for detail. Start low (6x) to find the target, then zoom in.

- **Trap 10: "Flare and cells are the same anterior-chamber finding."**
  Wrong. FLARE is protein leaked into the aqueous (a hazy "headlight-in-fog" beam). CELLS are discrete floating white blood cells. Flare = protein; cells = inflammatory cells. Both are graded in a fully dark room at high magnification (25–40x).

- **Trap 11: "Keep the overhead light off for the whole slit lamp exam."**
  Wrong. You DIM the room light, not kill it, for most of the exam — you still need light to position the patient and align the instrument. The room goes fully dark only for the anterior-chamber cell/flare assessment.

- **Trap 12: "Sweep the lids and lashes once and move on."**
  Wrong. The lids and lashes are examined in SECTIONS, not one quick sweep. A single pass misses the lid margin, meibomian orifices, and lash-base pathology (a hidden internal hordeolum of the meibomian gland, early trichiasis).

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 55-year-old patient has keratometry readings of OD: 43.50 @ 180 / 45.50 @ 90. Using Javal's rule, what is the expected total (refractive) astigmatism?

- A. −2.00 Cyl × 180°
- B. −2.00 Cyl × 90°
- C. −2.00 Cyl × 180° combined with internal correction
- D. −2.00 Cyl × 180° (Ac = 2.00D; At ≈ −2.00D WTR)

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Working:**
Ac = 45.50 − 43.50 = 2.00D. Weakest meridian is at 180° → Ac = −2.00 Cyl × 180° (WTR).

At = 1.25(−2.00 × 180) + (−0.50 × 90)
   = (−2.50 × 180) + (−0.50 × 90)
   = This cross-cylinder combination: resolves to −2.00 Cyl × 180° approximately.

Working through the cross-cylinder: −2.50 Cyl × 180 = −2.50 Cyl × 180; and −0.50 × 90 partially cancels the WTR component. The net total astigmatism is approximately **−2.00 Cyl × 180°**.

**D is correct.** The internal astigmatism (−0.50 × 90°) partially cancels the WTR corneal component in Javal's rule, reducing the predicted total slightly from 2.50D to ~2.00D.

</details>

**2.** During slit lamp examination of a patient being evaluated for dilation, the Van Herick shadow width appears equal to about one-quarter of the corneal optic section width. Which of the following actions is most appropriate?

- A. Proceed with dilation — the angle is wide open
- B. Dilate with caution and monitor for angle closure
- C. Do not dilate — the angle is closed
- D. Measure IOP before deciding, then dilate freely if normal

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Dilate with caution and monitor for angle closure.**
A shadow width equal to 1/4 of the corneal optic section width corresponds to **Van Herick Grade 2** (ratio 1:4). This indicates a narrow anterior chamber angle — not closed, but at risk. Dilation is not absolutely contraindicated but must be done with caution, the patient should be monitored, and the IOP checked after dilation. Grade 0–1 = absolute contraindication. Grades 3–4 = safe to dilate. Grade 2 = proceed with caution.

</details>

---

**3.** Which of the following is NOT part of the slit lamp's mechanical system?

- A. Joystick
- B. Light source
- C. Motorized table
- D. Patient-positioning frame

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Light source.** The light source belongs to the ILLUMINATION system (source, condenser, slit, filters). The mechanical system moves and steadies the instrument and patient: the joystick, the sliding/motorized base and table, and the positioning frame (chin and forehead rest). Watch for this "which is NOT mechanical" phrasing — the light source is the trap answer.

</details>

**4.** You need to survey a patient's ocular adnexa, then examine the cornea, then check the anterior chamber for cells and flare. Which magnification progression is most appropriate?

- A. 40x → 16x → 6x
- B. 6x → 16x → 25–40x
- C. 16x → 16x → 16x
- D. 25x → 6x → 40x

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. 6x → 16x → 25–40x.** Adnexa is a wide low-power survey (**6x**), the cornea is a middle-layer view (**16x**), and cells/flare in the anterior chamber need the highest magnification in a fully dark room (**25–40x**). Remember: higher magnification always means a **smaller** field of view, so you start low to find the target and zoom in for detail. The Van Herick angle estimate sits in the middle at 16x with the arm at 60°.

</details>

**5.** On slit lamp exam in a dark room, the beam through the anterior chamber shows a hazy "headlight-in-fog" glow with no discrete particles. What does this indicate?

- A. Inflammatory white blood cells (cells)
- B. Protein leaked into the aqueous (flare)
- C. Pigment on the corneal endothelium
- D. A closed anterior chamber angle

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Protein leaked into the aqueous (flare).** Flare is the Tyndall haze of leaked protein — a diffuse glow with no distinct particles. CELLS are discrete floating white blood cells that look like dust in a sunbeam. Flare = protein; cells = inflammatory cells. Both are assessed at high magnification (25–40x) in a fully dark room. This is separate from Van Herick, which grades the angle, not inflammation.

</details>
