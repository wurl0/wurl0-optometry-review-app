## C3-01 — Preliminary Examination
*Personal study notes | TOS: C.3 | 150 items (Subject C)*

---

## The Simple Version

The preliminary examination is everything done before you pick up a retinoscope. It gives you a snapshot of the patient's functional vision status: how clearly they see, how their pupils behave, how their eyes move and align, and how close they can focus. Each test answers a specific clinical question. When you understand the question each test is answering, you stop memorizing procedures and start reasoning through what any abnormal finding means. A case history is not small talk — it is a diagnostic tool. The first test is observation. Everything follows from there.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "If a patient walks in and you could do only one preliminary test before the refraction, which would it be and why?" The argument forces them to think about what information is irreplaceable and what builds on what. Visual acuity is the answer — it is the baseline against which every other finding is interpreted.

---

## But Why?

**Why is the case history done first, before any tests?**
Because it directs which tests are necessary.

**Why does the chief complaint determine the test sequence?**
Because a patient with diplopia needs motility and cover testing prioritized. A patient with blur needs VA and refraction first. Without the history, you run every test without knowing which finding matters.

**Why does the history have to proceed from general to specific?**
Because asking specific questions first introduces bias. A patient asked "is your headache in your forehead?" will say yes even if it is not. Open-ended first, then focused.

**Why is the preliminary examination done before the refraction?**
Because several preliminary findings directly modify the refraction approach. A reduced NPC tells you to add convergence testing to the BV analysis. A positive Marcus Gunn tells you to investigate the optic nerve before attributing poor VA to refractive error. Pupil anomalies change your pharmacology decisions. The preliminary examination is the map that tells you where the refraction will lead.

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Word Roots / Origin | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Asthenopia | Greek: *asthenes* (weak) + *ops* (eye) | Eye strain — umbrella term for discomfort, fatigue, blur from visual effort |
| Version | Latin: *versio* (turning) | Conjugate binocular eye movement — both eyes move same direction simultaneously |
| Duction | Latin: *ducere* (to lead) | Monocular eye movement in any direction |
| Vergence | Latin: *vergere* (to incline) | Binocular eye movements in opposite directions (convergence/divergence) |
| NPC | Near Point of Convergence | Closest point the eyes can maintain convergence without diplopia or deviation |
| NPA | Near Point of Accommodation | Closest point the eye can still focus clearly (maximum accommodation used) |
| PD / IPD | Pupillary Distance / Interpupillary Distance | Distance between the centers of the two entrance pupils |
| Phoria | Greek: *phora* (carrying) | Latent deviation — controlled by fusion; only appears when fusion is broken |
| Tropia | Greek: *tropos* (turning) | Manifest deviation — constant misalignment even with both eyes open |
| Angle kappa | Angle between visual axis and pupillary axis | Normally +5° / 0.5mm nasal; negative = temporal shift; large positive mimics exotropia |
| RAPD | Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect | Paradoxical dilation on swinging flashlight = afferent defect ipsilateral side |
| Marcus Gunn pupil | Named after Robert Marcus Gunn | RAPD; impaired optic nerve or retinal input compared to fellow eye |
| Hirschberg test | Named after Julius Hirschberg | Corneal reflex method to estimate tropia magnitude in prism diopters |
| PERRL | Pupils Equal Round Reactive to Light | Standard abbreviation for normal pupil findings |
| Anisocoria | Greek: *aniso* (unequal) + *kore* (pupil) | Unequal pupil sizes; physiologic if difference ≤1mm and consistent in light and dark |

> Lookalike trap: **Phoria vs Tropia** — a phoria is latent (only seen on cover testing); a tropia is manifest (visible to inspection). A tropia is NEVER normal.

> Lookalike trap: **NPC vs NPA** — NPC is about convergence (eyes crossing); NPA is about accommodation (lens focusing). Both are "near points" but measure completely different functions.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**The flow of preliminary examination:**

```
CASE HISTORY
    → Chief complaint directs test priority
    → Profile, ocular/medical/family history, medications, visual demands

ENTRANCE TESTS (done in dimly lit room first for pupil assessment)
    1. Pupil size (static observation)
    2. IPD measurement
    3. Visual acuity (far and near, unaided then aided)
    4. Pinhole VA (if VA is reduced)
    5. Corneal reflex / fixation test (Hirschberg)
    6. Cover-Uncover Test (phoria vs tropia)
    7. Version / Ocular Motility Test
    8. NPC
    9. Near Point of Accommodation (NPA)
   10. Pupillary Reflex Tests (direct, indirect, near, swinging flashlight)
   (+ color vision, visual field if indicated)
```

**Why this order matters:**
- IPD is measured before phoropter setup to avoid induced prism
- VA is taken unaided first (before any lenses) to capture baseline and predict the refractive error
- Cover test is done before corneal reflex because cover test is more sensitive for phorias
- Pupils are assessed in dim room to see maximum pupil size and reaction range

### Formula / Law / Rule / Theory Breakdown

---

**Near PD Calculation — Formula 2 (Simplified)**

```
NPD = (DPD × WD) / (WD + 27)

DPD = far (distance) PD in mm
WD  = near working distance in mm
27  = constant (13.5mm behind cornea to center of rotation + 13.5mm spectacle plane)
```

**What it states in plain language:**
As the eyes converge for near, they physically move closer together. The near PD is always smaller than the distance PD. This formula calculates how much the PD decreases based on the working distance.

**What each part means:**
- **DPD** — the measured distance PD; the baseline measurement when the eyes are parallel
- **WD** — the patient's intended reading distance in mm; closer working distance = greater convergence = smaller near PD
- **27** — accounts for the location of the eyes' center of rotation relative to the spectacle plane; standard anatomical constant (13.5mm × 2)
- The formula says: the near PD is proportional to the working distance, scaled by the total distance from the lens to the eye's rotation point

**The analogy:**
Think of two spotlights on ceiling tracks. When they both point straight ahead (infinity), they are maximally separated (distance PD). When you angle them to shine at a point 40cm below on the floor, they tilt inward — the beams are now closer at the floor (near PD). The tighter the target, the more they cross inward.

**Cross-subject connections:**
- **D2-01 (Dispensing):** The near PD is critical for bifocal and near lens decentration. Wrong near PD creates unwanted prism at the reading position.
- **B5 (BV):** Convergence demand is set by the working distance relative to the PD. This formula is the basis for calculating how many prism diopters of convergence the patient must maintain at near.

**Reading the Result:**

| NPD result | What it means |
|---|---|
| NPD < DPD | Normal (expected — eyes converge at near) |
| NPD = DPD | Abnormal — no convergence occurring; suspect severe CI or strabismus |
| NPD > DPD | Error in measurement |

---

**Amplitude of Convergence**

```
Amp. of Conv. (Δ) = (NPD in cm × 10) / (NPC in cm + 3)

NPD = near pupillary distance in cm
NPC = near point of convergence distance from bridge of nose, in cm
3   = distance from bridge of nose to the spectacle plane (constant)
```

**What it states in plain language:**
The amplitude of convergence is the maximum amount of convergence available to the patient, expressed in prism diopters.

**Why the "+3":** The NPC is measured from the bridge of the nose. The spectacle plane is approximately 13mm in front of the center of rotation. The "+3" converts NPC from the bridge-of-nose reference to the spectacle plane reference.

**Clinical note:** Normal amplitude of convergence varies but a rough clinical norm is 30–60 prism diopters for a young adult. The actual demand at a given working distance can be calculated separately.

---

**Hirschberg Method — Corneal Reflex to Prism Diopters**

```
Corneal reflex displacement → Approximate deviation:
  Slightly nasal (0.5mm)  = physiologic (+angle kappa, 5°, ~10Δ)
  At pupillary margin      = 15° = 30 prism diopters
  Between pupil and limbus = 30° = 60 prism diopters
  At limbus                = 45° = 90 prism diopters
```

**What it states in plain language:**
Each millimeter the corneal light reflex is displaced from its normal nasal position represents approximately 15 prism diopters of deviation (or equivalently, 7 degrees).

**Interpretation direction:**
- Reflex displaced NASALLY beyond normal → ESO deviation (eye deviated inward)
- Reflex displaced TEMPORALLY → EXO deviation (eye deviated outward)
- Reflex displaced UP → HYPO deviation; displaced DOWN → HYPER deviation

**Cross-subject connections:**
- **F2 (Pediatric):** Hirschberg is the go-to alignment test for young children who cannot cooperate with cover testing.
- **D1 (Prisms):** The 1mm = 7° = ~15Δ relationship follows from the geometry of the corneal reflex — the same principle as the prismatic deviation formula.

**Visual Acuity — Recording and Classification**

```
NOTATION SYSTEMS:
  Snellen Imperial (English):  20/20  (distance in feet)
  Snellen Metric:               6/6   (distance in meters)
  Decimal/International:        1.0

CONVERSION: 20 feet = 6 meters
  20/20 = 6/6 = 1.0
  20/40 = 6/12 = 0.5
  20/200 = 6/60 = 0.1

ORDER OF TESTING:
  1. Unaided (habitual without glasses)    → OD, then OS, then OU
  2. Habitual (with current glasses)        → OD, then OS, then OU
  3. Pinhole                               → only if VA < 20/30 to 20/40
  4. Near VA                               → 40cm (16 inches) standard distance

PINHOLE INTERPRETATION:
  VA improves through pinhole → uncorrected refractive error (correctable)
  VA does NOT improve through pinhole → organic pathology (not correctable by lenses)
  VA WORSENS through pinhole → fully corrected already, or dense media opacity
```

**Subnormal Vision Techniques (when VA < 20/200 and chart cannot approach)**

```
20/200 at close distance → record as "20/200 at _ft"
Counting Fingers (CF)    → CF at 3ft
Hand Motion (HM)         → HM at 4ft
Light Projection (LP)    → can locate penlight in each quadrant
Light Perception (LP)    → can detect light present/absent
No Light Perception (NLP)→ complete absence of vision
```

**Cover Test Logic Tree**

```
Cover the FIXING eye
        |
        Does the NON-COVERED eye move?
        |
        +-- NO MOVEMENT → Orthotropia (no tropia; normal or phoria)
        +-- MOVES OUT (temporal) → ESOTROPIA (non-covered eye was turned inward)
        +-- MOVES IN (nasal) → EXOTROPIA (non-covered eye was turned outward)
        +-- MOVES UP → HYPOTROPIA of non-covered eye
        +-- MOVES DOWN → HYPERTROPIA of non-covered eye

Uncover the covered eye
        |
        Does it drift back into position?
        +-- YES, returns to straight → PHORIA (latent; fusion restored it)
        +-- NO, stays deviated → TROPIA (manifest; no fusion control)

ALTERNATING COVER TEST:
  Blocks fusion completely → reveals MAXIMUM deviation (phoria + any tropia)
  Movement seen in ACT but NOT in unilateral = PHORIA
  Movement seen in BOTH = TROPIA
```

**Pupillary Reflex Pathway (afferent and efferent arms)**

```
AFFERENT ARM (light signal in):
Retina → Optic Nerve → Optic Chiasm → Optic Tract → Pretectal Nucleus
  (light signal leaves main visual pathway here — does NOT go to LGN for reflex)
  Pretectal Nucleus → bilateral Edinger-Westphal Nucleus (both sides = consensual)

EFFERENT ARM (constriction signal out):
Edinger-Westphal Nucleus → Preganglionic fiber (CN3)
  → Ciliary Ganglion → Postganglionic fiber → Sphincter Pupillae → CONSTRICTION

THREE REFLEXES:
  Direct (DLR):      Light → ipsilateral pupil constricts
  Indirect/Consensual (ILR): Light → CONTRALATERAL pupil also constricts (via bilateral E-W)
  Near/Accommodative (PNR): Near object → miosis + accommodation + convergence (near triad)
```

**NPC Procedure and Normal Values**

```
PROCEDURE:
  1. Patient with habitual Rx seated at room illumination
  2. Target (penlight or near accommodative target) held at 40cm midline
  3. Move target slowly toward patient's nose
  4. Patient reports when target doubles → BREAK POINT
  5. Move target slowly away → patient reports single again → RECOVERY POINT
  6. Record as: break/recovery (e.g., 7/10cm)

NORMAL VALUES (Scheiman and Wick):
  Accommodative target: Break 5cm ± 2.5 / Recovery 7cm ± 4.0
  Penlight or red lens:  Break 7cm ± 4.0 / Recovery 10cm ± 5.0

INTERPRETATION:
  6–10cm expected NPC (general guideline)
  < 5cm: convergence EXCESS
  > 10cm: convergence INSUFFICIENCY (CI)
  No doubling at all: suppression or wandering attention (not normal)
```

### Comparisons That Matter

**Three Types of Cover Tests**

| Test | What it detects | Binocularity during test | Board clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unilateral cover test | TROPIA (manifest deviation) | One eye covered; fellow eye observed | Movement in non-covered eye = tropia |
| Cover-uncover test | PHORIA (latent deviation) | Cover then uncover; eye returns = phoria | Movement only after uncovering = phoria |
| Alternating cover test | MAXIMUM deviation (phoria + tropia combined) | Fusion broken repeatedly | Does NOT differentiate phoria from tropia |

**Visual Acuity Types (Forms)**

| Type | Alternate name | What it measures | Example test |
|---|---|---|---|
| Minimum visible | Detection acuity | Presence/absence of object vs background | Single dot on white background |
| Minimum separable | Resolution acuity | Two separate lines/dots as distinct | Grating, Landolt C, Verhoeff rings |
| Minimum cognoscible | Recognition acuity | Identify complex target/letter | Snellen chart (most used clinically) |
| Minimum discriminable | Vernier acuity | Relative position of two aligned elements | Vernier alignment task |
| Board clue | Lowest form | — Most commonly tested | Highest form: minimum separable (by some) |

**Direct vs Indirect Pupillary Reflex**

| Feature | Direct Light Reflex (DLR) | Indirect / Consensual (ILR) |
|---|---|---|
| Eye illuminated | Same eye that constricts | OPPOSITE eye constricts |
| Pathway tested | Afferent (retina → optic nerve → E-W) + Efferent | Afferent of illuminated eye + contralateral efferent |
| Abnormal finding | Impaired DLR = ipsilateral afferent or efferent problem | Impaired ILR = ipsilateral afferent pathway problem |
| Board clue | No DLR in illuminated eye = CN3 efferent palsy or severe ipsilateral afferent defect | ILR absent when direct is present = ipsilateral afferent problem confirmed |

**Phoria vs Tropia (full comparison)**

| Feature | Phoria | Tropia |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Latent deviation; controlled by fusion | Manifest deviation; present even with both eyes open |
| Visible on inspection | No — looks normal | Yes — eye appears turned |
| Detected by | Cover-uncover test | Unilateral cover test OR Hirschberg |
| Binocular vision | Preserved (fusion controls it) | Absent or disrupted |
| Normal finding? | Yes — small phorias are normal | A tropia is NEVER normal |
| Expected norms | Distance: 1 eso to 3 exo / Near: ortho to 6 exo | None; any tropia is abnormal |

### Memory Anchors

**Cover test movement directions (phoria and tropia):**
For PHORIA: "The eye moves away from where it wants to hide."
- ESophoria: eye HIDES inward (nasal) when covered, MOVES OUT (temporal) when uncovered back to fusion
- EXophoria: eye HIDES outward (temporal) when covered, MOVES IN (nasal) when uncovered

For TROPIA: observed in the NON-COVERED eye
- ESotropia: uncovered eye is turned IN → moves OUT when you cover the fixing eye
- EXotropia: uncovered eye is turned OUT → moves IN when you cover the fixing eye

**Pupillary reflex pathway mnemonic: RONPC-EWC-GS**
- **R**etina → **O**ptic nerve → **N**ot going to LGN (branches off at) → **P**retectal nucleus → **C**rosses to both **E**dinger-**W**estphal nuclei → **C**iliary **G**anglion → **S**phincter

**VA interpretation quick rules (habitual vision):**
- 20/20 = emmetropia OR compensated hyperopia
- Less than 20/20 = ametropia present
- Miscalled letters = astigmatism
- Binocular < monocular = muscle imbalance or binocular difficulty
- Variable / unstable VA = accommodative spasm

**NPC mnemonics:**
- "Break is when it doubles; recovery is when it singles." (This order never changes)
- "Below 5 = too much; above 10 = too little." (Convergence excess and insufficiency)

**Headache location to refractive cause:**
- **F**rontal = **F**arsightedness (hyperopia) / convergence excess
- **T**emporal = **T**orque (astigmatism)
- **O**ccipital = **O**ld age (presbyopia) / CI / vertical imbalance
- **P**arietal = **P**sychological / tension headache

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1: "Visual acuity of 20/20 means no refractive error."**
  Wrong. A patient can have facultative hyperopia corrected by accommodation and still measure 20/20 without glasses. The 20/20 is clear FUNCTIONAL vision — it does not rule out an underlying refractive error.

- **Trap 2: "Pinhole always improves VA."**
  Wrong. Pinhole improves VA when the cause is uncorrected refractive error (by eliminating peripheral aberrations). If the cause is organic (optic neuropathy, macular degeneration), pinhole does NOT improve VA. If the patient is already fully corrected, pinhole may WORSEN VA slightly.

- **Trap 3: "Corneal reflex displaced temporally = exo deviation."**
  Correct. Temporal reflex placement means the fovea is not aligned with the target — the eye is deviated inward (ESO), so the corneal reflex ends up temporally. This is the opposite of what students initially expect.
  > Nasal reflex = exo deviation; Temporal reflex = eso deviation.

- **Trap 4: "A phoria means the patient has strabismus."**
  Wrong. Phorias are common and often asymptomatic if fusional reserves are adequate. The expected norms include a small amount of exophoria. Only a tropia (manifest) is always abnormal.

- **Trap 5: "NPC is tested with one eye occluded."**
  Wrong. NPC is a BINOCULAR test — both eyes are open. You are testing the maximum point at which both eyes can still maintain convergence. If you occlude one eye, you are testing something else.

- **Trap 6: "The direct light reflex pathway passes through the LGN."**
  Wrong. The pupillary reflex pathway branches off the optic tract at the pretectal nucleus — BEFORE reaching the LGN. This is why patients with cortical blindness (LGN or beyond) can still have intact pupillary reflexes.

- **Trap 7: "The alternating cover test distinguishes a phoria from a tropia."**
  Wrong. The alternating cover test breaks fusion completely and reveals the TOTAL deviation (phoria + tropia combined), but it does NOT tell you whether the deviation is a phoria or tropia. For that distinction, you need the unilateral cover test.

- **Trap 8: "Monocular VA better than binocular VA is normal."**
  Wrong. Binocular VA should be equal to or BETTER than the better monocular eye. If OU VA < OD or OS monocular VA, it indicates a binocular vision problem (suppression, muscle imbalance, or binocular difficulty).

## Practice Questions

**1.** During a preliminary examination, the examiner covers the patient's right eye. The uncovered left eye is observed to move inward (nasally). Upon removing the occluder from the right eye, the left eye moves back outward. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

- A. Left esotropia
- B. Left exophoria
- C. Left esophoria
- D. Right exotropia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Left exophoria.**
The uncovered left eye moved INWARD (nasal) when the right was covered. This means the left eye wanted to drift outward (exo direction) when fusion was released by covering. When the cover was removed, the left eye moved back out to its normal position — consistent with a PHORIA (latent deviation controlled by fusion). The inward movement of the left eye when uncovered means the left eye was EXO under cover. Since it corrected on uncovering and there was no movement on the unilateral cover test, this is a phoria, not a tropia.

</details>

---

**2.** A patient's VA is OD 20/60, OS 20/20. Pinhole placed over the right eye improves VA to 20/30 but not to 20/20. The most likely explanation is:

- A. The right eye has a purely organic pathology
- B. The right eye has an uncorrected refractive error only
- C. The right eye has both an uncorrected refractive error AND an organic component
- D. The pinhole result is invalid — a 20/60 eye should always reach 20/20 through pinhole

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. The right eye has both an uncorrected refractive error AND an organic component.**
Pinhole improvement indicates a refractive component (since aberrations were reduced and VA improved from 20/60 to 20/30). However, 20/20 was not reached, indicating the improvement is incomplete — an organic component (amblyopia, early cataract, macular pathology) is also limiting the final VA. Pure refractive error alone would be expected to reach 20/20 (or close to it) through pinhole. A = wrong because some improvement occurred. B = wrong because the improvement was incomplete.

</details>
