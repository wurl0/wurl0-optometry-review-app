## 1. Refractive Status

**Refractive status** describes the relationship between the posterior principal focus of the eye's refractive mechanism and the retina, with accommodation completely relaxed.

Think of it this way: does the eye's focal point land exactly on the retina when it is not working to focus? That answer defines the refractive status.

### Factors That Influence Refractive Status
1. Length of the eyeball (axial length)
2. Power of the cornea
3. Power of the lens
4. Depth of the anterior chamber

### Classification
- **Emmetropia** — posterior principal focus lands exactly on the retina
- **Ametropia** — posterior principal focus does NOT land on the retina (either in front or behind)

### Refraction (Clinical Meaning)
- **Static refraction** — measurement of refractive status when accommodation is completely at rest
- **Dynamic refraction** — measurement when accommodation is active (not at rest)

### Ametropia (Error of Refraction)
Also called refractive error. Classified by:

**By axial length:**
- Axial length is the primary determining factor

**By refractive mechanism (power of the media), dependent on:**
- Index of refraction of the media
- Curvature of the media

**Three types:**
1. **Myopia** — posterior principal focus lands in front of the retina
2. **Hyperopia** — posterior principal focus falls virtually behind the retina
3. **Astigmatism** — multiple foci, located either in front of or behind the retina

## 3. Emmetropia and Emmetropization

### Emmetropia
A refractive condition in which, with accommodation relaxed, parallel rays of light converge to focus exactly on the retina.

From Greek *emmetros* = "well-proportioned" or "fitting." The eye's optical power and axial length are perfectly balanced.

### Emmetropization
The natural developmental process by which the axial length and refractive power of the eye gradually balance out to approach emmetropia.

This mechanism coordinates the development of:
- Axial length
- Corneal refracting power
- Anterior chamber depth

The goal is to prevent ametropia. It explains why most children are mildly hyperopic at birth but trend toward emmetropia as the eye grows.

### Ametropia Classification (2 Types from Sorsby)

| Type | Also called | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Correlative** | Simple | Caused by normal biological variation; largely hereditary; not progressive beyond normal growth; 0.25 to 6.00 D |
| **Component** | Pathological | Due to abnormal development or acquired disease; can progress beyond normal limits; above 6.00 D |

## 4. Refractive Status of Children

### At Birth (Full-Term Babies)
- Usually slightly hyperopic: **+0.60 D to +2.60 D**, mean **+1.80 D**
- Reason: eyeball is not yet fully developed (shorter axial length)

### Premature Babies
- Larger range: −1.30 D to +1.10 D, mean **+0.60 D**
- High myopia in premature infants may be associated with retrolental fibroplasia (ROP)
- Astigmatism present at birth disappears more rapidly if the more hyperopic axis is horizontal

### Early Childhood

| Age Range | Typical Change |
|---|---|
| Birth to 3 years | Axial length increases by ~5 mm on average |
| 3 to 13 years | Increase of ~1 mm axial length per year |
| Pre-school | Globe increases most rapidly |

### School Age Patterns
- Myopia typically appears at 5–6 years old; if present, tends to remain or increase
- Hyperopia above +1.50 D at 5–6 years old tends to remain
- Spherical refraction of +0.50 to +1.25 D at 5–6 → tends to become emmetropic by 13–14 years
- Prevalence of myopia is significantly less in rural than urban children (environmental exposure)

## 5. Accommodation

**Accommodation** is the process by which the crystalline lens changes its focal length in response to changes in the vergence (direction) of incoming light — allowing the eye to focus on near objects.

More simply: when you look at something close, your lens bulges to increase its power. When you look far away, the lens flattens.

### Historical Background (Proof of Existence)

| Researcher | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Christopher Scheiner | 1619 | Scheiner's disc — used card with two pinholes; object not on retina appears double |
| Thomas Young | 1801 | Demonstrated that the crystalline lens (not the cornea or axial length) is responsible for accommodation |
| Bowman and Bruke | 1847 | Each independently identified the ciliary muscle as responsible for lens power changes |
| Fincham | 1937 | Described ciliary muscle structure; supported Helmholtz Theory |
| Helmholtz | 1855 | Used Purkinje images to observe changes during accommodation |

### Helmholtz Theory of Accommodation
- Natural (resting) shape of the lens is spherical (fully accommodated form)
- When lens is in unaccommodated state: zonular fibers are **taut/stretched**, holding the lens in its flattest, thinnest form
- When ciliary muscle **contracts**: tension on zonular fibers is **released**, allowing the elastic lens capsule to assume greater curvature — lens becomes thicker and more spherical (more power)

### Changes During Accommodation (Helmholtz, 1855)
1. Pupil **constricts** (miosis)
2. Pupillary margin of iris and anterior lens surface move **forward**
3. Anterior surface of lens becomes **more convex**
4. Posterior surface becomes **slightly more convex**
5. Lens sinks slightly **downward** (due to gravity)
6. Choroid moves **forward**

### Ciliary Muscle Structure (Fincham, 1937)
Three types of fibers:

| Fiber Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Longitudinal (meridional) | Origin at scleral spur; insert into elastic lamina at equatorial portion |
| Radial | Short fibers from scleral spur going axially backward; give rise to circular fibers |
| Circular | Innermost fibers; act like a sphincter |

Moses (1975) described 3 bundles:
- **Anterior bundle** — strongest/thickest; greatest effect holding lens in unaccommodated position
- **Equatorial fibers** — relatively few in number
- **Posterior fibers** — many but thin

### Triad of Accommodation (Three simultaneous responses)
1. **Constriction/Miosis** — pupil constricts
2. **Convergence** — eyes turn in
3. **Contraction** — ciliary muscle contracts, lens rounds up

**Hallmark signs:**
- Accommodation failure → **blurring**
- Convergence failure → **doubling** (diplopia)

## 6. Amplitude of Accommodation

**Amplitude of accommodation (AOA)** is the total amount of change in the eye's focusing power from its far point to its near point, measured in diopters.

### Methods of Determination
- Push-up method
- Minus lens technique
- By age (Hofstetter's formula)
- Donder's table
- VT #19 (amplitude of accommodation test)

### Hofstetter's Formulas

```
Minimum AOA = 15 − 0.25 × age
Average AOA = 18.5 − 0.30 × age
Maximum AOA = 25 − 0.40 × age
```

**Formula depth:** These are empirical regression equations based on population data. The constant (15, 18.5, 25) is the theoretical maximum at age zero. The coefficient (0.25, 0.30, 0.40) is the rate of loss per year. By age 40, minimum AOA = 15 − 10 = 5 D — right at the clinical threshold for presbyopia.

**Cross-subject connection:** Presbyopia is clinically relevant when AOA drops below 5.00 D. The ADD for presbyopia is directly calculated from AOA.

### Worked Example

Patient age 20:
```
Minimum AOA = 15 − 0.25(20) = 10.00 D
Average AOA = 18.5 − 0.30(20) = 12.50 D
Maximum AOA = 25 − 0.40(20) = 17.00 D
```

### Presbyopia ADD Determination

**By AOA:**
```
ADD = Working Distance − ½(AOA)
```

**Middle third technique:**
```
ADD = NRA − ½(PRA − NRA)
```

## 7. Far Point, Near Point, Depth of Focus

### Far Point of Accommodation
The farthest point in space where the eye can form a clear image without accommodation.

- In emmetropia: optical infinity (6 m or farther)
- In myopia: a real point in front of the eye (the far point is closer than infinity)
- In hyperopia: an imaginary point behind the eye (accommodation is needed even for distance)

### Near Point of Accommodation
The closest point in space where the eye can form a clear image with maximum accommodation.

### Depth of Focus
The extent to which the retinal image may be located in front of or behind the retina and still appear sharp and clear.

### Depth of Field
The extent to which the visual acuity chart (object) may be moved toward or away from the eye while the optical image remains focused on the retina.

**Key relationship:** Depth of focus **increases** as the blur circle **decreases** (smaller pupil = larger depth of focus).

## 8. Myopia

### Definition
A refractive condition in which, with accommodation relaxed, parallel rays of light converge to focus **in front of the retina**.

The classic "nearsighted" eye — clear at near, blurry at distance.

### Historical Background

| Person | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Aristotle | — | First mentioned nearsightedness |
| Johannes Kepler | 1611/1617 | Gave the actual definition of myopia |
| Donders | 1866 | Definitively described and classified myopia |

### Etiology
1. **Elongated axial length** — most common cause
2. **Refractive system too strong** for axial length
 - Index of refraction too high: aqueous, cornea, lens nucleus/core
 - Index of refraction too low: lens cortex, vitreous
 - Curvature: radius too short/steep
3. **Increased depth of anterior chamber**
4. **Cataract formation** (nuclear sclerosis increases n of nucleus = myopic shift)
5. **Diabetes mellitus** (osmotic lens changes)

### Classification of Myopia

**1. By Amount**

| Category | Range |
|---|---|
| Low degree | 0.25 to 3.00 D |
| Medium | 3.00 to 6.00 D |
| High | 6.00 D and above |

**2. By Sorsby**

| Type | Range |
|---|---|
| Correlative (simple) | Up to 6.00 D |
| Component (pathological) | More than 6.00 D |

**3. By Origin**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Correlative (simple) | Benign, stationary; mild; customary type; axial or refractive |
| Component (degenerative) | Pathological, progressive, malignant; due to abnormal ocular development |

Subtypes of correlative:
- Axial myopia
- Refractive myopia

**4. By Onset and Course**
- Stationary myopia
- Temporarily progressive
- Permanently progressive

**5. By Form**
- Simple myopia
- Degenerative myopia

**6. By Etiology**
- Self-inflicted vitreous glaucoma (simple myopia)
- Active anterior chamber glaucoma
- Inactive glaucoma

**7. By Age of Onset**
- Congenital myopia (present at birth)
- Youth-onset (6–15 years)
- Early adult-onset
- Late adult-onset (>18 years)

### Symptoms (Subjective)
1. Blurring of vision at far
2. Asthenopic headache (due to extended near use)

### Signs (Objective)
1. Dilated appearance of pupil
2. Squinting of eyes
3. Staring expression
4. Exophthalmos (high myopia)
5. Myopic crescent (visible on ophthalmoscopy)

### Distribution / Prevalence

| Category | Rate |
|---|---|
| Overall prevalence | 25 to 40% |
| Congenital | 1 to 2% |
| Early onset (6–15 y/o) | 15 to 30% |
| Late onset (>18 y/o) | 8 to 10% |
| Below 6.00 D | 20% more common in males |
| Above 6.00 D | Twice as common in females |

**Factors affecting myopia prevalence:** age, race (Chinese and Japanese highest), family income (diet/lifestyle), years of education, amount of near work.

### Irregular Conditions in Myopia

**Pseudomyopia (False Myopia)**
- Tonic spasm of accommodation making an emmetrope or hyperope appear myopic
- Other names: school myopia, college myopia, false myopia, refractive myopia, functional myopia
- Approaches to management:
 - Fogging lenses (strong plus lenses)
 - Near point corrections (plus at near)
 - Prism base-in corrections
 - Orthoptic techniques

**Night Myopia / Nocturnal Myopia**
- Myopia that appears in dim illumination; lesser error in daylight

**Keratoconus**
- Irregular/increasing corneal curvature → increasing myopia

### Prognosis
- Progression typically begins in childhood
- Rate: 0.25 to 0.55 D/year until teen years
- Earlier onset = faster progression = greater final myopia
- Myopic shift in elderly usually from crystalline lens changes (nuclear sclerosis)

### Management
1. Ophthalmic lenses (concave/minus)
2. Contact lenses
3. Visual training
4. Surgical approach (LASIK, PRK)

## 9. Hyperopia

### Definition
A refractive condition in which, with accommodation relaxed, parallel rays of light converge to focus **behind the retina**.

The classic "farsighted" eye — can often use accommodation to compensate for distance, but struggles at near.

### Historical Background

| Person | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Kastner | 1855 | First identified and described hyperopia |
| Donders | 1858 | Confirmed and suggested the term "hypermetropia" |
| Helmholtz | 1859 | Used the word "hyperopia" |

### Etiology

**Simple (biological variation):**
1. Axial length too short for focal length
2. Refractive system too weak:
 - Index too high: lens cortex or vitreous
 - Index too low: aqueous, lens nucleus, or cornea
 - Curvature: radius too long/flat
3. Anterior chamber depth decreased (less refractive power)
4. Old age (normal senile changes)

**Pathological (outside normal variation):**
1. Deformational: axial diameter pathologically short (microphthalmos, optic edema, tumors, trauma)
2. Curvature: cornea plana (flattened cornea), ulcers
3. Index of refraction: metabolic changes
4. Absence of a component: aphakia (absent crystalline lens)
5. Lens displacement:
 - Anterior displacement → hyperopia decreases (present in shallow-angle glaucoma)
 - Lateral displacement → partial aphakia, possible diplopia

**Functional:**
- Paralysis of accommodation
- Spasm of accommodation

**Developmental facts:**
- At birth, 80–90% of eyes are hyperopic
- Degree of hyperopia decreases during adolescence as axial length increases
- Established at approximately age 16 (about 48% of all eyes)

### Classification of Hyperopia

**By Degree/Amount**

| Category | Range |
|---|---|
| Low degree | 0.25 to 3.00 D |
| Medium | 3.00 to 5.00 D |
| High | 5.00 D and above |

**By Sorsby**

| Type | Range |
|---|---|
| Correlative | 0.25 to 6.00 D |
| Component | Above 6.00 D |

**By Action of Accommodation**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Facultative** | Amount that CAN be overcome by accommodation; patient sees clearly using accommodation |
| **Absolute** | Amount that CANNOT be overcome by accommodation; vision is subnormal |
| **Latent** | Amount masked by tonic ciliary tone; not revealed by routine refraction; revealed in presbyopia; two subtypes: tonic (permanent spasm) and clonic (temporary spasm) |
| **Manifest** | Amount revealed by routine refraction; subdivided into facultative and absolute |
| **Total** | Entire amount of hyperopia = latent + manifest |

> Board tip: Total = Latent + Manifest. Manifest = Facultative + Absolute.

### Symptoms (Subjective)
1. Asthenopia (headache, frontal and occipital)
2. Blurring of vision at near
3. Difficulty of fixation (near to far, far to near)
4. Nausea
5. Vomiting
6. General fatigue
7. Photophobia
8. Tearing

### Signs (Objective)
1. Constricted pupil (from constant accommodation effort)
2. Conjunctival irritation
3. Convergent strabismus (esotropia)
4. Vertical brow wrinkles
5. Endophthalmic (deep-set) eyes

### Pathological Predispositions
1. **Glaucoma** — shallower aqueous chamber
2. **Strabismus** — macula's relative distance from visual axis
3. **Infection** — excessive accommodative apparatus activity and circulatory congestion

### Management by Age

| Age | Approach |
|---|---|
| Up to 6 years | Correct only if vision subnormal, strabismus present, or asthenopia |
| 6 to adolescence | Correct even small amounts if asthenopia or vision affected |
| Adolescence to presbyopia | Full correction since error is now fully manifest |
| Presbyopia | Increase distance correction (index of refraction changes) |
| Pre-cataract | "Second sight" — lens sclerosis temporarily restores accommodation; hyperopia may diminish |
| Cataract development | Second sight lost; prescription changes rapidly; stronger plus may be needed |

### Management by Muscle Anomaly

| Condition | Approach |
|---|---|
| Esotropia | Maximum plus that doesn't handicap vision |
| Esophoria | Maximum plus that doesn't blur vision |
| Exophoria | Partial correction; if full needed, add prism base-in or orthoptics |

### Management by Latency

| Situation | Approach |
|---|---|
| Full correction | Constant blur, much near work, marked eyestrain, reduced accommodation amplitude (read 20/50) |
| Partial correction | Divergent strabismus/exophoria, large latency, very young, maximum distance acuity needed |

## 10. Astigmatism

### Definition
A refractive condition in which a **point focus** cannot be formed on the retina. Instead, there are multiple foci — because the eye has different refracting power in different meridians.

Considered the most common refractive error corrected today.

### Structural Basis
- Two principal meridians that are 90° apart
- **Axis meridian** — meridian of least power
- **Power meridian** — meridian of greatest power
- Most astigmatism is of the regular type

### Historical Background

| Person | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Thomas Young | Had and measured his own astigmatism (~1.75 D); remained when eye was submerged (thus lens, not cornea) |
| George Biddle Airy | First to correct astigmatism with a spherocylindrical lens (USA, 1828) |
| John Green | First distance test chart for astigmatism; clock dial chart; suggested current axis location method |
| H. Knapp | First trial case containing cylindrical lenses |

### Etiology
- Different corneal curvatures in different meridians
- Different lenticular curvatures
- Traction of external muscles on scleral coat
- Eccentric position of fovea relative to visual axis
- Changes in vitreous gel (change in index)
- Irregularity in retinal surface
- Angle alpha (may cause ~0.50 to 0.75 D ATR astigmatism)

### Symptoms
1. Blurred vision (higher astigmatism)
2. Transient blurring (constant accommodation effort)
3. Headaches (frontal or temporal)
4. Tearing and smarting
5. Tiring of the eyes (even low grades)

### Signs
1. Tilting of the head
2. Squinting
3. Wrinkling and furrowing of brows
4. Holding reading material too close

### Classification of Astigmatism

#### A. By Location

**1. Total/Physiologic astigmatism**
The sum resultant astigmatism of all refracting surfaces:
- Anterior corneal (primary)
- Residual:
 - Posterior corneal
 - Lenticular surface
 - Lenticular zonule
 - Lenticular lamellae

**2. Corneal astigmatism** — variation of corneal curvature across meridians

**3. Lenticular astigmatism** — due to unequal curvature of lens surfaces or layers

---

#### B. Corneal Regular Astigmatism: Referred to Each Cornea

| Type | Definition | Axis |
|---|---|---|
| **With-the-rule (WTR) / Direct** | Greatest curvature (power) near vertical meridian | 60° to 120° |
| **Against-the-rule (ATR) / Inverse/Perverse** | Greatest power near horizontal meridian | 0° to 30° and 150° to 180° |
| **Oblique** | Greatest power between 30°–60° and 120°–150° | 30° to 60° / 120° to 150° |

---

#### C. Corneal Regular Astigmatism: Referred to Both Corneas

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Symmetric — Homologous | Both eyes have WTR; axes sum to 180° |
| Symmetric — Heterologous | Both eyes have ATR; axes sum to 180° |
| Asymmetric | Axes do NOT sum to 180° |
| Homonymous | Both eyes same type (both WTR or both ATR) |
| Heteronymous | One eye WTR, other eye ATR |

---

#### D. By Resultant (Related to Retinal Focus)

| Type | Abbreviation | Focus | Image at Point Source | Correction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Compound Hyperopic | CHA | Both meridians behind retina | Oval: vertical in ATR, horizontal in WTR | Plus sphere + plus cylinder |
| Simple Hyperopic | SHA | One on retina, other behind | Straight line: vertical in ATR, horizontal in WTR | Simple plus cylinder |
| Compound Myopic | CMA | Both meridians in front of retina | Oval: vertical in WTR, horizontal in ATR | Minus sphere + minus cylinder |
| Simple Myopic | SMA | One on retina, other in front | Straight line: vertical in WTR, horizontal in ATR | Simple minus cylinder |
| Mixed | MA | One in front, other behind | Circle (equal error) or oval (unequal) | Contrageneric (cylinder stronger than sphere); if cylinder weaker = congeneric |

> Board tip: CHA and CMA have **opposite** oval orientations. In WTR, the steeper vertical meridian focuses first (closer to eye) so myopic focus is vertical.

## 11. Anisometropia

### Definition
A refractive condition in which the refraction of one eye differs from the other.

- Difference as little as 0.25 D (Hartstein) or at least 1.00 D (Grosvenor) or 2.00 D (Borish)

### Related Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Isometropia | Refractive power of both eyes is equal |
| Isoanisometropia | Both eyes have the same type (both hyperopic or both myopic) |
| Antimetropia | One eye myopic, the other hyperopic; a mixed anisometropia |
| Iso-oxyopia | Equal visual acuity in both eyes when fully corrected |
| Aniso-oxyopia | Unequal visual acuity in both eyes when fully corrected |

### Prevalence

| Difference | Prevalence |
|---|---|
| 0.50 to 1.00 D | 23.1% |
| 1.00 to 1.50 D | 7.4% |
| 1.50 to 2.00 D | 2.9% |
| 2.00 D or more | 1.6% |

### Causes
- Congenital conditions
- Surgery, injury, systemic diseases
- Nuclear cataract in one eye

### Effects of Anisometropia
1. **Aniseikonia** — unequal image sizes
2. **Anisophoria** — different degree of heterophoria in various gaze directions
3. **Amblyopia and suppression** (in young patients)
4. **Strabismus** secondary to anisometropic amblyopia

### Classification by Borish (Classes I–V)

| Class | Difference | Clinical Profile |
|---|---|---|
| Class I | < 1.50 D | Both eyes used together; good fusion and stereopsis |
| Class II | 1.50 to 3.00 D | Fusion present most of the time; suppression in central field; mild amblyopia in nondominant eye |
| Class III | > 3.00 D | Deeper amblyopia; corrects only to 20/100 max; also called **refractive amblyopia**; peripheral fusion intact |
| Class IV | Equal VA, use one eye | Emmetropic in one eye and moderately myopic in other; alternating use for distance vs. near |
| Class V | Equal VA with muscle imbalance | Associated strabismus; also called **strabismic amblyopia** |

### Management

**Hyperopic anisometropia with esotropia:**
- Full correction of each eye
- Occlusion of the better eye
- Visuoscope analysis (fixation type — central or extrafoveal)
- Pleoptics may be required

**Hyperopic anisometropia without strabismus:**
- Atropine on non-amblyopic eye to prevent accommodation, forcing the amblyopic eye to work

**Myopic anisometropia:**
- Corrective lenses for myopia
- Differences up to 6.00 D can be tolerated (without astigmatism)
- Differences beyond 6.00 D cannot be helped by any form of treatment

### Instruments

**Visuoscope** — ophthalmoscope adapted with a small central opaque fixation target; projects a shadow onto the retina; position of shadow relative to fovea indicates fixation type (central or eccentric)

**Pleoptics** — treatment for amblyopia exanopsia; concentrated stimulation of the fovea; procedures:
1. Intense light stimulation of non-foveal area to render fovea receptive
2. Association of touch, hearing, and memory with visual perception

## 12. Aniseikonia

### Definition
A condition in which the ocular images are **unequal in size and shape** between the two eyes.

### Types

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Overall symmetrical | One ocular image is symmetrically larger than the other in all dimensions |
| Meridional symmetrical | One ocular image is larger in one meridian only (usually vertical or horizontal) |
| Compound symmetrical | Combination of overall and meridional types |

### Causes
1. Differences in size of dioptric images formed on the retina
2. Differences in distribution of retinal elements

### Symptoms
Headaches, asthenopia, reading difficulty, photophobia, nausea, vertigo/dizziness, general fatigue.

### Suspecting Aniseikonia
- Symptoms not relieved by corrective glasses
- Definite degrees of anisometropia causing distortion
- Patient reports comfort when one eye is closed

### Measurement
**Eikonometer** — instrument to measure aniseikonia
- Standard type — measures ocular images
- Space eikonometer — measures rotation of observed field using binocular cues

### Correction
**Isokonic / Iseikonic lenses** — alter magnification by changing lens thickness and back curve without altering dioptric power.
Methods:
- Toric surfaces on front, back, or both surfaces
- Doublet lenses (two lenses fixed together, like telescope objective)
- Fused bifocals

## 13. Presbyopia

### Definition
A condition in which the **amplitude of accommodation diminishes with increasing age** to the point where clear or comfortable vision at the near point is no longer achievable.

- Clinically, presbyopia = amplitude insufficient for the patient's near functional needs
- Acceptable onset age: typically **45**, but range is 40 to 48–50 years
- Not a disease — an expected, inevitable, and normal aging process
- Sometimes defined operationally as near point recession beyond **20 cm**

### Causes
1. **Sclerosis of lens fibers and capsule** — primary cause (Borish); index of lens changes as well
2. Sclerosis of connective tissue of ciliary body → reduced elastic action
3. Decreased elasticity of ciliary blood vessels → increased tension on suspensory ligaments
4. Nutritional changes with aging → lens sclerosis
5. Ultraviolet radiation — suspected to chemically alter lens substance (Schanz)
6. Infrared radiation — has caused cataracts in some exposures

### Classification (Cline, Hofstetter, and Griffin)

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Incipient presbyopia** | Early stage; small print can still be read without addition but with effort |
| **Absolute presbyopia** | Accommodative ability is commonly absent; some convex lens power necessary for print |
| **Premature presbyopia** | Manifests before age 40 |
| **Nocturnal presbyopia** | Apparent amplitude reduction induced by reduced illumination intensity |

### Historical Background

| Person | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Aristotle | 384 BC | Differentiated refractive error from presbyopia |
| Levene | 1977 | Reviewed Aristotle's detection and measurement of refractive errors |
| Levene, Hofstetter, Maurolycus | — | Theorized presbyopia results from changes in the crystalline lens |

### Common Symptoms and Signs

| Symptom/Sign | Notes |
|---|---|
| Blur at near; increased working distance | First complaint |
| Decreasing near VA (with distance correction) | Sign of inadequate add |
| Amplitude below 5.00 D | Clinical threshold for presbyopic add |
| Increasing lag of accommodation | Needs more plus/less minus at near |
| Increasing exophoria (or decreasing esophoria) at near | Some patients with high AC/A show esophoria early |
| Decreasing PRA (positive relative accommodation) | Reduced ability to overcome plus at near |
| No blur point on base-out/base-in vergence at near | Late sign of very low amplitude |

**Customary Near Working Distance (CNWD)** — influenced by:
- Patient stature
- Customary near tasks
- Vocational and avocational needs
- Patient's ametropia and lens effectivity

### Methods of Determining Reading Addition

| Method | Formula/Approach |
|---|---|
| By AOA | ADD = WD − ½(AOA) |
| Middle third technique | ADD = NRA − ½(PRA − NRA) |
| Cross-cylinder technique | 14A and 14B tests |
| By age | Donder's table or Hofstetter's formula |
| By dynamic retinoscopy | MEM or Nott retinoscopy |
| Push-up method | Find near point, calculate AOA |

### Management

**Spectacle lenses:**
- Multiple pairs of glasses
- Bifocal lenses
- Multifocal / progressive lenses

**Contact lenses:**
- Alternating vision contact lenses
- Simultaneous vision bifocal/multifocal CLs
- Monovision

## 14. Amblyopia

### Definition
Amblyopia ("lazy eye") is a disorder characterized by poor or blurry vision in an eye that is otherwise physically normal, or out of proportion to any structural abnormality.

- Reduced form vision not fully explained by organic ocular pathology
- No cloudiness of cornea/lens, no apparent retinal lesion
- Caused by poor or absent transmission of visual image to the brain during a critical period of visual development
- Visual system development is largely complete and "locked in" by **8 to 10 years of age**
- Usually unilateral; can be bilateral if both eyes are similarly deprived

### Etiology
- Deprivation of form vision, abnormal binocular interaction, or both during visual immaturity
- Physical occlusion (ptosis, cataract)
- Refractive errors
- Anisometropia
- Strabismus

### Classification

**Organic amblyopia** — structural cause

**Functional amblyopia (main types):**

| Type | Cause |
|---|---|
| Strabismic amblyopia | Brain suppresses poorer-quality image from deviated eye; treated by clarifying image + patching/penalization |
| Anisometropic (refractive) amblyopia | Anisometropia causes one eye to receive chronically blurred image; favored eye develops normal VA; treated with early correction + patching |
| Form-deprivation amblyopia (amblyopia ex anopsia) | Opaque media (cataract, corneal scar, ptosis) blocks visual input; treat by removing opacity + patching |
| Occlusion amblyopia | May complicate hemangioma blocking vision |
| Nutritional amblyopia | Nutritional deficiency |
| Toxic amblyopia | Toxic agents |
| Hysterical amblyopia | Psychological origin |
| Isometric amblyopia | — |
| Congenital amblyopia | Present from birth |

### Symptoms
1. Poor visual acuity
2. Separation difficulty (reduced crowding effect)
3. Anopsia
4. Reduced contrast sensitivity
5. "Higher-level" deficits (reduced motion sensitivity)
6. Problems with stereovision

### Signs
1. Strabismus
2. Difficulty leaning forward
3. Squinting
4. Head cocking/tilting
5. Attempts to look around occluder or push it away
6. Restlessness; refusal to continue testing

### Prevalence
Strabismus affects about 2–35% of the population.

### Prognosis
- Good prognosis: nutritional, toxic, hysterical, isometric, anisometric, and strabismic amblyopia
- Poor prognosis: light deprivation amblyopia
- Earlier detection and treatment = better recovery chances

### Management

| Type | Management |
|---|---|
| Strabismic amblyopia | Glasses ± patching of good eye; force use of amblyopic eye; surgical alignment if needed |
| Anisometropic amblyopia | Early prescription; patching or atropine in better eye; vision therapy |
| Form-deprivation amblyopia | Remove opacity ASAP; then patch or penalize good eye |
| All types | Caution against over-patching; can cause reverse amblyopia in the good eye |

### Memory Anchors

**Triad of Accommodation:** 3 C's
- **C**onstriction (pupil miosis)
- **C**onvergence (eyes turn in)
- **C**ontraction (ciliary muscle)

**Hallmark signs:**
- Accommodation disorder = **blurring**
- Convergence disorder = **doubling**

**Myopia causes** (mnemonic: INDEX too HIGH):
- Index too high in: aqueous, cornea, lens nucleus
- Index too low in: lens cortex, vitreous
- Axial too long, curvature too steep

**Hyperopia causes** (opposite of myopia):
- Index too high in: lens cortex, vitreous
- Index too low in: aqueous, lens nucleus, cornea
- Axial too short, curvature too flat

**Hyperopia by accommodation — mnemonic: FALTM**
- **F**acultative (can overcome)
- **A**bsolute (cannot overcome)
- **L**atent (hidden, not revealed)
- **T**otal = Latent + Manifest
- **M**anifest = Facultative + Absolute

### Miscellaneous Board Facts
- Angular size of fovea centralis: **54 mm of an arc**
- Standard luminance: **10 ft lamberts**
- Depth of focus increases as blur circle **decreases** (smaller pupil = more depth of focus)
- Presbyopia becomes clinically apparent when AOA drops below **5.00 D**
- Amblyopia visual system is "locked in" by **8 to 10 years** of age
- At birth: full-term babies hyperopic mean **+1.80 D**; premature babies mean **+0.60 D**
- Myopia rate of progression: **0.25 to 0.55 D/year** until teen years
- Critical onset age for myopia classification: early onset (6–15 y/o), late onset (>18 y/o)
