How this simulation behaves, and why that matters

The two line sets do not come to focus together. Each principal meridian of the lens forms a line image at right angles to itself, so the sphere lines sharpen at one drum setting and the cyl line sharpens at another. The gap between the two readings is the cylinder. That is the whole reason lensmetry takes two readings rather than one.

  • Sphere lines (the triple set) sharpen when the drum reaches the axis meridian. That reading is your sphere.
  • Cyl line (the single bold line) sharpens at sphere plus cyl. Subtract to get the cylinder.
  • Broken mires mean the axis wheel is wrong, not the power. The lines shear apart whenever the target sits off a principal meridian, and they snap back into one continuous line only at the true axis. Chase the break before you chase the blur.
  • With a pure sphere there is no break and both sets sharpen at the same setting.

Working order on the real instrument: focus the eyepiece on the reticle first, seat the lens back surface against the stop, centre the mires, then rotate the axis wheel until the lines are unbroken, then take your two readings.

Record the prism

Reading the rings, and the direction trap
  • Each reticle ring is one prism dioptre. Count rings from centre to the middle of the displaced mires.
  • The mires displace toward the base. If the target sits high in the field, it is base up. Reading the displacement backwards is the most common error at this station, and it is why the checker calls it out separately.
  • Base in and base out are defined relative to the nose, so they swap sides between OD and OS. The nasal side is labelled on the reticle. Watch it flip when you change eyes.
  • Compound prism is recorded as separate horizontal and vertical components, not as a single resultant, because that is how it gets ordered and verified.

Reticle orientation here assumes the frame is seated with the temples pointing away from you, so you view it as though facing the wearer. If your instructor seats the frame the other way, the nasal side mirrors. Confirm the convention on the actual instrument at MCU.

Record the add

Why the lens has to be flipped

The add sits on the front surface of the lens. Measured from the back, the distance portion behind it inflates the reading, and the error grows with the distance power. Turn the spectacle round so the front surface rests on the stop, take both readings that way, and the difference is the true add.

  • Back surface on the stop gives back vertex power. That is the correct way to read the distance Rx, and the wrong way to read the add.
  • Front surface on the stop gives front vertex (neutralising) power. The single reading is not the Rx, but the difference between distance and near zones is the add.
  • Try it deliberately: measure one target both ways and watch the add change on a high plus lens. On a low power the two agree, which is why the error hides until it matters.

The size of the error here is modelled approximately. The point being drilled is that it exists, which way it runs, and that it scales with the distance power.

Induced prism

Prentice, part by part

Δ = c × F

  • Δ is prism dioptres: 1Δ displaces the image 1 cm at 1 m. It is a tangent of deviation, not an angle, which is why the components add as vectors.
  • c is decentration in centimetres, measured from the optical centre to the point the eye actually looks through. Millimetres is the units trap: divide by 10.
  • F is the lens power in dioptres, which is itself 1/f in metres. So the formula is really "how far off centre, times how strongly the surface bends".

Where it comes from: a lens is a stack of prisms whose apex angle grows with distance from the centre. At the centre there is no prism at all. Step away and the local wedge gets steeper in direct proportion, which is why the relationship is linear rather than curved.

Physical picture: a plus lens is two prisms joined base to base at the optical centre, so any off-centre point acts base toward the centre. A minus lens is two prisms joined apex to apex, so it acts base away from the centre. That single image gives you the base direction without memorising a table.

Connections: this is the same quantity you neutralise in the lensmeter at station 2, it is what makes anisometropia produce vertical imbalance on downgaze, and it is the working formula behind slab-off and deliberate decentration in dispensing.
