## A2-02 — Eyelids, Conjunctiva, and Lacrimal System
*Personal study notes | TOS: A.2 | Syllabi 5.5, 5.6, 5.7, 5.8, 5.22–5.24*

---

## The Simple Version

The eyelids are the eye's first and most active line of defense — they blink 15 times a minute, spread tears across the cornea on every closure, and physically block foreign bodies from reaching the eye. The conjunctiva lines the inside of the lids and covers the front of the eyeball, and its goblet cells produce the mucin that makes the tear film stick to the cornea. The lacrimal system produces tears and drains them away — the gland makes them, the blink spreads them, the puncta collect them, and the nasolacrimal duct carries them into the nose (which is why you get a runny nose when you cry). The tear film itself is a three-layer structure, and each layer has one job: the lipid layer prevents evaporation, the aqueous layer carries oxygen and antibodies, and the mucin layer anchors the whole film to the cornea.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "Why does a contact lens wearer get dry eyes faster than a spectacle wearer?" That question leads directly to the tear film layers, blink rate, and goblet cell function simultaneously.

---

## But Why?

**Why does blinking matter for vision, not just protection?**
Because without a fresh tear film, the corneal surface becomes irregular.

**Why does an irregular corneal surface matter?**
Because the tear film's oily top layer creates the smooth optical surface the eye needs to focus light cleanly — if it breaks up, incoming light scatters instead of focusing.

**Why does the tear film break up at all?**
Because the oily lipid layer evaporates, thinning until it can no longer hold the aqueous layer beneath it in place.

**Why can't the eye just produce more tears to compensate?**
Because the real problem in most dry eye is not volume — it is either too little mucin (so the film doesn't stick) or too little lipid (so it evaporates too fast). Adding more aqueous layer doesn't fix either problem.

**Why does this matter clinically?**
Because Schirmer's test measures aqueous production, and TBUT measures film stability. A patient can have normal Schirmer's but low TBUT — meaning they produce enough tears but the film breaks up before the next blink. These are different problems requiring different management.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**The blink cycle — how eyelids make vision possible:**

Blinking is not just protective — it is optical maintenance. On every blink:

1. The upper lid descends (orbicularis oculi contracts, LPS relaxes)
2. The lid margin spreads a fresh layer of tear film across the cornea
3. The lid reopens (LPS contracts, orbicularis relaxes)
4. The fresh tear film gives the eye 10–15 seconds of stable optical surface before it begins to break up
5. The next blink renews it

Rate: 15 blinks/minute spontaneous. Contact lens wearers blink less, which is one reason their tear film breaks up faster.

**The lacrimal secretory pathway:**

Two levels of tear production:

```
BASIC SECRETION (reflex-independent)
→ Glands of Krause (at fornix) + Glands of Wolfring (at tarsus)
→ Always on — maintains baseline tear volume between emotional/reflex events

REFLEX SECRETION (nerve-controlled)
→ Main lacrimal gland
→ Triggered by: corneal irritation (CN5), bright light, emotion, CN7 stimulation
```

**The parasympathetic pathway to the lacrimal gland** (this is a board-tested sequence):

```
CN7 nucleus (pons) — lacrimal nucleus
    ↓
Motor fibers exit with CN7
    ↓
Geniculate ganglion (no synapse here — just passes through)
    ↓
Greater petrosal nerve (exits petrous temporal bone)
    ↓ joins with
Deep petrosal nerve (sympathetic post-ganglionic from carotid plexus)
    ↓ = 
Vidian nerve / Nerve of pterygoid canal
    ↓
Pterygopalatine (sphenopalatine) ganglion — SYNAPSE here
    ↓
Post-ganglionic fibers join CN5 maxillary → zygomatic nerve
    ↓
Communicating branch to lacrimal nerve (CN5 ophthalmic)
    ↓
Lacrimal gland → increased secretion
```

Why does this matter? Because CN7 damage above the geniculate ganglion causes decreased tearing (dry eye) in addition to facial paralysis. CN7 damage below the geniculate only causes facial paralysis — tearing is spared. The level of the lesion determines whether the lacrimal gland is involved.

**The tear drainage pathway:**

```
Tear film on cornea
    ↓ (blink pumps tears medially)
Lacrimal lake (medial canthus)
    ↓
Lacrimal puncta (upper + lower, 0.3mm opening)
    ↓
Lacrimal canaliculi (vertical 2–3.5mm → horizontal 8mm → meets lacrimal sac)
    ↓
Lacrimal sac (12mm long × 6mm wide; on medial orbital wall)
    ↓  guarded by valve of Beraud (inferior pouch = sinus of Arlt)
Nasolacrimal duct (12–24mm; runs in nasolacrimal canal)
    ↓  guarded by valve of Hasner
Inferior meatus of nose
```

The nasolacrimal duct is the last part of the drainage system to open during fetal development — this is why newborns can have congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (epiphora in neonates), which usually resolves by 6–12 months as the duct fully opens.

### Structure or Process Map

**Histologic layers of the eyelid — skin to conjunctiva:**

```
1. SKIN + SUBCUTANEOUS TISSUE
   — thinnest skin in the body (loose areolar connective tissue, no fat)
   — so thin you can see vessels through it when edematous

2. MUSCULAR LAYER
   a. Orbicularis oculi (CN7) — sphincter of eyelid
      ├── Pars orbitalis (peripheral) — forcible closure; pulls forehead/temple/cheek
      ├── Pars palpebralis (central)
      │    ├── Preseptal — over orbital septum
      │    └── Pretarsal — over tarsus; blink involves this layer only
      └── Pars lacrimalis — attached to lacrimal sac; pumps tears into drainage system
   b. Levator palpebrae superioris (CN3) — elevates upper eyelid
      Origin: lesser wing of sphenoid (above lateral Annulus of Zinn)
      Insertion: tarsal plate + skin of eyelid (forms superior orbital furrow)
   c. Müller's muscle (Sympathetic nerve) — tonic elevator
      Superior: origin = underside of LPS → inserts at superior tarsal plate
      Inferior: origin = fascial expansion of IR → inserts at inferior tarsal plate
      So what: Müller's is the fine-tuner. LPS does the heavy lifting (elevates 15mm);
      Müller's adds 2–3mm of tone. Horner's syndrome paralyzes Müller's → 1–2mm
      partial ptosis. CN3 palsy paralyzes LPS → complete ptosis.

3. TARSUS (fibrous skeleton)
   — dense fibrous connective tissue; contains Meibomian glands
   Upper tarsus: 11mm vertical diameter
   Lower tarsus: 5mm vertical diameter
   Thickness: 1mm | Length: 25–30mm

4. CONJUNCTIVA (innermost layer)
   — see Conjunctiva section below
```

**Eyelid glands — what each produces and where it opens:**

| Gland | Type | Opens at | Layer produced | So what |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Meibomian / Tarsal | Modified sebaceous; holocrine | Lid margin (posterior to gray line) | Lipid layer of tear film (mainly) | 30–40 upper lid, 20–30 lower lid; dysfunction = evaporative dry eye |
| Zeis | Sebaceous | At base of cilia | Lipid layer (minor contribution) | Infection = hordeolum externum (external stye) |
| Moll | Sweat (apocrine) | Between cilia at lid margin | Sweat component | Infection = hordeolum externum |
| Krause | Accessory lacrimal | Fornix (20 upper, 6–8 lower) | Aqueous layer (basic secretion) | Main basic secretors; present even without CN7 stimulation |
| Wolfring | Accessory lacrimal | At tarsus | Aqueous layer (basic secretion) | Located at tarsal border; also basic secretors |
| Goblet cells | Unicellular mucinous | Conjunctival surface | Mucin layer of tear film | Numerous at fornix, fewer at bulbar, absent at limbus and lid margins |

> The distinction between basic and reflex secretion is clinically important: after denervation of the main lacrimal gland (CN7 palsy), basic secretion from Krause and Wolfring is preserved. The eye is not completely dry, but reflex tearing (crying, irritation) is lost.

**Conjunctiva — 3 parts:**

```
PALPEBRAL (TARSAL) CONJUNCTIVA
— lines the inner surface of the eyelids
— firmly adherent to tarsus (cannot be separated)
— so tightly bound that chemosis (swelling) never affects this portion

BULBAR CONJUNCTIVA
— loosely covers the front of the sclera
— firmly adherent only at the limbus
— looseness = allows eyeball movement without tearing conjunctiva

FORNIX CONJUNCTIVA
— the deep pockets where palpebral and bulbar conjunctiva meet
— very loose = allows the greatest range of eye movement
— same looseness = first place to swell in chemosis
— Superior fornix: 10mm from limbus (more Krause glands here)
— Inferior fornix: 8mm from limbus (more lymphoid follicles here)
— Lateral fornix: 14mm from limbus
— No medial fornix (replaced by plica semilunaris and caruncle)
```

Cul-de-sac: the 2mm-deep space between bulbar and palpebral conjunctiva. Holds 20–30 µL of tears — this is the reservoir that absorbs eye drops. Drops instilled here spread across the conjunctival surface on the next blink.

**Conjunctiva histologic layers:**

```
Layer 1 — Stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium
  └── Contains goblet cells (unicellular mucin-secreting glands)
      — Most abundant: fornix
      — Moderate: bulbar conjunctiva
      — Absent: lid margins and limbus
      Why: goblet cells at the fornix serve as the main mucin reservoir.
      Loss of goblet cells (from trachoma, pemphigoid, chemical burns, SJS)
      = mucin-deficient dry eye = poor tear film stability.

Layer 2 — Lamina propria / Substantia propria
  ├── Blood vessels
  ├── Nerves
  ├── Glands of Krause (accessory lacrimal — aqueous basic secretion)
  ├── Glands of Wolfring (accessory lacrimal — aqueous basic secretion)
  ├── Mast cells (allergy responders — IgE → histamine release)
  ├── Lymphocytes, plasma cells, PMNs (immune surveillance)
  └── Lymphoid follicles (especially inferior fornix)
```

---

**Tear film — 3 layers (outermost to innermost):**

```
LAYER 1 — LIPID / OILY LAYER
Thickness: 0.2–0.9 µm (avg 0.5 µm) — thinnest layer
Source: Meibomian glands (mainly) + Zeis glands (minor)
Composition: wax esters (35%), cholesterol (30%), phospholipids (16%),
             triglycerides (4%), free fatty acids (2%), free sterols (2%)
Functions:
  a. Retards evaporation of the aqueous layer beneath it
  b. Creates the smooth, regular optical surface of the precorneal film
  c. Provides lubrication at the lid/eye interface
  d. Prevents overflow epiphora (hydrophobic barrier / lipid strip)
Board clue: Meibomian gland dysfunction (MGD) = lipid deficiency = evaporative dry eye.
            Schirmer's test will be NORMAL (aqueous is fine); TBUT will be LOW.

LAYER 2 — AQUEOUS LAYER
Thickness: 6.5–7.5 µm (avg 7 µm) — THICKEST layer
Source: Glands of Krause + Wolfring (basic) and main lacrimal gland (reflex)
Composition: 98% water + NaCl, K, HCO3, urea, glucose (2.5 mg%), proteins
             Proteins: albumin (60%), globulin (15–20%), lysozyme (21–25%)
             Immunoglobulins: IgA (major — 17mg/100ml), IgG, IgE, IgM (negligible)
Functions:
  a. Supplies atmospheric O2 to corneal epithelium (cornea is avascular)
  b. Carries antimicrobial agents (lysozyme + IgA)
  c. Flushes debris and desquamated cells
  d. Supports vertical tear film stability

LAYER 3 — MUCIN LAYER
Thickness: 0.02–0.05 µm — thinnest true layer
Source: Goblet cells of conjunctival epithelium
Function: Converts the hydrophobic corneal epithelium surface to hydrophilic
          so the aqueous layer can spread and stick.
Board clue: Without mucin, tear film cannot spread across the cornea regardless
            of how much aqueous is present. Goblet cell loss = mucin-deficient dry eye.
```

The three-layer model from outermost to cornea is: **Oil → Water → Mucus** (OWM).
Memory anchor: **"Oil covers Water covers Mucus"** — or think of salad dressing: oil on top, water underneath, and the "stickiness" (mucin) at the surface it clings to.

### Comparisons That Matter

**Schirmer's test vs TBUT — which problem does each detect:**

| Feature | Schirmer's Test | Tear Break-Up Time (TBUT) |
|---|---|---|
| What it measures | Volume of aqueous tear production | Stability of the tear film |
| Tool used | 35×5 mm Whatman #41 filter paper (IsoSol strip) | Fluorescein dye + cobalt blue filter on slit lamp |
| Procedure | Hook at lateral lower lid for 5 minutes | Instill fluorescein, measure seconds from last blink to first dry spot |
| Normal value (Schirmer I, no anesthesia) | 10–25 mm | >10 seconds |
| Abnormal | <6 mm = hyposecretion (KCS/dry eye); >25 mm = hypersecretion | <10 seconds = unstable film (dry eye) |
| What layer problem | Aqueous layer deficiency | Lipid or mucin layer deficiency |
| Board clue | Sjögren's syndrome → very low Schirmer's | MGD or goblet cell loss → normal Schirmer's, low TBUT |

**Basic vs Reflex secretion:**

| Feature | Basic Secretion | Reflex Secretion |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Glands of Krause + Wolfring | Main lacrimal gland |
| Nerve control | Not controlled by nervous system | CN7 (parasympathetic) via pterygopalatine ganglion |
| Rate | 0.9–2.2 µL/min | Variable (triggered) |
| Stimulus | Always on | Corneal irritation, bright light, emotion, nasal stimulation |
| Clinically | Preserved in CN7 palsy | Lost in CN7 palsy (no crying, no reflex tearing) |
| Board clue | "Basal tears" — the maintenance supply | "Lacrimation" — the overflow response |

**Crocodile tears (Bogorad syndrome):**
After CN7 lesions at the facial ganglion, regenerating salivary nerve fibers can be misdirected to the lacrimal gland — so the patient tears when eating (salivary stimulus triggers lacrimal secretion instead). This is the clinical meaning of "crocodile tears."

### Memory Anchors

**Tear film layers from outside to cornea — "Oil Waters Mucin" (OWM):**
Lipid (oil) → Aqueous (water) → Mucin

**Gland-to-layer source pairs:**
- Meibomian/Zeis → Lipid layer
- Krause/Wolfring + Main lacrimal gland → Aqueous layer
- Goblet cells → Mucin layer

**Lacrimal drainage order — "Pretty Little Canals Send Nasal Tears" (PLCSNT):**
Puncta → (Lacrimal) Canaliculi → (Lacrimal) Sac → Nasolacrimal duct → Nose

**CN7 lacrimal pathway — "7 Goes Petrosal to Vidian to Palate":**
CN7 → Greater petrosal → Vidian nerve → Pterygopalatine ganglion → Zygomatic → Lacrimal nerve → Lacrimal gland

**Müller's vs LPS ptosis rule:**
- Müller's paralyzed (Horner's): 1–2 mm droop (partial ptosis)
- LPS paralyzed (CN3 palsy): complete ptosis (eye fully closed)

**Goblet cell distribution rule — "Most at Fornix, None at Limbus":**
Fornix > bulbar > lid margin > limbus. Where cells are most loose, cells are most numerous.

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "The aqueous layer is produced only by the main lacrimal gland" — Wrong. The basic aqueous secretion comes from accessory glands (Krause + Wolfring). The main lacrimal gland only activates for reflex secretion.
- **Trap 2:** "Schirmer's test measures tear film stability" — Wrong. Schirmer's measures aqueous volume. TBUT measures stability (lipid + mucin integrity).
- **Trap 3:** "The thinnest layer of the tear film is the aqueous layer" — Wrong. The aqueous layer (7 µm) is the thickest. The lipid (0.5 µm) and mucin (0.03 µm) layers are much thinner.
- **Trap 4:** "Goblet cells are found throughout the conjunctiva equally" — Wrong. Most at fornix, fewer at bulbar, absent at lid margins and limbus.
- **Trap 5:** "Complete ptosis from Horner's syndrome" — Wrong. Horner's paralyzes Müller's muscle, causing only 1–2 mm partial ptosis. Complete ptosis requires LPS paralysis (CN3 palsy).
- **Trap 6:** "The nasolacrimal duct opens at the middle meatus" — Wrong. It opens at the inferior meatus, guarded by the valve of Hasner.
- **Trap 7:** "Pars lacrimalis of orbicularis aids blinking" — Misleading. Its specific role is pumping tears into the lacrimal sac, not blinking. Blinking involves pars palpebralis.
- **Trap 8:** "CN7 damage always causes dry eye" — Only if the lesion is above the geniculate ganglion (greater petrosal nerve affected). Below the geniculate = facial paralysis only; tearing is preserved.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 35-year-old patient complains of chronic eye irritation, foreign body sensation, and redness worsening in the afternoon. Schirmer's test (without anesthesia) measures 18 mm bilaterally. Tear break-up time is 5 seconds. Which of the following is the most likely underlying problem?

- A. Aqueous-deficient dry eye from lacrimal gland hyposecretion
- B. Lipid layer deficiency from Meibomian gland dysfunction
- C. Excessive tear drainage due to punctal stenosis
- D. Conjunctival goblet cell hyperplasia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Lipid layer deficiency from Meibomian gland dysfunction.**

The normal Schirmer's test (18 mm — well within 10–25 mm) rules out aqueous deficiency. The short TBUT (5 seconds, normal >10 seconds) indicates the tear film is breaking up too quickly despite adequate aqueous volume. The lipid layer (from Meibomian glands) is what prevents evaporation and maintains film stability. When it is deficient, the aqueous layer evaporates rapidly and the film collapses before the next blink. This is evaporative dry eye — the most common form. Symptoms worsen in the afternoon because Meibomian gland secretions accumulate and solidify with reduced blink-driven expression over the day.

</details>

---

**2.** A 6-week-old infant is brought in with persistent unilateral epiphora and a mucopurulent discharge since birth. The most likely diagnosis is:

- A. Congenital glaucoma
- B. Dacryocystitis secondary to nasolacrimal duct obstruction
- C. Neonatal conjunctivitis from gonorrhea
- D. Lacrimal gland agenesis

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Dacryocystitis secondary to nasolacrimal duct obstruction.**

The nasolacrimal duct is the last segment of the lacrimal drainage system to fully open during development. In up to 20% of newborns, the valve of Hasner (at the nasal opening of the nasolacrimal duct) remains membranous at birth. Tears cannot drain, accumulate in the lacrimal sac, and become secondarily infected. This presents as persistent unilateral epiphora with mucopurulent discharge starting shortly after birth. Management is conservative (massage over lacrimal sac, topical antibiotics) — most resolve spontaneously by 12 months. Probing is performed if obstruction persists beyond that. Congenital glaucoma would present with photophobia, tearing, buphthalmos, and corneal edema — not mucopurulent discharge. Neonatal gonorrheal conjunctivitis presents in the first 2–5 days of life with severe bilateral purulent discharge, not epiphora.

</details>
