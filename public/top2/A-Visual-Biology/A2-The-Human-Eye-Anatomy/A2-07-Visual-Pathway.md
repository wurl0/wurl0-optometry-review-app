## A2-07 — The Visual Pathway
*Personal study notes | TOS: A.2 | Syllabi 5.21*

---

## The Simple Version

The visual pathway is the brain's cable system for sight — a 6-station relay from retina to occipital cortex. Light signals start at the retinal ganglion cells, travel along the optic nerves to the optic chiasma where the nasal fibers from each eye cross over to the opposite side. From there, the mixed signals travel in optic tracts to the lateral geniculate body, then through the optic radiation to the primary visual cortex. The key clinical principle: where the lesion is determines what kind of visual field defect you see. One eye affected = optic nerve lesion. Both eyes affected in matching halves = chiasmal or post-chiasmal lesion. The more posterior the lesion, the more symmetric (congruous) the defect becomes.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "If a pituitary tumor compresses the optic chiasma from below, which visual field quadrants are lost first, and why?" The answer maps the entire fiber decussation pattern in one clinical scenario.

---

## But Why?

**Why do the nasal retinal fibers cross at the chiasma while the temporal fibers do not?**
Because each visual cortex needs to receive information from the opposite visual field. The nasal retina sees the temporal visual field; crossing it sends temporal field information to the contralateral cortex. The temporal retina sees the nasal visual field; it does not cross because it already projects to the ipsilateral cortex, which processes the contralateral (nasal) visual field from the same side.

**Why does crossing allow each visual cortex to see the entire opposite visual field?**
Because after the chiasma, each optic tract contains temporal fibers from the ipsilateral eye (seeing the nasal field) AND nasal fibers from the contralateral eye (seeing the temporal field) — both representing the same half of visual space. The brain combines them for a complete contralateral hemifield.

**Why do post-chiasmal lesions produce homonymous defects?**
Because post-chiasmal, every structure carries mixed fibers from both eyes representing the same visual field half. A lesion anywhere from the optic tract onward knocks out the same portion of both eyes' visual fields — homonymous (same-sided).

**Why are defects more congruous the more posterior the lesion?**
Because the fibers from the two eyes are physically further apart in the tract (less organized, more separation) but become more tightly interleaved and organized in the optic radiation and cortex. A cortical lesion knocks out exactly corresponding points in both eyes — perfectly matched defects. A tract lesion knocks out fibers that are less precisely organized — slightly mismatched defects.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Literal Meaning / Word Roots | What It Actually Means in Context |
|---|---|---|
| Hemianopsia | Greek *hemi* — half + *an* — without + *opsis* — sight | Blindness in half the visual field |
| Homonymous | Greek *homos* — same + *onymos* — named | Same-side field defects in both eyes (e.g., right homonymous = right field lost in both eyes) |
| Heteronymous | Greek *heteros* — different + *onymos* | Different-side field defects (e.g., bitemporal = temporal field lost in both eyes) |
| Congruous | Latin *congruere* — to agree | Field defects that are identical/symmetrical in both eyes; posterior lesion |
| Incongruous | Latin — to disagree | Field defects that are dissimilar between the two eyes; anterior lesion |
| Quadrantanopsia | Latin *quadrans* — quarter | Loss of one quadrant of the visual field |
| Scotoma | Greek *skotos* — darkness | A focal area of reduced or absent vision within an intact visual field |
| Area striata | Latin — striped area | Brodmann area 17; primary visual cortex; named for the prominent striae of Gennari |
| Striae of Gennari | Named after Francesco Gennari | The thick white band of geniculo-calcarine fibers visible in area 17 even with the naked eye |
| Wernicke's hemianopic pupil | Named after Carl Wernicke | Pupil does not react when light hits the blind half of retina; from optic tract lesion |

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**The 6-station visual pathway and what each does:**

```
STATION 1: OPTIC NERVE (CN2)
  — Ganglion cell axons from retina
  — Fibers:
    a. Pupillomotor/afferent (pupillary light reflex)
    b. Visual fibers (sensation — to LGB)
    c. Efferent/retinomotor (brain → retina)
    d. Intercurrent/commissural (link one retina to other)
  — LESION: Total blindness of that eye (all visual input from that eye lost)

STATION 2: OPTIC CHIASMA
  — Partial decussation:
    NASAL fibers (temporal field) → CROSS to opposite side
    TEMPORAL fibers (nasal field) → STAY ipsilateral
  — Enveloped by pia mater only
  — Located above pituitary gland → pituitary tumor compresses from below
  — LESION of crossing fibers: BITEMPORAL hemianopsia
  — LESION of uncrossed fibers: BINASAL hemianopsia
  — Both = HETERONYMOUS hemianopsia (different sides in each eye)

STATION 3: OPTIC TRACT
  — Posterior continuation of chiasma to lateral geniculate body
  — Each tract: temporal fibers from SAME eye + nasal fibers from OPPOSITE eye
    = represents one visual hemifield from both eyes
  — LESION: INCONGRUOUS homonymous hemianopsia
  — WERNICKE's hemianopic pupil (light on blind half of retina → no pupil response)

STATION 4: LATERAL GENICULATE BODY (LGB)
  — Subcortical visual center / relay station
  — Temporal fibers → layers 2, 3, 5
  — Nasal fibers → layers 1, 4, 6
  — LESION: CONGRUOUS homonymous hemianopsia
  — (Transition from incongruous to congruous begins here)

STATION 5: OPTIC RADIATION (Geniculo-calcarine tract)
  — From LGB to area striata (occipital cortex)
  — Superior fibers: through parietal lobe → serve inferior visual field
  — Inferior fibers: loop through temporal lobe (Meyer's loop) → serve superior visual field
  — LESION:
    Temporal lobe (Meyer's loop): CONTRALATERAL SUPERIOR quadrantanopsia
    ("pie in the sky" defect)
    Parietal lobe: CONTRALATERAL INFERIOR quadrantanopsia

STATION 6: AREA STRIATA (Brodmann Area 17)
  — Medial aspect of occipital lobe
  — True visual cortex; true receptive center
  — White line = striae of Gennari
  — Macular representation: at posterior pole of occipital lobe
  — LESION: HOMONYMOUS SCOTOMA (macular sparing common with posterior cerebral artery occlusion)
```

### Structure or Process Map

**Visual field defects map — lesion site to defect type:**

```
LEFT EYE                                              RIGHT EYE
Temporal field | Nasal field      Nasal field | Temporal field

[Retina — nasal fibers cross, temporal stay]

OPTIC NERVE (pre-chiasmal)
  Lesion here:
  Left optic nerve → Left eye BLIND (right eye normal)

OPTIC CHIASMA
  Crossing fibers lesion:
  → Left temporal field LOST + Right temporal field LOST
  = BITEMPORAL HEMIANOPSIA (most common: pituitary adenoma)

  Uncrossed fibers lesion:
  → Both nasal fields lost = BINASAL HEMIANOPSIA (rare; carotid aneurysm)

OPTIC TRACT (post-chiasmal)
  Right optic tract lesion:
  → Left homonymous hemianopsia (INCONGRUOUS)
  → Wernicke's hemianopic pupil (no pupil response to light from blind hemifield)

LATERAL GENICULATE BODY
  Right LGB lesion:
  → Left homonymous hemianopsia (CONGRUOUS)

OPTIC RADIATION
  Right temporal (Meyer's loop) lesion:
  → Left SUPERIOR quadrantanopsia ("pie in the sky")

  Right parietal lesion:
  → Left INFERIOR quadrantanopsia

AREA STRIATA (occipital cortex)
  Right occipital lesion:
  → Left homonymous SCOTOMA
  → Macular sparing often present (dual blood supply of macular area)
```

### Comparisons That Matter

**Congruous vs Incongruous defects — the posterior rule:**

| Feature | Incongruous | Congruous |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Field defects dissimilar between the two eyes | Field defects identical/symmetric in both eyes |
| Location of lesion | Anterior to LGB (optic tract) | Posterior — LGB, optic radiation, cortex |
| Reason | Fibers from two eyes are less organized anteriorly | Fibers are tightly integrated posteriorly |
| Clinical importance | Points to pre-geniculate lesion | Points to post-geniculate lesion |
| Board clue | "Incongruous = before the crossing is complete" | "Congruous = fibers now fully mixed" |

**The four key hemianopsias:**

| Type | Description | Lesion | Cause |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bitemporal hemianopsia | Both temporal fields lost | Chiasmal crossing fibers | Pituitary adenoma (compresses from below) |
| Binasal hemianopsia | Both nasal fields lost | Chiasmal uncrossed fibers | Bilateral carotid aneurysm (rare) |
| Left homonymous hemianopsia | Left field lost in BOTH eyes | Right optic tract / radiation / cortex | Stroke, tumor |
| Right homonymous hemianopsia | Right field lost in BOTH eyes | Left optic tract / radiation / cortex | Stroke, tumor |

**Wernicke's hemianopic pupil vs normal pupil reflex:**

| Scenario | Pupil response |
|---|---|
| Light shone on seeing half of retina (optic tract lesion) | NORMAL — both pupils constrict |
| Light shone on blind half of retina (optic tract lesion) | NO response — afferent signal missing from that retinal area |
| Significance | Confirms optic tract lesion; rules out cortical lesion (cortical lesions do NOT cause Wernicke's) |

### Memory Anchors

**The 6 stations — "Only Chimps Obtain Light Ordering Accurately":**
Optic nerve → Chiasma → Optic tract → Lateral Geniculate Body → Optic radiation → Area striata

**The decussation rule — "Nasal crosses; temporal stays":**
Nasal fibers cross at chiasma (temporal visual field).
Temporal fibers stay ipsilateral (nasal visual field).

**Congruous = cortex; Incongruous = tract:**
More posterior = more congruous. Tract = incongruous. Cortex = congruous.

**Bitemporal = pituitary tumor:**
Tumor grows up from below → compresses crossing fibers first (those carrying temporal field signals from both eyes) → bitemporal hemianopsia.

**Meyer's loop clinical trick — "Temporal lobe = pie in the sky":**
Inferior optic radiation fibers loop through temporal lobe (Meyer's loop). Temporal lobe stroke → superior quadrantanopsia ("pie in the sky" — the upper field is lost).

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "Optic nerve lesion causes contralateral blindness" — Wrong. Optic nerve lesion causes IPSILATERAL (same-side) blindness. The chiasma is where crossing occurs.
- **Trap 2:** "All post-chiasmal lesions cause congruous defects" — Wrong. Optic tract lesions are post-chiasmal but produce INCONGRUOUS defects. Congruity increases progressively — only at the LGB and beyond does it become fully congruous.
- **Trap 3:** "Bitemporal hemianopsia means both eyes are blind" — Wrong. Temporal fields are lost but nasal fields are preserved. Patients retain central vision and nasal fields.
- **Trap 4:** "A temporal lobe lesion causes an inferior quadrantanopsia" — Wrong. Temporal lobe (Meyer's loop) lesion causes SUPERIOR quadrantanopsia ("pie in the sky"). Parietal lobe lesion causes inferior quadrantanopsia.
- **Trap 5:** "Cortical lesions produce Wernicke's hemianopic pupil" — Wrong. Wernicke's occurs only with OPTIC TRACT lesions. Cortical lesions do not affect the pupillary light reflex (the pupil reflex pathway branches off at the pretectal area, before reaching the cortex).
- **Trap 6:** "Temporal fibers cross at the chiasma" — Wrong. NASAL fibers cross. Temporal fibers stay ipsilateral.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 45-year-old woman presents with gradual loss of her peripheral vision bilaterally. She bumps into objects on both sides. Visual field testing shows loss of both temporal fields while nasal fields are intact. MRI would most likely reveal a lesion at which location?

- A. Right optic nerve
- B. Optic chiasma
- C. Right optic tract
- D. Left occipital cortex

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Optic chiasma.**

Bitemporal hemianopsia — loss of both temporal fields with preserved nasal fields — is the hallmark of a chiasmal lesion affecting the crossing (decussating) fibers. These nasal fibers carry temporal field information from both eyes; their interruption at the chiasma eliminates both temporal fields simultaneously. The most common cause in a middle-aged woman is a pituitary adenoma growing upward to compress the inferior chiasma. A right optic nerve lesion would cause right monocular blindness. A right optic tract lesion would cause left homonymous hemianopsia (not bitemporal). Occipital cortex lesion causes homonymous scotoma — not bitemporal loss.

</details>

---

**2.** A 60-year-old man suffers a right-sided stroke involving the temporal lobe. Which visual field defect is most likely on examination?

- A. Left inferior quadrantanopsia
- B. Left superior quadrantanopsia
- C. Left homonymous hemianopsia with macular sparing
- D. Right bitemporal hemianopsia

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Left superior quadrantanopsia.**

The inferior fibers of the right optic radiation loop through the temporal lobe (Meyer's loop) before reaching the occipital cortex. These fibers carry information from the superior visual field of the left hemifield. A right temporal lobe stroke interrupts Meyer's loop, producing a left superior quadrantanopsia — classically called "pie in the sky" (the upper-left visual field is lost). Left inferior quadrantanopsia would result from a right parietal lobe lesion (superior radiation fibers). Left homonymous hemianopsia with macular sparing is a right occipital cortex finding. Bitemporal hemianopsia requires a chiasmal lesion.

</details>
