## A2-03 — Cornea and Sclera
*Personal study notes | TOS: A.2 | Syllabi 5.9, 5.10*

---

## The Simple Version

The cornea and sclera are both made of collagen — the same raw material — but they look and behave completely differently. The sclera is white and opaque because its collagen fibers are bundled randomly in every direction, scattering light. The cornea is clear because its collagen fibers are arranged in perfectly parallel, evenly spaced layers that let light pass straight through. Transparency is not a different substance — it is a different geometry. The cornea is also the eye's first and most powerful lens, bending light more than any other structure in the optical system. It is nourished without blood vessels, kept alive by the tear film in front and aqueous humor behind, and kept transparent by an endothelial pump that continuously removes water from the stroma. The moment that pump fails, the cornea swells, clouds over, and vision collapses.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "The cornea and sclera are both collagen. Why is one clear and the other white?" Follow that to the fiber arrangement, then to why the endothelium is the most critical layer in the whole cornea.

---

## But Why?

**Why does the cornea have no blood vessels?**
Because blood vessels are opaque — they would block the light path needed for vision.

**Why doesn't the cornea die without blood vessels?**
Because the tear film supplies oxygen from the air, the limbal capillaries supply it from the periphery, and the aqueous humor supplies glucose and oxygen from behind.

**Why does the cornea swell when the endothelium fails?**
Because the stroma is hydrophilic — it constantly pulls water in from the aqueous. The endothelium's Na-K ATPase pump actively pushes that water back out, maintaining a precise hydration level. When the pump fails, water accumulates in the stroma.

**Why does water in the stroma cause loss of transparency?**
Because swelling disrupts the regular spacing of collagen fibrils. When the regular spacing is lost, fibrils scatter light instead of letting it pass through — the cornea becomes cloudy, exactly like a wet piece of regular paper.

**Why is this clinically catastrophic?**
Because corneal endothelial cells do not regenerate. Unlike epithelial cells which divide and replace themselves rapidly, endothelial cells are post-mitotic — once damaged, they are gone permanently. This is why Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy is so serious and why endothelial cell count before surgery matters.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**Why the cornea is transparent — and why the sclera is not:**

Both are made of Type I collagen. The difference is purely organizational:

```
CORNEA (transparent)
Collagen fibrils:
  — small diameter (25–35 nm)
  — equal spacing between fibrils
  — arranged in parallel lamellae
  — each lamella runs perpendicular to the next
Result: Incoming light waves cancel out
        (destructive interference of scattered waves)
        → light passes straight through

SCLERA (opaque)
Collagen fibers:
  — large diameter (varies widely)
  — irregular spacing
  — randomly interlacing in all directions
Result: Light is scattered in every direction
        → white, opaque appearance
```

This is the most important concept in this note. **The same material, organized differently, produces completely opposite optical behavior.** Any condition that disrupts the regular fiber spacing of the cornea — edema, scarring, dystrophy — causes opacity by the same mechanism that makes the sclera white.

**The endothelial deturgescence cycle:**

The corneal stroma is hydrophilic — it actively draws water from the aqueous humor. Left alone, the stroma would absorb water until it swells and becomes opaque. The endothelium prevents this:

```
Aqueous humor (behind cornea)
    ↓ (water passively enters stroma — it cannot be stopped)
CORNEAL STROMA
    ↑ (endothelium actively pumps Na+ and HCO3- out)
    ↑ (water follows osmotically)
Na-K ATPase pump in endothelium → electrolytes out → water follows
    → stroma stays at 75–80% water (optimal for transparency)
```

When the pump is inhibited (ouabain, cyanide, contact lens-induced anoxia) or the cells are lost (Fuchs' dystrophy), water accumulates:
- Stroma swells → fibers spread apart → opacity
- Epithelial bullae form (bullous keratopathy) — painful, vision severely reduced

**Corneal wound healing — 5 stages:**

| Stage | Cellular response | Biochemical response |
|---|---|---|
| I | Stromal edema, epithelial cell mitosis + sliding | Swelling |
| II | PMN and mononuclear stromal invasion | Increased hydrolase activity; degradation of MPS and collagen |
| III | New fibroblasts and mitosis | New pre-collagen formation; new MPS (dermatan sulfate) |
| IV | Collagen formation | Collagen synthesis |
| V | Healed stroma | Normal hydrolase activity, normal MPS and collagen concentration |

Why this matters: corneal scars form because new collagen laid down in healing is not as regular as original corneal collagen — hence the scar is less transparent. Bowman's layer never regenerates at all; its loss is permanent.

### Structure or Process Map

**The 6 corneal layers — anterior to posterior:**

```
1. CORNEAL EPITHELIUM (10% of corneal weight)
   — Stratified squamous non-keratinized, 5–6 cell layers
   — Cell types:
     ├── Surface cells (squamous + microvilli) — traps tear film; smooth optical surface
     ├── Wing cells (polyhedral, middle)
     └── Basal cells (columnar; rest on basement membrane 60–65 nm thick)
   — Joined by tight junctions (desmosomes); attached to Bowman's by hemidesmosomes
   — CAN regenerate (rapid mitosis of basal cells)
   — Water content: 70%
   — Contains: ATP, glycogen, glutathione, Vit C, ACh, AChE

2. BOWMAN'S MEMBRANE (12 µm)
   — Acellular, hyalinized, felt-like collagen
   — Anterior condensation of stroma — not a true "membrane," just denser stroma
   — CANNOT regenerate — scar = permanent opacity
   — Rupture is rare
   — Less resistant to trauma and microorganisms than endothelium

3. CORNEAL STROMA / SUBSTANTIA PROPRIA (90% of total thickness — thickest)
   — Avascular; rich in unmyelinated sensory nerves (nasociliary, CN5)
   — Organized in 200–250 collagen lamellae, each perpendicular to adjacent lamellar
   — Contains:
     ├── Keratocytes (corneal corpuscles) — produce and maintain collagen
     ├── Collagen fibrils (regular spacing = transparency)
     └── MPS/glycosaminoglycans (4–4.5% dry weight) — bind water, maintain hydration
   — Water content: 75–80%
   — If fibers lose regular spacing (edema, scar) → opacity

4. DUA'S LAYER (6–15 µm)
   — Recently identified layer between stroma and Descemet's
   — Despite being very thin, extremely strong: withstands up to 2 bars (200 kPa)
   — Impervious to air

5. DESCEMET'S MEMBRANE (10 µm)
   — True basement membrane of endothelium (produced and secreted by endothelium)
   — Very elastic and resistant to perforation
   — When stroma perforates, Descemet's can bulge forward → DESCEMETOCELE
   — CAN regenerate (secreted by endothelium)
   — Rich in glycine and hydroxyproline (collagen-like material)
   — Rupture is COMMON (unlike Bowman's where rupture is rare)

6. CORNEAL ENDOTHELIUM
   — Single layer of flat hexagonal (mesothelial) cells
   — Bathed by aqueous humor
   — Na-K ATPase pump → active transport of Na+ and HCO3- out → water follows
   — Maintains stromal dehydration (deturgescence)
   — CANNOT regenerate — endothelial cell loss is permanent
   — Normal cell count: ~2500 cells/mm² (decreases with age and surgery)
   — Damaged by: ouabain, cyanide, large gas-impermeable CL (Sattler's veil), Fuchs' dystrophy
   — Innervation: NOT innervated by nasociliary nerve (the only corneal layer with no innervation along with Descemet's)
```

> **The regeneration rule:** Epithelium = YES (rapid). Bowman's = NO (permanent scar). Stroma = PARTIAL (new collagen is less organized). Dua's layer = limited data. Descemet's = YES (endothelium secretes it). Endothelium = NO (post-mitotic cells).

**The sclera — structure and measurements:**

```
SCLERA
  — "White of the eyeball"
  — Dense, fibrous, collagenous; 5/6 of outer eyeball coat
  — Covered externally by Tenon's capsule + bulbar conjunctiva
  — Normal fluid content: 65–70%
     → Increase to 80% = loses opacity (becomes more transparent)
     → Decrease to 40% = also loses opacity

THICKNESS (varies by location — clinically important)
  — Thickest: at entrance of CN2 = 1.0 mm
  — Thinnest: at intersection of recti muscles in front of equator = 0.3 mm
  Why this matters: the thinnest point is where the EOMs attach. Excessive
  intraocular pressure or staphyloma preferentially occurs here because the
  sclera cannot resist expansion at its weakest point.

RADIUS OF CURVATURE: 12 mm (vs cornea 7.8 mm — sclera is flatter)

SCLERAL OPENINGS:
  1. Anterior scleral foramina — at limbus
     → Perforated by anterior ciliary arteries (ACAs)
  2. Posterior scleral foramina — at entrance of optic nerve
     → CN2, central retinal artery (CRA), long + short posterior ciliary arteries (PCAs),
        posterior ciliary nerves

HISTOLOGIC LAYERS (3):
  1. Episclera — outermost; vascular (from ACA); loose connective tissue
     Clinical: episcleritis = inflammation here (superficial, benign)
               scleritis = deeper inflammation involving scleral stroma (more serious)
  2. Scleral stroma / proper — middle; dense, IRREGULARLY interlacing collagen
     This irregular arrangement is the reason the sclera is opaque
  3. Lamina fusca / dark layer — innermost; contains melanocytes
     Fuses with the suprachoroidal space — the interface between sclera and choroid
```

---

**The limbus — the clinically critical transition zone:**

The limbus is only 1–2 mm wide, but it contains the entire aqueous humor drainage apparatus. Understanding its landmarks is essential for glaucoma.

```
LIMBUS — anterior to posterior (from cornea toward angle):

Schwalbe's line
  — marks where Descemet's membrane ends
  — most anterior extension of trabecular meshwork
  — visible on gonioscopy as a thin white glistening line
  — Sampaolesi's line = pigment deposited along Schwalbe's line (seen in pigment dispersion, PXF)

Trabecular meshwork (TM)
  — band of connective tissue fibers from Descemet's periphery to scleral spur
  — filters aqueous humor into Schlemm's canal
  — anterior zone = less pigmented, less filtration
  — posterior zone = more pigmented, more filtration (where most AH exits)

Schlemm's canal (Sinus Venosis Sclerae)
  — tube encircling the anterior chamber within the scleral sulcus
  — collects aqueous humor from TM and drains into episcleral veins
  — normally not visible — seen only when filled with blood (on gonioscopy)

Scleral spur
  — posterior boundary of TM / anterior boundary of ciliary body band
  — insertion point for longitudinal ciliary muscle fibers
  — appears as whitish band on gonioscopy

GONIOSCOPY LANDMARKS (anterior to posterior — mnemonic: "She Takes Some Cash Paying")
  S — Schwalbe's line
  T — Trabecular meshwork
  S — Scleral spur
  C — Ciliary body band
  P — Peripheral iris
```

### Comparisons That Matter

**Cornea vs Sclera — the same material with opposite properties:**

| Feature | Cornea | Sclera |
|---|---|---|
| Collagen fiber arrangement | Regular, parallel, uniform spacing | Irregular, randomly interlacing |
| Optical property | Transparent | Opaque (white) |
| Proportion of eyeball | 1/6 | 5/6 |
| Radius of curvature | 7.8 mm (steeper) | 12 mm (flatter) |
| Blood supply | Avascular (nourished by tear film + AH + limbal capillaries) | ACA + long + short PCA |
| Innervation | Nasociliary nerve (CN5) — except Descemet's and endothelium | Long + short posterior ciliary nerves |
| Functions | Refraction (43D); protective window | Protection; maintains globe shape; attachment for EOMs |
| Board clue | Fiber regularity = transparency | Fiber irregularity = opacity |

**Bowman's membrane vs Descemet's membrane — the two corneal "barriers":**

| Feature | Bowman's Membrane | Descemet's Membrane |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Between epithelium and stroma | Between stroma and endothelium |
| Thickness | 12 µm | 10 µm |
| Composition | Acellular hyalinized collagen (anterior stroma condensation) | True basement membrane; collagen-like (glycine, hydroxyproline) |
| Produced by | Stroma (not a true membrane) | Endothelium (secreted by it) |
| Can it regenerate? | NO — permanent scar if damaged | YES — endothelium regenerates it |
| Rupture frequency | RARE | COMMON |
| When it protrudes | Cannot (has no elasticity) | Descemetocele — balloon-like bulge through stroma defect |
| Board clue | Scar from keratitis = Bowman's damaged | Descemetocele = stroma gone, Descemet's intact |

**Epithelium vs Endothelium — clinical consequences of damage:**

| Feature | Epithelial damage | Endothelial damage |
|---|---|---|
| Regeneration | YES — basal cells divide rapidly | NO — post-mitotic, loss is permanent |
| Effect on cornea | Localized staining with fluorescein; usually heals | Corneal edema, bullous keratopathy, permanent clouding |
| Severity | Usually temporary | Potentially vision-threatening and permanent |
| Example condition | Corneal abrasion, contact lens-related SPK | Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy; surgical trauma |
| Board clue | "Heals in 24–72 hours" | "Cannot regenerate; cell count matters" |

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "The thickest corneal layer is the epithelium" — Wrong. Stroma is 90% of corneal thickness. Epithelium is 10% of corneal weight.
- **Trap 2:** "Bowman's membrane can regenerate if damaged" — Wrong. It cannot regenerate. Descemet's membrane can regenerate because endothelium secretes it.
- **Trap 3:** "The cornea is thicker at the center than the periphery" — Wrong. Central thickness (0.52–0.56 mm) is THINNER than peripheral (0.7–1.0 mm). The cornea thickens toward its edges.
- **Trap 4:** "The cornea is a perfect circle" — Wrong. Horizontal diameter (11–12mm) is wider than vertical diameter (10–11mm). The cornea is an oval, wider horizontally.
- **Trap 5:** "Endothelial cells regenerate after damage" — Wrong. Endothelium is post-mitotic. Each cell lost is gone permanently. Remaining cells spread to fill the gap, which reduces cell density over time.
- **Trap 6:** "The sclera is thickest at the EOM insertion zone" — Wrong. Thinnest (0.3mm). Thickest at the CN2 entrance (1.0mm).
- **Trap 7:** "Descemetocele means Descemet's membrane has ruptured" — Wrong. Descemetocele is the intact Descemet's bulging through a perforation in the overlying stroma. The membrane is intact but exposed.
- **Trap 8:** "Nasociliary nerve innervates all corneal layers" — Wrong. It innervates all layers EXCEPT Descemet's membrane and endothelium.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 70-year-old patient presents with progressive, painless bilateral vision loss. Slit lamp examination shows corneal edema with a beaten-metal appearance of the endothelium on specular reflection. Central pachymetry is 680 µm bilaterally. Which of the following best explains the pathophysiology?

- A. Failure of goblet cells to produce mucin for the tear film
- B. Loss of endothelial Na-K ATPase pump function leading to stromal edema
- C. Disruption of tight junctions in the corneal epithelium
- D. Degeneration of Bowman's membrane preventing epithelial adhesion

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Loss of endothelial Na-K ATPase pump function leading to stromal edema.**

This is Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy. The endothelium normally pumps Na+ and HCO3- out of the stroma — water follows osmotically, keeping the stroma at 75–80% water. In Fuchs', endothelial cells degenerate and are not replaced (endothelium is post-mitotic). As cell density falls below a critical threshold, the pump cannot maintain deturgescence. Water accumulates in the stroma, disrupting the regular collagen fiber spacing and causing light scatter (opacity). Pachymetry above 600 µm (normal 520–560 µm) confirms stromal swelling. The "beaten-metal" or "hammered silver" appearance on specular microscopy is the endothelial guttata — excrescences on Descemet's membrane that are pathognomonic of Fuchs'. Goblet cells, tight junctions, and Bowman's membrane are unrelated to this presentation.

</details>

---

**2.** During gonioscopy, the examiner identifies a thin, white, glistening line at the most anterior portion of the trabecular meshwork. What structure is this?

- A. Scleral spur
- B. Schwalbe's line
- C. Sampaolesi's line
- D. Schlemm's canal

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Schwalbe's line.**

Schwalbe's line marks the peripheral termination of Descemet's membrane and the most anterior extension of the trabecular meshwork. It appears as a thin, white, glistening line projecting slightly into the anterior chamber. The gonioscopic order from anterior to posterior is: Schwalbe's line → trabecular meshwork → scleral spur → ciliary body band → peripheral iris. Sampaolesi's line (option C) is pigment deposited along Schwalbe's line — seen in pigment dispersion syndrome and pseudoexfoliation — and would appear as a pigmented line, not a white one. The scleral spur appears as a whitish band posterior to the TM. Schlemm's canal is not normally visible unless blood-filled.

</details>
