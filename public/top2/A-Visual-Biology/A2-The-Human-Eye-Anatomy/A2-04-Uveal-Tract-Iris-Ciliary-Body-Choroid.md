## A2-04 — Uveal Tract: Iris, Ciliary Body, and Choroid
*Personal study notes | TOS: A.2 | Syllabi 5.12, 5.13, 5.15, 5.17*

---

## The Simple Version

The uveal tract is the eye's vascular middle coat — a continuous layer of blood vessels, pigment, and smooth muscle running from front to back. The iris at the front controls how much light enters by changing pupil size. The ciliary body in the middle produces the fluid that fills the eye (aqueous humor) and drives the accommodation mechanism through its muscle. The choroid at the back is a dense vascular blanket that nourishes the outer retina and absorbs stray light to keep vision sharp. All three share the same blood supply and are connected — inflammation in one frequently spreads to the others, which is why uveitis can affect the entire tract simultaneously.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "If you could only keep one layer of the uveal tract, which would it be and why?" The answer reveals whether they understand accommodation, aqueous production, or retinal nutrition — all three are clinically irreplaceable.

---

## But Why?

**Why does the ciliary body produce aqueous humor?**
Because the avascular cornea and lens need nutrients and oxygen but cannot receive blood directly.

**Why can't they receive blood directly?**
Because blood vessels are opaque — they would obstruct the light path and destroy visual acuity.

**Why does the ciliary muscle need to relax the zonules to achieve accommodation?**
Because the lens is naturally elastic and would round up on its own if unsupported. The zonules under tension from the ciliary ring actually flatten the lens. When the ciliary muscle contracts, it reduces the ring diameter, reducing zonule tension, and the lens elasticity rounds it up.

**Why does the choroid need to be so heavily pigmented?**
Because it sits directly behind the photoreceptors. Any stray light that passes through the retina without being absorbed by a photoreceptor would reflect back and blur the image. The melanin in the choroid absorbs it, functioning like the black paint inside a camera body.

**Why does uveitis spread easily within the uveal tract?**
Because the iris, ciliary body, and choroid are continuous — one unbroken vascular tissue. The same blood vessels and inflammatory mediators flow through all three, so inflammation triggered at one point propagates along the tract.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**Accommodation — how the ciliary body changes lens power:**

```
RELAXED STATE (distance vision)
Ciliary muscle RELAXED → ciliary ring diameter LARGE
→ Zonules of Zinn TAUT (under tension)
→ Lens pulled flat (less convex)
→ Less refractive power → focused for distance

NEAR VISION (accommodation)
Stimulus: blur on retina (near object)
→ Parasympathetic signal via CN3 short ciliary nerve
→ Ciliary muscle CONTRACTS (annular action)
→ Ciliary ring diameter DECREASES
→ Zonules RELAX (tension released)
→ Lens springs into natural round shape (more convex)
→ More refractive power → focused for near
```

Think of the zonules as a set of rubber bands stretching the lens flat. When the ciliary muscle contracts, it narrows the ring they're attached to, releasing the stretch. The lens is not being squeezed into shape by the muscle — it is being released from the flattening the zonules were imposing on it.

**Aqueous humor production at the ciliary process:**

The non-pigment epithelium of the pars plicata secretes aqueous through three mechanisms:
1. Ultrafiltration — from ciliary capillaries (pressure-driven)
2. Active secretion — Na-K ATPase pumps Na+ into the posterior chamber; water follows osmotically
3. Active transport — carbonic anhydrase drives HCO3- secretion

The aqueous flows from the posterior chamber → through the pupil → into the anterior chamber → drains through the trabecular meshwork → Schlemm's canal → episcleral veins.

**Iris pupillary reflexes:**

```
DIRECT LIGHT REFLEX:
Light → retina → optic nerve → pretectal nucleus (midbrain)
→ bilateral Edinger-Westphal nucleus → CN3 → ciliary ganglion
→ short ciliary nerve → sphincter pupillae → MIOSIS (both eyes)

CONSENSUAL: same pathway but the contralateral eye constricts
(pretectal nucleus projects bilaterally to both EW nuclei)

PSYCHIC REFLEX:
Fear → hypothalamus → sympathetic pathway → dilator pupillae → MYDRIASIS
```

### Structure or Process Map

**The Iris:**

```
IRIS (12mm diameter; hangs behind cornea, in front of lens)
  |
  ├── PUPILLARY ZONE (central; from pupil to collarette)
  |     — flat, due to atrophy of pupillary membrane
  |     — sphincter pupillae sits here (circular muscle; CN3; miosis)
  |
  └── CILIARY ZONE (peripheral; from collarette to iris root)
        — irregular (iris crypts): inner smooth, middle furrowed, marginal cribriform
        — dilator pupillae sits here (radial muscle; sympathetic; mydriasis)

HISTOLOGIC LAYERS (anterior to posterior):
1. Anterior border layer — flat hexagonal cells; no tight junctions (aqueous can enter stroma)
2. Anterior limiting membrane — condensation of anterior stroma
3. Iris stroma — loose CT; sphincter, dilator, blood vessels (GIC), pigment cells in GAGs
4. Posterior pigment epithelium (2 layers)
   a. Anterior pigment epithelium — spindle cells; ciliary zone
   b. Posterior pigment epithelium — polyhedral cells; pupillary + ciliary zone
```

**Blood supply of iris:** Greater iris circle (circulus iridis major) = 2 long posterior ciliary arteries + 7 anterior ciliary arteries — forms a ring at the ciliary body. Lesser iris circle (at collarette) = branches from GIC.

**The Ciliary Body (6mm wide):**

```
CILIARY BODY
  |
  ├── UVEAL PORTION (adjacent to sclera)
  |     — Lamina suprachoroidea
  |     — Ciliary muscles (3 types of smooth muscle)
  |     — Blood vessel layer
  |     — Lamina vitrea
  |
  └── EPITHELIAL PORTION (adjacent to posterior chamber)
        |
        ├── PARS PLANA (4mm wide; from ora serrata to pars plicata)
        |     — LEAST vascular → preferred surgical entry site
        |
        └── PARS PLICATA (2mm wide; ciliary processes)
              — 60–70 folds/processes (most vascular)
              — Produces aqueous humor

CILIARY MUSCLES (smooth, innervated by CN3):
  ├── Longitudinal (Brücke's) — outermost; from scleral spur; bulk of muscle
  ├── Radial — from anterior Brücke's; oblique fibers
  └── Circular — innermost; parallel to lens equator; site of zonule attachment
      [CONTRACTS TOGETHER → annular action → reduces ring diameter → relaxes zonules]
```

**The Choroid:**

```
CHOROID (from ora serrata to optic nerve entrance)
  — dark pigmented membrane; firmly adherent at optic nerve
  — separated from sclera by perichoroidal space of Schwalbe
    (contains: long PCA + 2 long ciliary nerves)

Thickness:
  — posterior pole: 0.25mm (THICKEST — most metabolically active)
  — anterior pole: 0.10mm (THINNEST)

HISTOLOGIC LAYERS (outer to inner):
1. Lamina fusca / Suprachoroidea
   — collagenous + elastic fibers
   — contains: smooth muscle fibers, melanocytes, endothelial cells,
               long + short PCA, long + short ciliary nerves
   — interface between sclera and choroid

2. Blood vessel layer (3 sub-layers):
   a. Haller's layer — large anastomosing veins (outer)
   b. Sattler's layer — medium anastomosing veins (middle)
   c. Choriocapillaris (Membrane of Ruysch) — wide-bore capillaries (inner)
      → supplies RPE and outer retinal photoreceptors
      → gives 65–85% of total retinal blood flow
      → ischemia >1 hour = irreparable photoreceptor damage

3. Bruch's membrane (Lamina Vitrea / Lamina Basalis Choroidea)
   — 7 µm thick; structureless
   — RPE firmly adherent to its inner surface
   — In AMD: Bruch's membrane thickens and drusen accumulate beneath RPE
```

### Comparisons That Matter

**Sphincter vs Dilator pupillae:**

| Feature | Sphincter Pupillae | Dilator Pupillae |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Pupillary zone (central iris stroma) | Ciliary zone (radial in iris stroma) |
| Muscle type | Circular (ring) | Radial (spoke-like) |
| Innervation | Parasympathetic CN3 (short ciliary nerve) | Sympathetic (long ciliary nerve) |
| Action when contracted | Miosis (constriction) | Mydriasis (dilation) |
| Drug that mimics | Pilocarpine (miotic) | Phenylephrine (mydriatic) |
| Drug that blocks | Atropine (cycloplegic/mydriatic) | — |
| Board clue | "Fight or flight dilates — rest constricts" | Same rule |

**Pars plana vs Pars plicata:**

| Feature | Pars Plana | Pars Plicata |
|---|---|---|
| Width | 4 mm | 2 mm |
| Location | Posterior (near ora serrata) | Anterior (near iris root) |
| Vascularity | Least vascular | Most vascular |
| Function | Structure; surgical access zone | Produces aqueous humor |
| Surgical use | Preferred entry for vitreoretinal surgery | Avoids |
| Board clue | "Flat and safe — surgical entry here" | "Vascular and productive" |

**Pupillary disorders — the 4 key ones:**

| Disorder | Mechanism | Lesion site | Key sign | Board clue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Adie's (tonic) pupil | Damaged parasympathetic post-ganglionic | Ciliary ganglion or short ciliary nerve | Slow/tonic reaction to light; responds to 0.125% pilocarpine; loss of deep tendon reflex | "Accommodates but reacts slowly; benign, peripheral" |
| Argyll-Robertson | Central pretectal lesion blocks light reflex but spares near reflex | Pretectal area (midbrain) | Accommodates but doesn't react to light; small, irregular, bilateral; neurosyphilis | "Prostitute pupils — accommodates but doesn't react; tabes dorsalis" |
| Horner's syndrome | Sympathetic pathway interruption | Anywhere along 3-neuron sympathetic chain | Miosis + partial ptosis (1–2mm) + anhidrosis | "Cocaine test: doesn't dilate in all Horner's" |
| Marcus-Gunn (RAPD) | Relative afferent defect | Optic nerve or severe retinal disease | Pupil dilates (escapes) when light swings from normal to affected eye | Swinging flashlight test — affected eye = paradoxical dilation |

### Numbers and Values to Know

| Parameter | Value | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Iris diameter | 12 mm | Reference for normal iris size |
| Normal pupil size | 3–4 mm | Miosis = <2mm; mydriasis = >5mm |
| Ciliary body width | 6 mm total | Pars plana (4mm) + pars plicata (2mm) |
| Ciliary processes | 60–70 folds | Each secretes aqueous — drugs that reduce these reduce IOP |
| Choroid posterior thickness | 0.25 mm (thickest) | Most metabolically active; first affected in choroidal ischemia |
| Choroid anterior thickness | 0.10 mm (thinnest) | Near ora serrata |
| Bruch's membrane thickness | 7 µm | Thickens with age and in AMD |
| Choriocapillaris blood flow | 65–85% of retinal blood flow | Loss = outer retinal ischemia within 1 hour |
| Adie's pupil — pilocarpine dose | 0.125% | Denervation supersensitivity — responds to very low dose |
| Normal C/D ratio | 0.3 | >0.5 suspicious for glaucoma |

### Memory Anchors

**Uveal tract layers front to back — "I Can Change":**
Iris → Ciliary body → Choroid

**Ciliary muscle fibers front to inner — "Long Ropes Circle":**
Longitudinal (Brücke's) → Radial → Circular

**Pupillary reflex rule — "PNS = Constrict; SNS = Dilate":**
- Parasympathetic (CN3) → sphincter → miosis
- Sympathetic → dilator → mydriasis

**Argyll-Robertson vs Adie's — "Both accommodate but don't react":**
- Adie's: peripheral (ciliary ganglion); unilateral; benign; responds to 0.125% pilocarpine
- Argyll-Robertson: central (pretectal); bilateral; neurosyphilis; does NOT respond to pilocarpine

**Choroid layers outer to inner — "Lamina Haller Sattler Chorio Bruch's":**
Lamina fusca → Haller's → Sattler's → Choriocapillaris → Bruch's

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "Ciliary muscle contraction tightens the zonules to round the lens" — Wrong. Ciliary contraction RELAXES the zonules. The lens rounds up on its own (elastic recoil) once tension is released.
- **Trap 2:** "The pars plicata is used for vitreoretinal surgery access" — Wrong. Pars plana is the surgical entry site (least vascular). Pars plicata is avoided (most vascular).
- **Trap 3:** "Argyll-Robertson pupil is benign peripheral neuropathy" — Wrong. Adie's is benign peripheral. Argyll-Robertson is central neurosyphilis (tabes dorsalis).
- **Trap 4:** "Choroid is thickest at the anterior pole" — Wrong. Thickest at the posterior pole (0.25mm). Thinnest anteriorly (0.10mm).
- **Trap 5:** "Pilocarpine 0.125% constricts all pupils" — Wrong. Normal pupils do not respond to this low dose. Only denervation-supersensitive pupils (Adie's) respond.
- **Trap 6:** "The sphincter pupillae is in the ciliary zone" — Wrong. Sphincter is in the pupillary zone (central). Dilator is in the ciliary zone (peripheral).

## Practice Questions

**1.** A patient is asked to look at a near target. Both pupils constrict. When a bright light is shone into the right eye, neither pupil constricts, but both constrict with near gaze. The left eye shows the same pattern. Which of the following best describes this condition?

- A. Bilateral Adie's pupils
- B. Argyll-Robertson pupils
- C. Bilateral Marcus-Gunn pupil
- D. Bilateral Horner's syndrome

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Argyll-Robertson pupils.**

Argyll-Robertson pupils are pathognomonic for neurosyphilis (tabes dorsalis). The hallmark is light-near dissociation: "accommodates but does not react to light." The pretectal lesion disrupts the afferent arc of the pupillary light reflex while sparing the accommodation-convergence near reflex pathway (which travels more ventrally in the brainstem). This produces bilateral small, irregular pupils that constrict with near gaze but not with light. Bilateral Adie's is possible but would show tonic (slow) near constriction and loss of deep tendon reflexes, not the complete absence of light response. Marcus-Gunn involves a relative afferent defect — it does not cause bilateral failure of the light reflex in both eyes.

</details>

---

**2.** During a vitreoretinal surgical procedure, the surgeon enters the eye through which of the following structures to minimize bleeding risk?

- A. Pars plicata
- B. Limbus
- C. Pars plana
- D. Iris root

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Pars plana.**

The pars plana is the posterior, flat, avascular portion of the ciliary body (4mm wide, extending from the ora serrata to the pars plicata). Its relative avascularity makes it the preferred and safest entry point for vitreoretinal surgery, reducing hemorrhage risk. The pars plicata (anterior 2mm, most vascular) contains the ciliary processes and is avoided. The limbus is used for anterior segment surgeries (e.g., cataract). The iris root is fragile and its incision would damage the angle and drainage structures.

</details>

## Extended Topics: Anterior Chamber, Angle, and Posterior Chamber

*(Syllabi 5.11 and 5.14 — added to complete the uveal tract note)*

**Anterior Chamber (5.11):**

```
ANTERIOR CHAMBER
  Location: Between cornea (front) and iris/pupil (back)
  Shape: Biconvex space
  Volume:
    Total: 0.20–0.25 mL
    Central portion (deepest): 3 mm deep
    Peripheral portion: shallowest
  Contents: Aqueous humor
  Index of refraction: 1.336

BOUNDARIES:
  Anterior: Corneal endothelium + Descemet's membrane
  Posterior: Anterior face of iris + lens (through pupil)
  Periphery: The angle (trabecular meshwork)

THE ANGLE (iridocorneal angle):
  — The drainage site of aqueous humor
  — Contains: trabecular meshwork → Schlemm's canal → episcleral veins
  — Gonioscopy landmarks (anterior to posterior):
    Schwalbe's line → Trabecular meshwork → Scleral spur → Ciliary body band → Peripheral iris
```

Why the angle matters: Aqueous flows from posterior chamber → through the pupil → into anterior chamber → drains at the angle. Open-angle glaucoma occurs when TM resistance increases without angle closure. Angle-closure glaucoma occurs when iris physically blocks the angle, preventing drainage entirely. The difference in these two mechanisms drives their completely different management.

**Posterior Chamber (5.14):**

```
POSTERIOR CHAMBER
  Location: Between posterior iris surface and anterior lens + zonules + anterior vitreous face
  Volume: 0.06 mL (much smaller than anterior chamber)
  Boundaries:
    Anterior: Posterior iris surface
    Posterior: Anterior lens capsule + zonular fibers
    Lateral: Ciliary body processes (pars plicata)
  Contents: Aqueous humor (produced here by ciliary processes)
  
  Flow path: Produced in posterior chamber → through pupil → anterior chamber → angle → drainage
```

Clinical significance: Posterior synechiae (iris-to-lens adhesions from uveitis) can seal the pupil and block aqueous flow from posterior to anterior chamber → iris bombé (iris bows forward) → secondary angle-closure glaucoma. This is the mechanism that makes anterior uveitis management urgent.

**Numbers added:**

| Structure | Measurement | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| AC depth (central) | 3 mm | Shallow AC = risk for angle closure |
| AC volume | 0.20–0.25 mL | Reference |
| PC volume | 0.06 mL | Site of AH production |
| AH index of refraction | 1.336 | Same as vitreous |
