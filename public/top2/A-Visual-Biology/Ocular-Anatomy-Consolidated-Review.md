## 1. Embryology of the Eye

The eye develops from three tissue types. Think of it as a building from three contractors, each responsible for different floors.

### Ectoderm (Neural Ectoderm)
Neural ectoderm becomes the internal neural structures:
- Retina (sensory and pigment epithelium)
- Optic nerve
- Dilator and sphincter pupillae muscles
- Vitreous body (contributed)

### Ectoderm (Surface Ectoderm)
Surface ectoderm becomes the external and accessory glands:
- Lens
- Lacrimal gland and accessory lacrimal glands
- Meibomian/tarsal glands
- Glands of Zeis and Moll
- Epithelium of cornea and conjunctiva

### Neural Crest Cells
Neural crest gives rise to connective tissue and drainage structures:
- Trabecular meshwork
- Bony orbit (partly)
- Optic nerve meninges
- Stroma of iris and cornea
- Ciliary body (stromal component)

### Mesoderm
Mesoderm forms the muscular and vascular middle layers:
- Tenon's capsule
- Extraocular muscles
- Ciliary muscle
- Vitreous (partly)
- Choroidal and scleral vessels

### Mixed Origin
- **Zonular fibers** arise from both neural crest and neuroepithelium, making them a mixed-origin structure.

## 2. Bony Orbit

### Shape and Dimensions
The orbit is shaped like a quadrilateral pyramid. The base faces anteriorly (orbital margin); the apex points posteriorly toward the optic foramen. Think of an ice cream cone with the opening facing you.

| Measurement | Value |
|---|---|
| Height | 35 mm |
| Width | 40 mm |
| Depth | 40-45 mm |
| Volume | 29-30 mL |
| Weight of eyeball | 10 grams |
| Volume of eyeball | 6.5 mL |
| Orbit-to-eye volume ratio | ~5.5:1 |

The widest part of the orbit is 1.5 mm behind the orbital margin, not at the rim itself.

### Seven Bones of the Orbit (Mnemonic: "My Zipper Lies Flat, Since Every Pair Slips")
- **Paired bones (4):** Maxillary, Zygomatic, Lacrimal, Palatine
- **Unpaired bones (3):** Frontal, Ethmoid, Sphenoid

### Orbital Walls

| Wall | Bones | Key Features | Clinical Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Roof (Vault)** | Frontal bone + lesser wing of sphenoid | Fovea for trochlea of SO; lacrimal fossa | Thin and fragile; penetrating trauma can reach frontal lobe |
| **Medial Wall** | Frontal process of maxilla + lacrimal bone + orbital plate of ethmoid (lamina papyracea) + body of sphenoid | Lacrimal fossa for lacrimal sac | **Thinnest wall** (lamina papyracea = paper-thin plate of ethmoid); fractures here communicate with ethmoid sinus |
| **Floor** | Maxilla + palatine + zygomatic | Maxillary antrum (sinus) below | **Blowout fracture** site; inferior rectus entrapment; enophthalmos on exam |
| **Lateral Wall** | Greater wing of sphenoid + zygomatic | Lateral orbital tubercle of Whitnall | **Thickest wall**; most protected |

### Orbital Apertures (Foramina)

| Aperture | Location | Contents |
|---|---|---|
| Optic foramen | Apex, lesser wing of sphenoid | CN II, ophthalmic artery, sympathetic nerve fibers |
| Superior orbital fissure (SOF) | Between greater and lesser sphenoid wings | CN III, IV, V1 (nasociliary, frontal, lacrimal branches), CN VI, superior ophthalmic vein |
| Inferior orbital fissure (IOF) | Between greater sphenoid wing and maxilla | CN V2 (maxillary), infraorbital artery, inferior ophthalmic vein |
| Anterior ethmoidal foramen | Medial wall | Anterior ethmoidal artery and nerve |
| Posterior ethmoidal foramen | Medial wall | Posterior ethmoidal artery and nerve |
| Nasolacrimal canal | Floor (medial) | Nasolacrimal duct |

**Mnemonic for SOF contents:** "I Ate So Many Freshwater Clams" = III, IV, V1 (nasociliary), V1 (lacrimal), V1 (frontal), CN VI, superior ophthalmic vein

**CN IV** (trochlear) does NOT pass through the annulus of Zinn; it is the only cranial nerve motor branch to emerge from the dorsal brainstem, and it's the most slender (2,150-3,400 fibers).

### Orbital Index
```
Orbital Index = (Height of orbit / Width of orbit) x 100
```
- **Megaseme** (index ≥ 89): Round opening; Mongoloid races
- **Mesoseme** (index 83-89): European, Caucasian
- **Microseme** (index ≤ 83): Rectangular opening; Black races

## 3. Orbital Fascia and Paranasal Sinuses

### Orbital Fascia Components

**Periorbita (Periosteum of orbit)**
The lining of the bony orbit that is loosely adherent to the bone. It condenses at the orbital margins to become the orbital septum.

**Orbital Septum**
A fibrous sheet from the periorbita that fuses with the tarsal plates of the eyelids. It is the anatomical landmark that separates preseptal (superficial) from postseptal (orbital) space -- surgically critical in distinguishing periorbital cellulitis from orbital cellulitis.

**Tenon's Capsule (Fascia Bulbi)**
A membranous envelope that surrounds the posterior 4/5 of the eyeball from the limbus to the optic nerve. It acts like a "socket liner" to allow free rotation of the globe.
- Derived from mesoderm
- The ligament of Lockwood is the thickening of Tenon's capsule below the globe; it suspends the eyeball like a hammock.

### Surgical Procedures
- **Evisceration:** Removal of ocular contents but the scleral shell and Tenon's capsule are left intact.
- **Enucleation:** Complete removal of the eyeball with preservation of other orbital structures.
- **Exenteration:** Removal of the eyeball plus all orbital contents (reserved for malignant tumors).

### Paranasal Sinuses Adjacent to the Orbit
Four sinuses border the orbit and are important for spread of infection:

| Sinus | Position | Special Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Maxillary | Below the floor | Largest paranasal sinus; "antrum of Highmore" |
| Frontal | Above the roof | Can extend into orbital roof |
| Ethmoidal | Medial wall | Honeycomb-like; infection spreads easily through lamina papyracea |
| Sphenoidal | Apex region | Lies near the optic canal |

## 4. Eyelids

The eyelids are the two movable curtains in front of the eye, 2 mm thick and 30 mm long.

### Divisions of the Eyelid
The eyelid is divided by the gray line (intramarginal sulcus) into:
- **Anterior portion:** Contains cilia (lashes), glands of Zeis, and Moll glands
- **Posterior portion:** Contains meibomian (tarsal) glands and their ducts

The lid is also divided vertically into:
- **Lacrimal portion:** Medial 1/6; no cilia or gland openings; round lid margin
- **Ciliary portion:** Lateral 5/6; has cilia and gland openings; square lid margin

### Palpebral Fissure
The elliptical opening between upper and lower lids.
- Height: 8-11 mm (or 12-15 mm in some references)
- Width: 27-30 mm
- **Lateral canthus** forms a 60-degree angle; sits higher in Asians (5 mm) and Europeans (2 mm) compared to the medial canthus.

### Lines of Defense of the Eyelids

| Defense Line | Structure |
|---|---|
| 1st | Supercilia (eyebrows) and cilia (lashes) |
| 2nd | Oily layer from glands |
| 3rd | Motor system (muscles of the lids) |

**Cilia facts:** Average lifespan is 3-5 months.
- Poliosis = white cilia (depigmentation)
- Madarosis = absence of cilia
- Trichiasis = misdirected cilia (toward the eye)
- Distichiasis = extra row of lashes
- Entropion = eyelid turned inward
- Ectropion = eyelid turned outward

### Histologic Layers of the Eyelid (Anterior to Posterior)
1. **Skin and subcutaneous tissue:** Thinnest skin in the body; made of loose areolar connective tissue devoid of fat.
2. **Muscular layer:** Orbicularis oculi, LPS, and Muller's muscle
3. **Tarsus:** Dense fibrous skeleton of the lids containing meibomian glands
4. **Conjunctiva:** Lines the posterior surface (palpebral conjunctiva)

### Eyelid Muscles

**Orbicularis Oculi** (sphincter of the eyelids)
Thin, oval striated muscle that surrounds and occupies the palpebral fissure.

| Part | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Pars orbitalis | Peripheral; surrounds orbital margins | Forcible closure; pulls forehead/temple/cheek skin |
| Pars palpebralis (pretarsal + preseptal) | Central | Gentle blink and wink |
| Pars lacrimalis | Attached to lacrimal sac | Aids lacrimal pump mechanism |

Types of eyelid closure:
- **Blink:** Involuntary, involves pars palpebralis; normal rate = 15x/min
- **Wink:** Voluntary, involves pars orbitalis and palpebralis
- **Squeezing/Blepharospasm:** Voluntary, involves all parts including brow muscles

**Levator Palpebrae Superioris (LPS)**
- Origin: Periosteal covering of lesser wing of sphenoid (above the annulus of Zinn)
- Insertion: Proximal margins of tarsus and skin of upper eyelid (forms the superior orbital furrow)
- Action: Elevates the upper eyelid
- Innervation: CN III (superior branch)
- Fear and excitement cause LPS contraction (wide-eyed look)
- Paralysis causes **complete ptosis**

**Muller's Muscle (Involuntary Palpebral Muscles)**
- Superior Muller's: Origin from undersurface of LPS; inserts to superior tarsal plate
- Inferior Muller's: Origin from fascial expansion of inferior rectus; inserts to inferior tarsal plate
- Function: Maintains upper lid elevation (increases LPS tonus)
- Innervation: **Sympathetic nerve**
- Paralysis causes **slight ptosis** (1-2 mm droop, as seen in Horner's syndrome)

**Bell's Phenomenon:** When eyelids close, the eyeball rotates upward and outward due to the associated action of superior rectus and orbicularis oculi. This protects the cornea.

**Dalrymple's Sign:** Widening of the palpebral fissure due to excessive tonus of Muller's muscle or LPS (seen in thyroid eye disease).

### Tarsus
Dense fibrous connective tissue that forms the structural skeleton of the eyelids.
- Thickness: 1 mm
- Length: 25-30 mm
- **Upper tarsus:** 11 mm vertical height
- **Lower tarsus:** 5 mm vertical height
- Contains meibomian glands whose ducts open at the lid margin
- Attached to medial and lateral walls by palpebral ligaments

### Medial Canthus Structures
- **Lacrimal Lake (Lacus Lacrimalis):** Triangular space at the medial canthus
- **Lacrimal Caruncle:** Small pink body in the lacrimal lake; formed from conjunctiva
- **Semilunar Fold (Plica Semilunaris):** Vertical half-moon fold of conjunctiva; rudiment of the nictating membrane (3rd eyelid of lower animals)
- **Lacrimal Papilla:** Small elevation on each lid margin
- **Lacrimal Puncta:** 0.3 mm openings at the apex of each papilla; entry points for the lacrimal drainage system

### Functions of Eyelids
1. Protect the eye from injury, foreign bodies, excessive light
2. Distribute tears and secretions to lubricate the eyeball
3. Keep the cornea moist and transparent
4. Remove dust by blinking
5. Regulate the amount of light entering the eye

### Innervation Summary
| Structure | Nerve |
|---|---|
| LPS | CN III |
| Orbicularis oculi | CN VII |
| Muller's muscle | Sympathetic |
| Sensory (upper lid) | CN V1 (ophthalmic: frontal and lacrimal branches) |
| Sensory (lower lid) | CN V2 (maxillary: infraorbital nerve) |

## 5. Conjunctiva

The conjunctiva is a thin, transparent mucous membrane that lines the inner surface of the eyelids and covers the anterior sclera up to the limbus.

### Regions of the Conjunctiva

| Region | Location | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| Palpebral/Tarsal conjunctiva | Lines the eyelids; firmly adherent to tarsus | Never swells significantly (firmly attached) |
| Bulbar conjunctiva | Covers anterior sclera | Loosely attached by episcleral connective tissue; firmly attached only at limbus |
| Fornix conjunctiva | Transition zone; plica semilunaris to caruncle | Very loose; swells first in chemosis |

**Fornix depths from the limbus:**
- Superior fornix: 10 mm (more accessory lacrimal glands of Krause)
- Inferior fornix: 8 mm (more lymphoid follicles and inflammatory cells)
- Lateral fornix: 14 mm (deepest)
- No medial fornix (interrupted by the lacrimal caruncle)

**Cul-de-sac:** 2 mm deep space between bulbar and palpebral conjunctiva; holds 20-30 µL of tears.

**Chemosis:** Swelling of the conjunctiva. The fornix swells first (loosest), then bulbar conjunctiva swells slightly, and palpebral conjunctiva almost never swells (firmly adherent to tarsus).

### Blood Supply
- Posterior conjunctival artery
- Anterior ciliary artery (at the limbus region)

### Histologic Layers
1. **Stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium:** Contains goblet cells that secrete mucin (the wetting agent of the precorneal tear film). Goblet cells are most numerous at the fornix, fewer in bulbar, and absent at lid margins and limbus.
2. **Lamina propria (Substantia propria/Stroma):** Contains blood vessels, nerves, accessory lacrimal glands of Krause and Wolfring, mast cells, macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells, PMNs, and lymphoid follicles.

## 6. Topography of the Eyeball

### Basic Measurements
- Weight: 10 grams
- Volume: 6.5 mL
- Suspended in orbit by the ligament of Lockwood
- Optic nerve enters 3 mm nasally and 1 mm below the posterior pole

### Key Dimensions

| Diameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Sagittal (anteroposterior) | 22-27 mm; average 24 mm |
| Vertical | 23.5 mm |
| Transverse (horizontal) | 24.5 mm |
| Circumference | 69-85 mm |

### Axes and Reference Points
- **Anterior Pole:** Center of curvature of the cornea
- **Posterior Pole:** Center of curvature of the eyeball; located slightly temporal to the optic nerve; 32 mm behind the limbus
- **Equator:** Line equidistant from both poles; 16 mm posterior to the limbus on the scleral surface; divides the globe into anterior and posterior hemispheres
- **Meridian:** Any line from limbus to posterior pole (32 mm); passes through both poles and crosses the equator at right angles
- **Geometric Axis:** Line connecting anterior and posterior poles; runs from the fovea through the center of rotation to a point slightly nasal on the cornea
- **Optical Axis:** Straight line joining the centers of curvature of all refracting surfaces
- **Visual Axis:** Line connecting the fovea to the point of fixation, passing through the nodal point; usually 5 degrees temporal to the optical axis

### Coats (Layers) of the Eyeball

| Layer | Structure | Position |
|---|---|---|
| Fibrous coat (outermost) | Sclera (5/6 posterior) + Cornea (1/6 anterior) | Outer |
| Uveal coat (middle, "most dangerous") | Iris (anterior) + Ciliary body + Choroid (posterior) | Middle |
| Nervous coat (innermost, most important) | Retina | Inner |

## 7. Sclera

The sclera is the "white of the eye" -- a dense, fibrous, opaque collagenous coat occupying the posterior 5/6 of the eyeball, from the optic nerve entrance to the limbus.

### Key Facts
- Covered externally by Tenon's capsule and bulbar conjunctiva
- Normal fluid content: 65-70%; if this increases to 80% or drops to 40%, the sclera loses its opacity
- Not vascular (except the episclera)
- Radius of curvature: 12 mm

### Thickness

| Location | Thickness |
|---|---|
| At entrance of optic nerve (CN II) | 1.0 mm (thickest) |
| At insertion of rectus muscles in front of equator | 0.3 mm (thinnest) |

The thinness at the muscle insertion point is why it is the most vulnerable site for scleral rupture from blunt trauma.

### Scleral Openings
- **Anterior scleral foramina:** At the limbus; perforated by anterior ciliary artery (ACA)
- **Posterior scleral foramina:** At the optic nerve; carries CN II, central retinal artery (CRA), long and short posterior ciliary arteries (PCA), and posterior ciliary nerves

### Histologic Layers (Outer to Inner)
1. **Episclera:** Outermost; vascular; supplied by ACA; "the red you see when the eye is inflamed"
2. **Scleral proper (stroma):** Dense, irregularly arranged, interlacing layers of collagenous fibers; this irregular arrangement is the reason for its opacity (contrast with the cornea)
3. **Lamina fusca (dark layer):** Innermost; contains melanocytes (pigment cells)

### Functions
1. Contains and protects the eyeball contents
2. Maintains globular shape
3. Attachment for extraocular muscles

## 8. Cornea

The cornea is the main refractive structure of the eye -- the first and most powerful lens in the optical system. It occupies the anterior 1/6 of the eyeball.

### Key Measurements

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Radius of curvature | 7.0-8.5 mm (average 7.8 mm) |
| Vertical diameter | 10-11 mm |
| Horizontal diameter | 11-12 mm |
| Central thickness | 0.52-0.56 mm (measured by pachymeter) |
| Peripheral thickness | 0.7-1.0 mm |
| Index of refraction | 1.376 |
| Total refractive power | 43 diopters |

### Blood and Nerve Supply
- **Avascular** (except at the limbus, which is supplied by conjunctival artery from ACA)
- Nourished by aqueous humor, limbal capillaries, and precorneal tear film
- **Innervation:** Nasociliary nerve (branch of CN V1) -- innervates all layers except Descemet's membrane and endothelium
- The most densely innervated avascular tissue in the body

### Why Is the Cornea Transparent? (8 Reasons)
1. Avascular
2. No pigment; very few nuclei in corneal stroma
3. Regular, parallel arrangement of collagen fibers
4. Non-keratinized corneal epithelium
5. Collagen fibers are equally spaced
6. Collagen fibers are small relative to the wavelength of light (no scatter)
7. Anterior tear film forms a regular refracting surface
8. Tight junctions of epithelial cells are impermeable to water, and the Na-K ATPase pump in the endothelium actively maintains corneal deturgescence

### Corneal Neovascularization
Vessels grow in from the limbus when corneal oxygen demand is unmet. Causes include:
- Local anoxia (CO2 increases vasodilation)
- Acid metabolites
- Histamine
- Vitamin C deficiency
- Corneal swelling (from large, gas-impermeable contact lenses -- called Sattler's veil)

### Histologic Layers of the Cornea (Anterior to Posterior)

**Layer 1: Corneal Epithelium**
- Stratified squamous non-keratinized; 5-6 cell layers; joined by tight junctions (desmosomes); attached to Bowman's membrane by hemidesmosomes
- Comprises 10% of total corneal weight
- Has the capacity to **regenerate** (many epithelial cells)
- Three sub-types of cells:
  - **Surface (squamous) cells:** Microvilli that trap fluid and prevent drying; form a smooth convex mirror seen at the slit lamp
  - **Polyhedral (wing) cells:** Middle layer
  - **Columnar/basal cells:** Innermost, resting on a 60-65 nm basement membrane

**Layer 2: Bowman's Membrane**
- 12 µm thick; acellular; homogenous; hyalinized; contains randomly arranged collagen fibers (felt-like structure)
- Less resistant to trauma and microorganism penetration
- **No capacity to regenerate** -- damage leaves a permanent scar
- It is an anterior condensation of the corneal stroma, not a true membrane

**Layer 3: Corneal Stroma (Substantia Propria)**
- **Thickest layer:** 90% of total corneal thickness
- Avascular but richly supplied with unmyelinated sensory fibers from the nasociliary nerve (CN V)
- Contents:
  - Water: 75-80%; Solids: 20-25%
  - **Keratocytes** (corneal corpuscles): Fixed, flat cells that produce collagen
  - **Collagen fibrils:** Forms the skeleton; high in glycine, proline, hydroxyproline; converted to gelatin when boiled; dissolved by collagenase, pepsin, and papain
  - **MPS (mucopolysaccharides):** 4-4.5% dry weight; bind cations and water; maintain hydration and transparency
    - Keratan sulfate: 50-60%
    - Chondroitin sulfate: 20%
    - Chondroitin: 25%

**Layer 3.5: Dua's Layer (Pre-Descemet's Layer)**
- Described recently; located between corneal stroma and Descemet's membrane
- Thickness: 6-15 µm; very strong and impervious to air
- Can withstand up to 2 bars (200 kPa) of pressure

**Layer 4: Descemet's Membrane**
- Elastic, resistant; can bulge outward (like a balloon) without rupturing -- this bulging is called **descemetocele**
- Made of collagen-like material rich in glycine and hydroxyproline
- Secreted by the endothelium (serves as its basement membrane)
- Thickness: 10 µm
- **Can regenerate** (unlike Bowman's membrane)
- Rupture of Descemet's is more common than rupture of Bowman's

**Layer 5: Corneal Endothelium**
- Single layer of flat, hexagonal (cuboidal) mesothelial cells
- Lines the inner surface of Descemet's; bathed by aqueous humor
- Responsible for **deturgescence** via the Na-K ATPase pump (extrudes excess ions, which draws water out)
- This is why damage to the endothelium (e.g., Fuchs' endothelial dystrophy) causes serious, permanent corneal swelling and loss of transparency
- Damage to the epithelium causes localized, temporary swelling; endothelial damage is far more severe and permanent

### Corneal Barriers
- **Lipid barrier:** Phospholipids of cell membranes
- **Structural barrier:** Tight junctions (zonulae adherentes, occludentes) of corneal epithelium

### Clinical Manifestations of Corneal Inflammation
1. Corneal edema
2. Corneal neovascularization
3. Superficial punctate keratopathy (SPK)
4. Infiltration and ulceration

## 9. Uveal Tract

The uveal tract is the middle vascular coat. It has three parts: choroid (posterior), ciliary body (middle), and iris (anterior). It is the "most dangerous coat" because it is highly vascular.

### 9a. Choroid

A thin, dark, pigmented membrane extending from the ora serrata to the entrance of the optic nerve (where it is firmly adherent). The perichoroidal space of Schwalbe separates it from the sclera and houses the long posterior ciliary arteries and long ciliary nerves.

**Thickness:**
- At posterior pole: 0.25 mm (thickest)
- At anterior pole: 0.10 mm (thinnest)

**Functions:**
1. Provides a dark chamber (absorbs stray light)
2. Prevents dispersion of light to maintain a clear image
3. Nourishes the RPE and photoreceptors

**Blood supply:** ACA and long and short PCAs
**Innervation:** Short ciliary nerves

**Histologic Layers (Outer to Inner):**

1. **Lamina Fusca / Suprachoroidea:** Contains collagenous and elastic fibers, smooth muscle fibers, melanocytes, endothelial cells, long and short PCAs, and long and short ciliary nerves.
2. **Blood Vessel Layers:**
   - **Haller's Layer:** Large anastomosing veins
   - **Sattler's Layer:** Medium-sized anastomosing veins
   - **Choriocapillaris (Membrane of Ruysch):** Wide-bore capillaries; supplies RPE and photoreceptors
3. **Lamina Vitrea / Bruch's Membrane (Lamina Basalis Choroidea):** Thin, structureless membrane (7 µm; 1.5 µm in some sources); the RPE is firmly adherent to this layer.

**Site of retinal detachment:** Between RPE and the photoreceptor layer (NOT between the choroid and RPE). This is a plane of potential separation.

### 9b. Ciliary Body

Often called the "most dangerous part of the eye" because it is the most vascular structure. It extends from the ora serrata to the limbus (scleral spur), spanning 6 mm.

**Parts:**
- **Uveal portion:** Adjacent to the sclera; contains lamina suprachoroidea, ciliary muscles, blood vessel layers, lamina vitrea
- **Epithelial portion:** Adjacent to the posterior aqueous chamber

Within the epithelial portion, two sub-regions exist:
- **Pars Plana (Orbiculus Ciliaris):** 4 mm wide; extends from ora serrata to pars plicata; **least vascular structure** -- preferred site for surgical entry (vitrectomy, intravitreal injections)
- **Pars Plicata (Corona Ciliaris):** 2 mm wide; contains the ciliary processes; **most vascular structure**

**Ciliary Processes (60-70 per eye):**
Finger-like folds that produce aqueous humor and nourish the cornea and lens. Each process has two epithelial layers:
- **Non-pigmented epithelium:** Outer layer; anterior continuation of the sensory retina; secretes aqueous humor
- **Pigmented epithelium:** Inner layer; anterior continuation of the RPE

**Ciliary Muscles:**
Muscles of accommodation; innervated by CN III. When they contract, they draw the ciliary processes and choroid forward, relaxing the zonules of Zinn and allowing the lens to become more biconvex (rounder). This is the basis of accommodation.

Three fiber types:
| Type | Location | Origin | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| Longitudinal (Brücke's muscle) | Outermost | Scleral spur | Main bulk; accommodation |
| Radial | Middle | Anterior Brücke's fibers | Connects longitudinal and circular |
| Circular | Innermost | Parallel to lens equator | Site of zonular fiber attachment; accommodation |

### 9c. Iris

A thin, circular membrane hanging behind the cornea and in front of the lens, perforated at its center by the pupil. Its peripheral attachment (iris root) connects to the ciliary body.
- Diameter: 12 mm
- Color depends on the amount of melanin in the iris stroma

**Iris Surface Regions (divided by the collarette):**
- **Collarette:** Circular ridge marking the site of the lesser iris circle (minor arterial circle)
- **Pupillary zone:** Central; extending from pupil to collarette; relatively flat; terminates the posterior pigment epithelium
- **Ciliary zone:** Peripheral; extending from collarette to iris root; irregular (iris crypts)

**Smooth Muscles of the Iris:**

| Muscle | Location | Action | Normal Result | Innervation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sphincter pupillae (constrictor) | Pupillary zone stroma; annular band | Contracts --> constriction | Pupil < 2 mm (miosis) | Parasympathetic (CN III via short ciliary nerve) |
| Dilator pupillae (radial muscles) | Ciliary zone stroma; radial fibers | Contracts --> dilation | Pupil > 5 mm (mydriasis) | Sympathetic nerve |

Normal pupil size: 3-4 mm

**Functions of the Iris:**
1. Reduces chromatic and spherical aberration (improves VA)
2. Regulates amount of light entering the eye
3. Increases depth of focus

**Blood Supply:**
The **greater iris circle (circulus iridis major)** is formed by the union of 2 long posterior ciliary arteries and 7 anterior ciliary arteries. This supplies the iris and ciliary body.

**Histologic Layers (Anterior to Posterior):**
1. **Anterior endothelium:** Flat hexagonal cells (outermost)
2. **Anterior limiting membrane:** Condensation of anterior iris stroma
3. **Iris stroma:** Loose connective tissue with pigment cells, sphincter pupillae, dilator pupillae, blood vessels, nerves, and mucopolysaccharides (MPS)
4. **Posterior epithelium:** Two dense, melanin-packed cell layers
   - Anterior pigment epithelium: Spindle cells in the ciliary zone
   - Posterior pigment epithelium: Polyhedral cells in both zones

## 10. Pupillary Reflexes and Disorders

### Pupillary Reflexes

**1. Direct Light Reflex**
Light in one eye -> that eye's pupil constricts.
Pathway: Retina → optic nerve → optic tract → posterior 1/3 of optic tract exits to brachium of superior colliculus → pretectal nucleus (midbrain) → Edinger-Westphal nucleus (bilateral: ipsilateral and contralateral) → pre-ganglionic parasympathetic fibers (with inferior division of CN III) → ciliary ganglion → post-ganglionic fibers via short ciliary nerve → sphincter pupillae.

**2. Consensual (Indirect) Light Reflex**
Light in one eye -> the other eye's pupil also constricts (due to bilateral projection from pretectal nucleus to both Edinger-Westphal nuclei).

**3. Accommodation-Near Reflex (Synkinesis -- not a true reflex)**
The near triad response when focusing on a close object:
1. **Accommodation:** Ciliary muscle contracts; lens becomes more convex
2. **Miosis:** Sphincter pupillae contracts
3. **Convergence:** Medial recti contract
Each component can occur without the others (they are coordinated but separable).

**4. Trigeminal Reflex**
Irritation of the cornea, conjunctiva, or facial skin (CN V) causes miosis.

**5. Psychic Reflex**
Fear causes mydriasis via sympathetic stimulation.

### Pupillary Disorders

**Adie's (Tonic) Pupil**
- Lesion: At pre-ganglionic, ciliary ganglion, or post-ganglionic parasympathetic fibers (CN III pathway via short ciliary nerve)
- The sphincter pupillae and ciliary muscles are denervated
- The pupil constricts **very slowly** to light and accommodation, but does constrict with 0.125% pilocarpine (supersensitivity of denervated muscle; normal requires 0.5-1.0%)
- Associated with Adie's syndrome: tonic pupil + absent deep tendon reflexes
- Signs: Mydriasis unresponsive to light, diaphoresis, loss of deep tendon reflex, hyperopia, photophobia, difficulty reading

**Argyll Robertson Pupil (ARP)**
- Cause: Neurosyphilis (Treponema pallidum affecting the CNS -- tabes dorsalis)
- Bilateral; selective damage to the light reflex pathway at the Edinger-Westphal nucleus level; accommodation pathways are spared (more ventral course in brainstem)
- Result: Accommodates but does not react to light
- Called "prostitute pupils" (accommodates but does not react)
- Pathognomonic sign: "Near response exceeds light response"

**Adie's vs. Argyll Robertson:**
| Feature | Adie's | Argyll Robertson |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Peripheral neuropathy (ciliary ganglion damage) | Central (neurosyphilis) |
| Laterality | Usually unilateral | Bilateral |
| Pilocarpine response | Constricts to 0.125% (supersensitivity) | Normal pilocarpine dose |
| Deep tendon reflexes | Absent | Usually intact |
| Both: accommodates but does not react to light | Yes | Yes |

**Marcus Gunn Pupil / RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect)**
- Lesion: Optic nerve (before chiasm) or severe retinal disease on one side
- Detected by the **swinging flashlight test:** when light swings from the normal eye to the affected eye, the affected pupil dilates (or constricts less) because the afferent signal is weaker through the damaged optic nerve
- The affected eye's direct reflex is reduced; the consensual reflex from the good eye is normal
- Graded 1-4 based on degree of pupillary dilation

**Swinging Flashlight Test:**
1. Patient fixates on a distant object
2. Swing a light from one eye to the other, equal illumination time for each (2-3 sec per eye)
3. Normal: symmetric constriction in both eyes
4. Abnormal: pupil enlarges when light hits the affected eye (less constriction compared to the unaffected eye)

**Horner's Syndrome**
- Lesion: Sympathetic pathway to the eye (superior cervical sympathetic ganglion or pupillary fibers via nasociliary → long ciliary nerve)
- Classic triad:
  1. Partial blepharoptosis (1-2 mm droop) -- Muller's muscle
  2. Miosis -- dilator pupillae
  3. Facial anhidrosis -- sweat glands of face and neck
- Anisocoria is more pronounced in dim light (unaffected eye has large pupil in dim light; affected eye cannot dilate)

**Tests for Horner's Syndrome:**
- **5-10% Cocaine test:** Normal pupil dilates (blocks NE reuptake); in all Horner's lesions (central, pre-, or post-ganglionic), the affected pupil does NOT dilate because NE is lacking at the neuromuscular junction
- **1% Hydroxyamphetamine test:** Releases stored NE; performed 24-48 hours after cocaine test
  - Central or pre-ganglionic lesion: Affected pupil dilates MORE than normal (enhanced NE stores at intact post-ganglionic terminal)
  - Post-ganglionic lesion: Affected pupil does NOT dilate (post-ganglionic neuron is damaged, no NE to release)

**CN Lesion Pupillary Effects Summary:**

| Lesion | Direct reflex (affected eye) | Consensual reflex (affected eye) |
|---|---|---|
| Left optic nerve damage | LOST (light to L eye → no signal) | INTACT (light to R eye → both pupils respond) |
| Left oculomotor (CN III) damage | LOST (L pupil cannot constrict) | LOST (L pupil cannot constrict even with R eye stimulus) |

## 11. Retina

The retina is a thin, transparent nervous membrane extending from the ora serrata anteriorly to the optic disc posteriorly. It is the "expansion" of the optic nerve, lying between the vitreous and choroid.

- Thickness: 0.1-0.5 mm
- Site of retinal detachment: Between RPE and photoreceptor layer (NOT between RPE and choroid)
- Permanent loss of vision: Within 24-72 hours if detachment is not repaired

### Regions

| Region | Features |
|---|---|
| Ora serrata | 6 mm from limbus; scalloped anterior edge of sensory retina; junction with ciliary epithelium |
| Peripheral retina | Mainly rods |
| Central retina (Macula lutea = Yellow Spot) | 4.5 mm diameter; mainly cones; 2-3 mm temporal to optic disc |
| Fovea centralis | 1.5 mm (450-600 µm) diameter; center of macula; thinnest part of retina; greatest visual acuity |
| Foveola | 0.4 mm (200 µm) diameter; all cones, no rods; maximum packing density (hexagonal mosaic); capillary-free |
| Optic disc (optic papilla) | 1.5 mm diameter; entrance of optic nerve; physiologic blind spot; 3 mm nasal to macula |
| Physiologic cup | Funnel-shaped depression at optic disc center; normal cup-to-disc ratio = 0.3 |

**Macula location:** 3 mm (2 disc diameters) temporal to, and 0.8 mm above, the foveola. Distance from center of optic disc to center of macula: 21 mm. Corresponds to 17 degrees temporal to optic disc.

### Photoreceptors

| Type | Function | Peak Wavelength |
|---|---|---|
| Rods | Dim illumination (scotopic vision) | 507 nm |
| Red-sensitive cones | Color vision (photopic) | 750 nm (longest) |
| Green-sensitive cones | Color vision | 535 nm |
| Blue-sensitive cones | Color vision | 440 nm (shortest) |

Parts of rods and cones:
- **Outer segment:** Contains visual pigment (rods: rhodopsin/visual purple; cones: photopigments); separated from inner segment by a cilium
- **Inner segment:** No visual pigment
- **Neck:** Constricted inner end
- **Fiber (axon)**
- **Terminal end:** Cone foot (cone) or neuropodium (rod)

### Neurons of the Retina

| Neuron Order | Cell Type | Function |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Photoreceptors (rods and cones) | Convert light to electrical signal |
| 2nd (Modulator cells) | Bipolar cells (synapse w/ single cone or rod group) | Transmit signal inward |
| 2nd (Modulator) | Horizontal cells (synapse mainly with rods) | Act as condensers; inhibit transmission |
| 2nd (Modulator) | Amacrine cells (pear-shaped; no dendron) | Contain dopamine; inhibit visual impulse |
| Support | Mueller's (Muller's) cells (glial/stellate astrocytes) | Mechanical support; glucose supply; form inner and outer limiting membranes |
| 3rd | Ganglion cells | Axons form the optic nerve; 5-7 layers at fovea, 1 layer at periphery |

### Histologic Layers of the Retina (Outer to Inner)
1. RPE (retinal pigment epithelium) -- single layer of brown columnar cells; firmly on lamina vitrea of choroid; absorbs light via melanin
2. Photoreceptor layer (rods and cones)
3. External limiting membrane -- fenestrated membrane; outer ends of Mueller's cells
4. Outer nuclear layer -- nuclei of rods and cones
5. Fibers of Henle -- thin layer of rod and cone axons (no nuclei)
6. Outer plexiform layer -- synapses between photoreceptors and inner nuclear layer cells
7. Inner nuclear layer -- bipolar, horizontal, amacrine, and Mueller's cell bodies
8. Inner plexiform layer -- synapses between modulator cells and ganglion cells
9. Ganglion cell layer -- 3rd neurons
10. Nerve fiber layer (stratum opticum) -- non-myelinated axons of ganglion cells; also contains CRA and choroidal veins
11. Internal limiting membrane -- inner ends of Mueller's cells

### Retinal Pigments

| Pigment | Location | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Melanin | RPE | Absorbs stray light |
| Lipofuscin | RPE (appears ~age 30) | Oxidative byproduct of photoreceptor disc shedding |
| Xanthophyll | Axons of cones (Fibers of Henle) | Yellow screening pigment; appears as macula lutea (yellow spot) |
| Lutein | Foveal area | Protective; absence associated with ARMD |
| Zeaxanthin | Foveal area | Protective |
| Meso-xanthin | Derived from lutein | Protective |
| Beta-carotene | Precursor to vitamin A | Found in carrots; important for rhodopsin synthesis |

### Blood Supply
- **Choriocapillaris:** Supplies RPE and photoreceptors; gives 65-85% blood flow to retina; ischemia > 1 hour causes irreparable photoreceptor damage
- **Central retinal artery (CRA):** Supplies inner sensory retina; 20-30% blood flow; 4 branches; branches to superior and inferior temporal papillary arteries supply the macula
- **No sensory innervation to the retina:** Blindness from retinal disease is painless

## 12. Visual Pathways and Optic Nerve

### Optic Nerve (CN II)
Not a true peripheral nerve but a nerve fiber tract -- an extension of the brain (white matter), sheathed by all three meningeal layers.
- Length: 50 mm (5 cm)
- Fiber count: 1.1-1.3 million fibers (from axons of ganglion cells)
- Route: Optic disc → optic canal → optic chiasm → optic tract → lateral geniculate body

**Fiber types:**
- Pupillary/afferent pupillomotor fibers: Carry pupillary light reflex signals
- Visual fibers: Carry visual sensation to lateral geniculate body
- Efferent/retino-motor fibers: Carry impulses from brain to retina
- Intercurrent/commissural fibers: Link the two retinas

**Parts of the Optic Nerve:**

| Segment | Length | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Intraocular | 0.7-1.0 mm | At optic disc; non-myelinated; spans lamina cribrosa (sieve-like structure at posterior sclera) -- thinnest part of sclera |
| Intraorbital | 30 mm (longest) | S-shaped curve for free globe movement; myelinated; all three meningeal sheaths |
| Intracanalicular | 4-10 mm (avg 6 mm) | Passes through optic foramen with ophthalmic artery and sympathetic fibers |
| Intracranial | 10 mm | Ends at optic chiasm |

**Fiber bundles:**
- Papillomacular bundle: 1/3 of total retinal fibers; from macular region; travels to temporal optic disc

### Visual Pathway and Field Defects

| Station | Lesion Result |
|---|---|
| One optic nerve | Total monocular blindness (affected eye) |
| Optic chiasm (crossing fibers) | Bitemporal hemianopsia (classic "tunnel vision"; pituitary tumor) |
| Optic chiasm (uncrossed fibers) | Binasal hemianopsia |
| Optic tract | **Incongruous** homonymous hemianopsia + Wernicke's hemianopic pupil |
| Lateral geniculate body | **Congruous** homonymous hemianopsia |
| Optic radiation (geniculo-calcarine tract) | **Congruous** homonymous hemianopsia |
| Area striata (Brodmann area 17) | Homonymous scotoma |

**Congruous** = visual field defects are symmetrical in both eyes (lesions posterior to LGB).
**Incongruous** = visual field defects are dissimilar in both eyes (lesions anterior to LGB).

**Wernicke's hemianopic pupil:** The pupil does not react when light is thrown on the blind half of the retina but reacts normally when light falls on the seeing portion.

**White matter tracts within LGB:**
- Lateral (inferior retinal) fibers in LGB → injury causes superior quadrantanopsia on the opposite side
- Medial (superior retinal) fibers in LGB → injury causes inferior quadrantanopsia on the opposite side

**LGB layers:** Temporal retinal fibers terminate in layers 2, 3, 5; nasal retinal fibers terminate in layers 1, 4, 6.

**Area Striata:** Contains the white line of **striae of Gennari** -- a prominent band of geniculo-calcarine fibers visible to the naked eye.

## 13. Autonomic Nervous System

The ANS has two divisions relevant to the eye:

### Sympathetic (Adrenergic; Thoraco-Lumbar)
- Originates in lateral gray matter of T1-L2 spinal cord; ocular fibers from T1-T3
- Controls: Dilator pupillae, Muller's muscle, choroidal/conjunctival vessels, lacrimal glands (vasoconstriction; no direct effect on secretion)

**Pathway:**
Hypothalamus → cervical spinal cord → pre-ganglionic fibers exit via T1-T3 ventral roots → superior cervical ganglion (near C2-C3) → post-ganglionic fibers form carotid plexus → enters orbit with CN V1 (ophthalmic division) → nasociliary nerve → long ciliary nerve → innervates dilator pupillae, ciliary muscles (inhibitory), and Muller's muscle.

### Parasympathetic (Cholinergic; Cranio-Sacral)
- Originates at brainstem and sacral spinal cord; ocular fibers from midbrain (CN III) and pons (CN VII)
- Controls: Sphincter pupillae, ciliary muscles (excitatory/accommodation), lacrimal gland secretion, choroidal/conjunctival vessels

**Pathway to intraocular muscles (pupil and accommodation):**
Edinger-Westphal nucleus (midbrain) → pre-ganglionic fibers with inferior division of CN III → ciliary ganglion (small, 2mm flat structure within muscle cone, between lateral rectus and CN II, 1 cm anterior to optic canal) → post-ganglionic fibers exit as short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae (3% of fibers) and ciliary muscles (97% of fibers).

**Pathway to lacrimal glands:**
Lacrimal nucleus (CN VII, pons) → pre-ganglionic fibers travel with CN VII → geniculate ganglion (no synapse) → greater petrosal nerve → joins deep petrosal nerve (sympathetic) → Vidian nerve → pterygopalatine (sphenopalatine) ganglion (synapse) → post-ganglionic fibers → zygomatic nerve (V2) → communicating branch to lacrimal nerve (V1) → lacrimal gland.

## 14. Cranial Nerves

### Ocular-Relevant Cranial Nerves Summary

| CN | Name | Key Ocular Role |
|---|---|---|
| II | Optic | Vision; afferent limb of pupillary light reflex |
| III | Oculomotor | All EOM except SO and LR; LPS; ciliary muscle; sphincter pupillae (parasympathetic) |
| IV | Trochlear | Superior oblique; thinnest CN (2,150-3,400 fibers); longest intracranial course (75 mm); only CN emerging from dorsal brainstem; fully crossed |
| V | Trigeminal | Sensory to cornea, conjunctiva, eyelids, lacrimal gland; corneal reflex (afferent); largest sensory CN |
| VI | Abducens | Lateral rectus; shortest intracranial course; 3,600 fibers |
| VII | Facial | Orbicularis oculi (CN VII, efferent limb of corneal reflex); lacrimal gland secretion; Bell's palsy |

### CN III (Oculomotor) -- Largest Motor Nerve
- Contains 24,000 fibers
- Enters orbit via **medial** part of SOF (through annulus of Zinn)
- Nucleus at mesencephalon (level of superior colliculus)
- The SR nucleus innervates the **contralateral** SR muscle; all other muscles ipsilateral
- Branches:
  - **Superior branch:** SR and LPS
  - **Inferior branch:** MR, IR, IO; carries parasympathetic fibers to ciliary ganglion

Paralysis of CN III:
- Unable to move eye upward, downward, or inward
- Divergent strabismus (exotropia; LR and SO still working)
- Complete blepharoptosis
- Cycloplegia (paralysis of accommodation)
- Dilated, fixed pupils (mydriasis)

### CN IV (Trochlear)
- Thinnest and most slender; 2,150-3,400 fibers
- Only CN emerging from **dorsal** surface of brainstem
- Only CN that is **completely crossed** (decussated)
- Enters orbit via **lateral** part of SOF; does NOT pass through annulus of Zinn
- Longest intracranial course: 75 mm
- Innervates the **contralateral** SO

Paralysis of CN IV:
- Hypertropia (vertical strabismus)
- Extorsion

### CN V (Trigeminal) -- Largest Cranial Nerve
Three divisions:
- **V1 (Ophthalmic; pure sensory):** Passes through SOF; innervates skin above orbit, upper eyelid, cornea, conjunctiva, nose, nasal mucosa, frontal sinuses
  - Branches: Nasociliary (long ciliary nerves → cornea and dilator; infratrochlear → lacrimal sac), Frontal (supraorbital + supratrochlear → upper lid and forehead), Lacrimal (sensory to lacrimal gland)
- **V2 (Maxillary; sensory + motor):** Passes through foramen rotundum; innervates lower lid, cheek, upper teeth, nares, maxillary sinus
- **V3 (Mandibular; sensory + motor; largest branch):** Passes through foramen ovale; motor to muscles of mastication; sensory to lower lip, teeth, chin, external ear

Mnemonic for foramen: **S**tanding **R**oom **O**nly (SOF → Rotundum → Ovale)

### CN VI (Abducens)
- Shortest intracranial course
- 3,600 fibers
- Enters orbit via **medial** part of SOF (inside annulus of Zinn)
- Innervates **ipsilateral** LR

Paralysis: Convergent strabismus (esotropia); diplopia on lateral gaze; patient turns head toward the affected side to avoid double vision.

### CN VII (Facial)
- Primarily a sensory nerve, but with important motor functions
- Exits skull via stylomastoid foramen
- **Motor branches (ZTBMC):** Temporal, Zygomatic, Buccal, Mandibular (Marginal), Cervical
- **Sensory/autonomic branches:** Greater petrosal nerve (lacrimal gland parasympathetics), chorda tympani (anterior 2/3 tongue taste sensation)

Injury → Bell's palsy: facial asymmetry, lagophthalmos (inability to close eyelid), dry mouth, loss of taste

## 15. Extraocular Muscles

Six muscles move each eye: four recti and two obliques.

### EOM Origins and Insertions

**Annulus of Zinn (Annulus tendineus communis)**
Funnel-shaped tendinous ring at the optic foramen. Structures passing through it: CN II, CN III, CN VI, ophthalmic artery. (CN IV does NOT pass through it.)

**Muscles originating from annulus of Zinn:** 4 recti + LPS + SO

| Muscle | Origin | Insertion (distance from limbus) | Special Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Superior rectus (SR) | Annulus of Zinn | 7.7 mm (furthest from limbus) | Longest rectus muscle (body only) |
| Medial rectus (MR) | Annulus of Zinn | 5.5 mm (closest to limbus) | Strongest rectus muscle |
| Inferior rectus (IR) | Annulus of Zinn | 6.5 mm | -- |
| Lateral rectus (LR) | Annulus of Zinn (2 heads -- upper and lower portions, bridging the SOF) | 6.9 mm | Partially supplied by lacrimal artery |
| Superior oblique (SO) | Periosteum of lesser wing of sphenoid (above and medial to optic foramen) | Behind equator (supero-posterior quadrant) via trochlea | Longest EOM from origin to insertion; tendon bends 55° at trochlea; passes under SR |
| Inferior oblique (IO) | Orbital plate of maxilla, floor of orbit (behind inferior orbital margin, lateral to nasolacrimal fossa) | Behind equator (supero-posterior quadrant) | Shortest EOM; crosses under IR; 51° angle to visual axis |

The trochlea is on the undersurface of the frontal bone at the roof of the orbit, just behind the superior orbital margin.

### Actions of EOM

**Primary, secondary, tertiary positions start from primary gaze (looking straight ahead):**

| Muscle | Primary Action | Secondary | Tertiary |
|---|---|---|---|
| MR | Adduction | -- | -- |
| LR | Abduction | -- | -- |
| SR | Elevation | Intorsion | Adduction |
| IR | Depression | Extorsion | Adduction |
| SO | Intorsion | Depression | Abduction |
| IO | Extorsion | Elevation | Abduction |

MR and LR are the only two EOM whose muscle axis aligns with the optical axis in primary position. All others have oblique angles that create secondary and tertiary actions.

### Cardinal Directions of Gaze

| Direction | Term | Movement |
|---|---|---|
| Nasally inward | Adduction | Vertical axis rotation |
| Temporally outward | Abduction | Vertical axis rotation |
| Upward | Elevation/Supraduction | Horizontal axis rotation |
| Downward | Depression/Infraduction | Horizontal axis rotation |
| 12 o'clock nasally | Incycloduction/Intorsion | Torsional |
| 12 o'clock temporally | Excycloduction/Extorsion | Torsional |

### Vergence and Version Movements

**Versions (conjugate):** Both eyes move in the same direction simultaneously.

| Version | Also Called | Muscles Involved |
|---|---|---|
| Looking right | Dextroversion | R LR + L MR |
| Looking left | Levoversion | L LR + R MR |
| Looking straight up | Supraversion | Both SR + Both IO |
| Looking straight down | Infraversion | Both IR + Both SO |
| Right and up | Dextroelevation | R SR + L IO |
| Right and down | Dextrodepression | R IR + L SO |
| Left and up | Levoelevation | L SR + R IO |
| Left and down | Levodepression | L IR + R SO |

**Vergences (disconjugate):** Both eyes move in opposite directions.
- Convergence: Both eyes move nasally (for near fixation)
- Divergence: Both eyes move temporally (for far fixation)

### Laws of EOM

**Hering's Law:** Yoke muscles (muscles that move both eyes in the same direction) receive equal and simultaneous innervation. Explains why you cannot move one eye without the other moving in the same conjugate direction.

**Sherrington's Law:** When an agonist muscle contracts, its antagonist in the same eye is simultaneously inhibited (reciprocal innervation).

### Innervation

| Muscle(s) | Nerve |
|---|---|
| All except SO and LR | CN III |
| SO | CN IV |
| LR | CN VI |

Mnemonic: **LR6(SO4)3** = Lateral Rectus by CN 6, Superior Oblique by CN 4, rest by CN 3.

### Blood Supply
- **Muscular arteries** (branches of ophthalmic artery): Each rectus muscle receives 2 muscular arteries, **except LR which receives only 1**, with partial supply from the lacrimal artery.
  - Lateral branch: LR, SR, SO, LPS
  - Medial branch: IR, MR, IO

## 16. Ophthalmic Artery

The first intracranial branch of the internal carotid artery. It enters the orbit via the optic canal, lying infero-lateral to the optic nerve, then crosses over (or under) the nerve to run medially.

### Major Branches

| Branch | Supplies |
|---|---|
| Central retinal artery (CRA) | First intra-orbital branch; enters optic nerve 10-12 mm behind globe; supplies inner sensory retina; 4 branches: nasal + temporal (superior and inferior each) |
| Short posterior ciliary arteries | Choroid (10-20 branches per PCA group; enter in a ring around the optic nerve) |
| Long posterior ciliary arteries (2) | Travel in perichoroidal space; form the greater iris circle with 7 ACA; supply iris and ciliary body |
| Anterior ciliary arteries (ACA) | Travel with rectus muscles; pierce sclera anteriorly; form greater iris circle |
| Lacrimal artery | Lacrimal gland; partial LR supply |
| Muscular arteries | EOM (lateral branch: LR, SR, SO, LPS; medial branch: IR, MR, IO) |
| Supraorbital artery | LPS, frontal bone, sinuses, upper eyelid, forehead, scalp |
| Supratrochlear artery | Forehead and scalp skin |
| Ethmoidal arteries | Anterior and posterior ethmoidal sinuses; nasal cavity |
| Palpebral arteries | Eyelids and conjunctiva |
| Nasal artery | Root of nose; lacrimal sac; anastomoses with facial artery |

The **greater iris circle (circulus iridis major)** = 2 long PCA + 7 ACA.

## 17. Aqueous Humor and IOP

### Aqueous Humor

Aqueous humor (AH) is the clear, serous fluid that fills the anterior and posterior aqueous chambers of the eye. It is produced by the non-pigmented epithelium of the ciliary processes (pars plicata).

**Aqueous Chambers:**
| Chamber | Volume |
|---|---|
| Anterior aqueous chamber (anterior to iris) | 0.20-0.25 mL; central portion deepest (3 mm) |
| Posterior aqueous chamber (between iris and lens) | 0.06 mL |

**AH Refractive index:** 1.336

**Rate of formation:** 1-4 µL/min; normal flow rate 2 µL/min.

**Outflow routes:**
- 80% via trabecular meshwork into Schlemm's canal → episcleral veins
- 20% via uveoscleral pathway (space of Schwalbe and ciliary muscle spaces)

**Mechanisms of AH formation:**
1. **Ultrafiltration:** From ciliary vessel capillaries
2. **Secretion:** Via Na-K ATPase (active transport of Na+)
3. **Active transport** of other ions

**Enzymes involved:**
- **Na-K ATPase:** Actively transports Na+ in exchange for K+; target of ouabain (inhibitor)
- **Carbonic anhydrase:** Expels HCO3- into posterior aqueous chamber, followed by Na+, Cl-, and water; inhibited by acetazolamide (Diamox)
- **Adenylyl (adenylate) cyclase:** Synthesizes cyclic AMP from ATP; functions as a second messenger

**Composition of AH:**
| Component | Value |
|---|---|
| Water | 98.1% |
| Proteins (albumin, transferrin, IgG, orosomucoid) | 25 mg% |
| Electrolytes | Na+, K+, Ca++, Mg, Cl- |
| Antioxidants | Ascorbic acid (vitamin C), glutathione |

**Functions of AH:**
1. Maintain IOP
2. Support metabolism of lens, cornea, and trabecular meshwork
3. Nourish cornea and lens; remove metabolic waste
4. Maintain proper convexity of cornea

### Intraocular Pressure (IOP)

IOP must exceed atmospheric pressure to prevent collapse of the globe.
- **Normal IOP:** 10-20 mmHg (some sources: 15-21 mmHg)
- Normally fluctuates 2-5 mmHg daily; varies 1-2 mmHg with each heartbeat and respiration
- **Highest IOP** in the morning; lowest at night

**Factors that maintain IOP:**
1. Rate of AH formation
2. Ease of outflow through trabecular meshwork (75% of outflow resistance)
3. Episcleral venous pressure (normal 10 mmHg)

**Factors that increase IOP:**
- Valsalva maneuver (increases venous pressure)
- Rapid IV infusion of normal saline
- Drinking large amounts of water on an empty stomach

**Factors that decrease AH formation:**
- Ouabain or vanadate (poisons Na-K ATPase)
- Acetazolamide (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor)
- Cooling the ciliary body to 19°C

### Trabecular Meshwork (Ligamentum Pectinatum Iridis)

Broad, circular, loosely arranged band of connective tissue that filters AH into Schlemm's canal.
- Extends from scleral spur to Schwalbe's line
- Composed of uveal and limbal meshwork
- Posterior zone has more pigmentation and more filtration

### Schlemm's Canal (Sinus Venosus Sclerae)

Collection channel within the scleral sulcus that receives AH from the trabecular meshwork and delivers it to episcleral veins.

### Key Gonioscopic Structures (from anterior to posterior)

| Structure | Appearance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Schwalbe's line (SL) | Thin, white, glistening line | Termination of Descemet's membrane; anterior boundary of trabecular meshwork |
| Trabecular meshwork (TM) | Light gray; granular | 75% of outflow resistance |
| Scleral spur (SS) | White band | Anterior boundary of ciliary body band; insertion for longitudinal ciliary muscle |
| Ciliary body (CB) | Brown/gray band | |
| Pupillary border | | |
| Peripheral iris | | |

Sampaolesi's line: Pigment collected along Schwalbe's line (sign of pigment dispersion syndrome or pseudoexfoliation).

### Tonometry

**Contact tonometers:**
- **Schiotz tonometer:** Mechanical indentation; errors from: ocular rigidity, lid squeezing, mechanical issues; large volume displacement can give falsely low IOP
- **Goldman applanation tonometer:** Gold standard; flattens cornea to measure IOP; uses slit-lamp biomicroscope; most accurate
- **Mackay-Marg tonometer:** Electronic indentation; useful for scarred/edematous corneas
- **Tono-Pen:** Hand-held; Mackay-Marg principle; beeps for acceptable reading

**Non-contact tonometer (NCT):**
- Air puff flattens cornea
- Less reliable with irregular or edematous corneas
- Patient apprehension (lid squeezing) falsely elevates readings

**Shaffer grading of angle width:**
| Grade | Angle | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| 3-4 | 20-45 degrees | Wide open |
| 2 | 20 degrees | Moderately narrow |
| 1 | 10 degrees | Extremely narrow |
| 0 | 0 degrees | Closed (angle closure) |

## 18. Vitreous Humor

The vitreous is a transparent, colorless, gelatinous mass that fills the posterior 4/5 of the eyeball (vitreous chamber). It is firmer than egg white and constitutes the largest single optical element of the eye.

- **Total volume:** 4.5 mL
- 80% gel and 20% liquid at birth; becomes 50% liquid with aging

**Composition:**
| Component | Details |
|---|---|
| Water | 98.5% |
| Thin collagen fibrils | Provides framework |
| Electrolytes | Na+, Cl- |
| Proteins | Albumin; vitrein (residual collagen-gelatin protein) |
| Hyalocytes | Connective tissue cells (macrophages) of the vitreous |
| Hyaluronic acid | Viscous polysaccharide; gives gel property |

**Hyaluronic acid is lysed by:** Hyaluronidase (spreading factor), ascorbic acid (vitamin C), H2O2.

**Functions:**
1. Distends and maintains the shape of the eyeball
2. Keeps the retinal surface at the proper focal distance from the lens

**Blood supply:** Avascular; nourished by the choroid, ciliary body, and retina.

### Key Anatomical Features

**Fossa patellaris (Lenticular fossa):** Saucer-shaped anterior depression of the vitreous that corresponds to the posterior convexity of the lens.

**Zonular cleft of Salzman:** Base/origin of the vitreous; firmly adherent to the pars plana and ora serrata.

**Hyaloid canal:** Former course of the hyaloid artery (during fetal development); starts at optic disc as funnel-shaped area of Martegiani, passes through vitreous as a narrow canal (1-2 mm), then expands anteriorly in the fossa lenticularis.

**Limiting membrane:** Condensation of vitreous present everywhere except at the zonular cleft and area of Martegiani.
- **Anterior hyaloid:** Starts 2 mm from ora serrata; attached to posterior lens capsule by the hyaloideo-capsular ligament
- **Posterior hyaloid:** Starts from zonular cleft; attached to the internal limiting membrane of the retina up to the area of Martegiani

**Muscae volitantes:** Vitreous floaters; remnants of the hyaloid system; appear as opacities in the vitreous.

## 19. Crystalline Lens and Zonules of Zinn

### Crystalline Lens

A transparent, biconvex structure suspended behind the iris and pupil, in front of the vitreous. Its refractive power is adjustable (unlike the cornea), making accommodation possible.

**Measurements:**
| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Diameter | 9-10 mm |
| Thickness | 4-5 mm (greater convexity posteriorly) |
| Anterior pole radius | 10 mm |
| Posterior pole radius | 6 mm |
| Refractive power | 10-15 diopters |
| Index of refraction (central) | 1.43 |
| Index of refraction (peripheral) | 1.386 |

**Composition:**
| Component | Value |
|---|---|
| Water | 65% |
| Proteins | 30-35% |
| Phospholipids | 19% |
| Cholesterol | 0.66% |
| Electrolytes | 0.8-1.2% |

**Blood supply:** Avascular; nourished by aqueous humor.
**Innervation:** None.

### Structures of the Lens

**Lens capsule:**
- Smooth, homogenous, transparent, acellular, highly elastic envelope around the lens
- Formed by secretions of anterior lens epithelial cells
- Thicker anteriorly than posteriorly
  - Anterior: 15 µm (thickest at periphery: 18.2 µm -- site of zonular attachment); acts as the basement membrane of anterior lens epithelium (thickest basement membrane in the body)
  - Posterior: 2.2 µm

**Lens epithelium:**
- Anterior: Single row of cuboidal cells beneath anterior lens capsule; cells become columnar at the equator and elongate into lens fibers
- Posterior: Present only during fetal life

**Lens substance:**
- **Cortex:** Newly formed, soft, nucleated lens fibers (outer)
- **Nucleus:** Dense, old, non-nucleated lens fibers (inner); formed by compression of old cortical fibers
- **Cement substance:** Amorphous material that glues the capsule, epithelium, and lens fibers together

**Lens fibers:**
- Long, prismatic, six-sided bands containing albuminoid materials
- First lens fibers formed from posterior lens epithelium
- New fibers are laid externally in concentric laminated/onion-like layers (hence the cataracts can be graded by depth)

### Zonules of Zinn (Suspensory Ligament of the Lens)

140 bundles of delicate fibers extending from the circular muscle fibers (Müller's) of the ciliary body to the anterior and posterior lens capsule.

**Function:** Holds the lens in position; transmits ciliary muscle contraction to the lens during accommodation.

**Types of zonular fibers:**
| Type | Origin | Insertion |
|---|---|---|
| Orbiculo-posterior capsular | Ora serrata | Posterior lens capsule; alongside hyaloideo-capsular ligament |
| Orbiculo-anterior capsular | Pars plana of ciliary body (most anterior and thickest) | Anterior lens capsule |
| Cilio-posterior capsular | Ciliary valley | Posterior lens capsule |
| Cilio-equatorial capsular | Ciliary valley | Lens equator (disappears in old age) |

## 20. Lacrimal Apparatus and Tear Film

### Lacrimal Apparatus: Secretory Portion

**Main Lacrimal Gland**
Located at the supero-lateral roof of the orbit in the lacrimal fossa. The lateral aponeurosis of LPS divides it into:
- **Superior (main) lacrimal gland:** 2-8 ducts opening at the fornix
- **Inferior (accessory) lacrimal gland:** 6-10 ducts opening at the fornix

**Innervation:**
- CN VII (secretory -- increases tearing)
- CN V (via lacrimal nerve -- sensory)
- Cervical sympathetic nerve (innervates blood vessels only; no direct effect on secretion)
- Parasympathetic nerve (innervates the ducts)

**Basic secretors** (not controlled by the nervous system; sole tear supply during sleep and in neonates):
- Accessory lacrimal glands of Krause (at fornix): 20 glands in upper fornix, 6-8 in lower
- Accessory lacrimal glands of Wolfring (at tarsus)
- Goblet cells (conjunctiva) -- mucin layer
- Glands of Zeis -- oily layer
- Glands of Moll (apocrine) -- oily layer

**Reflex secretors** (controlled by CN V -- nervous system):
- Main lacrimal gland: Responds to psychic stimuli, corneal irritation, excessive light, heat on tongue (CN VII reflex), etc.

### Lacrimal Apparatus: Excretory Portion (in order of flow)

1. **Lacrimal puncta:** 0.3 mm opening at the apex of each lacrimal papilla; first part of drainage
2. **Lacrimal canaliculi:** 0.5 mm wide; vertical portion 2-3.5 mm; horizontal portion 8 mm
3. **Lacrimal sac:** Oval, dilated upper part of the nasolacrimal duct; 12 mm long, 6 mm wide; located in the lacrimal fossa of the medial orbital wall
   - Sinus of Maier: Lateral pouch
   - Sinus of Arlt: Inferior pouch (guarded by valve of Beraud)
4. **Nasolacrimal duct:** Extension of lacrimal sac; 12-24 mm long, 4-6 mm wide; opens at the inferior meatus of the nose; guarded by valve of Hasner; last to open during development

### Lacrimal Secretions (Tears)

- **pH:** 7.3-7.7 (average 7.4); subjective discomfort if pH < 6.6 or > 7.8
- **Rate of secretion:** 0.9-2.2 µL/min
- **Evaporation:** 20-25% lost by evaporation; 10-20x higher in absence of oily layer

**Composition:**
| Component | Value / Notes |
|---|---|
| Water | 98% |
| NaCl (osmotic pressure) | 0.6-1.3% NaCl (average 0.9%); higher than plasma; secreted by active transport |
| Protein | 7 mg/100 mL total |
| Albumin | 60% of protein |
| Globulin | 15-20% of protein |
| Lysozyme (Muramidase) | 21-25% of protein; lyses bacterial cell walls; increased in suppurative eye infection, after drinking water; decreased in epiphora and elderly |
| IgA (secretory) | 17 mg/100 mL; major Ig; neutralizes viruses; inhibits bacterial adherence |
| IgG | 14 mg/100 mL; promotes phagocytosis and complement-mediated lysis |
| IgE | Elevated in allergic conjunctivitis and blepharitis |
| Glucose | 2.5 mg% (use tears instead of urine to assess glucose in comatose patients) |
| Lactoferrin | Chelates iron |
| Interferon | Inhibits herpes viral replication |

**Crocodile tears:** After lesions of the facial ganglion, regenerating salivary nerve fibers (CN VII) are misdirected to the lacrimal gland, causing tearing during eating.

### Schirmer's Test

Measures the quantity of lacrimal secretions.
- **Equipment:** 35 x 5 mm Whatman #41 filter paper (IsoSol Strip)
- **Procedure:** Fold 5 mm at one end; hook at lateral portion of lower eyelid; measure wet length after 5 minutes; done in a dimly lit room with patient facing away from light

| Test | Condition | Normal Value | Abnormal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Schirmer's I | Without anesthesia (reflex stimulation) | 10-25 mm | > 25 mm (hypersecretion) |
| Schirmer's II | With anesthesia (basal secretion) | 8-15 mm | -- |
| Schirmer's III | With nasal mucosa irritation (NH3 on cotton tip) | > 25 mm | < 15 mm in 2 min (hyposecretion) |

### Jones (Fluorescein Dye) Test

Tests the patency of the lacrimal drainage system.

**Jones Test I:**
- Instill 1% fluorescein at cul-de-sac
- Cotton tip moistened with epinephrine + cocaine inserted under inferior nasal turbinate
- After 1 minute: Recovery of fluorescein in the nose = patent system
- Failure = functional or anatomic blockage; proceed to Jones Test II

**Jones Test II:**
- Flush residual fluorescein with NSS instilled at lacrimal sac
- NSS heavily stained with fluorescein in nose: Upper system (puncta/canaliculi) is normal; problem is in lacrimal sac or nasolacrimal duct
- NSS lightly stained: Lacrimal canaliculus is affected
- Reflux from same punctum = canalicular obstruction
- Reflux from opposite punctum = obstruction distal to common canaliculus
- Reflux of purulent material = nasolacrimal duct obstruction
- Bloody reflux = tumor, inflammation, or trauma

### Tear Film Break-Up Time (TBUT / BUT)

Assesses the stability of the precorneal tear film; specifically tests for **mucin deficiency**.
- **Definition:** Time from the last complete blink to the first appearance of a random dry spot on the corneal surface (using fluorescein dye under cobalt blue filter on slit lamp)
- **Normal:** 15-34 seconds; > 10 seconds generally considered normal
- **Abnormal:** < 10 seconds (marginal 5-10 sec, clearly low < 5 sec)
- Dry spots appearing < 10 seconds indicate mucin deficiency (goblet cell loss)
- Causes of mucin deficiency: Avitaminosis A, pemphigoid, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, trachoma, chemical burns

**Schirmer's + TBUT in Dry Eye:**
- Dry eye: TBUT < 10 seconds AND Schirmer's < 6 mm
- Normal: TBUT > 10 seconds AND Schirmer's > 6 mm

### Rose Bengal Dye

Stains dead or damaged conjunctival and corneal cells at the lower 2/3 of bulbar conjunctiva and cornea (bright red). Water-soluble dye. Used to assess the cornea and conjunctiva in dry eye syndrome.

### Precorneal Tear Film

The thin, stagnant layer of tears covering the corneal epithelium. It provides the first optical surface of the eye.

**Measurements:**
| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Volume | 7.4 µL |
| Thickness | 6.5-7.7 µm |
| pH | 6.5-7.6 |
| Rate of secretion | 1-2 µL/min |
| Osmolarity | 296-308 mOsm/L |
| Refractive index | 1.3361-1.3379 |

**Layers of the Precorneal Tear Film (Outer to Inner):**

| Layer | Thickness | Source | Key Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Oily (Lipid) layer | 0.9 µm (avg 0.5 µm) | Mainly Meibomian (tarsal) glands; some Zeis | Retards evaporation; provides smooth optical surface; lubrication; maintains hydrophobic barrier (prevents overflow) |
| Aqueous layer | 6.5-7.5 µm (avg 7 µm); thickest | Accessory lacrimal glands of Krause and Wolfring | Supplies O2 to cornea; antibacterial Ig; washes debris |
| Mucin layer | 0.002-0.05 µm; thinnest | Goblet cells of conjunctiva | Wets corneal microvilli; retains the tear film; hydrophilic coating on corneal epithelium |

**Oily layer composition:** 35% wax ester, 30% cholesterol, 16% phospholipids, 4% triglycerides, 2% free fatty acids, 2% free sterols.

**Mucin layer composition:** Carbohydrates (75%), proteins (25%), sialic acid, hyaluronic acid (spreading factor), mucins.

**Mucin deficiency seen in:** Avitaminosis A (Xerophthalmia); **mucin excess** seen in hyperthyroidism, toxic goiter, Grave's disease, thyrotoxicosis.
