## G2 — Autonomic (Ocular) Drugs
*Personal study notes | TOS: G Ocular Pharmacology | Source: Ocular Pharmacology, pp.17-34 + autonomic framework*

---

## The Simple Version

Almost every drug that acts on the eye works through the **autonomic nervous system**, and the pupil is the clearest place to see it. Think of the pupil as a tug of war between two muscles:

- **Sphincter pupillae** (circular, **parasympathetic**, M3 receptor) pulls the pupil **closed** (miosis).
- **Dilator pupillae** (radial, **sympathetic**, α1 receptor) pulls the pupil **open** (mydriasis).

To move the pupil you can either **push your own muscle** (give an agonist) or **release the opponent** (block the other side). That single idea generates a 2x2 grid that explains every mydriatic, miotic, and cycloplegic on the board.

| | Sympathetic (adrenergic) | Parasympathetic (cholinergic) |
|---|---|---|
| **Agonist** (mimic, turn ON) | Sympathomimetic → **dilate**, no cycloplegia | Parasympathomimetic → **constrict** (miosis) |
| **Antagonist** (block, turn OFF) | Sympatholytic → **constrict** | Parasympatholytic → **dilate + cycloplegia** |

**Teaching angle:** never memorize the four effects. For any drug, name the system and whether it mimics or blocks, then ask "am I pushing my muscle or releasing the opponent?" The effect rebuilds itself.

---

## But Why?

**Why do the two dilators behave differently (one keeps near vision, one loses it)?**
The dilators sit on a diagonal: a sympathomimetic (push the dilator) and a parasympatholytic (release the sphincter). The fork between them is the **ciliary muscle**, which is run by the *same* parasympathetic M3 nerve as the sphincter. So a parasympatholytic that relaxes the sphincter also paralyzes the ciliary muscle and **adds cycloplegia**. A sympathomimetic never touches the ciliary muscle, so it dilates with near vision intact.

**Why does pilocarpine lower eye pressure?**
It is a direct M3 agonist. It contracts the ciliary muscle (Brucke's fibers), which pulls open the trabecular meshwork and lets aqueous drain. So a "constrictor" is also a glaucoma drug, because the same muscle that focuses the lens also tugs the drain open.

**Why are all mydriatics dangerous in a narrow angle?**
Dilation bunches the peripheral iris into the angle and blocks the trabecular meshwork, which can trigger acute angle-closure. This is the one contraindication that applies to every dilating drug in this note.

**Why do we test pupils with cocaine and hydroxyamphetamine?**
Because they act at different points along the sympathetic chain. Cocaine needs a working nerve releasing NE; hydroxyamphetamine needs an intact final neuron to release stored NE. Putting them in sequence localizes a Horner lesion to a specific neuron.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Mydriasis | Greek *mydros* | Pupil dilation; α1 agonist or M3 antagonist |
| Miosis | Greek *meioun* = to lessen | Pupil constriction; M3 agonist or α1 antagonist |
| Cycloplegia | *kyklos* = circle + *plege* = paralysis | Ciliary muscle paralysis; loss of accommodation |
| Sympathomimetic | mimics the sympathetic system | Adrenergic agonist; dilates without cycloplegia |
| Parasympatholytic | lyses the parasympathetic system | Antimuscarinic; dilates plus cycloplegia |
| Agonist | Greek *agon* = contest | Binds and activates the receptor ("push your muscle") |
| Antagonist | *anti* = against | Blocks the receptor ("release the opponent") |
| Denervation supersensitivity | upregulated receptors | Why an Adie's pupil responds to weak 0.1% pilocarpine |
| ISA | intrinsic sympathomimetic activity | A beta blocker that also acts as a partial agonist |
| MSA | membrane-stabilizing activity | Local-anesthetic property of some beta blockers |
| Decongestant | vasoconstrictor | α1 agonist that blanches conjunctival vessels |

### Neurotransmitters and their handling

| | Sympathetic (adrenergic) | Parasympathetic (cholinergic) |
|---|---|---|
| Transmitter | Norepinephrine (NE) | Acetylcholine (ACh) |
| Degrading enzyme | MAO + COMT | Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) |
| Breakdown product | Vanillylmandelic acid (VMA) | Acetate + choline |
| Receptors | α1, α2, β1, β2, β3 | Nicotinic, Muscarinic M1/M2/M3 |

**NE synthesis chain (rate-limiting step is the first one):**
Tyrosine → (tyrosine hydroxylase, **rate-limiting**) → DOPA → (DOPA decarboxylase) → Dopamine → (dopamine β-hydroxylase) → **NE** → (in adrenal medulla) → Epinephrine. Reserpine blocks transport into the vesicle.

### Receptor number rule

**Number = action: 1 constricts/contracts, 2 dilates.**

| Receptor | Ocular location | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| α1 | Dilator pupillae, conjunctival vessels, trabecular meshwork, Mueller's muscle | Mydriasis, vasoconstriction, opens trabecula, lid retraction |
| α2 | Ciliary process (inhibitory, presynaptic) | **Decrease** aqueous secretion (the glaucoma brake) |
| β1 | Heart | Tachycardia, increased output |
| β2 | Ciliary process (excitatory), ciliary muscle, bronchi | **Increase** aqueous secretion; relax ciliary muscle; bronchodilation |
| M3 | Sphincter pupillae, ciliary (Brucke's) muscle | Miosis; accommodation; opens trabecula indirectly |
| Nicotinic | EOM | Contraction |

### Box 1 — Parasympathomimetics (cholinergic agonists): MIOSIS

Effects: miosis, accommodation, increased aqueous outflow, conjunctival/ciliary injection.

**Direct-acting** (bind the receptor):
- **Acetylcholine 1%:** destroyed instantly by AChE; intracameral to constrict pupil after cataract extraction.
- **Methacholine 2.5%:** resists AChE; diagnoses the tonic pupil in **Adie's**.
- **Carbachol (0.75–3%):** not hydrolyzed by AChE; powerful miotic, second-line when pilocarpine fails; poor corneal penetration so combined with BAK.
- **Pilocarpine (0.25–10%):** the prototype. Onset 2 h, duration 8 h. Lowers IOP (open and angle-closure), constricts pupil, and counters mydriasis from epinephrine/phenylephrine/tropicamide. **0.1% pilocarpine constricts an Adie's pupil** (denervation supersensitivity) but not a normal one; 0.5–1% constricts a normal pupil. Contraindicated in malignant glaucoma. Most common side effect: miosis and accommodative spasm (drug-induced myopia).

**Indirect-acting** (AChE inhibitors, raise ACh):
- Reversible (carbamyl ester): **Physostigmine** (0.25–0.5%) and **Demecarium** (two physostigmines). Uses: open-angle glaucoma, lice on lid margins.
- Irreversible (organophosphate): **Echothiopate** and **DFP** (diisopropylfluorophosphate). Long-acting miotics for **accommodative esotropia** and aphakic glaucoma. Classic side effect: **iris cysts** (prevented/reversed with 2.5% phenylephrine), and echothiopate can cause retinal detachment.
- Poisoning agents in the same family: parathion, malathion (insecticides), and nerve gas (tabun, soman, sarin). **Antidote: atropine.**

**Clinical test:** **Tensilon (edrophonium) test** for myasthenia gravis. Short-acting AChE inhibitor; muscle strength improves in myasthenia. Atropine is kept ready as the antidote for cholinergic crisis.

---

### Box 2 — Parasympatholytics (cholinergic antagonists): MYDRIASIS + CYCLOPLEGIA

Block M3 → sphincter relaxes (mydriasis) and ciliary muscle relaxes (cycloplegia). **All are contraindicated in angle-closure glaucoma.**

Uses: uveitis (break/prevent synechiae), cycloplegic refraction, relieve ciliary spasm, penalization in amblyopia.

**The cycloplegic duration table (very high yield):**

| Drug | Mydriasis onset | Cycloplegia onset | Duration | Note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Atropine** 0.5–3% | 30 min | 1 h | **14 days** | Strongest, longest. From *Atropa belladonna*. Amblyopia penalization, child cycloplegic refraction, anterior uveitis |
| **Homatropine** 2–5% | 30 min | 30–90 min | 6 h–4 days | Intermediate. Primary use: iridocyclitis |
| **Scopolamine** 0.25% | 40 min | 40 min | 24 h | From *Scopolia*. Rarely used diagnostically |
| **Cyclopentolate** 0.5–2% | 15–30 min | 15–45 min | 6–24 h | **Drug of choice for routine cycloplegia.** CNS effects in children (use 0.5–1%) |
| **Tropicamide** 0.5–1% | 20–30 min | 20–25 min | 4–6 h | **Most common mydriatic;** weak cycloplegic, DOC for routine mydriasis. Safest in children and cardiac patients (no vasopressor effect) |

Listed strongest → weakest cycloplegic. **Atropine poisoning treatment: physostigmine 1–4 mg IV.**

**Eucatropine:** used as a provocative test for suspected narrow-angle glaucoma.

**Botulinum toxin A** (from *Clostridium botulinum*): blocks ACh release at the neuromuscular junction (inhibits calcium at the synaptic cleft). Max effect 1–2 weeks, lasts 2–6 months. Uses: strabismus surgery, blepharospasm, hemifacial spasm, lid retraction in Graves. Side effects: ptosis, diplopia, exposure keratopathy.

---

### Box 3 — Sympathomimetics (adrenergic agonists): MYDRIASIS without cycloplegia

**Direct-acting:**
- **Decongestants** (α1, vasoconstrict conjunctival vessels): naphazoline, tetrahydrozoline, oxymetazoline.
- **Apraclonidine (0.5–1%):** α2 agonist (clonidine derivative). Lowers IOP after laser (trabeculoplasty, iridotomy, YAG capsulotomy) without affecting BP or pulse.
- **Brimonidine (0.2%, newest α2 agonist):** lowers IOP by decreasing aqueous secretion.
- **Epinephrine (0.5–2%):** non-selective; lowers IOP three ways (α1 opens trabecula, α2 cuts secretion, but β raises secretion). Dilates a Horner pupil at 0.1% (denervation supersensitivity). Contraindicated in angle-closure, aphakia (cystoid macular edema), CVD, diabetes. Rarely used now; replaced by **dipivefrin** (a more lipophilic pro-drug converted to epinephrine).
- **Phenylephrine (0.12 / 2.5 / 10%):** synthetic, selective **α1** agonist on the dilator. 0.12% = decongestant; 2.5% and 10% = mydriasis with **no cycloplegia**. Onset 30–60 min, duration 4–6 h. 2.5% delivers 1.25 mg systemically, 10% delivers 5 mg (avoid 10% in cardiac/HPN patients).

**Indirect-acting** (change NE level, do not bind receptor):
- **Hydroxyamphetamine 1%:** releases stored NE; does not cross the blood-brain barrier.
- **Cocaine 4%:** blocks NE reuptake; also a local anesthetic. From *Erythroxylon coca*.

**The two pupil tests (commit these cold):**

- **Horner's syndrome** (sympathetic lesion): ptosis (Mueller), miosis (dilator), anhidrosis.
 - **Cocaine test:** a Horner pupil **fails to dilate** (no NE present to block reuptake of). Confirms Horner at any level.
 - **Hydroxyamphetamine test:** localizes the lesion. Dilates in **central/preganglionic** lesions (final neuron intact, NE stores releasable); **fails to dilate in postganglionic** lesions (final neuron dead).
- **Adie's tonic pupil** (parasympathetic, ciliary ganglion): constricts to **0.1% pilocarpine** (supersensitive); a normal pupil needs 0.5–1%.

---

### Box 4 — Sympatholytics (adrenergic antagonists): MIOSIS / lower IOP

Two extra properties to know: **ISA** (intrinsic sympathomimetic activity, acts as partial agonist) and **MSA** (membrane-stabilizing activity, local-anesthetic effect).

**Alpha-1 blockers** (effects: miosis, ptosis, conjunctival hyperemia):
- **Dapiprazole 0.5% (Rev-Eyes):** reverses mydriasis from phenylephrine and tropicamide.
- **Thymoxamine 0.5–1%:** orphan drug, reverses 2.5% phenylephrine; no effect on aqueous secretion; helps differentiate glaucoma types.

**Beta blockers** (MOA: decrease aqueous secretion):
- **Non-selective:** Timolol 0.25–0.5% (no ISA/MSA), Levobunolol, Metipranolol, Carteolol. Contraindicated in asthma/COPD and insulin-dependent diabetes.
- **Cardio-selective (safer in asthma):** Betaxolol, Metoprolol.
- Systemic side effects: bradycardia, hypotension (most common), bronchoconstriction (non-selective), masking of hypoglycemia.

## Memory Anchors

- **The 2x2 grid:** agonist/antagonist × sympathetic/parasympathetic. Dilators on the diagonal.
- **Number = action:** 1 constricts, 2 dilates. (α1 mydriasis, α2 cuts aqueous, β2 raises aqueous.)
- **Cycloplegic ladder "A Home So Cool Today":** Atropine, Homatropine, Scopolamine, Cyclopentolate, Tropicamide (strongest to weakest).
- **Adie's = 0.1% pilo; Horner = 0.1% epi.** Both "weak drug works because the muscle is supersensitive."
- **Cocaine confirms Horner, hydroxyamphetamine localizes it.**
- **Suffix decoder:** -olol = beta blocker, -onidine = α2 agonist, -zoline = α1 decongestant, -stigmine = AChE inhibitor, -tropine = antimuscarinic.
- **Iris cysts = echothiopate/DFP; fix with 2.5% phenylephrine.**
- **All mydriatics: avoid in angle-closure glaucoma.**

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** tropicamide is the strongest cycloplegic. Wrong. It is the **weakest and shortest**; atropine is strongest. Cyclopentolate is the routine cycloplegic of choice.
- **Trap:** phenylephrine causes cycloplegia. Wrong. It is α1 only; mydriasis with **no** cycloplegia (ciliary muscle has no α1).
- **Trap:** a Horner pupil dilates with cocaine. Wrong. It **fails** to dilate (no NE to block reuptake of). That failure is the positive test.
- **Trap:** β2 stimulation lowers eye pressure. Wrong. β2 on the ciliary process **increases** aqueous secretion. The IOP-lowering adrenergic targets are α2 (brake) and beta **blockade**.
- **Trap:** Adie's pupil responds to 1% pilocarpine like normal. Careful. It constricts to **0.1%** because of denervation supersensitivity; that low-dose response is the diagnostic feature.
- **Trap:** pilocarpine is contraindicated in angle-closure. Wrong. It is used in angle-closure (it constricts and pulls the iris out of the angle); the contraindication is **malignant glaucoma**. The mydriatics are the ones contraindicated in angle-closure.
- **Trap:** beta blockers are safe in asthma. Wrong for non-selective (timolol). Use cardio-selective (betaxolol) if airway disease.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A drug dilates the pupil but the patient's near vision stays sharp. The drug most likely acts on:

- A. M3 muscarinic receptors (antagonist)
- B. α1 adrenergic receptors (agonist)
- C. β2 receptors (agonist)
- D. Nicotinic receptors

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Mydriasis with intact accommodation means the ciliary muscle was untouched. Only the sympathetic α1 dilator pathway (phenylephrine) does this. M3 blockade (A) would also paralyze the ciliary muscle and cause cycloplegia.
</details>

**2.** A tonic, poorly reactive pupil constricts briskly to 0.1% pilocarpine. The diagnosis is:

- A. Horner's syndrome
- B. Third nerve palsy
- C. Adie's tonic pupil
- D. Argyll Robertson pupil

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Adie's tonic pupil.** Denervation supersensitivity at the ciliary ganglion makes it constrict to a dose (0.1%) too weak to affect a normal pupil. Horner's is a sympathetic lesion tested with cocaine/hydroxyamphetamine.
</details>

**3.** In a suspected Horner's syndrome, which result confirms the diagnosis?

- A. The affected pupil dilates widely with 4% cocaine
- B. The affected pupil fails to dilate with 4% cocaine
- C. Both pupils dilate equally with hydroxyamphetamine
- D. The affected pupil constricts with pilocarpine

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Cocaine blocks NE reuptake, but a Horner eye is not releasing NE, so there is nothing to potentiate and the pupil fails to dilate. Hydroxyamphetamine is then used to localize the lesion level.
</details>

**4.** Which agent is the drug of choice for routine cycloplegic refraction, balancing strength and duration?

- A. Atropine 1%
- B. Tropicamide 1%
- C. Cyclopentolate 1%
- D. Phenylephrine 2.5%

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Cyclopentolate 1%.** It gives reliable cycloplegia with a far shorter duration than atropine (14 days). Tropicamide is too weak a cycloplegic; phenylephrine has none.
</details>

**5.** A glaucoma patient with asthma needs an IOP-lowering beta blocker. The safest choice is:

- A. Timolol (non-selective)
- B. Levobunolol (non-selective)
- C. Betaxolol (cardio-selective)
- D. Carteolol (non-selective)

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Betaxolol.** Cardio-selective (β1) blockers spare the β2 receptors in the airway, so they are safer in asthma and COPD. Non-selective blockers risk bronchoconstriction.
</details>
