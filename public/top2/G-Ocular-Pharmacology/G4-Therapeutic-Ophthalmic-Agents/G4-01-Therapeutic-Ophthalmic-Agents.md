## G4 — Therapeutic Ophthalmic Agents
*Personal study notes | TOS: G Ocular Pharmacology | Source: Ocular Pharmacology, pp.44-66*

---

## The Simple Version

Therapeutic agents treat disease (diagnostic agents only reveal it). Three big jobs dominate the board:

1. **Lower eye pressure** (anti-glaucoma): either make less aqueous or drain more.
2. **Kill the bug** (anti-infectives): antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals.
3. **Calm inflammation** (corticosteroids, NSAIDs, antihistamines).

The glaucoma drugs are the heart of this note. Everything about them comes from one equation: **IOP depends on aqueous made minus aqueous drained.** Every drug either turns the tap down (less secretion) or opens the drain wider (more outflow).

**Teaching angle:** for any glaucoma drug, ask one question: "does this cut production or boost outflow?" Then the side effects and combinations follow.

---

## But Why?

**Why are prostaglandin analogs first-line for glaucoma?**
They open the **uveoscleral** outflow, a second drainage route that bypasses the diseased trabecular meshwork. Once-daily dosing, strong effect, and few systemic risks make them the modern first choice. The price is cosmetic: iris darkening and longer lashes.

**Why do beta blockers and CAIs lower pressure the same way but cause different harms?**
Both cut aqueous **production** at the ciliary body. Beta blockers do it by blocking adenylate cyclase (so they hit the lungs and heart too: asthma and bradycardia). CAIs do it by blocking carbonic anhydrase (so they cause paresthesia, diuresis, and a metallic taste, because the same enzyme works in the kidney). Same goal, different collateral.

**Why does long-term steroid use cause glaucoma and cataract?**
Steroids let glycosaminoglycans pile up in the trabecular meshwork, clogging outflow (steroid-response glaucoma after 4 to 6 weeks; permanent after about 18 months). They also drive posterior subcapsular cataract. So the drug that calms the eye can quietly blind it, which is why steroid courses are monitored.

**Why can a beautiful antibiotic still poison the eye or ear?**
Aminoglycosides are excellent against gram-negative bugs but are ototoxic and nephrotoxic beyond five days. Chloramphenicol risks aplastic anemia. Choosing an antibiotic is always benefit against its specific toxicity.

| Mast-cell stabilizer | blocks degranulation | Cromolyn; prevents calcium influx in allergy |

### Anti-Glaucoma Agents (the core)

**The aqueous equation:** IOP rises from too much secretion or too little outflow (trabecular ~80%, uveoscleral 10 to 20%). Normal IOP 10 to 21 mmHg, glaucoma usually >21.

**Three enzymes that make aqueous** (drug targets): Na/K-ATPase, **carbonic anhydrase** (CAI target), **adenylate cyclase** (beta-blocker target).

**The five medical classes, sorted by mechanism:**

| Class | Example (cap color) | Mechanism | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Prostaglandin analogs** | Latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost (turquoise) | ↑ **uveoscleral** outflow | First-line. S/E: iris heterochromia, longer/darker lashes |
| **Beta blockers** | Timolol (yellow/blue) | ↓ secretion (block adenylate cyclase) | Avoid non-selective in asthma/COPD/IDDM; use betaxolol |
| **Alpha-2 agonists** | Brimonidine, apraclonidine (purple) | ↓ secretion (+ some outflow) | Apraclonidine lowers IOP after laser |
| **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors** | Dorzolamide, brinzolamide; oral acetazolamide (orange) | ↓ secretion (block carbonic anhydrase) | S/E: paresthesia (most common), diuresis, metallic taste, acidosis |
| **Cholinergics / miotics** | Pilocarpine, carbachol (green) | ↑ **trabecular** outflow (ciliary muscle pulls drain open) | Drug-induced myopia, miosis |

**Acetazolamide specifics:** oral CAI, 250 mg every 4 h. S/E: nephrolithiasis, potassium loss, transient myopia (ciliary swelling shallows the chamber), aplastic anemia, metabolic acidosis. Contraindicated in sulfa allergy, Addison's, kidney/liver disease.

**Osmotic agents** (acute angle-closure, pre-op): pull water out by osmotic gradient.
- **Mannitol:** IV, large molecule stays in blood, safe in renal disease, most used.
- **Glycerin:** oral, raises blood glucose (avoid in diabetes).
- **Isosorbide:** oral, safe in diabetes (not metabolized to glucose).
- **Urea:** IV, avoid in renal disease.

**Open angle = treat medically; angle-closure = treat surgically** (laser peripheral iridotomy with YAG/argon). Pilocarpine is used in acute angle-closure to pull the iris out of the angle.

**Gonioscopy grading (Shaffer):**

| Grade | Structures seen | Angle | Closure risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | Ciliary body | 35–45° | Impossible |
| 3 | Scleral spur | 25–35° | Impossible |
| 2 | Trabeculum only | 20° | Possible |
| 1 | Schwalbe's line only | 10° | Probable |
| 0 | Iridocorneal contact | 0° | Closed/imminent |

**The comprehensive glaucoma exam:** Tonometry (pressure), Ophthalmoscopy (disc), Perimetry (field), Gonioscopy (angle), Pachymetry (corneal thickness; thin cornea is a strong risk predictor).

### Anti-Infective Agents

**Antibiotics by mechanism (high yield):**

| Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|
| Inhibit **cell wall** synthesis | Penicillin, cephalosporin, bacitracin, vancomycin |
| Alter cell **membrane** | Polymyxin B |
| Inhibit **protein** synthesis | Aminoglycosides, tetracycline, macrolides, chloramphenicol |
| Inhibit **nucleic acid** (DNA gyrase) | Quinolones, rifampicin |
| Block **folic acid** (antimetabolite) | Sulfonamides |

**Ocular pearls:**
- **Tetracycline:** drug of choice for **trachoma** and inclusion conjunctivitis. Avoid in children and pregnancy (teeth, bone).
- **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, tobramycin, neomycin): gram-negative coverage; **ototoxic and nephrotoxic** beyond 5 days. Neomycin also covers Acanthamoeba.
- **Fluoroquinolones** (cipro, oflox, levoflox): broad; avoid in children/pregnancy (cartilage).
- **Chloramphenicol:** DOC for typhoid; risk of aplastic anemia.
- **Macrolides** (erythromycin): for penicillin-allergic patients.

**Antifungals:** inhibit ergosterol synthesis. **Natamycin** (polyene) is the topical workhorse for filamentary fungi (Fusarium, Aspergillus); amphotericin B (most potent, IV) for systemic; fungi are linked to contact-lens solution contamination.

**Antivirals:** topical **trifluridine, vidarabine, acyclovir** for herpes/VZV; IV **ganciclovir/foscarnet** and intravitreal **fomivirsen** for CMV retinitis. Idoxuridine was the first approved antiviral.

**Antimalarial ocular toxicity (chloroquine/hydroxychloroquine):** corneal verticillata (whorl deposits), **bull's-eye maculopathy**, halos, color (tritan) defects, decreased VA (earliest sign).

**Anti-TB ocular toxicity:** **ethambutol and isoniazid cause optic neuritis**; streptomycin is ototoxic/nephrotoxic.

### Anti-Inflammatory Agents

**Corticosteroids** (the big one):
- **MOA:** inhibit **phospholipase A2** → less prostaglandins AND leukotrienes (upstream of NSAIDs).
- **Anterior segment disease:** topical is most effective. **Posterior segment:** systemic, periocular, or intravitreal.
- **Ocular side effects (must know):** steroid-response **open-angle glaucoma** (4 to 6 weeks; permanent after ~18 months), **posterior subcapsular cataract**, secondary infection (fungal, herpes), delayed healing, mydriasis, ptosis.
- **Contraindications:** herpetic/viral keratitis, fungal disease, untreated infection, post-FB removal (delays healing).
- **Potency ladder:** cortisone (least) < hydrocortisone < prednisone/prednisolone (10x cortisone) < dexamethasone/betamethasone (30 to 50x). **Fluorometholone** has the least IOP rise, so it is the safer steroid in glaucoma-prone eyes.
- **Systemic side effects:** Cushingoid moon face, osteoporosis, PUD, hyperglycemia, immunosuppression, mood change.

**NSAIDs:** inhibit **cyclooxygenase** → less prostaglandins. Effects: reduce surgical miosis, stabilize blood-aqueous barrier, no IOP change, can worsen herpes. Examples: ketorolac (allergic conjunctivitis), diclofenac, flurbiprofen.

**Antihistamines / mast-cell stabilizers:** block H1 (levocabastine) or stabilize mast cells (**cromolyn sodium 4%**, prevents calcium influx and degranulation) for vernal/atopic/seasonal allergic conjunctivitis.

## Memory Anchors

- **The aqueous equation:** IOP = made − drained. Every glaucoma drug cuts production or boosts outflow.
- **Cut production (3):** Beta blockers, Alpha-2 agonists, CAIs. **Boost outflow (2):** Prostaglandins (uveoscleral), Miotics (trabecular). "Three down, two out."
- **Cap colors:** Red mydriatic, Green miotic, Yellow/blue beta blocker, Orange CAI, Turquoise prostaglandin, Purple adrenergic, Pink steroid.
- **CAI = carbonic anhydrase = kidney too** (paresthesia, diuresis, metallic taste, stones).
- **Steroid eye = glaucoma + posterior subcapsular cataract.** Fluorometholone is the safe one.
- **Antibiotic toxicities:** aminoglycoside = ears/kidney; chloramphenicol = aplastic anemia; ethambutol = optic neuritis; chloroquine = bull's-eye macula.
- **Tetracycline = trachoma.**
- **Steroids work upstream (phospholipase A2); NSAIDs work downstream (COX).**

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** beta blockers lower IOP by increasing outflow. Wrong. They **decrease production** (block adenylate cyclase).
- **Trap:** prostaglandins drain through the trabecular meshwork. Wrong. They open the **uveoscleral** route.
- **Trap:** acetazolamide is safe in sulfa allergy. Wrong. It is a sulfonamide; avoid in sulfa allergy.
- **Trap:** steroids and NSAIDs share the same enzyme target. Wrong. Steroids block phospholipase A2 (upstream, also stops leukotrienes); NSAIDs block COX only.
- **Trap:** the safest steroid for an eye at glaucoma risk is dexamethasone. Wrong. **Fluorometholone** raises IOP least.
- **Trap:** angle-closure glaucoma is treated medically first. Wrong. Angle-closure is a surgical emergency (laser iridotomy); medical drops are temporizing.
- **Trap:** chloroquine's earliest sign is the bull's-eye. Careful. The earliest functional sign is **decreased VA / color change**; the bull's-eye is the classic late fundus finding.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Which glaucoma drug class lowers IOP by increasing uveoscleral outflow?

- A. Beta blockers
- B. Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors
- C. Prostaglandin analogs
- D. Alpha-2 agonists

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Prostaglandin analogs.** They open the uveoscleral route and are first-line. The other three all reduce aqueous production.
</details>

**2.** A patient on long-term topical dexamethasone develops gradually rising IOP and a posterior subcapsular cataract. The mechanism of the pressure rise is:

- A. Increased aqueous production
- B. Glycosaminoglycan accumulation in the trabecular meshwork
- C. Pupillary block
- D. Ciliary body swelling

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Steroids cause GAG buildup in the trabecular meshwork, reducing outflow (steroid-response glaucoma after 4 to 6 weeks). Posterior subcapsular cataract is the classic lens side effect.
</details>

**3.** Which antibiotic is the drug of choice for trachoma?

- A. Gentamicin
- B. Tetracycline
- C. Penicillin
- D. Polymyxin B

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Tetracycline.** It is the DOC for trachoma and inclusion conjunctivitis. Avoid in children and pregnancy.
</details>

**4.** A glaucoma patient with a sulfa allergy should avoid which agent?

- A. Timolol
- B. Latanoprost
- C. Acetazolamide
- D. Pilocarpine

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Acetazolamide.** It is a sulfonamide (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor), contraindicated in sulfa allergy. The others are unrelated chemically.
</details>

**5.** Which steroid is preferred when an anti-inflammatory is needed in an eye prone to high IOP?

- A. Dexamethasone
- B. Betamethasone
- C. Prednisolone
- D. Fluorometholone

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**D. Fluorometholone.** It produces the least IOP elevation, making it the safer choice in steroid responders and glaucoma-prone eyes.
</details>
