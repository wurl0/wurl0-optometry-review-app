## G5 — Special Populations and Ocular Side Effects of Systemic Drugs
*Personal study notes | TOS: G Ocular Pharmacology | Source: Ocular Pharmacology, Dr. Ado pp.84-91*

---

## The Simple Version

This note has two halves:

1. **Systemic drugs that hurt the eye.** A patient swallows a pill for the heart, lungs, or joints, and it leaves a fingerprint somewhere in the eye: cornea, lens, retina, optic nerve, or color vision. The board loves to give you the drug and ask for the finding (or the reverse). These are pattern-matching points, almost free if you drill the pairs.
2. **Special populations.** Pregnancy, children, and the elderly change which drug you reach for, because their bodies handle drugs differently.

**Teaching angle:** for the toxicity half, do not memorize long lists. Anchor on the **signature pairs** (chloroquine = bull's-eye macula, digitalis = yellow vision, ethambutol = optic neuritis). The classic exam item is a single drug and a single finding.

---

## But Why?

**Why is tropicamide the preferred dilator in pregnancy?**
All ocular agents are FDA Category C (risk not ruled out). Tropicamide has the shortest duration and least systemic absorption, so when you must dilate, it exposes mother and fetus the least. Always add nasolacrimal occlusion. (See [[G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles]] and [[F1-01-DPA-Examination]].)

**Why do children react badly to cyclopentolate, and the elderly to many systemic drugs?**
Children have an immature liver, so a normal dose builds up and reaches the brain (cyclopentolate causes hallucinations and seizures, especially at 2%). The elderly have a declining liver and kidney, so drugs clear slowly and accumulate. Both are dosing problems caused by the same organ: a metabolizing system that is either not ready or wearing out.

**Why do so many drugs cause cornea verticillata (a whorl pattern)?**
Amphiphilic drugs (chloroquine, amiodarone, indomethacin, tamoxifen) bind lipids and get trapped in the basal corneal epithelium, where they deposit in a golden-brown whorl that follows the cornea's natural cell-migration spiral. Different drugs, one mechanism, one pattern.

**Why do anticholinergic drugs trigger acute angle-closure?**
They dilate the pupil. In a narrow angle, dilation bunches the iris into the drain and blocks outflow. So antihistamines, tricyclics, and motion-sickness scopolamine can all precipitate an attack in a susceptible eye.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Cornea verticillata | whorl keratopathy | Golden whorl from amphiphilic drugs (CATI) |
| Xanthopsia | Greek *xanthos* = yellow | Yellow vision; the classic digitalis effect |
| Bull's-eye maculopathy | ring of RPE depigmentation | Classic late chloroquine retinal finding |
| Pseudotumor cerebri | raised ICP, no mass | Papilledema from vitamin A excess, OCPs, tetracycline |
| Amphiphilic drug | lipid- and water-loving | Binds lipids; deposits in cornea (verticillata) |
| FDA Category C | risk not ruled out | The class of all ocular drugs in pregnancy |
| Idiosyncratic reaction | illogical response | e.g. a mydriatic paradoxically causing miosis |
| Tritan defect | blue-yellow color loss | Earliest color change in several drug toxicities |
| Polypharmacy | many concurrent drugs | Raises interaction/toxicity risk in the elderly |
| Retrobulbar neuritis | inflammation behind the globe | Ethambutol / INH optic toxicity pattern |

### Ocular Side Effects by Structure

**Cornea:**
- **Cornea verticillata (whorl keratopathy):** chloroquine, **amiodarone** (anterior deposits), indomethacin, tamoxifen, chlorpromazine, suramin.
- Band keratopathy: excess vitamin D.
- Gold salts: chrysiasis. Deferoxamine: corneal discoloration.

**Lens (cataract):**
- **Corticosteroids:** posterior subcapsular cataract.
- **Phenothiazines** (thioridazine, chlorpromazine): stellate **anterior** subcapsular deposits; also corneal endothelial dusting.
- Statins (lovastatin), strong miotics.

**Retina:**
- **Chloroquine / hydroxychloroquine:** bull's-eye maculopathy.
- Phenothiazines, indomethacin: pigmentary retinopathy, cystoid macular edema.
- Tamoxifen: crystalline retinopathy.
- Oral contraceptives: vascular occlusion (CRAO, CRVO).
- Quinine: vessel attenuation. Niacin: cystoid macular edema.

**Optic nerve:**
- **Ethambutol, isoniazid (INH), chloramphenicol, streptomycin:** optic/retrobulbar neuritis and atrophy.
- Lithium, oral contraceptives, amiodarone, **excess vitamin A:** papilledema / pseudotumor cerebri.
- Ethanol: toxic amblyopia (VA loss, tritan defect, centrocecal scotoma).

**Color vision:**
- **Digitalis: xanthopsia** (yellow/"snowy" vision).
- Amiodarone: colored halos.
- **Ethambutol, INH: red-green defect.**
- Indomethacin, chloroquine, phenothiazine: tritan (blue-yellow, earliest, hits ganglion cells).

**IOP and angle:**
- **Steroids:** open-angle glaucoma (GAG buildup in trabecular meshwork).
- **Anticholinergics, antihistamines, tricyclics, benzodiazepines, scopolamine, amphetamines:** acute angle-closure with pupillary block.
- Drugs that **lower** IOP: beta blockers, diuretics, CAIs, marijuana.

**Refraction shift:**
- **Transient myopia:** sulfonamides, tetracycline, acetazolamide, steroids, hydrochlorothiazide (ciliary body edema shifts the lens forward).
- **Hyperopia:** oral hypoglycemic agents (fluid uptake lowers lens index).

**Surface and contact lens:**
- **Dry eye:** oral retinoids, antihistamines, anticholinergics, diuretics.
- **Blepharoconjunctivitis:** isotretinoin, amiodarone, gold salts, methotrexate, rifampin.
- **Contact lens intolerance (CLI):** isotretinoin (8 to 10%), vitamin A, antihistamines, tricyclics, oral contraceptives, phenothiazines.

### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:**
- All ocular drugs are FDA **Category C**.
- If dilation is required: **tropicamide** (shortest duration, least exposure) + nasolacrimal occlusion.
- Avoid phenylephrine 10% (cardiovascular), tetracycline/fluoroquinolones (fetal bone/cartilage).

**Pediatric:**
- Immature liver → systemic toxicity. **Cyclopentolate** CNS effects (hallucination, seizure) at 2%; use **0.5 to 1%** and press the lacrimal sac.
- Atropine is used for cycloplegic refraction and amblyopia penalization but watch for systemic anticholinergic toxicity (flush, fever, tachycardia).

**Geriatric:**
- Declining liver and kidney → slowed clearance, drug accumulation, higher toxicity risk.
- More polypharmacy → more drug interactions and more of the systemic toxicities above (chronic amiodarone, statins, diuretics).
- Higher baseline angle-closure risk; be cautious dilating.

**Idiosyncratic / sensitivity terms** (from [[G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles]]): women carry a higher unexplained toxicity risk; idiosyncratic reactions are illogical (a mydriatic causing miosis).

## Memory Anchors

- **Signature pairs first.** Chloroquine = bull's-eye; digitalis = yellow; ethambutol = optic neuritis; amiodarone = halos + whorls; steroid = cataract + glaucoma.
- **Cornea verticillata "CATI":** Chloroquine, Amiodarone, Tamoxifen, Indomethacin.
- **Lower IOP drugs:** Beta blockers, Diuretics, CAIs, Marijuana.
- **Special populations = one organ story:** immature liver (kids), failing liver/kidney (elderly), shared fetus (pregnancy).
- **Anticholinergic = dilation = angle-closure risk.**
- **Xanthopsia = digitalis** (think Van Gogh's yellow period, a digitalis legend).

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** amiodarone whorl keratopathy means the drug must be stopped. Usually not. The corneal deposits are common, dose-related, and reversible; the dangerous amiodarone effect to watch is **optic neuropathy**.
- **Trap:** chloroquine's earliest sign is the bull's-eye. The bull's-eye is the classic **late** macular finding; early change is reduced VA and color (tritan).
- **Trap:** steroids cause anterior subcapsular cataract. Wrong. Steroids cause **posterior** subcapsular cataract; **phenothiazines** cause the anterior stellate one.
- **Trap:** ethambutol causes a blue-yellow defect. Wrong. Ethambutol/INH classically cause a **red-green** defect.
- **Trap:** all dilators are equally safe in pregnancy. Wrong. Tropicamide is preferred; phenylephrine 10% is avoided.
- **Trap:** digitalis causes blue vision. Wrong. Digitalis causes **xanthopsia** (yellow).

## Practice Questions

**1.** A patient on long-term hydroxychloroquine for lupus reports trouble with central vision. The classic fundus finding is:

- A. Cherry-red spot
- B. Bull's-eye maculopathy
- C. Cotton-wool spots
- D. Disc neovascularization

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Bull's-eye maculopathy.** Chloroquine and hydroxychloroquine cause a ring of RPE depigmentation around a spared center. Early change is reduced VA and tritan color loss.
</details>

**2.** A cardiac patient on digitalis complains that everything looks yellow ("snowy"). This is:

- A. Cyanopsia
- B. Erythropsia
- C. Xanthopsia
- D. Chloropsia

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Xanthopsia.** A yellow tinge to vision is the classic digitalis effect.
</details>

**3.** A TB patient on ethambutol reports dimming vision and trouble telling red from green. The toxicity is:

- A. Optic neuritis with red-green defect
- B. Cataract
- C. Angle-closure glaucoma
- D. Pigmentary retinopathy

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**A.** Ethambutol (and INH) cause optic neuritis/neuropathy with a red-green color defect. This is a reason to monitor color vision and fields in patients on anti-TB therapy.
</details>

**4.** Which drug is preferred when a pregnant patient requires pupil dilation?

- A. Phenylephrine 10%
- B. Atropine 1%
- C. Tropicamide 0.5%
- D. Cyclopentolate 2%

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Tropicamide 0.5%.** Shortest duration and least systemic exposure among Category C agents. Add nasolacrimal occlusion.
</details>

**5.** A child given 2% cyclopentolate becomes disoriented and hallucinates. The cause is:

- A. Allergic reaction
- B. Systemic anticholinergic CNS toxicity from immature hepatic metabolism
- C. Angle-closure glaucoma
- D. Local anesthetic overdose

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** A child's immature liver lets the drug accumulate and cross into the CNS, producing anticholinergic toxicity. Use 0.5 to 1% and apply lacrimal sac pressure.
</details>

## Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue → rule → trap`.

**Autonomic pupil effect** — cue: agonist/antagonist + system.
- Number rule: **1 constricts, 2 dilates** (α1 mydriasis, α2 cuts aqueous, β2 raises aqueous). Dilate w/o cycloplegia = sympathomimetic; dilate + cycloplegia = parasympatholytic; constrict = parasympathomimetic.
- Trap: "β2 stimulation lowers IOP" — β2 **increases** aqueous.

**Cycloplegic strength** — cue: strongest/longest vs routine.
- Atropine = strongest/longest (14 d); tropicamide = weakest/shortest; cyclopentolate = routine DOC.
- Trap: "tropicamide is the strongest cycloplegic."

**Glaucoma drug mechanism** — cue: cut production vs boost outflow.
- Cut production (3): β-blockers, α2 agonists, CAIs. Boost outflow (2): prostaglandins (**uveoscleral**), miotics (**trabecular**).
- Trap: "β-blockers increase outflow" or "prostaglandins drain trabecular."

**Pupil-test pairs** — cue: cocaine / hydroxyamphetamine / pilocarpine.
- Cocaine **confirms** Horner (fails to dilate); hydroxyamphetamine **localizes**; 0.1% pilocarpine constricts an **Adie's** pupil.
- Trap: a Horner pupil "dilates with cocaine" — it fails to.

**Drug → ocular sign** — cue: the drug named.
- Chloroquine = bull's-eye (late); digitalis = xanthopsia (yellow); ethambutol/INH = optic neuritis + red-green; steroid = PSC cataract + glaucoma.
- Trap: "ethambutol = blue-yellow defect" — it's **red-green**.
