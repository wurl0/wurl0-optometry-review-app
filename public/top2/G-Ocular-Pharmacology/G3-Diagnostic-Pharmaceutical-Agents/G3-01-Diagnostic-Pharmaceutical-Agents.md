## G3 — Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agents (Anesthetics & Dyes)
*Personal study notes | TOS: G Ocular Pharmacology | Source: Ocular Pharmacology, Dr. Ado pp.34-43*

---

## The Simple Version

Diagnostic agents are drugs you use to *see* something, not to treat. They fall into three families:

1. **Mydriatics and cycloplegics** (to open the pupil and study the fundus or refraction). Covered in detail in [[G2-01-Autonomic-Drugs]].
2. **Topical anesthetics** (to numb the cornea so you can touch it: tonometry, foreign body, gonioscopy).
3. **Dyes** (to stain and reveal: epithelial defects, tear film, leaks, drainage).

This note focuses on the anesthetics and dyes. The single most testable idea is matching the right dye to what it stains, and the right anesthetic to the right procedure.

**Teaching angle:** anesthetics work by one mechanism (block the sodium channel). Dyes differ by one question: does it stain *living* defects (fluorescein) or *dead/devitalized* cells and mucus (rose bengal, lissamine)? Sort every dye by that question.

---

## But Why?

**Why does an anesthetic fail in an infected, inflamed eye?**
Local anesthetics are weak bases. They cross the nerve membrane only in the **uncharged (non-ionized)** form. Infected tissue is acidic, which pushes the drug into its charged form, so less enters the nerve and the block is weak. This is why you cannot reliably numb an abscess.

**Why does fluorescein glow only where the epithelium is broken?**
Intact epithelium is a lipid barrier the water-soluble dye cannot enter. Where cells are missing, the dye floods the intercellular spaces and the stroma, then fluoresces yellow-green under cobalt blue. No defect, no stain.

**Why use rose bengal before fluorescein in a dry eye workup?**
Rose bengal stains devitalized cells and mucus (the hallmark of dry eye) and is read in red light without fluorescence. Fluorescein would flood the tear film and obscure the subtle devitalized-cell pattern, so you stain with rose bengal first.

**Why does proparacaine sting so little and act so fast?**
It is an ester with very low tissue irritation and poor corneal penetration, so it numbs the surface in seconds and wears off in about 20 minutes without deep effects. That speed and gentleness make it the everyday tonometry drop.

### Topical Anesthetics

**Mechanism:** block the **sodium (Na+) channel** on sensory nerves, stopping depolarization and pain conduction. Two theories: specific receptor (block the channel) and membrane expansion (insert into the membrane and deform the channel). Calcium normally regulates Na+ movement, so anesthetics also compete with calcium.

**Ester vs Amide (count the letter "i" in the name before -caine):**

| Feature | Esters (one "i") | Amides (two "i"s) |
|---|---|---|
| Examples | Co**c**aine, Pro**c**aine, Tetra**c**aine, Benzo**c**aine, Proparacaine, Benoxinate | L**i**doca**i**ne, Bup**i**vaca**i**ne, Mep**i**vaca**i**ne, Pr**i**loca**i**ne |
| Metabolized by | Plasma pseudo-cholinesterase | Liver |
| Toxicity / allergy | Less toxic, less allergic | More potent, longer acting |
| Duration | Shorter | Longer |

**The four topical agents to know:**

| Agent | Conc | Key facts |
|---|---|---|
| **Proparacaine** | 0.5% | Most used. Onset 5–15 sec, duration ~20 min. Least irritating/allergic. Tonometry, gonioscopy, minor procedures |
| **Tetracaine** | 0.5% | 10x as potent as cocaine, longer acting, more stinging. FB removal, corneal scraping, ERG |
| **Benoxinate** | 0.4% | Combined with fluorescein (Fluress) for applanation. Has antibacterial property |
| **Cocaine** | 2–10% | The only natural anesthetic; also mydriatic + vasoconstrictor. Diagnoses **Horner's** (4–10%). NOT used for tonometry (softens cornea, false low reading). Addictive |

**Key uses by procedure:** Goldmann applanation = proparacaine or benoxinate. ERG = tetracaine. Forced duction test = cocaine or lidocaine. Corneal epithelial debridement = cocaine.

**Injectable note:** add **epinephrine** (1:100,000–1:200,000) to prolong the block by vasoconstriction; add **bicarbonate** to raise the non-ionized fraction and speed absorption; add **hyaluronidase** (spreading factor) to widen tissue spread.

**Side effects:** mild stinging/burning (most common), shortened TBUT, reduced reflex tearing and blinking, slowed epithelial healing. **Never prescribe for home use** (masks pain → corneal damage, see [[F1-01-DPA-Examination]]). Systemic toxicity is CNS (excitation then seizures, the most serious) and CVS (arrhythmia, hypotension).

### Diagnostic Dyes

**The organizing question: what does it stain?**
- **Fluorescein** stains **epithelial defects** (where cells are missing).
- **Rose bengal, lissamine, methylene blue** stain **devitalized cells and mucus** (dead/dying tissue).
- **Trypan blue, ICG** stain **endothelium** (used in surgery).

**Fluorescein** (the workhorse):
- Low molecular weight (376). Orange crystal, yellow in solution. Fluoresces **yellow-green under cobalt blue (465–490 nm absorption)** in weak alkali.
- Stains epithelial defects bright green; will **not** stain dead cells or mucus.
- **Stains soft contact lenses** (remove them first); promotes *Pseudomonas* growth.
- **Quenching phenomenon:** fluorescence drops above pH 8 or above ~0.001% concentration.
- Uses: TBUT, applanation tonometry, RGP fit pattern, Seidel test, Jones test, IV fluorescein angiography (retinal/choroidal vessels).

**Diagnostic tests built on fluorescein:**
- **TBUT:** time for the first dry spot to appear in the tear film.
- **Seidel test:** detects aqueous leak from a wound (dye streams green against the yellow-orange background).
- **Jones I:** dye reaches the nose = patent drainage. **Jones II:** irrigate after a negative Jones I; dye recovered = partial block at duct; no dye = block at canaliculi/puncta.

**The dye comparison table (memorize this grid):**

| Dye | Epithelial defect | Devitalized cells / mucus | Endothelium | Extra property |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Fluorescein** | ✓ | – | – | – |
| **Fluorexon** | ✓ | ✓ | – | High MW, safe on soft CL |
| **Rose Bengal** | – | ✓ | – | Antiviral; use **before** fluorescein |
| **Indocyanine green** | – | – | ✓ | IV; choroidal angiography (805 nm) |
| **Methylene blue** | – | ✓ | – | Antibacterial; stains corneal nerves |
| **Trypan blue** | – | ✓ | ✓ | Stains anterior capsule for capsulorhexis |
| **Lissamine green** | – | ✓ | – | Antiviral; screens vitamin A deficiency |
| **Argyrol** | – | ✓ | – | Checks pre-op scrub adequacy |

- **Rose bengal:** brilliant red, seen with the naked eye, stains nucleus more than cytoplasm. Best for early **KCS / Sjogren's** and herpetic dendrites. Stings (the anesthetic added to ease it can itself kill cells and give a false positive).
- **Indocyanine green:** protein-bound, infrared, better for choroidal neovascular membranes than fluorescein.
- **Trypan blue:** anionic, no side effects; checks endothelial integrity for grafting and stains the capsule in phaco.
- **Lissamine green:** gentler alternative to rose bengal; screens xerophthalmia.

## Memory Anchors

- **Anesthetic mechanism:** "block the Na channel, stop the pain signal."
- **Ester vs amide:** count the i's. One i = ester (cocaine, procaine, tetracaine, proparacaine). Two i's = amide (lidocaine, bupivacaine).
- **Dye sorting:** fluorescein = the *living* gap; rose bengal/lissamine = the *dead* cells; trypan/ICG = the *endothelium*.
- **"Rose before fluorescein"** in a dry eye workup.
- **Cocaine: numbs, dilates, and diagnoses Horner's,** but never for tonometry (softens cornea).
- **Fluorescein + soft CL = permanent stain. Remove first.**
- **Jones I to the nose; Jones II irrigate to localize.**

---

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** fluorescein stains dead cells. Wrong. It stains **epithelial defects** (missing cells); rose bengal and lissamine stain dead/devitalized cells.
- **Trap:** cocaine is a good tonometry anesthetic. Wrong. It softens the cornea and gives a falsely low IOP. Use proparacaine or benoxinate.
- **Trap:** anesthetics work better in infected tissue because of more blood flow. Wrong. Acidic infected tissue ionizes the drug and **weakens** the block.
- **Trap:** rose bengal is read under cobalt blue like fluorescein. Wrong. It is a red dye seen in white light; it does not depend on fluorescence the way fluorescein does.
- **Trap:** apply fluorescein first, then rose bengal. Wrong order for dry eye. **Rose bengal first**, then fluorescein.
- **Trap:** amides are metabolized by plasma cholinesterase. Wrong. Amides are metabolized by the **liver**; esters by plasma pseudo-cholinesterase.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A topical anesthetic fails to numb a heavily infected, inflamed cornea. The best explanation is:

- A. Infection destroys the sodium channels
- B. The acidic tissue ionizes the drug, so less enters the nerve
- C. Inflammation washes the drug away too quickly
- D. Bacteria metabolize the anesthetic

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Anesthetics cross the nerve in their uncharged form. Acidic (infected) tissue shifts the weak base to its charged form, reducing penetration and weakening the block.
</details>

**2.** Which dye is best for detecting the devitalized cells of early keratoconjunctivitis sicca?

- A. Fluorescein
- B. Trypan blue
- C. Rose bengal
- D. Indocyanine green

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Rose bengal.** It stains devitalized cells and mucus, the early marker of dry eye/Sjogren's. Fluorescein stains epithelial *defects*, not dead cells; ICG and trypan blue are for endothelium/surgery.
</details>

**3.** For Goldmann applanation tonometry, the appropriate anesthetic and dye combination is:

- A. Cocaine and rose bengal
- B. Proparacaine (or benoxinate) and fluorescein
- C. Tetracaine and ICG
- D. Lidocaine and lissamine green

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Applanation needs a fast gentle anesthetic (proparacaine or benoxinate, the latter pre-mixed with fluorescein as Fluress) plus fluorescein to define the tear meniscus and applanation area. Cocaine is contraindicated (softens cornea).
</details>

**4.** A patient has a post-cataract wound. You instill fluorescein and see a green stream flowing from the incision against a yellow-orange background. This is a positive:

- A. Jones I test
- B. Seidel test
- C. TBUT
- D. Schirmer test

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Seidel test.** A streaming green flow of diluted aqueous against the concentrated dye background confirms a wound leak.
</details>

**5.** Which statement about ester versus amide anesthetics is correct?

- A. Amides are metabolized by plasma cholinesterase
- B. Esters have two "i"s in their generic name
- C. Esters are metabolized by plasma pseudo-cholinesterase and are less allergenic per dose
- D. Amides are shorter acting than esters

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** Esters (one "i") are broken down by plasma pseudo-cholinesterase and tend to be less toxic per the source. Amides (two "i"s) are liver-metabolized and longer acting.
</details>
