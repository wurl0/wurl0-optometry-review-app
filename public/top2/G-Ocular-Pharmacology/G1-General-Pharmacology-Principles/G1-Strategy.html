G1 — Strategy, Rules & Mnemonics

Personal study notes · General Pharmacology Principles · the extra read before you test

The one idea that organizes everything

Sort every fact into one of two buckets. Kinetics = what the body does to the drug (ADME). Dynamics = what the drug does to the body (receptor + effect). If you can place a fact, you understand it.
ADME = Absorption → Distribution → Metabolism → Excretion. The drug as a traveler: gets in, spreads out, gets broken down, leaves.

Core rules to never miss

Liver metabolizes, kidney excretes. Liver uses cytochrome P-450 to make drugs water-soluble; kidney flushes them out.
Lipid = Uncharged = Non-ionized = Alkaline = Absorbable. One chain. The body absorbs the uncharged form across membranes.
The cornea needs both. Epithelium is lipid (needs lipid-soluble drug), stroma is water (needs water-soluble drug). A good eye drug is biphasic. Too lipophilic gets stuck.
0.693 is ln 2. It is the math meaning of "half," not a random constant. k = 0.693 / t½.

Mnemonics

Potency vs Efficacy: "Potency = Position (how far left on the dose axis, low dose). Efficacy = Elevation (how high the ceiling)." Potent ≠ effective.
Drug combinations: Additive = 1+1=2. Synergism = 1+1>2. Potentiation = 0+1>1 (a dud boosts a real drug).
Bonds: "Covalent = Committed (irreversible, strongest). Van der Waals = Wavering (weakest, reversible, most drugs)."
Rx parts in order: "Please Picture Doctors Saying I Sell Sealed Recipes" → Practitioner, Patient, Date, Superscription, Inscription, Subscription, Sig, Refill.
Controlled schedules: Number up, abuse down. I = Illegal (heroin, LSD, no medical use). V = Very low (loperamide).

Board traps (the wrong answers they bait you with)

Potency = efficacy? No. Potency is dose needed; efficacy is max effect. Independent.
IV undergoes absorption? No. IV, IA, IC skip absorption. 100% bioavailable, zero-order.
Liver excretes? No. Liver metabolizes/eliminates; kidney excretes.
More lipid = better eye penetration? No. Too lipophilic stays trapped in epithelium.
Competitive antagonism is permanent? No. Competitive is reversible (beaten by more agonist). Non-competitive (covalent) is permanent.
Schedule I can be prescribed? No. No accepted medical use.

Exam-room strategy for this subject

  • Spot the keyword. "Reaches circulation" = bioavailability. "Unexpectedly low dose works" = hypersensitivity. "Few doses then tolerance" = tachyphylaxis. Tag the keyword first.
  • Definitions are free points. G1 is heavy on vocabulary (potency, efficacy, posology, pharmacognosy). Lock these cold; they are the easiest marks in the whole subject.
  • When a question gives numbers, it usually wants a formula (half-life, TI, Vd) or a schedule rule (72 hours, 6 months, 5 refills). Memorize those exact figures.
  • Latin abbreviations show up. Know b.i.d, t.i.d, q.i.d, p.r.n, h.s, gtt, O.D./O.S./O.U., a.c., p.o.
  • Tie it to the eye. Every principle has an ocular example in the source (atropine excretion, tropicamide dosing every 5 min, corneal biphasic penetration). The board loves the ocular version.
