G1 — General Pharmacology Principles

The Simple Version

Before you can understand any eye drop, you need the rules that apply to every drug. Pharmacology splits into two halves that mirror each other:
  • Pharmacokinetics is what the body does to the drug. Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion (ADME). Think of the drug as a traveler: how it gets in, where it goes, how it gets broken down, how it leaves.
  • Pharmacodynamics is what the drug does to the body. Mechanism of action and the effect it produces, almost always by binding a receptor.

Everything else in this note (routes, dosing, half-life, therapeutic index, the prescription, the controlled-substance schedules) hangs off those two ideas.

Teaching angle: ask "what the body does to the drug, and what the drug does to the body." If you can sort any fact into one of those two buckets, you understand where it belongs.

But Why?

Why split pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics at all?

Because they answer different questions. Kinetics decides how much drug reaches the target and for how long. Dynamics decides what happens once it gets there. A drug can have a perfect mechanism (good dynamics) and still fail because it never reaches the eye in useful amounts (bad kinetics). Both must work.

Why does the liver matter so much?

The liver is the main site of metabolism. It chemically rewrites drugs, usually turning fat-soluble drugs into water-soluble metabolites the kidney can flush out. This is also why the route of administration matters: anything swallowed passes through the liver first (first-pass effect) and loses some potency before it ever reaches the blood.

Why are most drugs weak bases or weak acids?

Because the body absorbs the uncharged (non-ionized) form across lipid membranes. Lipid-soluble equals uncharged equals absorbable. The pH of the environment decides how much of the drug is in that absorbable form. This is the single idea behind absorption, corneal penetration, and even why you alkalinize urine to dump a barbiturate overdose.

Why do we care about half-life?

Because it tells you how often to dose and how long an effect lasts. It is the bridge between a chemistry fact and a clinical schedule.

Pharmacokinetics — ADME

1. Absorption — how the drug gets into the blood

Five transport mechanisms:

  • Main factor affecting diffusion is molecular size (then shape, then charge).
  • Lipid-soluble = uncharged = non-ionized = non-polar = alkaline form. Examples: alkaloids, anesthetics, weak bases, corticosteroids.
  • Extremely lipophilic drugs get trapped in the corneal epithelium and release slowly, so too much lipid solubility also hurts penetration. The eye needs a drug that is both lipid- and water-soluble to cross all corneal layers (epithelium = lipid, stroma = water).

First-pass effect: gastric acid plus liver metabolism degrade an oral drug before it reaches circulation. Routes that bypass it: sublingual, buccal, rectal (partially), and all parenteral routes.

2. Distribution — where the drug goes

Drug travels in two forms:

  • Free / unbound = active, reaches the target, produces effect.
  • Bound (to albumin) = inactive reservoir, stays in blood until released or excreted.

Only the free fraction works. Barriers that limit distribution: blood-brain barrier and blood-ocular barrier.

3. Metabolism / Biotransformation — mostly in the liver

Goal: make the drug more polar (water-soluble) so the kidney can excrete it. Enzyme system: hepatic microsomal cytochrome P-450.

PhaseWhat happensReactions
Phase IUnmask or add a polar group (OH, SH, NH₂)Oxidation (most common, via P-450), Reduction, Hydrolysis
Phase IIConjugate the drug to a polar molecule (glucuronate, sulfate, glycine, acetate, methyl, glutathione)Conjugation

Organs of metabolism: liver (main), kidneys, lungs, adrenal glands.

4. Excretion — how the drug leaves

Main organ: kidneys (glomerular filtration + tubular secretion). Minor routes: liver/bile, lungs, GIT, and glands (lacrimal, salivary, sweat, sebaceous, mammary). The mammary route is why nursing mothers need caution.

  • Atropine is excreted unchanged in urine, which is why a urine test for atropine toxicity exists (the "cat eye test").
  • Penicillin is not metabolized by the liver; it is removed by renal excretion.

Formula Breakdowns

Half-life (t½) and the elimination constant

k = 0.693 / t½

  1. Plain language: half-life is the time for the plasma drug level to fall by 50%. The number 0.693 links half-life to the elimination rate constant k.
  2. The parts: t½ = half-life (time). k (or Ke) = elimination rate constant (fraction removed per unit time). 0.693 = the natural log of 2 (ln 2).
  3. Where it comes from: drug elimination is usually first-order (exponential decay). Solving "half remains" in an exponential gives ln 2 = 0.693. So 0.693 is not arbitrary, it is what "half" equals in exponential math.
  4. Analogy: like a phone battery that always loses half its remaining charge in the same time, no matter how full it started.
  5. Why this form: writing k = 0.693/t½ lets you convert an easily measured half-life into the rate constant used in clearance and dosing math.
  6. Limits: only valid for first-order kinetics. Zero-order drugs (alcohol, phenytoin at high dose) eliminate at a constant amount, not a constant fraction, so half-life is not fixed.
  7. Cross-subject link: same exponential decay math as radioactive isotopes in physics and as light absorbance fall-off, which you have already met in optics.

Two half-lives to name:

  • Distribution half-life (t½ α): fast early drop as drug spreads into tissues.
  • Elimination half-life (t½ β): slower drop from metabolism and excretion.

Volume of distribution

Vd = total amount of drug in body / concentration of drug in plasma

  • Most important PK parameter. It estimates how much the drug hides in tissues versus stays in plasma.
  • A large Vd means the drug is heavily tissue-bound (little left in blood). A small Vd means it stays mostly in plasma.

Body clearance

Cl = Vd × Ke = Vd × (0.693 / t½)

  • Clearance is the volume of plasma fully cleared of drug per unit time. It ties the two parameters above together: how much is around (Vd) times how fast it leaves (Ke).

Therapeutic index (margin of safety)

TI = LD₅₀ / ED₅₀

  1. Plain language: how safe a drug is. The gap between the dose that helps and the dose that kills.
  2. Parts: LD₅₀ = dose lethal to 50% of subjects. ED₅₀ = dose effective in 50% of subjects.
  3. Origin: both come from quantal dose-response curves run across a population.
  4. Analogy: the shoulder of a mountain road. A wide shoulder (high TI) is forgiving; a narrow one (low TI) means small errors are fatal.
  5. Why this form: a ratio makes safety comparable across drugs of very different potency.
  6. Limits: a high TI does not mean zero side effects, only a wide lethal margin.
  7. Cross-link: related to the therapeutic window, the dose range that sits above the effective dose but below the toxic dose. Narrow-window drugs (and narrow TI drugs) need blood-level monitoring.

  • Alimentary advantages: safest, convenient, no sterile technique. Disadvantage: variable bioavailability, first-pass loss.
  • Parenteral advantages: rapid (ER use), accurate dose. Disadvantage: needs sterile technique and trained staff, rapid absorption can be toxic.

Dosing

  • Loading (initial) dose: higher first dose to reach therapeutic plasma level fast.
  • Maintenance dose: the amount that keeps the effect going.
  • Therapeutic dose: computed from body weight (kg).
  • Toxic dose: approximate, independent of body weight.
  • Mydriatic dosing example: intermittent, one drop every 5 minutes until full mydriasis.

Drug interaction effects to separate carefully:

  • Additive: 1 + 1 = 2 (combined effect equals the sum).
  • Synergism: 1 + 1 > 2 (combined effect greater than the sum).
  • Potentiation: 0 + 1 > 1 (a drug with no effect of its own boosts a second drug).

Pharmacodynamics — Receptors and Antagonism

  • Receptor: the lock; drug is the key (lock-and-key principle). Sites: type I cell membrane, type II cytoplasm, type III nucleus.
  • Affinity: strength of attraction between drug and receptor. High affinity binds even at low concentration.
  • Bonds: covalent = strongest = irreversible. Van der Waals = weakest but most important (reversible, most drugs).

Prescription Order (PO) and the Law

Eight parts of a prescription:

  1. Practitioner information (name, degree, license, PTR, S2 number)
  2. Patient information (name, address, age, sex)
  3. Date
  4. Superscription = the symbol Rx (Latin recipe, "to take")
  5. Inscription = drug, concentration, dosage form
  6. Subscription = directions to the pharmacist (how much to dispense)
  7. Signatura (Sig.) = directions to the patient (route, amount, frequency, duration)
  8. Refill / labeling / signature
  • S2 number is required for controlled/regulated drugs and is not renewed after 6 months.
  • FDA monitors existing drugs and approves new ones; concerns are efficacy and safety.
  • PDEA is the Philippine enforcement agency for controlled substances.
  • OTC drugs dispense without a prescription; Legend/Rx drugs need a prescriber.

Three legal doctrines:

  • Informed consent: patient must understand the procedure, alternatives, pros/cons, and risks before deciding.
  • Respondeat superior ("let the master answer"): the superior is responsible for a subordinate's actions.
  • Minors and incompetents: need guardian consent.

Practice Questions

1. A drug produces its maximum possible effect at a relatively low dose. Which two properties does this describe?

  • A. Low potency, high efficacy
  • B. High potency, high efficacy
  • C. High potency, low efficacy
  • D. Low potency, low efficacy
Answer

B. Acting at a low dose describes high potency. Reaching the maximum possible effect describes high efficacy. The two are independent: potency is about the dose needed, efficacy is about the size of the effect.

2. Which route of administration delivers 100% of the drug to the systemic circulation?

  • A. Oral
  • B. Sublingual
  • C. Intravenous
  • D. Rectal
Answer

C. Intravenous. IV (along with intra-arterial and intracardiac) bypasses absorption entirely, so bioavailability is 100% and follows zero-order kinetics. The other routes all involve an absorption step and a constant fraction reaching the blood (first-order).

3. A drug is excreted unchanged in the urine and forms the basis of a urine toxicity test. Which drug?

  • A. Penicillin
  • B. Atropine
  • C. Tropicamide
  • D. Phenobarbital
Answer

B. Atropine. It is excreted unchanged in urine, the basis of the atropine toxicity ("cat eye") test. Penicillin is also renally excreted but is the classic example of a drug not metabolized by the liver, not the toxicity-test drug. Phenobarbital is excreted unchanged too but the source ties the urine test specifically to atropine.

4. Two drugs are given together. Drug X has no effect of its own but increases the effect of Drug Y. This is:

  • A. Additive effect
  • B. Synergism
  • C. Potentiation
  • D. Antagonism
Answer

C. Potentiation. A drug lacking its own effect that boosts another is potentiation (0 + 1 > 1). Additive is sum of two active drugs; synergism is two active drugs producing more than their sum.

5. Which best describes a competitive antagonist?

  • A. Forms an irreversible covalent bond and cannot be displaced
  • B. Binds a different receptor to produce an opposite effect
  • C. Reversibly competes at the same receptor and is overcome by more agonist
  • D. Combines with the agonist to form an inactive compound
Answer

C. A competitive antagonist uses a weak, reversible Van der Waals bond at the same receptor, so adding more agonist overcomes it. Option A is non-competitive, B is physiologic antagonism, D is chemical antagonism.

G2 — Autonomic (Ocular) Drugs

The Simple Version

Almost every drug that acts on the eye works through the autonomic nervous system, and the pupil is the clearest place to see it. Think of the pupil as a tug of war between two muscles:
  • Sphincter pupillae (circular, parasympathetic, M3 receptor) pulls the pupil closed (miosis).
  • Dilator pupillae (radial, sympathetic, α1 receptor) pulls the pupil open (mydriasis).

To move the pupil you can either push your own muscle (give an agonist) or release the opponent (block the other side). That single idea generates a 2x2 grid that explains every mydriatic, miotic, and cycloplegic on the board.

Sympathetic (adrenergic)Parasympathetic (cholinergic)
Agonist (mimic, turn ON)Sympathomimetic → dilate, no cycloplegiaParasympathomimetic → constrict (miosis)
Antagonist (block, turn OFF)Sympatholytic → constrictParasympatholytic → dilate + cycloplegia

Teaching angle: never memorize the four effects. For any drug, name the system and whether it mimics or blocks, then ask "am I pushing my muscle or releasing the opponent?" The effect rebuilds itself.

But Why?

Why do the two dilators behave differently (one keeps near vision, one loses it)?

The dilators sit on a diagonal: a sympathomimetic (push the dilator) and a parasympatholytic (release the sphincter). The fork between them is the ciliary muscle, which is run by the same parasympathetic M3 nerve as the sphincter. So a parasympatholytic that relaxes the sphincter also paralyzes the ciliary muscle and adds cycloplegia. A sympathomimetic never touches the ciliary muscle, so it dilates with near vision intact.

Why does pilocarpine lower eye pressure?

It is a direct M3 agonist. It contracts the ciliary muscle (Brucke's fibers), which pulls open the trabecular meshwork and lets aqueous drain. So a "constrictor" is also a glaucoma drug, because the same muscle that focuses the lens also tugs the drain open.

Why are all mydriatics dangerous in a narrow angle?

Dilation bunches the peripheral iris into the angle and blocks the trabecular meshwork, which can trigger acute angle-closure. This is the one contraindication that applies to every dilating drug in this note.

Why do we test pupils with cocaine and hydroxyamphetamine?

Because they act at different points along the sympathetic chain. Cocaine needs a working nerve releasing NE; hydroxyamphetamine needs an intact final neuron to release stored NE. Putting them in sequence localizes a Horner lesion to a specific neuron.

Box 1 — Parasympathomimetics (cholinergic agonists): MIOSIS

Effects: miosis, accommodation, increased aqueous outflow, conjunctival/ciliary injection.

Direct-acting (bind the receptor):

  • Acetylcholine 1%: destroyed instantly by AChE; intracameral to constrict pupil after cataract extraction.
  • Methacholine 2.5%: resists AChE; diagnoses the tonic pupil in Adie's.
  • Carbachol (0.75–3%): not hydrolyzed by AChE; powerful miotic, second-line when pilocarpine fails; poor corneal penetration so combined with BAK.
  • Pilocarpine (0.25–10%): the prototype. Onset 2 h, duration 8 h. Lowers IOP (open and angle-closure), constricts pupil, and counters mydriasis from epinephrine/phenylephrine/tropicamide. 0.1% pilocarpine constricts an Adie's pupil (denervation supersensitivity) but not a normal one; 0.5–1% constricts a normal pupil. Contraindicated in malignant glaucoma. Most common side effect: miosis and accommodative spasm (drug-induced myopia).

Indirect-acting (AChE inhibitors, raise ACh):

  • Reversible (carbamyl ester): Physostigmine (0.25–0.5%) and Demecarium (two physostigmines). Uses: open-angle glaucoma, lice on lid margins.
  • Irreversible (organophosphate): Echothiopate and DFP (diisopropylfluorophosphate). Long-acting miotics for accommodative esotropia and aphakic glaucoma. Classic side effect: iris cysts (prevented/reversed with 2.5% phenylephrine), and echothiopate can cause retinal detachment.
  • Poisoning agents in the same family: parathion, malathion (insecticides), and nerve gas (tabun, soman, sarin). Antidote: atropine.

Clinical test: Tensilon (edrophonium) test for myasthenia gravis. Short-acting AChE inhibitor; muscle strength improves in myasthenia. Atropine is kept ready as the antidote for cholinergic crisis.

Box 2 — Parasympatholytics (cholinergic antagonists): MYDRIASIS + CYCLOPLEGIA

Block M3 → sphincter relaxes (mydriasis) and ciliary muscle relaxes (cycloplegia). All are contraindicated in angle-closure glaucoma.

Uses: uveitis (break/prevent synechiae), cycloplegic refraction, relieve ciliary spasm, penalization in amblyopia.

Box 3 — Sympathomimetics (adrenergic agonists): MYDRIASIS without cycloplegia

Direct-acting:

  • Decongestants (α1, vasoconstrict conjunctival vessels): naphazoline, tetrahydrozoline, oxymetazoline.
  • Apraclonidine (0.5–1%): α2 agonist (clonidine derivative). Lowers IOP after laser (trabeculoplasty, iridotomy, YAG capsulotomy) without affecting BP or pulse.
  • Brimonidine (0.2%, newest α2 agonist): lowers IOP by decreasing aqueous secretion.
  • Epinephrine (0.5–2%): non-selective; lowers IOP three ways (α1 opens trabecula, α2 cuts secretion, but β raises secretion). Dilates a Horner pupil at 0.1% (denervation supersensitivity). Contraindicated in angle-closure, aphakia (cystoid macular edema), CVD, diabetes. Rarely used now; replaced by dipivefrin (a more lipophilic pro-drug converted to epinephrine).
  • Phenylephrine (0.12 / 2.5 / 10%): synthetic, selective α1 agonist on the dilator. 0.12% = decongestant; 2.5% and 10% = mydriasis with no cycloplegia. Onset 30–60 min, duration 4–6 h. 2.5% delivers 1.25 mg systemically, 10% delivers 5 mg (avoid 10% in cardiac/HPN patients).

Indirect-acting (change NE level, do not bind receptor):

  • Hydroxyamphetamine 1%: releases stored NE; does not cross the blood-brain barrier.
  • Cocaine 4%: blocks NE reuptake; also a local anesthetic. From Erythroxylon coca.

The two pupil tests (commit these cold):

  • Horner's syndrome (sympathetic lesion): ptosis (Mueller), miosis (dilator), anhidrosis.
  • Cocaine test: a Horner pupil fails to dilate (no NE present to block reuptake of). Confirms Horner at any level.
  • Hydroxyamphetamine test: localizes the lesion. Dilates in central/preganglionic lesions (final neuron intact, NE stores releasable); fails to dilate in postganglionic lesions (final neuron dead).
  • Adie's tonic pupil (parasympathetic, ciliary ganglion): constricts to 0.1% pilocarpine (supersensitive); a normal pupil needs 0.5–1%.

Box 4 — Sympatholytics (adrenergic antagonists): MIOSIS / lower IOP

Two extra properties to know: ISA (intrinsic sympathomimetic activity, acts as partial agonist) and MSA (membrane-stabilizing activity, local-anesthetic effect).

Alpha-1 blockers (effects: miosis, ptosis, conjunctival hyperemia):

  • Dapiprazole 0.5% (Rev-Eyes): reverses mydriasis from phenylephrine and tropicamide.
  • Thymoxamine 0.5–1%: orphan drug, reverses 2.5% phenylephrine; no effect on aqueous secretion; helps differentiate glaucoma types.

  • Non-selective: Timolol 0.25–0.5% (no ISA/MSA), Levobunolol, Metipranolol, Carteolol. Contraindicated in asthma/COPD and insulin-dependent diabetes.
  • Cardio-selective (safer in asthma): Betaxolol, Metoprolol.
  • Systemic side effects: bradycardia, hypotension (most common), bronchoconstriction (non-selective), masking of hypoglycemia.

G3 — Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agents (Anesthetics & Dyes)

The Simple Version

Diagnostic agents are drugs you use to see something, not to treat. They fall into three families:
  1. Mydriatics and cycloplegics (to open the pupil and study the fundus or refraction). Covered in detail in G2-01-Autonomic-Drugs.
  2. Topical anesthetics (to numb the cornea so you can touch it: tonometry, foreign body, gonioscopy).
  3. Dyes (to stain and reveal: epithelial defects, tear film, leaks, drainage).

This note focuses on the anesthetics and dyes. The single most testable idea is matching the right dye to what it stains, and the right anesthetic to the right procedure.

Teaching angle: anesthetics work by one mechanism (block the sodium channel). Dyes differ by one question: does it stain living defects (fluorescein) or dead/devitalized cells and mucus (rose bengal, lissamine)? Sort every dye by that question.

But Why?

Why does an anesthetic fail in an infected, inflamed eye?

Local anesthetics are weak bases. They cross the nerve membrane only in the uncharged (non-ionized) form. Infected tissue is acidic, which pushes the drug into its charged form, so less enters the nerve and the block is weak. This is why you cannot reliably numb an abscess.

Why does fluorescein glow only where the epithelium is broken?

Intact epithelium is a lipid barrier the water-soluble dye cannot enter. Where cells are missing, the dye floods the intercellular spaces and the stroma, then fluoresces yellow-green under cobalt blue. No defect, no stain.

Why use rose bengal before fluorescein in a dry eye workup?

Rose bengal stains devitalized cells and mucus (the hallmark of dry eye) and is read in red light without fluorescence. Fluorescein would flood the tear film and obscure the subtle devitalized-cell pattern, so you stain with rose bengal first.

Why does proparacaine sting so little and act so fast?

It is an ester with very low tissue irritation and poor corneal penetration, so it numbs the surface in seconds and wears off in about 20 minutes without deep effects. That speed and gentleness make it the everyday tonometry drop.

Diagnostic Dyes

The organizing question: what does it stain?

  • Fluorescein stains epithelial defects (where cells are missing).
  • Rose bengal, lissamine, methylene blue stain devitalized cells and mucus (dead/dying tissue).
  • Trypan blue, ICG stain endothelium (used in surgery).

Fluorescein (the workhorse):

  • Low molecular weight (376). Orange crystal, yellow in solution. Fluoresces yellow-green under cobalt blue (465–490 nm absorption) in weak alkali.
  • Stains epithelial defects bright green; will not stain dead cells or mucus.
  • Stains soft contact lenses (remove them first); promotes Pseudomonas growth.
  • Quenching phenomenon: fluorescence drops above pH 8 or above ~0.001% concentration.
  • Uses: TBUT, applanation tonometry, RGP fit pattern, Seidel test, Jones test, IV fluorescein angiography (retinal/choroidal vessels).

Diagnostic tests built on fluorescein:

  • TBUT: time for the first dry spot to appear in the tear film.
  • Seidel test: detects aqueous leak from a wound (dye streams green against the yellow-orange background).
  • Jones I: dye reaches the nose = patent drainage. Jones II: irrigate after a negative Jones I; dye recovered = partial block at duct; no dye = block at canaliculi/puncta.

G4 — Therapeutic Ophthalmic Agents

The Simple Version

Therapeutic agents treat disease (diagnostic agents only reveal it). Three big jobs dominate the board:
  1. Lower eye pressure (anti-glaucoma): either make less aqueous or drain more.
  2. Kill the bug (anti-infectives): antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals.
  3. Calm inflammation (corticosteroids, NSAIDs, antihistamines).

The glaucoma drugs are the heart of this note. Everything about them comes from one equation: IOP depends on aqueous made minus aqueous drained. Every drug either turns the tap down (less secretion) or opens the drain wider (more outflow).

Teaching angle: for any glaucoma drug, ask one question: "does this cut production or boost outflow?" Then the side effects and combinations follow.

But Why?

Why are prostaglandin analogs first-line for glaucoma?

They open the uveoscleral outflow, a second drainage route that bypasses the diseased trabecular meshwork. Once-daily dosing, strong effect, and few systemic risks make them the modern first choice. The price is cosmetic: iris darkening and longer lashes.

Why do beta blockers and CAIs lower pressure the same way but cause different harms?

Both cut aqueous production at the ciliary body. Beta blockers do it by blocking adenylate cyclase (so they hit the lungs and heart too: asthma and bradycardia). CAIs do it by blocking carbonic anhydrase (so they cause paresthesia, diuresis, and a metallic taste, because the same enzyme works in the kidney). Same goal, different collateral.

Why does long-term steroid use cause glaucoma and cataract?

Steroids let glycosaminoglycans pile up in the trabecular meshwork, clogging outflow (steroid-response glaucoma after 4 to 6 weeks; permanent after about 18 months). They also drive posterior subcapsular cataract. So the drug that calms the eye can quietly blind it, which is why steroid courses are monitored.

Why can a beautiful antibiotic still poison the eye or ear?

Aminoglycosides are excellent against gram-negative bugs but are ototoxic and nephrotoxic beyond five days. Chloramphenicol risks aplastic anemia. Choosing an antibiotic is always benefit against its specific toxicity.

  • Macrolides (erythromycin): for penicillin-allergic patients.

Antifungals: inhibit ergosterol synthesis. Natamycin (polyene) is the topical workhorse for filamentary fungi (Fusarium, Aspergillus); amphotericin B (most potent, IV) for systemic; fungi are linked to contact-lens solution contamination.

Antivirals: topical trifluridine, vidarabine, acyclovir for herpes/VZV; IV ganciclovir/foscarnet and intravitreal fomivirsen for CMV retinitis. Idoxuridine was the first approved antiviral.

Antimalarial ocular toxicity (chloroquine/hydroxychloroquine): corneal verticillata (whorl deposits), bull's-eye maculopathy, halos, color (tritan) defects, decreased VA (earliest sign).

Anti-TB ocular toxicity: ethambutol and isoniazid cause optic neuritis; streptomycin is ototoxic/nephrotoxic.

Anti-Inflammatory Agents

Corticosteroids (the big one):

  • MOA: inhibit phospholipase A2 → less prostaglandins AND leukotrienes (upstream of NSAIDs).
  • Anterior segment disease: topical is most effective. Posterior segment: systemic, periocular, or intravitreal.
  • Ocular side effects (must know): steroid-response open-angle glaucoma (4 to 6 weeks; permanent after ~18 months), posterior subcapsular cataract, secondary infection (fungal, herpes), delayed healing, mydriasis, ptosis.
  • Contraindications: herpetic/viral keratitis, fungal disease, untreated infection, post-FB removal (delays healing).
  • Potency ladder: cortisone (least) < hydrocortisone < prednisone/prednisolone (10x cortisone) < dexamethasone/betamethasone (30 to 50x). Fluorometholone has the least IOP rise, so it is the safer steroid in glaucoma-prone eyes.
  • Systemic side effects: Cushingoid moon face, osteoporosis, PUD, hyperglycemia, immunosuppression, mood change.

NSAIDs: inhibit cyclooxygenase → less prostaglandins. Effects: reduce surgical miosis, stabilize blood-aqueous barrier, no IOP change, can worsen herpes. Examples: ketorolac (allergic conjunctivitis), diclofenac, flurbiprofen.

Antihistamines / mast-cell stabilizers: block H1 (levocabastine) or stabilize mast cells (cromolyn sodium 4%, prevents calcium influx and degranulation) for vernal/atopic/seasonal allergic conjunctivitis.

G5 — Special Populations and Ocular Side Effects of Systemic Drugs

The Simple Version

This note has two halves:
  1. Systemic drugs that hurt the eye. A patient swallows a pill for the heart, lungs, or joints, and it leaves a fingerprint somewhere in the eye: cornea, lens, retina, optic nerve, or color vision. The board loves to give you the drug and ask for the finding (or the reverse). These are pattern-matching points, almost free if you drill the pairs.
  2. Special populations. Pregnancy, children, and the elderly change which drug you reach for, because their bodies handle drugs differently.

Teaching angle: for the toxicity half, do not memorize long lists. Anchor on the signature pairs (chloroquine = bull's-eye macula, digitalis = yellow vision, ethambutol = optic neuritis). The classic exam item is a single drug and a single finding.

But Why?

Why is tropicamide the preferred dilator in pregnancy?

All ocular agents are FDA Category C (risk not ruled out). Tropicamide has the shortest duration and least systemic absorption, so when you must dilate, it exposes mother and fetus the least. Always add nasolacrimal occlusion. (See G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles and F1-01-DPA-Examination.)

Why do children react badly to cyclopentolate, and the elderly to many systemic drugs?

Children have an immature liver, so a normal dose builds up and reaches the brain (cyclopentolate causes hallucinations and seizures, especially at 2%). The elderly have a declining liver and kidney, so drugs clear slowly and accumulate. Both are dosing problems caused by the same organ: a metabolizing system that is either not ready or wearing out.

Why do so many drugs cause cornea verticillata (a whorl pattern)?

Amphiphilic drugs (chloroquine, amiodarone, indomethacin, tamoxifen) bind lipids and get trapped in the basal corneal epithelium, where they deposit in a golden-brown whorl that follows the cornea's natural cell-migration spiral. Different drugs, one mechanism, one pattern.

Why do anticholinergic drugs trigger acute angle-closure?

They dilate the pupil. In a narrow angle, dilation bunches the iris into the drain and blocks outflow. So antihistamines, tricyclics, and motion-sickness scopolamine can all precipitate an attack in a susceptible eye.

Ocular Side Effects by Structure

Cornea:

  • Cornea verticillata (whorl keratopathy): chloroquine, amiodarone (anterior deposits), indomethacin, tamoxifen, chlorpromazine, suramin.
  • Band keratopathy: excess vitamin D.
  • Gold salts: chrysiasis. Deferoxamine: corneal discoloration.

Lens (cataract):

  • Corticosteroids: posterior subcapsular cataract.
  • Phenothiazines (thioridazine, chlorpromazine): stellate anterior subcapsular deposits; also corneal endothelial dusting.
  • Statins (lovastatin), strong miotics.

Retina:

  • Chloroquine / hydroxychloroquine: bull's-eye maculopathy.
  • Phenothiazines, indomethacin: pigmentary retinopathy, cystoid macular edema.
  • Tamoxifen: crystalline retinopathy.
  • Oral contraceptives: vascular occlusion (CRAO, CRVO).
  • Quinine: vessel attenuation. Niacin: cystoid macular edema.

Optic nerve:

  • Ethambutol, isoniazid (INH), chloramphenicol, streptomycin: optic/retrobulbar neuritis and atrophy.
  • Lithium, oral contraceptives, amiodarone, excess vitamin A: papilledema / pseudotumor cerebri.
  • Ethanol: toxic amblyopia (VA loss, tritan defect, centrocecal scotoma).

Color vision:

  • Digitalis: xanthopsia (yellow/"snowy" vision).
  • Amiodarone: colored halos.
  • Ethambutol, INH: red-green defect.
  • Indomethacin, chloroquine, phenothiazine: tritan (blue-yellow, earliest, hits ganglion cells).

IOP and angle:

  • Steroids: open-angle glaucoma (GAG buildup in trabecular meshwork).
  • Anticholinergics, antihistamines, tricyclics, benzodiazepines, scopolamine, amphetamines: acute angle-closure with pupillary block.
  • Drugs that lower IOP: beta blockers, diuretics, CAIs, marijuana.

Refraction shift:

  • Transient myopia: sulfonamides, tetracycline, acetazolamide, steroids, hydrochlorothiazide (ciliary body edema shifts the lens forward).
  • Hyperopia: oral hypoglycemic agents (fluid uptake lowers lens index).

Surface and contact lens:

  • Dry eye: oral retinoids, antihistamines, anticholinergics, diuretics.
  • Blepharoconjunctivitis: isotretinoin, amiodarone, gold salts, methotrexate, rifampin.
  • Contact lens intolerance (CLI): isotretinoin (8 to 10%), vitamin A, antihistamines, tricyclics, oral contraceptives, phenothiazines.

Special Populations

Pregnancy:

  • All ocular drugs are FDA Category C.
  • If dilation is required: tropicamide (shortest duration, least exposure) + nasolacrimal occlusion.
  • Avoid phenylephrine 10% (cardiovascular), tetracycline/fluoroquinolones (fetal bone/cartilage).

Pediatric:

  • Immature liver → systemic toxicity. Cyclopentolate CNS effects (hallucination, seizure) at 2%; use 0.5 to 1% and press the lacrimal sac.
  • Atropine is used for cycloplegic refraction and amblyopia penalization but watch for systemic anticholinergic toxicity (flush, fever, tachycardia).

Geriatric:

  • Declining liver and kidney → slowed clearance, drug accumulation, higher toxicity risk.
  • More polypharmacy → more drug interactions and more of the systemic toxicities above (chronic amiodarone, statins, diuretics).
  • Higher baseline angle-closure risk; be cautious dilating.

Idiosyncratic / sensitivity terms (from G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles): women carry a higher unexplained toxicity risk; idiosyncratic reactions are illogical (a mydriatic causing miosis).

Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue→rule→trap`.

Autonomic pupil effect — cue: agonist/antagonist + system.

  • Number rule: 1 constricts, 2 dilates (α1 mydriasis, α2 cuts aqueous, β2 raises aqueous). Dilate w/o cycloplegia = sympathomimetic; dilate + cycloplegia = parasympatholytic; constrict = parasympathomimetic.
  • Trap: "β2 stimulation lowers IOP" — β2 increases aqueous.

Cycloplegic strength — cue: strongest/longest vs routine.

  • Atropine = strongest/longest (14 d); tropicamide = weakest/shortest; cyclopentolate = routine DOC.
  • Trap: "tropicamide is the strongest cycloplegic."

Glaucoma drug mechanism — cue: cut production vs boost outflow.

  • Cut production (3): β-blockers, α2 agonists, CAIs. Boost outflow (2): prostaglandins (uveoscleral), miotics (trabecular).
  • Trap: "β-blockers increase outflow" or "prostaglandins drain trabecular."

Pupil-test pairs — cue: cocaine / hydroxyamphetamine / pilocarpine.

  • Cocaine confirms Horner (fails to dilate); hydroxyamphetamine localizes; 0.1% pilocarpine constricts an Adie's pupil.
  • Trap: a Horner pupil "dilates with cocaine" — it fails to.

Drug → ocular sign — cue: the drug named.

  • Chloroquine = bull's-eye (late); digitalis = xanthopsia (yellow); ethambutol/INH = optic neuritis + red-green; steroid = PSC cataract + glaucoma.
  • Trap: "ethambutol = blue-yellow defect" — it's red-green.
