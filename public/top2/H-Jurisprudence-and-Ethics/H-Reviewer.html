H1 — RA 8050: The Revised Optometry Law of 1995

The Simple Version

RA 8050 is the Revised Optometry Law of 1995. It is the single law that defines what optometry is, who may practice it, how you get licensed, who regulates you, and what gets you punished. It repealed the old RA 1998.

Think of it as answering five questions:

  1. What is the practice of optometry? (the acts you are allowed to do)
  2. Who may practice? (registered, licensed optometrists only, with narrow exceptions)
  3. How do you become one? (the licensure exam and registration)
  4. Who watches over you? (the Board of Optometry, under the PRC)
  5. What is forbidden, and what is the penalty? (prohibited acts and penal provisions)

Teaching angle: the board loves the numbers (5 board members, 75% passing, 3-year renewal, 1 to 8 years jail). Lock the numbers first; they are the cheapest marks in jurisprudence.

But Why?

Why does optometry need its own law at all?

Because optometrists use diagnostic drugs and make clinical decisions that affect health. The State declares optometry part of primary health care, so it regulates entry and conduct to protect the public, the same reason medicine and pharmacy are licensed.

Why can a physician examine the eye without an optometry license, but a public health worker can only do a visual acuity test?

The law protects scope by training. A physician with post-graduate eye training is more qualified, so examining the eye is allowed and is not "practicing optometry." A public health worker in a blindness-prevention program is trained only for screening, so they may do visual acuity and screening only, nothing more. Scope follows competence.

Why does the law require a separate pharmacology certification before using DPAs?

Diagnostic drugs are real pharmaceuticals with real risks (see F1-01-DPA-Examination and G2-01-Autonomic-Drugs). Section 31 requires those trained before the law to pass a special pharmacology certification before they may instill DPAs. Using DPAs untrained is a prohibited act, injury or not.

Why is the Board term non-renewable and staggered?

Five-year non-renewable terms, with one member rotating out each year, keep the Board independent and continuous: no one entrenches, and experience is never lost all at once. The senior member becomes chairperson in the final year.

What the Law Is

  • Title: Revised Optometry Law of 1995 (RA 8050). Repealed RA 1998 (the old Optometry Law).
  • State policy: optometry is an essential profession and a regular component of the primary health care system.
  • Optometry (definition): the science and art of examining the eye, analyzing ocular function, prescribing and dispensing lenses/prisms/contact lenses/low-vision aids, conducting ocular exercises and orthoptics, installing prosthetics, using authorized DPAs, and other measures for the aid, correction, or relief of the human eye.

Historical Milestones (jurisprudence background)

The board loves "firsts" and the law lineage. Source: Cinco, "Optometry: Legal and Professional Aspects."

  • Law lineage: Optometry Law of 1917 (first, written in Spanish; amended 1919) to RA 1998 (1957) to RA 8050 (1995). The 1917 law created the Junta Examinadora (Board of Examiners), later renamed the Board of Optometry by PD 223 (as amended by PD 657).
  • First optometric clinic with refraction + first lens-grinding shop: R.M. Clark, Manila, 1902.
  • First optometry school: Philippine College of Optometry (to 1925). First university-affiliated college: Centro Escolar University, 1928.
  • Registration Certificate No. 1 (per PRC records): issued March 11, 1918 to Sabater.
  • Associations: OAP (1936, Justo Gonzalez first president) + PODA + PROA merged in 1979 into Samahan Ng Optometrist Sa Pilipinas (SOP), the integrated national body.
  • Regional/world: IFAPAO founded at the 1st APOC (Manila, 1978); later became APCO. IOOL became the WCO. First International Optometrist of the Year (1988) was M. Cinco.
  • Deeper coverage in the Law Book (full) reviewer linked from the H reviewer bar.

Acts Constituting Practice (Sec 4)

(a) Examining the eye (subjective + objective, with DPAs and instruments); (b) prescribing and dispensing lenses, prisms, contact lenses, frames; (c) ocular exercises, vision training, orthoptics, prosthetics; (d) counseling on vision and eye care; (e) establishing offices/clinics; (f) collecting professional fees for acts a to d.

Who May Practice (Sec 5 exceptions)

Only registered, licensed optometrists. Exceptions:

  • Physicians with post-graduate eye training (and a physician's eye exam during a physical is not "practicing optometry").
  • Public health workers in the blindness-prevention program: visual acuity test and visual screening only.

Disclosure (Sec 6): indicate license number and expiry on documents; display the certificate of registration conspicuously in the clinic.

Licensure (Sec 17 to 29)

  • Exam given at least once a year by the Board.
  • Requirements (Sec 19): Filipino citizen, good moral character, Doctor of Optometry (O.D.) from an accredited school after a 6-year course.
  • Passing (Sec 20): general average of at least 75%, with no subject below 50%.
  • Results reported to PRC within 60 days.
  • Fail 3 times → must take a refresher course before re-examination (Sec 22).
  • Take the oath, then PRC issues the certificate of registration and professional license.
  • License renewal every 3 years with continuing education and fees (Sec 29).
  • Appeals (Sec 27): appeal a revocation/suspension to the PRC within 15 days.

DPAs and Pharmacology (Sec 31)

Optometrists practicing before the law must take a pharmacology course and pass a special certification exam before using DPAs. The 5 approved DPAs are detailed in F1-01-DPA-Examination: phenylephrine, tropicamide, cyclopentolate, proparacaine, fluorescein (plus miotics).

Prohibited Acts (Sec 32) and Penalties (Sec 33)

Prohibited: (a) false evidence to the Board; (b) posing as a registered optometrist without a license; (c) comparative/price advertising ("better than others," cheaper fees/lenses); (d) DPA use by an untrained optometrist; (e) using unauthorized drugs; (f) unauthorized practice; (g) any violation of the Act or its rules.

Penalty (Sec 33): imprisonment of 1 to 8 years, or a fine of P10,000 to P40,000, or both, at the court's discretion. Administrative penalties (Sec 26 revocation/suspension) also apply.

Integration and Ethics (Sec 36 to 40)

  • All optometrists are integrated into one national organization, which promulgates the Code of Professional Ethics (adopted by the Board, approved by PRC).
  • Six ethical duties (Sec 40): primary responsibility to the visual welfare of the public; promote best standards; continuously improve proficiency; serve all equally regardless of race/creed/status; respect confidentiality; and refer when medical or other attention is needed. (Expanded in H2-01-Bioethics-and-Code-of-Ethics.)

Practice Questions

1. A candidate scores a 78% general average but gets 48% in one subject. Under RA 8050, the candidate:

  • A. Passes (average is above 75%)
  • B. Fails (a subject is below 50%)
  • C. Passes conditionally
  • D. Must retake only that subject
Answer

B. Fails. Section 20 requires at least a 75% average AND no rating below 50% in any subject. The 48% disqualifies despite the high average.

2. Who may legally examine the human eye without an optometry license under RA 8050?

  • A. Any pharmacist
  • B. A physician with post-graduate eye training
  • C. An optician
  • D. A contact lens salesperson
Answer

B. Section 5 exempts physicians with post-graduate training in diagnosis and treatment of eye disease. Public health workers may do only visual acuity testing and screening.

3. The Board of Optometry is composed of how many members, and from how many nominees?

  • A. 3 members from 6 nominees
  • B. 5 members from 10 nominees
  • C. 7 members from 14 nominees
  • D. 5 members from 5 nominees
Answer

B. 5 members from 10 nominees, appointed by the President on PRC recommendation, from the accredited national organization's list.

4. An optometrist places an ad claiming "lower fees than any clinic in town." Under RA 8050 this is:

  • A. Allowed if the claim is true
  • B. A prohibited act
  • C. Allowed with Board approval
  • D. Allowed only in print media
Answer

B. A prohibited act. Section 32(c) prohibits advertising conveying that one optometrist is better or charges lower fees. Truth is no defense.

5. A person convicted of unauthorized practice of optometry faces what penalty?

  • A. Fine only, up to P5,000
  • B. Imprisonment of 1 to 8 years, or fine of P10,000 to P40,000, or both
  • C. License suspension only
  • D. Community service
Answer

B. Section 33 sets imprisonment of 1 to 8 years, or a fine of P10,000 to P40,000, or both, at the court's discretion, plus applicable administrative penalties.

H2 — Bioethics and the Code of Professional Ethics

The Simple Version

Ethics is not law. Law tells you what you must do or face punishment; ethics tells you what you ought to do as a professional. The whole field rests on four principles (Beauchamp and Childress):
  1. Autonomy: respect the patient's right to decide (the basis of informed consent).
  2. Beneficence: do good, act in the patient's best interest.
  3. Non-maleficence: do no harm (primum non nocere).
  4. Justice: treat patients fairly and equally.

The RA 8050 Code of Professional Ethics (Sec 40) is just these four principles written into optometric duties. Master the four, and the code, the scenarios, and the traps all fall out of them.

Teaching angle: for any ethics scenario, name which of the four principles is in play. Most questions are testing autonomy (consent) or non-maleficence (harm/referral).

But Why?

Why is informed consent the same thing as autonomy, not just a signature?

Autonomy means a competent patient has the right to accept or refuse care. That right is meaningless unless the patient actually understands what they are agreeing to, so informed consent requires disclosure of the diagnosis, the options, the risks, the benefits, and the alternatives. A signed form without understanding is not consent; it is paperwork.

Why does non-maleficence not mean "never do anything risky"?

Every useful treatment carries some risk (dilating drops, contact lenses, even a referral that worries a patient). Non-maleficence is a balance, not a ban: the expected benefit must outweigh the expected harm. Doing nothing is also a choice with consequences, so "first do no harm" means weigh, do not freeze.

Why must you keep monitoring a patient who refuses your referral?

Autonomy lets the patient refuse, but beneficence and non-maleficence do not let you abandon them. You document the refusal, re-explain the risk, and keep caring for them at each visit. The principles work together; one does not cancel the others.

Why is treating a PhilHealth patient worse than a private patient an ethics violation?

Justice requires the same standard of care for similar cases regardless of ability to pay, race, creed, or status. Care quality is not a product tier. Distributive justice is exactly this: equal access to the same standard.

Informed Consent (the heart of autonomy)

Four components: the patient is informed of (1) the diagnosis and its implications, (2) the treatment options and expected outcomes, (3) the risks and alternatives, and (4) voluntarily agrees or declines without coercion.

Special populations:

  • Minors: a parent or guardian consents; seek assent from older children.
  • Incompetent patients: a guardian or surrogate decides.
  • Emergency: consent may be waived when delay would cause serious harm.

(These mirror the legal doctrines in G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles: informed consent, respondeat superior, and consent for minors/incompetents.)

A violation of the Code is unethical conduct, punishable by suspension or prohibition from practice (RA 8050 Sec 26).

Additional Professional Principles

  • Confidentiality: records and diagnoses are privileged; no disclosure without consent.
  • Referral obligation: refer when a condition is beyond your competence or scope.
  • Continuous competence: keep current through continuing education (ties to the 3-year license renewal in H1-01-RA8050-Optometry-Law).
  • Collegiality: professional respect; do not disparage colleagues.
  • Protection of the vulnerable: extra care for children, elderly, and cognitively impaired patients.

Worked Scenarios

  • Patient refuses referral for a suspicious disc: autonomy lets them refuse; document clearly, re-counsel the risk, keep monitoring, do not abandon.
  • Company offers a gift to prescribe their lenses exclusively: justice and beneficence say lens choice must follow clinical merit, not commercial ties; decline.
  • A minor arrives alone for a contact lens fit: autonomy requires parent/guardian consent; defer until obtained.

Practice Questions

1. An optometrist instills a dilating drop without telling the patient it will blur near vision and cause light sensitivity, and without asking permission. Which principle is violated?

  • A. Justice
  • B. Beneficence
  • C. Autonomy (informed consent)
  • D. Collegiality
Answer

C. Autonomy. Failing to disclose effects and obtain consent violates the patient's right to make an informed decision.

2. Choosing not to fit extended-wear lenses in a non-compliant patient with a history of microbial keratitis primarily reflects which principle?

  • A. Autonomy
  • B. Non-maleficence
  • C. Justice
  • D. Confidentiality
Answer

B. Non-maleficence. Avoiding a foreseeable harm whose risk outweighs the benefit is "do no harm."

3. A competent adult refuses referral for a suspicious optic disc. The most ethical action is:

  • A. Refuse to see the patient again
  • B. Override the refusal and refer anyway
  • C. Document the refusal, re-counsel the risk, and continue monitoring
  • D. Report the patient to the Board
Answer

C. Autonomy allows refusal, but beneficence and non-maleficence require continued care: document, re-counsel, and keep monitoring.

4. Providing a faster, lesser examination to a PhilHealth patient than to a private patient with the same complaint violates:

  • A. Autonomy
  • B. Beneficence
  • C. Justice
  • D. Non-maleficence
Answer

C. Justice. Distributive justice requires the same standard of care regardless of ability to pay or status.

5. Under RA 8050 Section 40, which duty corresponds most directly to the principle of non-maleficence?

  • A. Consider the visual welfare of the public as the primary responsibility
  • B. Refer for medical attention when indicated
  • C. Render services with equal diligence to all
  • D. Respect confidentiality
Answer

B. Referring when a condition exceeds your competence prevents harm, the core of non-maleficence. Visual welfare is beneficence; equal diligence is justice; confidentiality protects trust/autonomy.

H3 — Practice Management and Labor Laws

The Simple Version

Practice management is the business and regulatory side of running a clinic. Three blocks:
  1. Setting up the business: the government registrations every clinic needs (DTI, Barangay, Mayor, BIR).
  2. Running a compliant clinic: what the Board Resolution No. 11 (Series of 2016) requires of your office (instruments, room size, displays, no commercialization).
  3. Advertising and conduct: what you may and may not say to attract patients (Board Resolution No. 1, Series of 2008, and RA 8050 Sec 32).

Teaching angle: the recurring theme is professionalism over commercialization. Almost every rule exists to keep optometry a profession, not a retail counter. When unsure on an exam item, pick the answer that protects professional dignity.

But Why?

Why so many separate registrations to open one clinic?

Each agency licenses a different thing: DTI registers your business name, the Barangay clears your location, the Mayor issues the business permit to operate, and the BIR registers you for taxes. They stack because each level of government has its own authority over a part of running a business.

Why must the exam room be enclosed, a set size, and free of the product display?

Because the law wants the clinical act separated from the sales act. A defined, professional examination space (minimum 10 square meters, 3-meter test distance) signals that the patient is receiving health care, not shopping. Frames and sunglasses must not be used to identify or highlight the practice, to protect optometry's image against commercialization.

Why is chair time the sole discretion of the doctor?

Because clinical decisions must be free of commercial pressure. The optical's manager or owner cannot tell the optometrist to rush an exam to see more customers. The doctor decides the method, procedure, and time; that independence is what makes it a profession.

Why are price and "free service" ads prohibited?

Because competing on price and giveaways turns eye care into a discount race, which degrades standards and dignity. The law allows only true, honest, dignified, objective information. You may say what you do, not that you are cheaper or better than colleagues.

  • The clinic must be manned by a licensed optometrist during operating hours.

Instruments and Equipment (Sec 46):

  • Ocular exam: slit-lamp, ophthalmoscope, retinoscope, phoropter, trial lens set, VA chart, Ishihara plates, Amsler grid, contrast sensitivity chart, tangent screen.
  • Binocular vision: penlight + occluder, prism bar + loose prisms, Maddox wing, RAF rule, Worth Four Dot, Stereo Fly.
  • Contact lens: keratometer (or topographer), CL trial lenses.
  • Optional: Schiotz/Goldmann tonometer, automated tangent screen, automated perimeter.

Physical Facilities (Sec 47):

  • Examination/treatment room: minimum 10 square meters, test distance at least 3 meters, optometrist's name posted outside.
  • Receiving area: for registration and forms; only optometry-related certificates displayed.
  • Product display area: frames/sunglasses must not be used to highlight or identify the practice; no commercialization; no advertisement of free services.
  • Ophthalmic laboratory: separate from the exam room.
  • Locker rooms, pantry, stockroom must be separate from the service area.

Service delivery (Sec 5, Board Res 11): minimum services for school-aged children, adults (18+), and seniors (60+); management options include spectacle correction, safety eyewear, contact lenses, and basic vision therapy.

Time and Manner of Examination (Sec 48): chair time, method, and procedure are the sole discretion of the handling doctor, free from external influence (owners, managers, staff). Wear professional attire.

3. Advertising and Conduct

RA 8050 Sec 32(c) and Board Resolution No. 1 (Series of 2008), Rule VII Sec 39:

  • An optometrist may use only true, honest, fair, dignified, objective information.
  • Prohibited: self-laudatory claims (better/superior services, exclusivity); any reference to fees or lower-than-customary prices; advertising free exams, discounts, bonuses, premiums, prizes; exaggerated or false warranties; undignified, immodest, or vulgar ads.
  • Prohibited solicitation: canvassing; employing solicitors, agents, entertainers, or lecturers to attract patronage; using groups or organizations to solicit patients.

4. Labor and Statutory Obligations (practical)

An optometrist who employs staff (optical assistants, technicians) is an employer with basic obligations under Philippine labor standards:

  • Mandatory government contributions: SSS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG for employees.
  • Basic standards: minimum wage, statutory benefits (13th month pay, holiday/overtime premiums), and safe working conditions.
  • The optometrist remains professionally responsible for delegated tasks (respondeat superior, see G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles): a non-optometrist staff member may not perform the acts that constitute the practice of optometry (H1-01-RA8050-Optometry-Law).

Practice Questions

1. What is the correct order of registrations to open a sole-proprietorship optometry clinic?

  • A. BIR → Mayor → Barangay → DTI
  • B. DTI → Barangay → Mayor → BIR
  • C. Mayor → DTI → BIR → Barangay
  • D. Barangay → BIR → DTI → Mayor
Answer
**B. DTI→Barangay→Mayor→BIR.** Register the name (DTI), clear the location (Barangay), get the permit (Mayor), then register for taxes (BIR).

2. An optical owner instructs the optometrist to limit each eye exam to ten minutes to serve more customers. This violates:

  • A. Nothing; the owner runs the business
  • B. The rule that chair time is the sole discretion of the handling doctor
  • C. Advertising rules
  • D. Labor law
Answer

B. Board Resolution No. 11 (Sec 48) makes chair time, method, and procedure the sole discretion of the optometrist, free from external influence by owners or managers.

3. Which advertisement is permitted under the rules?

  • A. "Lowest eyeglass prices in the city"
  • B. "Free eye exam with every purchase"
  • C. A dignified statement of the services the clinic offers
  • D. "The best optometrist in the region"
Answer

C. Only true, honest, dignified, objective information is allowed. Price claims, free-service offers, and self-laudatory superiority claims are prohibited.

4. The minimum examination room area and test distance required are:

  • A. 5 sq m and 6 m
  • B. 10 sq m and 3 m
  • C. 20 sq m and 6 m
  • D. 8 sq m and 4 m
Answer

B. 10 square meters with at least a 3-meter test distance (Board Resolution No. 11, Sec 47).

5. After securing clinic premises, within how many days must the optometrist notify the Board of the location?

  • A. 15 days
  • B. 30 days
  • C. 60 days
  • D. 90 days
Answer

B. 30 days, with photographs, to allow inspection and monitoring.

Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue→rule→trap`.

The numbers — cue: any RA 8050 figure.

  • Pass = 75% avg, none < 50%. Board = 5 from 10, 5-yr non-renewable, quorum 3. License renews every 3 yrs. Appeal 15 days. Penalty 1–8 yrs / ₱10k–40k.
  • Trap: "license renews every 5 years" (that's the board term, not the license — license is 3).

Which law is current — cue: RA 8050 vs RA 1998.

  • RA 8050 (1995) is current; it repealed RA 1998.
  • Trap: "RA 1998 is newer" because the number is bigger.

Four ethics principles — cue: the scenario's act.

  • Consent/refusal = autonomy; refer beyond scope = non-maleficence; equal care regardless of status = justice; act in best interest = beneficence.
  • Trap: matching "refer when indicated" to beneficence — it's non-maleficence.

Advertising — cue: an ad claim.

  • Allowed = true/honest/dignified statement of services. Prohibited = price, "better/best," free exams/discounts.
  • Trap: "lower-fee ad is fine if it's true" — truth is no defense; it's prohibited.

Clinic + setup — cue: a facility number or registration.

  • Exam room ≥10 sq m, ≥3 m test distance; notify Board within 30 days. Setup order: DTI → Barangay → Mayor → BIR.
  • Trap: a 5 sq m room or starting with the Mayor's permit.
