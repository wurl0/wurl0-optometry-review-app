*Personal study notes | TOS: H Jurisprudence and Ethics | Source: Bioethical Principles gap lecture + RA 8050 Sec 40*

## The Simple Version

Ethics is not law. Law tells you what you **must** do or face punishment; ethics tells you what you **ought** to do as a professional. The whole field rests on **four principles** (Beauchamp and Childress):

1. **Autonomy:** respect the patient's right to decide (the basis of informed consent).
2. **Beneficence:** do good, act in the patient's best interest.
3. **Non-maleficence:** do no harm (primum non nocere).
4. **Justice:** treat patients fairly and equally.

The RA 8050 **Code of Professional Ethics (Sec 40)** is just these four principles written into optometric duties. Master the four, and the code, the scenarios, and the traps all fall out of them.

**Teaching angle:** for any ethics scenario, name which of the four principles is in play. Most questions are testing autonomy (consent) or non-maleficence (harm/referral).

---

## But Why?

**Why is informed consent the same thing as autonomy, not just a signature?**
Autonomy means a competent patient has the right to accept or refuse care. That right is meaningless unless the patient actually understands what they are agreeing to, so informed consent requires disclosure of the diagnosis, the options, the risks, the benefits, and the alternatives. A signed form without understanding is not consent; it is paperwork.

**Why does non-maleficence not mean "never do anything risky"?**
Every useful treatment carries some risk (dilating drops, contact lenses, even a referral that worries a patient). Non-maleficence is a balance, not a ban: the expected benefit must outweigh the expected harm. Doing nothing is also a choice with consequences, so "first do no harm" means weigh, do not freeze.

**Why must you keep monitoring a patient who refuses your referral?**
Autonomy lets the patient refuse, but beneficence and non-maleficence do not let you abandon them. You document the refusal, re-explain the risk, and keep caring for them at each visit. The principles work together; one does not cancel the others.

**Why is treating a PhilHealth patient worse than a private patient an ethics violation?**
Justice requires the same standard of care for similar cases regardless of ability to pay, race, creed, or status. Care quality is not a product tier. Distributive justice is exactly this: equal access to the same standard.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Autonomy | Greek *autos* = self + *nomos* = rule | Patient's right to decide; basis of informed consent |
| Beneficence | Latin *bene* = good + *facere* = to do | Acting in the patient's best interest |
| Non-maleficence | Latin *male* = bad + *facere* | "Do no harm"; benefit must outweigh harm |
| Justice | Latin *justitia* = fairness | Equal standard of care regardless of status |
| Informed consent | knowing agreement | Understanding diagnosis, options, risks; not just a signature |
| Assent | Latin *assentire* = agree | A child's agreement alongside parental consent |
| Primum non nocere | Latin = "first, do no harm" | The motto of non-maleficence |
| Confidentiality | Latin *confidere* = to trust | Patient records and diagnoses are privileged |
| Distributive justice | fair allocation | Same standard for PhilHealth and private patients alike |
| Beauchamp & Childress | framework authors | Originated the four-principles model of bioethics |

### The Four Principles

| Principle | Meaning | Optometric application | Violation example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | Patient's right to decide | Informed consent; respect refusal | Instilling a DPA without explaining side effects or getting consent |
| **Beneficence** | Do good, best interest | Prescribe what the patient truly needs; monitor proactively | Selling an unneeded Rx upgrade for profit |
| **Non-maleficence** | Do no harm | Avoid contraindicated drugs; refer beyond scope | Fitting extended-wear lenses in a non-compliant patient with prior keratitis |
| **Justice** | Fairness, equal care | Same standard regardless of status | Substandard exam for a PhilHealth vs private patient |

### Informed Consent (the heart of autonomy)

**Four components:** the patient is informed of (1) the diagnosis and its implications, (2) the treatment options and expected outcomes, (3) the risks and alternatives, and (4) voluntarily agrees or declines **without coercion**.

**Special populations:**
- **Minors:** a parent or guardian consents; seek **assent** from older children.
- **Incompetent patients:** a guardian or surrogate decides.
- **Emergency:** consent may be **waived** when delay would cause serious harm.

(These mirror the legal doctrines in [[G1-01-General-Pharmacology-Principles]]: informed consent, respondeat superior, and consent for minors/incompetents.)

### The RA 8050 Code of Ethics (Sec 40) mapped to principles

| Sec 40 duty | Maps to |
|---|---|
| Primary responsibility = visual welfare of the public | Beneficence |
| Promote the best possible standards of care | Beneficence |
| Continuously improve proficiency | Continuous competence |
| Render services with equal diligence to all (race, creed, status) | Justice |
| Respect confidential information | Autonomy / trust |
| Refer for medical attention when indicated | Non-maleficence |

A violation of the Code is **unethical conduct**, punishable by suspension or prohibition from practice (RA 8050 Sec 26).

### Additional Professional Principles

- **Confidentiality:** records and diagnoses are privileged; no disclosure without consent.
- **Referral obligation:** refer when a condition is beyond your competence or scope.
- **Continuous competence:** keep current through continuing education (ties to the 3-year license renewal in [[H1-01-RA8050-Optometry-Law]]).
- **Collegiality:** professional respect; do not disparage colleagues.
- **Protection of the vulnerable:** extra care for children, elderly, and cognitively impaired patients.

### Worked Scenarios

- **Patient refuses referral for a suspicious disc:** autonomy lets them refuse; document clearly, re-counsel the risk, keep monitoring, do not abandon.
- **Company offers a gift to prescribe their lenses exclusively:** justice and beneficence say lens choice must follow clinical merit, not commercial ties; decline.
- **A minor arrives alone for a contact lens fit:** autonomy requires parent/guardian consent; defer until obtained.

- **Autonomy = informed consent** (the right, not the form).
- **Non-maleficence = primum non nocere = benefit must beat harm** (not "never risk").
- **Justice = same standard for everyone.**
- **Sec 40 = the four principles in optometric clothing.**
- **Most-tested: autonomy (consent) and non-maleficence (referral/contraindication).**
- **Refusal of referral: document, re-counsel, do not abandon.**

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** informed consent is just getting a signature. Wrong. It is the patient **understanding** diagnosis, options, risks, and alternatives. It is the expression of autonomy.
- **Trap:** non-maleficence means avoiding all risk. Wrong. It means the expected benefit must outweigh the expected harm.
- **Trap:** if a competent patient refuses care, you have done your duty and can discharge them. Wrong. You document, re-counsel, and continue to monitor; abandonment is unethical.
- **Trap:** a minor can consent to their own contact lens fitting. Wrong. A parent or guardian consents; the child gives assent.
- **Trap:** ethics and law are the same. Wrong. Law is enforceable rules with penalties; ethics is professional moral obligation (though RA 8050 makes a Code violation punishable).
- **Trap:** treating a charity patient with a quicker, lesser exam is acceptable resource management. Wrong. Justice requires the same standard of care.

## Practice Questions

**1.** An optometrist instills a dilating drop without telling the patient it will blur near vision and cause light sensitivity, and without asking permission. Which principle is violated?

- A. Justice
- B. Beneficence
- C. Autonomy (informed consent)
- D. Collegiality

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Autonomy.** Failing to disclose effects and obtain consent violates the patient's right to make an informed decision.
</details>

**2.** Choosing not to fit extended-wear lenses in a non-compliant patient with a history of microbial keratitis primarily reflects which principle?

- A. Autonomy
- B. Non-maleficence
- C. Justice
- D. Confidentiality

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Non-maleficence.** Avoiding a foreseeable harm whose risk outweighs the benefit is "do no harm."
</details>

**3.** A competent adult refuses referral for a suspicious optic disc. The most ethical action is:

- A. Refuse to see the patient again
- B. Override the refusal and refer anyway
- C. Document the refusal, re-counsel the risk, and continue monitoring
- D. Report the patient to the Board

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** Autonomy allows refusal, but beneficence and non-maleficence require continued care: document, re-counsel, and keep monitoring.
</details>

**4.** Providing a faster, lesser examination to a PhilHealth patient than to a private patient with the same complaint violates:

- A. Autonomy
- B. Beneficence
- C. Justice
- D. Non-maleficence

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Justice.** Distributive justice requires the same standard of care regardless of ability to pay or status.
</details>

**5.** Under RA 8050 Section 40, which duty corresponds most directly to the principle of non-maleficence?

- A. Consider the visual welfare of the public as the primary responsibility
- B. Refer for medical attention when indicated
- C. Render services with equal diligence to all
- D. Respect confidentiality

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Referring when a condition exceeds your competence prevents harm, the core of non-maleficence. Visual welfare is beneficence; equal diligence is justice; confidentiality protects trust/autonomy.
</details>
