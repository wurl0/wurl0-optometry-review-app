Optometry Law, Ethics and Jurisprudence (Board Review 2026)

Comprehensive reviewer built from "Optometry: Legal and Professional Aspects" (Claro M. Cinco, O.D.)

Personal study notes. Philippine Optometry Licensure Exam, Ethics and Jurisprudence (Subject H, 10%).

This is a study digest, reorganized and rewritten for recall. It condenses the high-yield law and ethics content of the source text. The statutes it covers (RA 8050, the Code of Ethics, PRC laws) are public documents. Numbers, names, and section references are the parts examiners love, so they are pulled out and repeated deliberately.

How to use this

Read Part 2 (RA 8050) and Part 3 (Code of Ethics) first. Those two carry most of the exam weight. The history in Part 1 is pure recall points (dates, names, firsts). Parts 5 to 9 are the "professional aspects" that show up as scenario questions. The High-Yield Numbers table near the end is your night-before sheet.

PART 1: History and Milestones of Philippine Optometry

The early record

  • Jose Rizal trained in ophthalmology under Louis de Wecker in Paris (1885), also under Otto Becker (Germany) and Ernst Fuchs (Vienna). He operated on the cataract of his mother, Teodora Alonzo. A trial case attributed to him survives in the de Ocampo collection.
  • First optometric clinic that actually did refraction, and the first lens-grinding shop, was set up in Manila by R.M. Clark in 1902.

The three laws (memorize this chain)

LawYearWhat it did
Optometry Law of 19171917First regulatory law. Written in Spanish. Created the "Junta Examinadora."
Amendment1919Amended the 1917 law.
RA 19981957Amended the 1917 law; a Code of Ethics was named.
RA 80501995The Revised Optometry Law. Replaced RA 1998. Current law.
  • Optometry gained legal recognition as a profession in the Philippines in 1917. In that same year the US states of South Carolina, Wyoming, and Pennsylvania, plus the territories of Hawaii and Alaska, passed their optometry laws.
  • The 1917 law created the Junta Examinadora (Board of Examiners). It carried a grandfather clause, jokingly called "examinacion de gracia," for those who already had an office at least one year before March 11, 1917 (Article 812). First Junta members: G.T. Herrmann, H.O. Haynor, William G. Moore, E.V. Bartholomai, and Justo Gonzalez.

Education firsts

  • First optometry school: the Philippine College of Optometry (operated up to 1925). William G. Moore was president, Justo Gonzalez was dean. First graduates: Bibiana Roxas de Jesus, C. Campomanes, and Pio Catoto.
  • Registration Certificate No. 1, per PRC records, was issued on March 11, 1918 to Dr. Manuel Sabater. (Federico Sarabia Sr. is often cited as the reputed first; he was the first Filipino to study optometry in the US, at the Northern Illinois College of Optometry, Chicago, 1905.)
  • First university-affiliated optometry college: Centro Escolar University (CEU), 1928, with Dr. Roy A. Thorson as its first dean.
  • Later schools: Philippine College of Optometry, 1946 (now De Ocampo Memorial College); Manila College of Optometry, 1951.
  • In 1980 the Council of Deans organized in Cebu, chaired by Dr. Claro M. Cinco (the author), and pushed the course from four years to six.

Professional associations

  • 1917: Philippine Association of Optometrists (first one; went inactive).
  • 1932: Philippine Optometric Association (Vicente Bernardo, president; lasted about four years).
  • 1936: Optometric Association of the Philippines (OAP), Justo Gonzalez as first president. Became a non-stock corporation in 1948.
  • 1960s: PODA (Philippine Optometrists-Dispensers Association, grew out of POSA) and PROA (Philippine Registered Optometrists Association).
  • 1979 merger (the PRC wanted a single association): OAP + PODA + PROA became Samahan Ng Optometrist Sa Pilipinas (SOP). Around 37 OAP chapters joined.
  • National optometric congress has run yearly since 1947. In 1997 the 50th National Optometric Congress (also the 19th SOP Convention) was held in Baguio City.

Regional and world bodies

  • The idea for a regional body was seeded at the joint AOA and Canadian congress in Toronto, July 1977. The 1st Asian-Pacific Optometric Congress (APOC), Manila, July 1978 founded IFAPAO (International Federation of Asian and Pacific Associations of Optometrists). First officers, elected at the 2nd APOC in Bali (1979): Claro M. Cinco (president), Fumio Morie of Japan (vice-president), Ali Asgar of Fiji (director), Damien Smith (secretary-general).
  • IFAPAO later became APCO (Asian-Pacific Council of Optometry).
  • The IOOL (International Optometric and Optical League) became the WCO (World Council of Optometry).
  • The International Optometrist of the Year award: the very first, in 1988, went to Dr. Claro M. Cinco (handed over in Sydney by IOOL president G. Burtt Holmes). Later recipients include Svein Hommerstad (Norway) and Henry W. Hofstetter (USA, the "Hofstetter formula" name, who also wrote this book's foreword).

PART 2: RA 8050, The Revised Optometry Law of 1995 (the core)

Full title: "An Act Regulating the Practice of Optometry, Upgrading Optometric Education, Integrating Optometrists, and for Other Purposes." Approved June 7, 1995 by President Fidel V. Ramos. It was a consolidation of House Bill 14100 and Senate Bill 1998 (Senate passed June 3, 1995; House June 5, 1995).

Foundational sections

  • Sec. 1 Title: "Revised Optometry Law of 1995."
  • Sec. 2 State Policy: optometry is promoted as a regular component of the primary health care system.

Sec. 3 Definitions

  • Optometry: the science and art of examining the eye, analyzing ocular function, prescribing and dispensing ophthalmic lenses, prisms, contact lenses and their accessories and solutions, low vision aids, ocular exercises, vision training, orthoptics, installing prosthetics, using diagnostic pharmaceutical agents, and other preventive or corrective measures.
  • Optometrist: certified by the Board and registered with the PRC.
  • Optometrist in good standing: holds a valid certificate of registration and a valid professional license.
  • Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agents (DPA), the five in the 2004 text (know the concentrations):
  1. Phenylephrine 2.5%
  2. Tropicamide 0.5%
  3. Proparacaine 0.5%
  4. Oxybuprocaine HCl 0.4%
  5. Fluorescein strips

Set by the Board, approved by the DOH on the recommendation of the BFAD. (Current-law update: the 2008 IRR added Cyclopentolate, so the modern count is six agents. If a question quotes the RA 8050 original text, five is correct; if it asks the current DPA list, six.)

  • Certificate of registration: signed by the PRC Commissioner and all Board members, bearing the seal.
  • Professional license: a renewable card issued by the PRC.
  • Code of professional ethics: the standards of conduct and moral duties, adopted and promulgated by the Board upon PRC approval.

Sec. 4 Acts that constitute the practice of optometry

(a) examination by subjective and objective procedures, including DPAs; (b) prescribing and dispensing ophthalmic lenses, prisms, contact lenses, frames and accessories; (c) ocular exercises, vision training, orthoptics, prosthetic devices; (d) counseling on vision and eye care and hygiene; (e) establishing offices and clinics; (f) collecting professional fees. Whether or not it was done for pay does not change whether an act counts as practice.

Sec. 5 Who is exempted from the practice

  1. Physicians with post-graduate training in the diagnosis and treatment of eye diseases.
  2. Physicians examining the eye during a physical exam.
  3. Government-trained public-health workers in the blindness-prevention program (they may do visual acuity testing and visual screening only).
  4. Foreigners from reciprocity countries.
  5. Internationally known foreign experts.
  6. An optometry student or graduate working under the direct supervision and control of a registered optometrist.

Sec. 6 Disclosure of authority to practice

The optometrist must show the professional license number and its expiration date on documents he signs, and must display the certificate of registration in a conspicuous place.

The Board of Optometry (Sec. 7 to 16)

  • Sec. 7 Composition: five (5) members, appointed by the President on the PRC's recommendation, chosen from a list of ten (10) nominees submitted by the integrated national organization.
  • Sec. 8 Qualifications of a Board member: natural-born Filipino citizen; registered optometrist in good standing; at least 35 years old; good moral character; in active practice for at least 10 years. A faculty member or anyone with a financial interest in an optometry school must give it up first. An officer of any optometric association is disqualified.
  • Sec. 9 Term: five years, non-renewable. The first appointees were staggered (chair 5 years; others 4, 3, 2, 1) so one seat opens every year. A member in the last year of his term automatically becomes chairperson. A vacancy is filled only for the unexpired portion.
  • Sec. 10 Removal or suspension: by the President, for neglect of duty, incompetence, malpractice, or unprofessional, unethical, immoral, or dishonorable conduct. The President may suspend a member for up to 60 days during an investigation.
  • Sec. 11 Compensation: as set by PRC rules.
  • Sec. 12 Functions and powers (a to p): promulgate and enforce rules; prepare and rate the licensure exam; oversee exam and registration; issue, suspend, revoke, reissue certificates and renew licenses; require continuing education for renewal; keep a registry; adopt and disseminate the Code of Ethics; administer oaths; conduct hearings and investigations and issue subpoena ad testificandum and subpoena duces tecum, and punish for contempt; conduct ocular inspections; enjoin illegal practice; impose fines and penalties; evaluate optometric education; update the DPA list (mydriatics, cycloplegics, miotics, anesthetics, diagnostic dyes) with DOH and BFAD approval; and study practice conditions.
  • Sec. 13 Quorum: three (3) members.
  • Sec. 14 Votes in an administrative case: a decision needs three (3) votes. A disqualified or inhibited member is replaced by an optometrist in good standing, designated with PRC approval.
  • Sec. 15 Supervision and records: the Board is under the general supervision of the PRC, and the PRC keeps all records.
  • Sec. 16 Annual report to the PRC at the end of each calendar year.

The board examinations (Sec. 17 to 22)

  • Sec. 17: given at least once a year, at places and dates the PRC sets.
  • Sec. 18 Scope: set by the Board, accounting for recent developments and CHED standards.
  • Sec. 19 Who may take it: a Filipino citizen, of good moral character, holding a Doctor of Optometry (O.D.) degree from an accredited school after a six-year course. The listed subjects include optometric ethics and jurisprudence, practice management, public health optometry, low vision and pediatric optometry, contact lens practice, orthoptics and vision training, and neuro-optometry and visual fields.
  • Sec. 20 Passing: a general average of at least 75% in all subjects, with no grade below 50% in any single subject.
  • Sec. 21 Results: the Board reports ratings to the PRC within 60 days of the last exam day; the PRC publishes the list of passers (alphabetical, no ratings).
  • Sec. 22 Reexamination: an applicant who fails a third time must show proof of a refresher course from an accredited school before sitting again.

Registration and licensure (Sec. 23 to 29)

  • Sec. 23 Oath: taken before the certificate is issued.
  • Sec. 24 Disqualification from registration: lacks the Sec. 19 qualifications; failed the exam; convicted by final judgment of a crime involving moral turpitude; found guilty by the Board of immoral or dishonorable conduct; or declared of unsound mind.
  • Sec. 25 Registration: the PRC registers passers who took the oath and grants their licenses.
  • Sec. 26 Revocation or suspension: the Board, after notice and hearing, may revoke a certificate or suspend a license for unprofessional or unethical conduct, malpractice, violation of the Act or its rules, or conviction of a crime involving moral turpitude.
  • Sec. 27 Appeals: a Board revocation or suspension becomes final unless appealed to the PRC within 15 days of receipt.
  • Sec. 28 Reinstatement, reissuance, replacement: the Board may reinstate a suspended certificate or replace a revoked one (and may waive re-examination), and may replace a lost, destroyed, or mutilated certificate.
  • Sec. 29 Renewal: licenses are renewable every three (3) years, with continuing education and fees.

Important trap: Section 29's continuing-education requirement for renewal was repealed by Section 20 of RA 8981 (the PRC Modernization Act of 2000). So under the modern setup, CPD is not a hard condition for the base license renewal in the way RA 8050 first wrote it.

Education, prohibitions, penalties (Sec. 30 to 34)

  • Sec. 30 Technical Panel on Optometric Education: convened by CHED under Section 12 of RA 7722 (the Higher Education Act). It sets the minimum curriculum and teaching standards and monitors schools.
  • Sec. 31 Competence in Pharmacology: anyone admitted to practice before RA 8050 must take a pharmacology course and pass a special certification exam before using DPAs.
  • Sec. 32 Prohibited acts: (a) giving false or forged evidence to get a certificate or license; (b) posing as a registered optometrist without one; (c) advertising that one optometrist is better than others, or that he charges lower fees or sells cheaper frames or lenses; (d) DPA use by an untrained optometrist (whether or not injury results); (e) using pharmaceutical agents beyond those authorized; (f) unauthorized practice under Sec. 5; (g) any other violation by a natural or juridical person.
  • Sec. 33 Penalty: imprisonment of not less than 1 year and not more than 8 years, or a fine of not less than P10,000 and not more than P40,000, or both, at the court's discretion, plus the administrative penalties in Sec. 26. If the violator is a company or corporation, its managing officers are held liable.
  • Sec. 34 Foreign reciprocity: a foreigner may sit the exam only if his country lets Filipinos practice there without restriction. The Board may still give a special permit to an internationally known foreign expert.

Integration and closing (Sec. 35 to 43)

  • Sec. 35 Rules: take effect 30 days after publication.
  • Sec. 36 Integration: all optometrists join one national organization recognized by the Board and PRC. Already-registered optometrists may join within three years of effectivity.
  • Sec. 37 Purposes of the integrated body: promulgate the Code of Ethics, define and enforce responsibilities, raise standards.
  • Sec. 38 Fees and dues. Sec. 39 Other organizations are still allowed.
  • Sec. 40 Code of Professional Ethics: the Board adopts the Code promulgated by the integrated body and gives it to new optometrists at their oath-taking.
  • Sec. 41 Separability. Sec. 42 Repealing: repeals RA 1998. Sec. 43 Effectivity: 15 days after publication in the Official Gazette or two national newspapers.

PART 3: The Code of Ethics for Optometrists

Adopted by the Board under Sections 37 and 40 of RA 8050. The duty of every optometrist:

  • keep the visual welfare of the patient uppermost at all times;
  • promote better care of the visual needs of mankind;
  • keep abreast of educational and technical developments;
  • see that no person is deprived of visual care regardless of race, creed, or financial status;
  • advise the patient when consultation or referral is needed;
  • respect the patient's right to privacy and keep information in confidence, using data only for the patient's benefit;
  • conduct himself as an exemplary citizen;
  • keep his office and practice at professional standards;
  • keep cordial and unselfish relations with his own and other professions.

AOA Supplement: Moral, Professional and Ethical Obligations

Responsibilities in general: optometry is a profession; the prime concern is service to humanity, and financial gain is secondary. The optometrist should be an upright person of high moral character, keep current, and hold a scientific attitude.

Toward patients: patience above all; a patient's character flaws are held in trust and never revealed except when the law requires; be frank and respectful; give timely notice of any sign of disease, without exaggerating or minimizing it; base fees on the type of service, skill, and knowledge involved.

Toward the public: behave as a gentleman and uphold the profession's dignity; do not enter a contract that makes fair dealing with the public impossible; do not mislead the public; be an exemplary citizen and help enforce optometric laws; assist judicial authorities on optometric matters.

Unethical and unprofessional acts (a long, testable list):

  • employing lay persons to do the visual examination;
  • examining outside the regular clinic (unless the request was unsolicited);
  • prescribing or fitting glasses where spectacle correction is contraindicated;
  • advertising to imply superiority;
  • practicing under a name other than the one on the certificate, or under a corporation or clinic name that is not the optometrist's own;
  • soliciting patients, using solicitors or agents, or house-to-house canvassing, or paying to get patients;
  • using the title "Doctor" or "Dr." without adding "optometry" or "optometrist";
  • displaying a name or title in letters larger than six inches at street level;
  • advertising any fee or price (exam, lenses, glasses, frames, mountings);
  • signboards with pictures of eyes or eyeglasses that strip away the professional image;
  • using mobile units to canvass or solicit.

Toward other optometrists: keep a cooperative and harmonious relationship; be a member of a professional association; expose corrupt or dishonest members to the Board; consult in difficult cases; a consultant must return the patient to the original optometrist; make no derogatory hints about a colleague whose patient you see; make no comment about a predecessor you succeed; treat a colleague's patient in an emergency as your own, then return him.

Toward other professions: treat their fields with dignity and proper regard; cooperate to protect patient welfare; take part in public-health measures led by other groups.

PART 4: Government Regulatory Bodies

The Professional Regulation Commission (PRC)

  • Supervision of the regulatory boards moved from the Bureau of Civil Service to the PRC. The PRC was created by PD 223 (as amended by PD 657). It was later reorganized by RA 8981, the PRC Modernization Act of 2000, which repealed PD 223.
  • Composition: one full-time Chairperson and two full-time Commissioners, appointed by the President for a seven (7) year term with no reappointment. A Chairperson or Commissioner must be at least 40 years old, hold a valid license, and have at least five years of executive or management experience. One Commissioner must be a past Chairperson or member of a regulatory board.
  • The Chairperson is the presiding officer and chief executive officer. Pay: the Chairperson at the level of a Department Secretary, the Commissioners at the level of an Undersecretary.
  • The PRC may create regional offices under RA 8981.

The Board of Optometry

  • PD 223 (as amended by PD 657) renamed the "Board of Examiners in Optometry" to the "Board of Optometry."
  • Its composition, qualifications, and functions repeat Sections 7, 8, and 12 of RA 8050 (five members, ten nominees, age 35, ten years of practice, and so on).
  • PMA v. Dr. Cruz: the President is not bound by the nominees the association recommends; a recommendation is only advice.

Prosecution guidelines (PRC Resolution No. 114, January 9, 1985)

  1. The Board has jurisdiction over administrative cases where the respondent is a registered professional.
  2. A person without a valid certificate who practices is guilty of illegal practice and may be prosecuted at the relation of any interested party.
  3. Other prohibited acts are criminal offenses.
  4. Starting a criminal action against a non-professional is the Board's primary responsibility.
  5. The action is filed with the Provincial or City Fiscal, who files the information in court on a prima facie case.
  6. A registered professional may face a criminal action in addition to the administrative case.

PART 5: The Practice of Optometry (professional aspects)

  • The practice of optometry is a privilege or franchise the state grants under its police power (the power of the legislature to pass reasonable laws for public welfare, health, morality, and safety). Classic cases: People v. Pomar (46 Phil. 440) and US v. Gomez Jesus (31 Phil. 218).
  • Useful definitions: Diagnosis is identifying the nature of the anomaly. Treatment is applying a remedy. Prescription is ordering the use of a remedy (State v. Whipple). Dispensing is issuing an optical appliance that corrects a defect. An ophthalmic dispenser supplies and adapts prescribed products.

The lay-corporation problem (a favorite scenario)

  • A corporation cannot practice optometry. It cannot meet the personal qualifications (good moral character, a degree, an oath), so it cannot practice directly, and it cannot do indirectly (by hiring licensed optometrists to sell lenses) what it cannot do directly.
  • The IRR backs this up: Rule VIII (5) bars using a trade name other than the optometrist's own registered name, and Rule X, Section 47(g) bars non-optometrists and juridical persons from engaging in practice by employing optometrists. An optometrist who aids or abets such an arrangement can be suspended or revoked.
  • An optometrist may work for a non-optometrist only as an in-house or retainer optometrist serving that employer's own employees, not the general public.

Faith healing and religious freedom

The Optometry Law does not exempt faith healers, but the Constitution (Art. III, Sec. 5) protects religious freedom in two parts: the freedom to believe and the freedom to act. A person who uses prayer alone is exercising religion. Once he picks up diagnostic instruments or optical devices, he is engaged in illegal practice. US precedent: People v. Handzik (1951) and People v. Wendel (1946).

PART 6: Rights of the Optometrist

Rights inherent in the privilege to practice:

  • Right to choose patients. The privilege does not force him to accept anyone. Even if he is the only optometrist in town, and even in an emergency, nothing in the Code compels him to serve.
  • Right to limit his practice by (a) specialty, (b) location or clinic, (c) days and hours, or (d) the dictates of his conscience. He can refuse cases outside his chosen specialty, the way an internist is not forced to do surgery. If he already accepted a case that needs continuing care, though, he must see it through even outside his usual hours.
  • Right to determine the appropriate management procedures.

Rights incidental to the privilege: exemption of his instruments and library from execution (tools of the trade); holding public or private office; performing certain services; compensation; and membership in optometric societies.

PART 7: Rights of Patients and Informed Consent

The patient has the right to be informed about a procedure and to consent to it. This rests on three ideas:

  1. The relationship is fiduciary (built on trust; the optometrist must disclose the true facts, and misrepresentation makes him liable).
  2. The patient has a right to self-determination. The classic line is Schloendorff v. Society of New York Hospital (Justice Cardozo, 1914): "Every human being of adult years and sound mind has the right to determine what shall be done with his own body." See also Natanson v. Kline (1960) and Salgo v. Leland Stanford (1957).
  3. The relationship is contractual; a procedure outside that contract is void.

When consent is not needed:

  • In an emergency (for example, imminent injury from contact lens over-wear or a broken lens; the optometrist may remove the lens without consent, then refer). But if the patient is conscious and competent, or a representative is present, consent must still be obtained.
  • When the law itself compels the procedure for public health, such as compulsory immunization under RA 3573 and PD 996. Refusing vaccination is punishable.

Right to refuse treatment: the patient may refuse care even when it could save his life; he is master of his own body. The state can still compel certain procedures (like immunization) under its police power.

Termination of the relationship happens when the patient recovers, the patient withdraws, or the optometrist withdraws. On withdrawal the optometrist must hand records to his successor and inform the patient. The patient may discharge the optometrist at any time.

PART 8: Professional Fees

What sets the fee: the labor and time involved; the importance and responsibility of the case; the optometrist's standing (specialists and established practitioners charge more than new graduates); the value of the result to the patient ("good result, good pay"); the patient's financial ability (charity work is expected; no one should be denied care for money); the purchasing power of the peso; the customary local fee; and the cost of the materials dispensed.

Who pays: primarily the patient (of legal age, sound mind, able to contract). Medical care counts as "support" under Article 290 of the Civil Code. If the patient dies or becomes incapable, the claim runs, in order, against the (1) spouse, (2) descendant of nearest degree, (3) ascendant of nearest degree, (4) brothers and sisters (Article 291). A third party's promise to pay does not release the patient. On a referral, the attending optometrist is not liable for the consultant's fee.

When the patient may refuse to pay: when the service was expressly free; when the fee was contingent on success and the treatment failed despite the patient's cooperation; or when the treatment was contrary to what was agreed. The book lists ten formal defenses to a fee claim, including that the charge is exorbitant, no service was rendered, the defendant is not the liable person, there was no consent, the optometrist was negligent, or the optometrist violated the contract.

PART 9: Liabilities of the Optometrist

Complaints come in three forms, and they can combine:

KindFiled withPenalty

CriminalCourtsfine, imprisonment, or both

Criminal liabilities

  • Negligence is a failure to pay proper attention and use due diligence. The Test for Negligence (Picart v. Smith, 37 Phil. 809): would a prudent person in the actor's place foresee harm? If yes, the law imposes a duty to take precaution, and failing to do so is negligence. This is the reasonable-person standard.
  • Fraud, deceit, and false advertising (to build up a clientele or to overstate one's skill).
  • Issuance of false certificates (for example, a false report of a patient's vision to get work exemption or a job).
  • Defamation (Art. 353, Revised Penal Code): a public and malicious imputation that causes dishonor. Libel is written (Art. 355). Slander is oral. Elements: an imputation causing dishonor, malice, and bad faith.

Civil liabilities (two causes of action)

  • Breach of contract. The optometrist is liable only if he specifically promised a cure or result and failed. He is not liable just for failing to cure when he never contracted to cure (Safian v. Aetna). In a breach-of-contract suit, expert testimony is not required and "pain and suffering" is not an element. The prudent rule: never guarantee results.
  • Tort, that is, quasi-delict or breach of legal duty (Art. 2176, Civil Code): "Whoever by act or omission causes damage to another, there being fault or negligence, is obliged to pay for the damage done." When there is no prior contract between the parties, this fault or negligence is a quasi-delict.

Rights of the respondent in an administrative case mirror the Constitution and the Rules of Court: presumed innocent, heard by counsel, informed of the charge, a speedy and impartial hearing, the right to confront witnesses, and no self-incrimination.

BOARD TRAPS

  • RA 8050 replaced RA 1998, not the 1917 law directly. The chain is 1917 to RA 1998 (1957) to RA 8050 (1995).
  • The DPA count depends on the source: RA 8050 as written lists 5; the current DPA list with Cyclopentolate is 6.
  • Section 29's CPD-for-renewal rule was repealed by RA 8981. Do not answer that CPD is a strict statutory condition of renewal under RA 8050 as it stands.
  • Board term is 5 years and non-renewable. The PRC Commissioner term is 7 years, no reappointment. Do not swap them.
  • Board decisions are appealed to the PRC within 15 days; then to the Office of the President. Court cases go to the Court of Appeals.
  • "Board of Examiners in Optometry" was renamed "Board of Optometry" by PD 223 (as amended by PD 657).
  • Passing is 75% average with nothing below 50%. A single grade below 50% fails you even with a 75% average.
  • In a breach-of-contract suit, expert testimony is not required and pain and suffering is not an element. That differs from a negligence (quasi-delict) suit.
  • The public-health-worker exemption covers VA testing and visual screening only, nothing more.

QUICK SELF-TEST (cover the answers)

  1. How many members compose the Board of Optometry, and from how many nominees? Five, from a list of ten.
  2. What is the passing requirement in the board exam? A 75% general average with no grade below 50%.
  3. Which law replaced RA 1998? RA 8050, the Revised Optometry Law of 1995.
  4. List the five DPAs in RA 8050 with concentrations. Phenylephrine 2.5%, Tropicamide 0.5%, Proparacaine 0.5%, Oxybuprocaine 0.4%, Fluorescein strips.
  5. What is the penalty under Section 33? 1 to 8 years imprisonment, or a P10,000 to P40,000 fine, or both.
  6. Term of a Board member versus a PRC Commissioner? 5 years non-renewable versus 7 years no reappointment.
  7. Where do you appeal a Board decision, and within how many days? To the PRC within 15 days.
  8. What renamed the Board of Examiners in Optometry to the Board of Optometry? PD 223, as amended by PD 657.
  9. Who received the first International Optometrist of the Year award? Dr. Claro M. Cinco (1988).
  10. State the Test for Negligence and its case. Would a prudent person foresee harm? Picart v. Smith, 37 Phil. 809.
