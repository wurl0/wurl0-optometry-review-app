## B5-01 — Binocular Vision and Fusion
*Personal study notes | TOS: B.5 | 50 items — HIGHEST YIELD in Subject B*

---

## The Simple Version

Binocular vision is the coordinated use of both eyes to produce a single, three-dimensional mental image. It requires alignment (both eyes pointing at the same target), sensory fusion (the brain merging two slightly different images), and motor fusion (the eye muscles maintaining alignment). When alignment breaks down or fusion fails, the brain adapts — either by seeing double (diplopia), suppressing one eye's image, or developing an abnormal retinal correspondence where the deviated eye recalibrates its directional sense. Understanding BV anomalies requires three things: knowing where the deviation is (distance, near, or both), knowing the AC/A ratio (how much convergence accompanies each diopter of accommodation), and knowing which criteria tell you if the fusion is sufficient (Sheard's, Percival's).

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "Why does a child with uncorrected high hyperopia often develop esotropia?" The answer explains AC/A ratio, accommodative esotropia, and why glasses alone can straighten the eyes.

---

## But Why?

**Why does a high AC/A ratio cause convergence excess?**
Because AC/A tells you how much convergence is triggered per diopter of accommodation. If this ratio is abnormally high, every time the child accommodates for near — which requires +4D if they are +4D hyperopic — they generate massive convergence (4 × high AC/A). The eyes cross at near, but are straight at distance because convergence demand at distance is zero.

**Why does suppression develop in strabismus?**
Because the deviated eye projects its image to a different visual direction from the fixating eye, causing diplopia. The brain resolves the conflict by actively inhibiting the signal from the non-fixating eye — suppression. Without this adaptation, constant diplopia would be intolerable.

**Why is NRC normal in heterophoria but ARC develops in strabismus?**
Because in heterophoria, binocular vision is maintained most of the time and the foveas retain their common visual direction (NRC). In constant strabismus with a fixed angle of deviation, the brain has time to rewire the retinal correspondence — the non-foveal area of the deviated eye that consistently receives the fixation target's image gradually takes over the fovea's visual direction (ARC).

**Why is Sheard's criterion a 2:1 ratio?**
Because research shows that patients are comfortable when their fusional reserve is at least twice the demand placed on their vergence system. If the demand exceeds half the reserve, the system is working at maximum capacity and symptoms (asthenopia, diplopia) result.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**Eye movement systems — speed and latency:**

```
SYSTEM VELOCITY LATENCY PURPOSE
Saccadic 500–1000°/s 200 ms Fast refixation; reading; scanning
Smooth pursuit 30°/s — Track moving targets; keeps image on fovea
Optokinetic 90°/s 125 ms Full-field visual stabilization
Vergence 20°/s 160 ms (slowest) Binocular alignment; convergence/divergence
VOR 90°/s 15 ms (fastest) Gaze stabilization during head movement
```

**AC/A ratio — the engine of accommodative vergence:**

```
Normal AC/A: 4:1 to 6:1 (4–6 PD of convergence per 1D of accommodation)

Formula (Gradient method): AC/A = (phoria at near – phoria at distance) ÷ accommodative stimulus
Formula (Calculated): AC/A = IPD (cm) + (near phoria – far phoria) ÷ near power (D)

HIGH AC/A (>6:1) → excess convergence for accommodation
 → Divergence excess: exo at far, ortho/less exo at near (high AC/A)
 → Convergence excess: ortho/eso at far, high eso at near (high AC/A)
 → Accommodative esotropia: hyperope who crosses at near

LOW AC/A (<4:1) → insufficient convergence for accommodation
 → Convergence insufficiency: exo at near, ortho at far (low AC/A)
 → Divergence insufficiency: eso at far, ortho/less eso at near (low AC/A)

NORMAL AC/A (4–6:1) → equal deviation at distance and near
 → Basic exophoria, basic esophoria, FVD
```

**Sheard's criterion:**
Fusional reserve must be at least **2× the demand** (phoria).
- If compensating reserve < 2× phoria → symptomatic (cannot maintain fusion comfortably)
- Example: Exophoria of 8PD; Positive Fusional Convergence (PFC) must be ≥16PD to pass

**Percival's criterion:**
The phoria must fall within the **middle third** of the fusional range.
- Take the full vergence range (NFC to PFC)
- Patient is comfortable if phoria is in the middle ⅓

**Fixation disparity criterion (Morgan's correlates):**
Used to assess whether BV is comfortable under natural conditions — not dissociated like phoria testing.

### Structure or Process Map

**BV anomalies master table:**

| Condition | Distance phoria | Near phoria | AC/A | Key feature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Convergence Insufficiency (CI) | Ortho / small exo | HIGH exo | **Low** | Receded NPC; near symptoms |
| Divergence Excess (DE) | HIGH exo | Ortho / less exo | **High** | Closes eye in sunlight; good near |
| Basic Exophoria | Exo | Exo (equal) | Normal | Similar deviation at all distances |
| Divergence Insufficiency (DI) | HIGH eso | Ortho / less eso | **Low** | Diplopia at distance; may be neurological |
| Convergence Excess (CE) | Ortho / small eso | HIGH eso | **High** | Accommodative; hyperopia + near eso |
| Basic Esophoria | Eso | Eso (equal) | Normal | High tonic vergence at all distances |
| Fusional Vergence Dysfunction (FVD) | Ortho | Ortho | Normal | Reduced fusional reserves; normal phorias |

> **High AC/A rule:** Excess conditions (DE, CE) = High AC/A (excess convergence per diopter)
> **Low AC/A rule:** Insufficiency conditions (CI, DI) = Low AC/A (insufficient convergence per diopter)

**Worth's three grades of binocular vision:**

```
GRADE 1 — Simultaneous Macular Perception (SMP)
 Most elementary; both maculae stimulated simultaneously
 Presence = no suppression of either eye
 Absence = complete suppression of one eye

GRADE 2 — Fusion (flat/superimposition fusion)
 Two images merged into one
 Has amplitude — can overcome prism dissociation to fuse
 Tests: Worth 4-dot test, Prism fusion ranges

GRADE 3 — Stereopsis (depth perception from binocular disparity)
 Highest grade; 3D perception from slightly disparate retinal images
 Tests: Randot, TNO, Frisby, Lang
 Normal: 20–40 seconds of arc
```

**Retinal correspondence:**

```
NORMAL RETINAL CORRESPONDENCE (NRC)
 Both foveas = same visual direction
 Nasal retina one eye ↔ temporal retina other eye
 Present in normals and heterophorias
 Tested by: Hering-Bielschowsky after-image test → harmonious result

ANOMALOUS RETINAL CORRESPONDENCE (ARC)
 Fovea of fixating eye ↔ extrafoveal area of deviating eye = same direction
 Develops in constant strabismus with fixed angle
 The brain rewires to avoid diplopia
 Harmonious ARC: anomalous neutral point = angle of deviation (fully adapted)
 Unharmonious ARC: anomalous neutral point ≠ angle of deviation
 Tested by: Bagolini striated lenses, after-image test (crossed = ARC)
```

**Physiologic diplopia:**

```
FIXATED OBJECT (on horopter) → single vision
OBJECT BEYOND HOROPTER:
 Stimulates nasal retina of both eyes simultaneously
 Nasal retina = temporal visual direction
 Both eyes see the object to the temporal side → UNCROSSED (homonymous) diplopia
 Objects in DISTANCE (esophoric tendency) = uncrossed

OBJECT NEARER THAN HOROPTER:
 Stimulates temporal retina of both eyes
 Temporal retina = nasal visual direction
 Both eyes see object to the nasal side → CROSSED (heteronymous) diplopia
 Objects NEAR (exophoric tendency) = crossed
```

**Mnemonic: "FAR = UNCROSSED; NEAR = CROSSED"**
Or: "Far objects appear same-sided; Near objects cross over"

**Suppression:**

```
FACULTATIVE suppression
 Only during binocular conditions
 Pattern: periphery suppression (central BV maintained)
 Common in: exodeviations (suppresses peripheral diplopia)

OBLIGATORY suppression
 Even when attempting binocular conditions
 Central suppression zone + peripheral
 Common in: constant esotropia, deep amblyopia
 Associated: dense amblyopia, ARC
```

**Amblyopia classification:**

| Type | Cause | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| Strabismic | Constant strabismus; one eye suppressed | Most common type; eccentric fixation possible |
| Anisometropic | Significantly different refractive errors; one eye blurred | Often no strabismus; treatment: correct Rx + patching |
| Deprivation | Physical obstruction (cataract, ptosis, corneal opacity) | Worst prognosis; must treat within critical period |
| Ametropic | Bilateral high refractive error (bilateral high hyperopia, astigmatism) | Both eyes affected; often missed because no obvious strabismus |
| Meridional | High uncorrected astigmatism in early life | One meridian of acuity affected |

> Critical period for amblyopia treatment: most sensitive 0–2 years; treatable up to ~8–10 years; progressively harder after age 7.

### Comparisons That Matter

**Hering's Law vs Sherrington's Law:**

| Feature | Hering's Law | Sherrington's Law |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Binocular (both eyes) | Monocular (one eye) |
| Rule | Yoke muscles receive equal + simultaneous innervation | Agonist contracts = antagonist relaxes equally |
| Example | Dextroversion: R-LR and L-MR get equal impulses | Supraduction: SR contracts; IR relaxes |
| Clinical use | Explains spread of paresis in gaze palsy | Explains mechanical limits of eye movement |

**Esophoria vs Exophoria management approach:**

| | Esophoria | Exophoria |
|---|---|---|
| Principle | Reduce convergence demand | Increase fusional convergence reserve |
| Lens approach | Plus lenses reduce accommodation → reduce AC/A-driven convergence | — |
| Prism approach | BI prism (relaxes convergence) | BO prism (temporarily relieves exo) |
| Vision therapy | Divergence training | Convergence training |
| Patching | Not indicated | Not indicated |

**NRC vs ARC clinical tests:**

| Test | NRC result | ARC result |
|---|---|---|
| After-image test (Hering-Bielschowsky) | Symmetric cross (harmonious) | Asymmetric / crossed |
| Bagolini striated lenses | X pattern passes through fixation | X pattern displaced |
| Worth 4-dot | 4 dots seen (fusion) | Variable |

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "Vergence is the fastest eye movement" — Wrong. Vergence is the SLOWEST (20°/s, 160ms latency). VOR is the fastest reflex (15ms latency); saccade is the fastest voluntary movement.
- **Trap 2:** "High AC/A causes convergence insufficiency" — Wrong. High AC/A causes EXCESS conditions (CE, DE). Convergence Insufficiency has a LOW AC/A.
- **Trap 3:** "Objects beyond the fixation point cause crossed diplopia" — Wrong. Far (beyond fixation) objects cause UNCROSSED diplopia. Near (closer than fixation) objects cause CROSSED diplopia.
- **Trap 4:** "ARC develops in heterophoria" — Wrong. ARC develops in constant strabismus. Heterophoria maintains NRC because binocular vision is maintained most of the time.
- **Trap 5:** "Hering's law applies to one eye (monocular)" — Wrong. Hering's law applies to BINOCULAR yoke muscles. Sherrington's law applies to MONOCULAR agonist-antagonist pairs.
- **Trap 6:** "Suppression permanently damages vision" — Wrong. Suppression is reversible when treated early (within the critical period). The danger is that prolonged suppression leads to amblyopia, which can become permanent if untreated beyond the critical period.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 12-year-old patient reports headaches and blurred vision after 20 minutes of reading. Distance acuity is 20/20 OU. Cover test at distance shows orthophoria; cover test at near reveals 12PD exophoria. NPC breaks at 14 cm and recovers at 22 cm. Positive Fusional Convergence at near is 8/10/6 (break/recovery). What is the most likely diagnosis and what does Sheard's criterion indicate?

- A. Basic exophoria; passes Sheard's
- B. Convergence insufficiency; fails Sheard's
- C. Divergence excess; passes Sheard's
- D. Convergence insufficiency; passes Sheard's

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Convergence insufficiency; fails Sheard's criterion.**

The pattern — orthophoria at distance + significant exophoria at near + receded NPC (>10cm) + near symptoms — defines convergence insufficiency (low AC/A). Sheard's criterion requires positive fusional convergence ≥ 2× the near exophoria: 2 × 12PD = 24PD needed. The PFC blur/break is only 10PD — well below the 24PD required. The patient fails Sheard's, which explains the symptoms (the system is working near capacity). Management: plus adds for near to reduce accommodative convergence demand, plus vision therapy (convergence exercises — pencil push-ups, Brock string).

</details>

---

**2.** A child with right esotropia is examined with the Hering-Bielschowsky after-image test. The after-image from the right eye appears displaced to the left of the after-image from the left eye, indicating a non-symmetric result. What does this finding indicate?

- A. Normal retinal correspondence with strabismus
- B. Anomalous retinal correspondence (harmonious)
- C. Anomalous retinal correspondence (unharmonious)
- D. Suppression of the right eye

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Anomalous retinal correspondence (harmonious ARC).**

In the Hering-Bielschowsky after-image test with NRC, the after-images would appear as a symmetric cross (equal displacement matching the angle of strabismus). Displacement in a non-symmetric pattern that exactly matches the angle of strabismus = harmonious ARC, where the anomalous neutral point has fully adapted to equal the angle of deviation. The brain has rewired — the extrafoveal area of the right eye now shares the same visual direction as the left fovea. Unharmonious ARC would show the neutral point NOT matching the deviation. Suppression would prevent the after-image from being seen at all.

</details>

---

**3.** A patient presents with spontaneous oscillating eye movements with no fast phase. The movements are equal in both directions. He reports the movements were present since birth and improve slightly when he converges. What type of nystagmus does this describe?

- A. Acquired pendular nystagmus
- B. Jerk nystagmus from a vestibular lesion
- C. Infantile nystagmus syndrome (congenital nystagmus) with null point
- D. Gaze-evoked nystagmus

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Infantile nystagmus syndrome (congenital nystagmus) with null point.**

The key features: (1) present since birth — congenital onset; (2) pendular in primary position (equal velocity both ways — no fast/slow phase); (3) dampens with convergence — this is the diagnostic hallmark of infantile nystagmus syndrome (INS), also called congenital nystagmus. Patients often adopt a head turn to bring gaze to their null point where the nystagmus is least intense. The convergence dampening explains why some patients read with the book held very close — the convergence demand reduces the nystagmus. Acquired pendular nystagmus (option A) has a known adult onset (MS, brainstem lesion) and does not dampen with convergence. Jerk nystagmus (option B) has fast/slow phases. Gaze-evoked (option D) only appears at eccentric gaze, not in primary position.

</details>

---

**4.** During binocular rivalry testing, a patient views a vertical grating with one eye and a horizontal grating with the other. The expected percept is:

- A. A diagonal grating (fusion of both)
- B. A plaid pattern (superimposition)
- C. Alternating suppression of each grating
- D. Permanent suppression of the non-dominant eye's grating

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Alternating suppression of each grating.**

Binocular rivalry occurs when the two eyes are shown incompatible images that cannot be fused. The brain cannot fuse a vertical and horizontal grating into a single stable percept. Instead, it alternates — one eye's image is perceived for a few seconds, then the other takes over, back and forth. This alternating suppression is the defining feature of binocular rivalry. It is NOT the same as permanent suppression (which occurs in constant strabismus with deep amblyopia — option D). It is also not fusion (option A/B) because the stimuli are too dissimilar for the disparity detectors to merge them.

</details>

### Morgan's Norms (Expected Values for BV Tests)

Morgan's norms are the EXPECTED normal values for the 21-step OEP examination sequence. These tell you what a normal finding looks like for each test — if a patient deviates significantly from these, it indicates a BV anomaly.

**Key Morgan's norms (most board-tested):**

| Test | Expected Normal Finding | Significance if outside range |
|---|---|---|
| NPC (Near Point of Convergence) | ≤8 cm break; ≤10 cm recovery | NPC > 10cm break = convergence insufficiency |
| Distance phoria (cover test) | Orthophoria or ≤1Δ exo | Any significant phoria = investigate |
| Near phoria (cover test at 40cm) | 3Δ exo (range 0–6Δ exo) | Exo > 6Δ or any eso at near = investigate |
| Distance PFC (positive fusional convergence) | Blur 9, Break 19, Recovery 10Δ | Reduced = fusional convergence deficiency |
| Distance NFC (negative fusional convergence) | Blur 7, Break 11, Recovery 8Δ | |
| Near PFC | Blur 17, Break 21, Recovery 11Δ | Reduced = CI if NPC also receded |
| Near NFC | Blur 13, Break 21, Recovery 13Δ | |
| NRA (Negative Relative Accommodation) | +2.00 D | Reduced = accommodative insufficiency |
| PRA (Positive Relative Accommodation) | −2.37 D | Reduced = convergence excess or high AC/A |
| AC/A ratio | 4:1 to 6:1 | High → CE or DE; Low → CI or DI |

### Sheard's Criterion (Detailed)

```
SHEARD'S CRITERION:
 The compensating fusional reserve must be AT LEAST TWICE the demand (phoria)

Formula:
 Compensating reserve ≥ 2 × phoria

For exophoria: PFC (positive fusional convergence) must be ≥ 2 × exophoria
For esophoria: NFC (negative fusional convergence) must be ≥ 2 × esophoria

Example:
 Exophoria 8Δ at near; Near PFC blur = 10Δ
 Sheard's requirement: PFC ≥ 2 × 8 = 16Δ
 Patient has 10Δ PFC → FAILS Sheard's → symptomatic

Prism needed to restore Sheard's:
 Prism = (2/3) × phoria − (1/3) × compensating reserve
 = (2/3)(8) − (1/3)(10) = 5.33 − 3.33 = 2.0Δ BI
```

---

### Percival's Criterion

```
PERCIVAL'S CRITERION:
 The phoria must fall within the MIDDLE THIRD of the total fusional vergence range

Method:
 1. Find the full vergence range: add NFC + PFC (total fusional range)
 2. The middle third = between 1/3 and 2/3 of the total range
 3. Patient is comfortable if their phoria is within this zone

Example:
 Distance: PFC = 20Δ, NFC = 10Δ → Total range = 30Δ
 Middle third = between 10Δ and 20Δ from the NFC end
 If phoria is 5Δ exo → phoria falls in the outer third → FAILS Percival's

When to apply Percival's vs Sheard's:
 Sheard's: best for EXOPHORIC patients (tests positive fusional reserve)
 Percival's: best for ESOPHORIC or high AC/A patients; also used when both NFC and PFC are reduced
```

---

### Neumuller's (Neumueller's) Criterion

```
NEUMULLER'S CRITERION:
 Applies specifically to ESOPHORIC patients

Rule: The prism prescribed should equal HALF the difference between:
 Phoria at near − Phoria at distance

Formula: Prism = (near eso − distance eso) / 2

Example:
 Distance: orthophoria (0Δ); Near: 8Δ esophoria
 Prism = (8 − 0) / 2 = 4Δ BI
 
This is used when the near eso is significantly larger than distance eso (high AC/A),
suggesting the near esophoria is the primary clinical problem.
```

### OEP Method and SOAP Analysis (Syllabi 3.4)

**OEP (Optometric Extension Program) Method:**
A systematic binocular vision examination sequence (21 steps) that measures all aspects of visual function in a standardized order.

```
KEY OEP PRINCIPLES:
1. Near vision is primary — vision is a learned skill and near vision drives most BV problems
2. Evaluate binocular vision under natural (associated) conditions when possible
3. Analysis uses Morgan's norms as expected values
4. Management follows a hierarchy: lenses first, prisms second, vision therapy third

OEP 21-STEP SEQUENCE (key steps for boards):
 Steps 1-5: Distant VA and refraction (objective + subjective)
 Steps 6-9: Distance BV analysis (phorias, fusional ranges)
 Steps 10-13: Near VA and refraction
 Steps 14-17: Near BV analysis (phorias, NPC, fusional ranges, AC/A)
 Steps 18-21: Additional tests (stereopsis, color vision, VF, DPA)
```

**SOAP Analysis:**
A problem-oriented approach to BV case analysis:
- **S**ubjective — patient's chief complaint and symptoms
- **O**bjective — measured test results (VA, phorias, fusional ranges, AC/A, NPC)
- **A**ssessment — diagnosis based on O compared to Morgan's norms and Sheard's/Percival's criteria
- **P**lan — management: spectacles, prisms, vision therapy referral, occlusion

### Amblyopia — Management (Completing the Classification)

**Types (recap):** Strabismic, Anisometropic, Deprivation, Ametropic, Meridional

**Management hierarchy:**

```
STEP 1: CORRECT THE CAUSE
 Prescribe full refractive correction (glasses/contact lens)
 Wait 4-8 weeks for glasses alone to improve VA before patching
 (Passive glasses therapy = some VA improvement from optical correction alone)

STEP 2: PENALIZE THE PREFERRED EYE (force use of amblyopic eye)
 a. Patching (occlusion therapy) — MOST EFFECTIVE
 Part-time patching (2-6 hours/day) = as effective as full-time for moderate amblyopia
 Duration: until VA equalizes or no further improvement over 2 cycles
 Compliance issue: children resist patching → atropine is alternative

 b. Atropine penalization
 1% atropine drops in the DOMINANT eye: blurs near vision in dominant eye
 Forces use of amblyopic eye for near tasks
 Comparable to patching for moderate amblyopia (PEDIG trials)
 Advantage: no compliance battle; child wears glasses
 Risk: photophobia in dominant eye

 c. Optical penalization (Bangerter filters, blur lenses)
 Opaque or diffusion filters on spectacle lens over dominant eye

STEP 3: ACTIVE VISION THERAPY (if passive methods plateau)
 Dichoptic training, binocular perceptual learning
 
CRITICAL PERIODS:
 Most sensitive: 0–2 years (amblyopia develops fastest here)
 Effective treatment: up to age 7–8 years (traditional)
 Possible improvement: up to age 12–18 years (with intensive therapy)
 Adult treatment: less responsive but not impossible
 KEY: The earlier treatment begins, the better the prognosis
```

### Eccentric Fixation — Clinical Detail

```
DEFINITION:
 Fixation not occurring at the fovea — the patient uses a non-foveal retinal area for fixation

CAUSES:
 Long-standing uncorrected refractive error
 Deep amblyopia with foveal suppression
 Strabismus (especially constant unilateral esotropia)
 Macular disease destroying foveal function

ASSESSMENT:
 Visuoscopy — examiner shines a target (star pattern) onto the patient's fundus
 while the patient fixates a small target
 Normal: star projects onto the fovea
 Eccentric: star projects onto extrafoveal area → measured in degrees/prism diopters

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
 Eccentric fixation is a marker of DEEP AMBLYOPIA
 Prognosis for full VA recovery is worse with eccentric fixation
 Management: same amblyopia treatment, but harder to improve
 
 Angle of eccentric fixation ≠ Angle of strabismus (these can differ)
 The degree of eccentricity is a poor prognostic factor for final VA

NOTATION:
 Measured in degrees from the fovea and direction (nasal, temporal, superior, inferior)
 E.g., "2° nasal eccentric fixation" = patient fixates 2° nasal to fovea
```

### Extraocular Muscles — Complete Coverage

**Overview:** The eye has 6 EOMs: 4 recti (straight muscles) and 2 obliques. All except the inferior oblique originate from the Annulus of Zinn at the orbital apex. They are controlled by CN III, IV, and VI.

**EOM origins and insertions:**

```
MUSCLE ORIGIN INSERTION NERVE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SR (Superior Rectus) Annulus of Zinn Anterior to equator (superior) CN III
IR (Inferior Rectus) Annulus of Zinn Anterior to equator (inferior) CN III
MR (Medial Rectus) Annulus of Zinn Anterior to equator (medial) CN III
LR (Lateral Rectus) Annulus of Zinn Anterior to equator (lateral) CN VI
SO (Superior Oblique) Annulus of Zinn Posterior to equator (superior) CN IV
IO (Inferior Oblique) Medial orbital wall Posterior to equator (inferior) CN III
 (floor of orbit,
 nasal side)

SPECIAL FACTS:
 SO = longest EOM; passes through the TROCHLEA (pulley at superomedial orbit)
 → trochlea changes direction of SO pull
 IO = ONLY EOM not originating from Annulus of Zinn
 LR = ONLY muscle innervated by CN VI (abducens)
 SO = ONLY muscle innervated by CN IV (trochlear)
 All others (SR, IR, MR, IO) = CN III (oculomotor)
```

**Primary, secondary, and tertiary actions:**

| Muscle | Primary action | Secondary action | Tertiary action |
|---|---|---|---|
| MR | Adduction | — | — |
| LR | Abduction | — | — |
| SR | Elevation | Intorsion | Adduction |
| IR | Depression | Extorsion | Adduction |
| SO | Intorsion | Depression | Abduction |
| IO | Extorsion | Elevation | Abduction |

**Memory rule for SO and IO:**
"SO4 and LR6" — Superior Oblique = CN IV; Lateral Rectus = CN VI; all others = CN III

**The Benzene Ring — Diagnostic Positions of Gaze**

The benzene ring (hexagonal diagram) maps which single muscle is primarily tested in each of the 6 cardinal positions of gaze. In each cardinal position, the muscle's primary action is isolation so that paresis of that muscle is most obvious.

```
 UP-LEFT UP-RIGHT
 (L SR, R IO) (R SR, L IO)
 \ /
 \ /
LEFT GAZE ─── (L LR, R MR) CENTER (R LR, L MR) ─── RIGHT GAZE
 / \
 / \
 (L IR, R SO) (R IR, L SO)
 DOWN-LEFT DOWN-RIGHT
```

**How to read the benzene ring:**

| Position of gaze | Right eye muscle (primary) | Left eye muscle (yoke) |
|---|---|---|
| Right gaze | R LR (lateral rectus) | L MR (medial rectus) |
| Left gaze | R MR (medial rectus) | L LR (lateral rectus) |
| Up-right | R SR (superior rectus) | L IO (inferior oblique) |
| Down-right | R IR (inferior rectus) | L SO (superior oblique) |
| Up-left | R IO (inferior oblique) | L SR (superior rectus) |
| Down-left | R SO (superior oblique) | L IR (inferior rectus) |

**Why the obliques matter most in the benzene ring:**

The obliques are tested ONLY in adduction:
- SO is tested by asking the eye to look DOWN-in (adduct first, then depress → SO depresses an adducted eye)
- IO is tested by asking the eye to look UP-in (adduct first, then elevate → IO elevates an adducted eye)

When the eye is ABDUCTED, the recti (SR/IR) are primary elevators/depressors.
When the eye is ADDUCTED, the obliques (SO/IO) are primary elevators/depressors.

```
ABDUCTED EYE: SR elevates, IR depresses (recti work best here)
ADDUCTED EYE: IO elevates, SO depresses (obliques work best here)
Board trap: SR palsy looks like IR palsy in adduction — test in cardinal positions!
```

**Ductions, Versions, and Vergences:**

```
DUCTIONS (monocular movements — one eye only):
 Abduction = outward (away from nose)
 Adduction = inward (toward nose)
 Supraduction = upward (elevation)
 Infraduction = downward (depression)
 Intorsion = top of cornea rotates nasally
 Extorsion = top of cornea rotates temporally

VERSIONS (binocular, conjugate — both eyes move same direction):
 Dextroversion = both eyes right
 Levoversion = both eyes left
 Supraversion = both eyes up
 Infraversion = both eyes down
 Dextrocycloversion = both eyes intort/extort together (right)
 Levocycloversion = both eyes intort/extort together (left)

VERGENCES (binocular, disconjugate — eyes move opposite directions):
 Convergence = both eyes adduct (near)
 Divergence = both eyes abduct (distance)
 Incyclovergence = both eyes intort
 Excyclovergence = both eyes extort
 Vertical vergence = one eye up, one eye down
```

**Hering's Law and Sherrington's Law (law breakdown):**

```
HERING'S LAW (Binocular — VERSIONS):
 Equal and simultaneous innervation to YOKE MUSCLES of both eyes
 
 What it means: When you look right, R LR and L MR get exactly equal nerve impulses
 Why: Ensures both eyes move the same amount at the same time (conjugate gaze)
 Clinical use: In a CN VI palsy (R LR paretic), the gaze command still fires equally
 to both yoke muscles — but R LR is weak, causing both eyes to drift
 left together (the secondary deviation = same angle as primary deviation)

SHERRINGTON'S LAW (Monocular — DUCTIONS):
 When an agonist contracts, the antagonist relaxes by an equal and opposite amount
 = Reciprocal innervation
 
 What it means: When R SR fires (elevation), R IR relaxes simultaneously
 Why: Prevents muscles from fighting each other; allows smooth, graded movement
 Clinical use: Explains why a tight IR (e.g., after thyroid eye disease) restricts SR
 elevation — even if SR is healthy, the co-contracted IR resists it
```

**Hering vs Sherrington — the board distinction:**

| Feature | Hering's Law | Sherrington's Law |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | BINOCULAR (both eyes) | MONOCULAR (one eye) |
| Movement type | Versions (conjugate) | Ductions |
| Rule | Yoke muscles = equal innervation | Agonist contracts = antagonist relaxes |
| Example | Looking right: R LR = L MR get equal impulse | Looking up: R SR contracts; R IR relaxes |

### Horopter and Panum's Fusional Area — Full Coverage

**The Horopter — what it is:**

The horopter is the curved surface in visual space through the fixation point that contains all object points whose images fall on corresponding retinal elements of both eyes at a given fixation distance.

- **Introduced by:** Francisco de Aguilonius (Aguilonius), 1613 — term comes from Greek *horos* (boundary) + *opter* (observer)
- **Clinically:** Objects on the horopter are seen as single. Objects off the horopter (too near or too far) are seen as double (physiological diplopia) — unless they fall within Panum's area.

**Two types of horopter:**

```
GEOMETRIC HOROPTER = VIETH-MÜLLER CIRCLE
 The theoretical circle passing through the fixation point and the nodal points
 of both eyes
 Based purely on geometric optics (corresponding retinal points = exact symmetry)
 
EMPIRICAL HOROPTER
 What is actually measured in a real human eye
 It is NOT the same as the Vieth-Müller circle
 
HILLEBRAND DEVIATION:
 The empirical horopter deviates from the Vieth-Müller circle in a predictable way:
 - At NEAR distances: empirical horopter falls INSIDE (in front of) the Vieth-Müller circle
 - At FAR distances: empirical horopter falls OUTSIDE (behind) the Vieth-Müller circle
 - The deviation = because corresponding retinal points are not perfectly geometric
 
ABATHIC DISTANCE:
 The distance at which the empirical horopter most closely matches the Vieth-Müller circle
 (the "true" geometric horopter)
 Approximately 1–2 meters
```

**Panum's Fusional Area:**

Named after Peter Ludvig Panum (1858). The zone around the horopter within which objects produce DISPARATE (non-identical) retinal images yet are still FUSED into a single percept — not diplopia.

```
DEFINITION:
 The zone of single binocular vision immediately surrounding the horopter
 Objects within Panum's area → single vision (despite some retinal disparity)
 Objects outside Panum's area → physiological diplopia

SIZE OF PANUM'S AREA:
 At the FOVEA: narrowest → 6–10 arcmin (very small; precise binocular alignment needed)
 At 12° eccentricity: ~30–40 arcmin (wider; more tolerance for misalignment)
 Panum's area INCREASES with distance from fixation (peripheral = more tolerant)

WHY IT GETS WIDER PERIPHERALLY:
 Peripheral retina has lower spatial resolution (larger receptive fields)
 A larger amount of disparity is tolerated before the visual system detects mismatch
 This is why you can fuse peripheral objects easily even if not perfectly aligned

THREE FUNCTIONS OF THE PERIPHERAL EXPANSION:
 1. Allows binocular fusion of large objects (wide disparity in periphery tolerated)
 2. Provides a "safety margin" for vergence errors without inducing diplopia
 3. Supports the perception of a continuous single visual world despite small deviations
```

**Physiological diplopia — the full mechanism:**

```
FIXATION POINT (on horopter) → retinal images on corresponding elements → SINGLE VISION

OBJECT BEYOND THE HOROPTER (farther than fixation):
 Image falls on NASAL retina of both eyes
 Nasal retina → projects to TEMPORAL visual field direction
 Both eyes "point" the object temporally → appears on same side (homonymous)
 RESULT: UNCROSSED (homonymous) diplopia
 Memory: "FAR = UNCROSSED" / FAR and UNcrossed both have 3 letters ending in consonants

OBJECT NEARER THAN THE HOROPTER (closer than fixation):
 Image falls on TEMPORAL retina of both eyes
 Temporal retina → projects to NASAL visual field direction
 Both eyes "point" the object nasally (but from opposite sides) → cross-pattern
 RESULT: CROSSED (heteronymous) diplopia
 Memory: "NEAR = CROSSED"
```

**Horopter and Panum's — the board comparison:**

| Parameter | Horopter | Panum's Fusional Area |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Curved surface of corresponding retinal points | Zone around horopter where fusion still occurs |
| What happens here | Single vision (no disparity) | Single vision (some disparity tolerated) |
| What happens outside it | Physiological diplopia | Physiological diplopia |
| Size/extent | A single theoretical surface | A zone (6–40 arcmin wide depending on eccentricity) |
| Clinical significance | Defines the limit of perfect binocular alignment | Defines the functional range of fusion |

- **Trap:** "CN III controls all 4 recti" — Wrong. CN III controls SR, IR, MR only. The LATERAL RECTUS is CN VI. Also CN III controls the inferior oblique (not SO — SO is CN IV).

### Retinal Rivalry (Binocular Rivalry)

**Plain version first:** Close one eye and look at a vertical striped pattern. Open the other eye and show it a horizontal striped pattern. Your brain cannot fuse these — they are too different to merge into one image. Instead of seeing both at once, your brain flips between them: you see vertical stripes for a moment, then horizontal stripes, then vertical again. You do not control the switching. This is called **retinal rivalry** (also called **binocular rivalry**).

```
DEFINITION:
 When the two eyes receive completely incompatible images (too different to fuse),
 the visual cortex alternates between the two images rather than superimposing them.
 Neither image is permanently suppressed — they take turns dominating perception.

WHEN IT OCCURS:
 Images presented to each eye are so dissimilar that sensory fusion is impossible.
 Examples: different colors, different shapes, different orientations in each eye.
 
 Contrast with suppression in strabismus:
 SUPPRESSION (strabismus): one image is constantly inhibited (same image repeatedly lost)
 RIVALRY: alternation happens — both images get perceived, just not simultaneously

MECHANISM:
 The dominant eye's image suppresses the other for ~1–5 seconds
 Then the other eye's image breaks through
 The switch is involuntary and roughly periodic
 Rate of switching: influenced by attention, fatigue, image strength
 
CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
 Used as a research tool to study visual consciousness and cortical dominance
 Anaglyph tests (red-green glasses with different images to each eye) use rivalry
 Rivalry is more likely with high-contrast, detailed, or moving images
 Slower alternation may indicate neurological or visual pathway disease
 
BOARD RULE:
 "Rivalry = alternation (not permanent suppression)"
 If both images are too different to fuse → rivalry
 If one image chronically dominates → suppression (strabismic)
```

---

### Amplitude of Fusion (Fusional Vergence Range)

**Plain version first:** Your eyes can converge or diverge a certain amount while still maintaining a single fused image. Push them too far in either direction and the image splits into two (diplopia). The total range — from where the image breaks going inward to where it breaks going outward — is the **amplitude of fusion**, also called the **fusional vergence range** or **fusional reserve**.

```
DEFINITION:
 The total range of vergence (in prism diopters) over which a person maintains
 single binocular vision (fusion) without diplopia.

COMPONENTS:
 POSITIVE FUSIONAL CONVERGENCE (PFC) — base-out prism tolerance
 How much convergence demand the system can handle while still fusing
 Measured with base-out (BO) prism until:
 Blur point: when accommodation/vergence can no longer keep up
 Break point: image splits into diplopia
 Recovery point: when fusion is re-established as prism is reduced
 
 NEGATIVE FUSIONAL CONVERGENCE (NFC) — base-in prism tolerance
 How much divergence demand the system can handle while still fusing
 Measured with base-in (BI) prism until blur/break/recovery

NORMAL VALUES (Morgan's norms at distance):
 PFC (BO): Blur 9Δ | Break 19Δ | Recovery 10Δ
 NFC (BI): Blur 7Δ | Break 11Δ | Recovery 8Δ
 
NORMAL VALUES (Morgan's norms at near):
 PFC (BO): Blur 17Δ | Break 21Δ | Recovery 11Δ
 NFC (BI): Blur 13Δ | Break 21Δ | Recovery 13Δ

CLINICAL USE:
 Reduced PFC + exophoria → convergence insufficiency
 Reduced NFC + esophoria → divergence excess or accommodative esotropia
 Reduced BOTH NFC and PFC with normal phorias → fusional vergence dysfunction (FVD)
 Applied to Sheard's and Percival's criteria to determine if reserves are adequate

BOARD RULE:
 "Amplitude of fusion = the vergence range that maintains single vision"
 Larger amplitude = more flexible binocular system = better tolerance of phorias
 Smaller amplitude = symptomatic even with small phorias
```

### The Plain Version First

Nystagmus is an involuntary, rhythmic oscillation of the eyes — the eyes drift or jerk repeatedly without the person choosing to move them. Think of it like a car windshield wiper that won't stop: the eyes keep moving back and forth, and the person cannot hold a steady gaze voluntarily.

The clinical term for this phenomenon is **nystagmus** (from Greek *nystagmos* = drowsiness, nodding). But unlike drowsiness, the eye movements are fast and sustained.

Two key things define any nystagmus: **the direction of movement** and **whether it is smooth on both sides or has a fast/slow phase**.

---

### Classification: The Framework

```
NYSTAGMUS
├── PHYSIOLOGICAL (normal — occurs in specific conditions)
│ └── End-point nystagmus, optokinetic nystagmus (OKN), caloric nystagmus
│
└── PATHOLOGICAL (abnormal — present in primary gaze or neutral position)
 ├── By WAVEFORM:
 ��� ├── JERK nystagmus — has a SLOW drift + FAST corrective jerk
 │ │ Named by direction of the FAST phase
 │ └── PENDULAR nystagmus — equal velocity in both directions (no fast phase)
 │ Like a pendulum swinging equally both ways
 │
 ├── By ONSET:
 │ ├── CONGENITAL (infantile) — present at birth or in first few months of life
 │ └── ACQUIRED — develops later in life (neurological cause until proven otherwise)
 │
 └── By PLANE:
 ├── Horizontal (most common)
 ├── Vertical (rare; almost always neurological)
 └── Torsional (rotary)
```

---

### Physiological Nystagmus Types

These are normal responses — the eye is working correctly.

```
1. END-POINT NYSTAGMUS
 What it is: Fine jerk nystagmus that appears when the eye is held at extreme lateral gaze
 Plain version: Hold your eyes all the way to the side and they start to drift back — the
 brain corrects with a jerk. This is normal.
 Key rule: Normal only at extreme gaze. Nystagmus in PRIMARY GAZE = always abnormal.

2. OPTOKINETIC NYSTAGMUS (OKN)
 What it is: Alternating slow pursuit + fast saccadic return when watching a moving
 repetitive pattern (like a train going by, or striped drum)
 Plain version: Your eyes follow a stripe (slow pursuit), then snap back to catch the next
 stripe (fast saccade). Over and over.
 Clinical use:
 - Tests integrity of pursuit system (slow phase) + saccade system (fast phase)
 - Used to CONFIRM visual acuity in pre-verbal infants — if OKN is present, child can see
 - Asymmetric OKN = parietal lobe lesion (drum moving toward lesion = absent/reduced OKN)
 Direction convention: Named by direction of the FAST (saccadic) phase

3. CALORIC NYSTAGMUS
 What it is: Nystagmus induced by irrigation of the external ear canal with warm or cold water
 Plain version: Water changes the temperature of the horizontal semicircular canal, creating
 artificial endolymph convection — tricks the brain into thinking the head is turning
 Cold water → nystagmus AWAY from irrigated ear (fast phase contralateral)
 Warm water → nystagmus TOWARD irrigated ear (fast phase ipsilateral)
 Mnemonic: COWS — Cold Opposite, Warm Same
 Clinical use: Tests brainstem/vestibular integrity in comatose patients
```

---

### Pathological Nystagmus — High-Yield Types

**Jerk nystagmus — slow drift + fast correction:**

```
VESTIBULAR NYSTAGMUS
 Cause: Inner ear (peripheral vestibular) or brainstem (central vestibular) lesion
 
 PERIPHERAL (labyrinth, CN VIII):
 Horizontal or horizontal-torsional
 Fast phase AWAY from the lesion side
 SUPPRESSED by visual fixation
 Associated: vertigo, nausea, unilateral hearing loss, tinnitus
 Example: Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), M��nière's disease
 
 CENTRAL (brainstem/cerebellar):
 Often vertical or purely torsional
 NOT suppressed by fixation
 Absent or mild vertigo despite severe nystagmus
 Example: Multiple sclerosis, stroke, tumor
 
 Board rule: "Peripheral = fixation suppresses; Central = fixation does NOT suppress"

GAZE-EVOKED NYSTAGMUS
 Beats in the direction of gaze (right-beating on right gaze, left-beating on left gaze)
 Cause: Cerebellar disease, drugs (anticonvulsants, sedatives, alcohol)
 Plain version: The brain can't hold the eyes in an eccentric position — they drift back,
 then correct. Most common drug-related nystagmus.

DOWNBEAT NYSTAGMUS
 Fast phase is DOWNWARD
 Primary gaze or worsens on downgaze/lateral gaze
 Localization: Cranio-cervical junction (Chiari malformation), cerebellar flocculus lesion
 Board clue: Downbeat in primary gaze → think Chiari malformation / foramen magnum

UPBEAT NYSTAGMUS
 Fast phase UPWARD
 Localization: Medulla (Wernicke's encephalopathy, thiamine deficiency) or anterior vermis
```

**Pendular nystagmus — equal velocity both ways:**

```
CONGENITAL MOTOR NYSTAGMUS (Infantile Nystagmus Syndrome — INS)
 Onset: First few months of life
 Waveform: Pendular (infants) → becomes jerk nystagmus (older children)
 Direction: Usually HORIZONTAL, even on up/down gaze
 Key features:
 - NULL POINT: a gaze position where nystagmus is LEAST intense
 - HEAD TURN/TILT to null point = compensatory posture (not pathological)
 - DAMPENED by convergence (near fixation reduces nystagmus)
 - No oscillopsia (brain adapts — patient does NOT see world shaking)
 Causes: Idiopathic (most common), OR sensory (albinism, cone dystrophy, aniridea, cataract)
 Board clue: Null point + dampens with convergence + no oscillopsia = congenital

ACQUIRED PENDULAR NYSTAGMUS
 Cause: Multiple sclerosis (brainstem demyelination), Whipple's disease
 Key difference from congenital: Patient HAS oscillopsia (world appears to shake)
 This is because the brain didn't adapt to it from birth

LATENT/MANIFEST-LATENT NYSTAGMUS
 LATENT: Only appears when one eye is COVERED (disappears with both eyes open)
 MANIFEST-LATENT: Present all the time but WORSENS when one eye is covered
 Common in: Infantile esotropia, early childhood strabismus
 Direction: Fast phase toward the UNCOVERED (viewing) eye
 Clinical trap: VA measured with one eye covered → nystagmus appears → falsely low VA
 Always measure VA binocularly or with a neutral density filter (not full occlusion)
```

---

### Nystagmus Features to Document Clinically

Every nystagmus description should include these 6 features — the clinical term for this assessment is the **nystagmus description protocol**:

```
1. DIRECTION: Which way does the fast phase beat? (horizontal, vertical, torsional)
2. CONJUGACY: Both eyes moving together (conjugate) or separately (dissociated)?
3. AMPLITUDE: Fine (small excursion) vs coarse (large excursion)
4. FREQUENCY: Rapid (fine/fast) vs slow (pendular-like)
5. NULL POINT: Is there a gaze position where nystagmus diminishes?
6. FIXATION EFFECT: Does fixation suppress or worsen it?
 (Suppresses = peripheral vestibular; doesn't suppress = central)
```

### Board Traps (Nystagmus)

- **Trap:** "Nystagmus is always pathological" — Wrong. Physiological nystagmus (OKN, end-point, caloric) is normal. Nystagmus in PRIMARY gaze is always abnormal.
- **Trap:** "Nystagmus is named by the slow phase direction" — Wrong. Jerk nystagmus is named by the FAST phase direction. The slow phase is the drift; the fast phase is the corrective saccade.
- **Trap:** "Patients with congenital nystagmus see the world shaking" — Wrong. Congenital nystagmus causes NO oscillopsia — the brain adapts from birth. Acquired nystagmus DOES cause oscillopsia.
- **Trap:** "Peripheral vestibular nystagmus is not suppressed by fixation" — Wrong. PERIPHERAL vestibular nystagmus IS suppressed by fixation. Central is not.
- **Trap:** "COWS means cold = same side fast phase" — Wrong. COWS = Cold Opposite, Warm Same. Cold water → fast phase toward the OPPOSITE (contralateral) ear.
- **Trap:** "The superior oblique elevates the eye" — Wrong. The IO elevates (especially in adduction). The SO DEPRESSES the adducted eye and also INTORTS. SR elevates the abducted eye.
- **Trap:** "The horopter is the same as the Vieth-Müller circle" — Wrong. The Vieth-Müller circle is the GEOMETRIC (theoretical) horopter. The actual EMPIRICAL horopter deviates from it — this is the Hillebrand deviation.
- **Trap:** "Panum's area is largest at the fovea" — Wrong. It is SMALLEST (narrowest) at the fovea (6–10 arcmin). It gets WIDER (30–40 arcmin) with increasing eccentricity.
- **Trap:** "Objects beyond the fixation point cause crossed diplopia" — Wrong. BEYOND fixation = nasal retina stimulated = UNCROSSED. NEARER than fixation = temporal retina = CROSSED.
