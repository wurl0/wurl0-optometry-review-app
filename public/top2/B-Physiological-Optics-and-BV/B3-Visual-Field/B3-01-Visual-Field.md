## The Simple Version

The visual field is the total area a person can see while fixating straight ahead. It extends further temporally (100°) than nasally (60°) because the nose blocks nasal vision, and further inferiorly (75°) than superiorly (60°) because the brow blocks the upper field. The two eyes' fields overlap in the center (120° binocular zone), with a 30° monocular crescent on each side. Different diseases affect different parts of the field in predictable patterns — and mapping those patterns with the right test reveals where in the visual pathway the problem is. Goldmann perimetry uses stimulus size and brightness to plot isopters (sensitivity contour lines), while automated perimetry uses static stimuli to build a threshold map.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "Why does glaucoma cause peripheral field loss while macular degeneration causes central field loss?" The answer maps retinal topography to disease location.

---

## But Why?

**Why is the temporal field larger (100°) than the nasal field (60°)?**
Because the nasal retina receives the temporal field — and nasal retina extends further than temporal retina due to the nose blocking peripheral nasal visual input. The temporal field of view is unobstructed.

**Why does color fail before form and motion in visual field loss?**
Because color perception requires the most precisely functioning cone pathways. Small lesions or early dysfunction preferentially knock out color sensitivity before the coarser form/motion channels are affected. This is why color is tested for early disease detection.

**Why does Goldmann perimetry use kinetic stimuli while automated perimetry uses static?**
Because kinetic testing (moving the target in) maps the boundary of sensitivity efficiently but misses patchy losses within the field. Static testing (fixed position, varying intensity) detects threshold at specific locations, catching scotomas the kinetic method would cross over without detection.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**Why the visual field has the shape it does:**

```
MONOCULAR FIELD (one eye):
  Superior:  60° (brow limits upward gaze)
  Inferior:  75° (less obstruction below)
  Temporal: 100° (no obstruction; temporal retina sees nasal field far out)
  Nasal:     60° (nose limits inward field)

  Total monocular field: approximately 200° horizontal, 135° vertical

BINOCULAR FIELD OVERLAP:
  Central 120° is seen by BOTH eyes simultaneously
  (30° monocular crescent on each temporal side = seen by one eye only)
```

**Goldmann perimetry — how it works:**

```
Patient fixates center of bowl
Examiner moves stimulus from non-seeing periphery toward center
At speed: 2–4° per second
Patient signals first detection point
That point is plotted on chart
Process repeated at multiple meridians (every 10–30°)
Connected points = ISOPTER for that stimulus
```

Multiple isopters nested from largest/brightest (V4e, outermost) to smallest/dimmest (I1e, innermost) create a topographic map of the visual field — the "island of vision."

**Goldmann stimulus notation:**

```
FORMAT: [Size (Roman I–V)] [Intensity (1–4)] [letter (a–e)]
Example: V4e = Size V (largest; 64mm²) + Intensity 4e (maximum)
         I4e = Size I (smallest; 0.25mm²) + Intensity 4e (maximum)

SIZE scale:
  I = 0.25 mm² (6 arc min angular size)
  II = 1 mm²
  III = 4 mm²
  IV = 16 mm²
  V = 64 mm² (96 arc min angular size)
Each step = 0.6 log unit (4× area increase)

INTENSITY:
  Letter e = maximum (no filter)
  Letter a = 0.4 log units below maximum
  Arabic number 4 = maximum; 1 = dimmest standard stimulus
```

### Structure or Process Map

**Visual field normal extents — "SIT-N = 60-75-100-60":**

```
         Superior 60°
              |
Nasal 60° ←──┼──→ Temporal 100°
              |
         Inferior 75°

Binocular overlap: 120° central
Temporal crescent (monocular): 30° each side
```

**Visual field categories and vulnerability:**

```
MOST RESILIENT (last affected)
  ↑ Motion perception
  | Form perception
  ↓ Color perception
MOST VULNERABLE (first affected)
```

**VF tests comparison:**

| Test | Coverage | What it detects | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Confrontation | Gross full field | Large hemianopias, dense defects | Bedside screening |
| Amsler Grid | Central 10° (at 28–30cm) | Metamorphopsia, central scotoma | Macular disease monitoring |
| Tangent Screen | Central 30° only | Scotomas in central field | Detailed central mapping |
| Goldmann Perimeter | Full field; isopter mapping | Any field defect; peripheral + central | Standard kinetic perimetry |
| Automated (Humphrey) | Central 24° or 30° | Threshold values per point | Glaucoma monitoring |

**VF defects by lesion (summary — see A2-07 for full detail):**

| Defect | Lesion | Clinical example |
|---|---|---|
| Monocular scotoma | Retina or optic nerve | Optic neuritis, ARMD |
| Bitemporal hemianopia | Optic chiasma (crossing fibers) | Pituitary adenoma |
| Homonymous hemianopia | Post-chiasmal | Stroke |
| "Pie in the sky" | Meyer's loop (temporal lobe) | Temporal lobe tumor |
| "Pie on the floor" | Baum's loop (parietal lobe) | Parietal stroke |
| Ring scotoma | Peripheral rod degeneration | Retinitis pigmentosa |
| Central scotoma | Macular disease | ARMD, CSR |
| Arcuate scotoma | Nerve fiber bundle loss | Glaucoma (superior/inferior arcades) |

**Goldmann isopter interpretation:**

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Constriction of all isopters | Generalized depression (advanced glaucoma, RP) |
| Isopters collapse centrally | Central field loss (macular disease) |
| Isopters absent temporally | Bitemporal defect (chiasmal lesion) |
| Absent on one side of vertical midline | Homonymous hemianopsia (retrochiasmal) |
| Island within isopter | Scotoma (absolute if no stimulus seen; relative if only dim stimuli missed) |

### Comparisons That Matter

**Goldmann vs Automated Perimetry:**

| Feature | Goldmann | Automated (Humphrey) |
|---|---|---|
| Stimulus type | Kinetic (moving) | Static (fixed position, varying intensity) |
| Coverage | Full field (peripheral + central) | Central 24° or 30° primarily |
| Scotoma detection | Less sensitive for small scotomas | Better for small scotomas |
| Reliability | Examiner-dependent | Standardized; reliability indices |
| Best for | Low vision, peripheral field, RP, neuro | Glaucoma monitoring, subtle defects |
| Board clue | "Map the boundaries" | "Map the threshold" |

**Amsler Grid specifics:**
- Measures central 10° of visual field
- Distance: 28–30 cm (approximately 1 foot)
- Tests for metamorphopsia (wavy/distorted lines — macular disease)
- Each small square = 1° of visual field

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "The temporal visual field extends 60°" — Wrong. Temporal = 100° (widest). Nasal and superior = 60°.
- **Trap 2:** "All of the visual field is seen binocularly" — Wrong. The central 120° is binocular; each side has a 30° monocular crescent seen only by the temporal retina.
- **Trap 3:** "Goldmann V4e uses the smallest stimulus" — Wrong. V is the LARGEST size (64mm²). I is the smallest (0.25mm²).
- **Trap 4:** "Amsler grid tests the entire visual field" — Wrong. Amsler grid tests only the central 10° of the visual field.
- **Trap 5:** "Motion is the most vulnerable visual function" — Wrong. Color is the most vulnerable. Motion is the most resilient.

## Practice Questions

**1.** On Goldmann perimetry, all isopters are severely constricted, with only a small central island of vision remaining. The patient reports difficulty seeing in dim light and has had peripheral vision loss for years. What condition does this pattern suggest?

- A. Pituitary adenoma
- B. Glaucoma
- C. Retinitis pigmentosa
- D. Central serous retinopathy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Retinitis pigmentosa.**

Retinitis pigmentosa is a rod dystrophy that begins peripherally and progresses centrally, creating a classic "ring scotoma" that expands until only a small central island of cone vision remains. On Goldmann perimetry, this appears as severely constricted isopters around a preserved central island. The history of night blindness (rod dysfunction precedes cone loss in RP) and progressive peripheral loss over years is consistent with RP. Pituitary adenoma causes bitemporal hemianopia — not concentric constriction. Glaucoma causes arcuate scotomas and nasal steps, typically not this degree of peripheral constriction early. CSR causes a central scotoma, not peripheral.

</details>
