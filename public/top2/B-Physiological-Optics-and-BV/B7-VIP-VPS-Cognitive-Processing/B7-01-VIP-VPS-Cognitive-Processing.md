## B7-01 — VIP, VPS, and Cognitive Visual Processing
*Personal study notes | TOS: B.7 | 10 items*

---

## The Simple Version

Visual Information Processing (VIP) is what happens after the image reaches the brain — how the brain interprets, organizes, stores, and acts on visual information. A person can have 20/20 acuity and still fail at VIP tasks. Visual Perceptual Skills (VPS) is the non-motor subset of VIP — the seven cognitive visual skills that let a person recognize shapes, recall sequences, and locate targets in busy backgrounds, without needing to use their hands or body to respond. These are assessed with three main tools: TVPS-3 (pure non-motor VPS), DTVP-2 (separates motor vs non-motor), and Beery VMI (visual-motor integration). Boards test the definitions, which tool measures what, and the seven VPS subtopics.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "Can a child fail to read not because of poor eyesight but because of poor visual processing?" Yes — and that distinction drives the referral decision between optometry and occupational therapy.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Meaning | Context |
|---|---|---|
| VIP | Visual Information Processing | Broad term; includes all stages of how the brain processes visual input |
| VPS | Visual Perceptual Skills | Non-motor subset of VIP; 7 specific cognitive visual skills |
| VMI | Visual-Motor Integration | Coordination of visual perception with fine motor response |
| TVPS-3 | Test of Visual Perceptual Skills, 3rd Ed. | Non-motor VPS; patient points only; ages 4–18 |
| DTVP-2 | Developmental Test of Visual Perception, 2nd Ed. | Separates motor-free vs motor-enhanced scores; ages 4–10 |
| Beery VMI | Beery-Buktenica Dev. Test of VMI | Copy geometric shapes; tests visual-motor integration; ages 2–100 |

### Structure or Process Map

**VIP vs VPS distinction:**

```
VIP (Visual Information Processing) — BROAD umbrella
 ├── VPS (Visual Perceptual Skills) — NON-MOTOR subset
 │ Assessment: TVPS-3 (points only; no drawing)
 │
 └── Visual-Motor Integration (VMI) — requires motor output
 Assessment: Beery VMI (copies shapes)
```

**The 7 VPS subtopics (assessed by TVPS-3):**

| VPS Skill | Definition | Example failure |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual Discrimination** | Detect differences between similar forms/shapes | Cannot identify which letter differs among similar letters |
| **Visual Memory** | Recall visual characteristics of a stimulus after removal | Cannot recognize a shape seen 5 seconds ago |
| **Visual Spatial Relationships** | Determine position of objects relative to self/others | Left-right confusion; cannot judge orientation |
| **Visual Form Constancy** | Recognize a form despite changes in size/rotation/context | Cannot recognize a triangle rotated 90° |
| **Visual Sequential Memory** | Recall a series of visual stimuli in correct order | Cannot reproduce a sequence of shapes |
| **Visual Figure-Ground** | Locate a target within a complex background | Cannot find a word on a busy page |
| **Visual Closure** | Recognize an incomplete figure as whole | Cannot read a word with missing letter parts |

**Assessment tools comparison:**

| Tool | What it measures | Motor required? | Ages | Key feature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TVPS-3 | 7 non-motor VPS subtopics | NO (patient only points) | 4–18 yrs | Gold standard for non-motor VPS |
| DTVP-2 | Motor-free + motor-enhanced VPS | Partially | 4–10 yrs | Separates perception from motor output |
| Beery VMI | Visual-motor integration | YES (copies shapes) | 2–100 yrs | Broadest age range; copying task |

**Clinical relevance:**

```
VIP deficits seen in:
 ├── Amblyopia / strabismus (suppression disrupts higher visual processing)
 ├── Learning difficulties (reading, spelling, spatial math)
 ├── Traumatic brain injury (acquired VIP deficits)
 └── Cerebral visual impairment (global VIP disruption)

Management:
 ├── Vision therapy targeting specific VPS subtopics
 ├── Referral to OT for VMI-specific intervention
 └── Educational psychologist for school accommodations
```

## Practice Question

**1.** A 7-year-old has 20/20 acuity OU, no strabismus, and normal binocular vision. The teacher reports he cannot find objects on a busy worksheet and frequently loses his place while reading. Which assessment would best evaluate the most likely deficit?

- A. Humphrey visual field testing
- B. Ishihara color vision testing
- C. TVPS-3
- D. Beery VMI

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. TVPS-3.**

The described difficulties — finding objects on a busy page (figure-ground), losing place while reading (visual sequential memory and figure-ground) — point to visual perceptual skills deficits. With normal acuity and binocular vision ruling out optical and alignment problems, the deficit is at the VPS level. TVPS-3 is the gold standard for assessing all 7 non-motor VPS subtopics in children ages 4–18. It requires only pointing, so motor output doesn't confound the results. Beery VMI would test visual-motor integration (copying shapes) — appropriate if handwriting or drawing difficulties were the chief complaint. VF testing and color testing are not relevant here.

</details>

---

**2.** A 6-year-old scores poorly on the Beery VMI. Before concluding she has a visual perceptual deficit, which assessment should be done first to separate perception from motor output?

- A. Humphrey visual field test
- B. TVPS-3
- C. DTVP-2 motor-enhanced subtest
- D. Snellen acuity testing

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. TVPS-3.**

Beery VMI requires copying geometric shapes — a visual-motor task. A low score could reflect poor visual perception OR poor fine motor skills (shaky hands, poor pencil grip). TVPS-3 is the appropriate next step because it tests all 7 VPS subtopics with pointing responses only — no motor output is required. If TVPS-3 is normal, the Beery VMI deficit is motor (refer to OT for motor base work). If TVPS-3 is also poor, the deficit is perceptual (vision therapy targeting VPS). DTVP-2 (option C) also separates motor from non-motor but tests ages 4–10 and is less specific than TVPS-3 for identifying the perceptual subtype. Visual fields and acuity testing (A, D) do not assess VPS.

</details>

### Visual Spatial Skills — Three Subtypes

The the review course breaks "Visual Spatial Relationships" (one of the original 7 VPS subtopics) into three distinct cognitive skills. The board may test these as separate concepts.

```
SPATIAL VISUALIZATION
 Plain version: You can mentally pick up an object and rotate or flip it in your mind.
 Definition: The ability to mentally manipulate, rotate, twist, or invert a visual image
 without physically touching it.
 Example failure: Cannot figure out how a folded piece of paper will look unfolded.
 Clinical significance: Needed for spatial math, reading maps, understanding 3D diagrams.

MENTAL ROTATION
 Plain version: You see a shape at one angle and can figure out what it looks like 
 if it were rotated 90° or upside down.
 Definition: The ability to mentally rotate a 2D or 3D object around any axis
 and compare it to another orientation.
 Example failure: Cannot match a rotated letter to its upright version
 (e.g., confuses "b", "d", "p", "q").
 Clinical significance: Strongly related to reading reversals and letter confusion in children.

SPATIAL REASONING
 Plain version: You use position information to solve problems — like following a map
 or understanding where you are relative to other objects.
 Definition: The ability to understand, reason about, and mentally manipulate spatial
 relationships between objects in an environment.
 Example failure: Gets lost in familiar environments; cannot read a blueprint or map.
 Clinical significance: Needed for navigation, understanding spatial layouts,
 sports and physical coordination.
```

---

### Visual Analysis

This is listed as a separate VPS skill in the the review course. It is related to figure-ground but focuses on breaking down complex visual information.

```
VISUAL ANALYSIS
 Plain version: You can look at something complex and identify its individual parts,
 then understand how those parts relate to each other.
 Definition: The ability to examine visual information closely, identify its components,
 recognize patterns, and understand how those parts relate to one another.
 Example failure: Cannot break a word into letter components;
 cannot identify which part of a diagram belongs to which section.
 Clinical significance: Reading comprehension, diagram interpretation, visual reasoning tasks.
 
 Distinguish from Visual Discrimination:
 Visual Discrimination = detect differences BETWEEN objects (this vs that)
 Visual Analysis = break apart one complex visual INTO its components
```

### The Visual Base and Motor Base — Foundation Skills

The the review course explicitly states VPS is built on two foundational bases:

```
VISUAL BASE (prerequisite for VPS development)
 Established through FUNCTIONAL VISUAL SKILLS:
 - Convergence (sustained near binocular alignment)
 - Eye tracking (smooth pursuit for reading)
 - Saccades (accurate jumps between words/targets)
 - Accommodation (clear focus at near)
 
 Clinical implication:
 A child with convergence insufficiency will struggle with VPS tasks NOT because
 their brain can't process — but because the visual input is unstable.
 Fix the functional visual skills FIRST before attributing failure to VPS deficit.

MOTOR BASE (prerequisite for VMI development)
 Established through:
 - Shoulder girdle stability
 - Fine motor skills (pencil grip, hand control)
 - Gross motor skills (bilateral coordination)
 - Eye-hand coordination
 
 Clinical implication:
 Poor Beery VMI score may reflect motor deficit (shaky hands) rather than
 visual processing deficit. Use TVPS-3 (non-motor) to separate the two.
```

### Updated VPS Map (the review course Review Version)

```
VPS SUBTOPICS (the review course expanded version)
├── Visual Discrimination — detect differences between objects
├── Visual Memory — recall visual information
├── Visual Form Constancy — recognize despite size/rotation/position change
├── Visual Closure — complete an incomplete figure
├── Visual Figure Ground — locate target in busy background
├── Visual Analysis — break complex image into parts
└── Visual Spatial Skills
 ├── Spatial Visualization — mentally manipulate/rotate objects
 ├── Mental Rotation — rotate 2D/3D object in mind
 └── Spatial Reasoning — understand spatial relationships for problem-solving

VMI (separate from VPS):
└── Visual Motor Integration — coordinate vision with hand movement (Beery VMI)
```

### Board Trap — Extended

- **Trap:** "Poor Beery VMI score always means a VPS deficit" — Wrong. Beery VMI requires motor output (copying shapes). A low score could be due to fine motor difficulty, not visual perception. Always compare with TVPS-3 (non-motor) to determine if the deficit is perceptual or motor.
- **Trap:** "Visual analysis and visual discrimination are the same skill" — Wrong. Visual discrimination compares two objects for differences. Visual analysis breaks one complex object into its component parts.

## Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue → rule → trap`.

**Color theory by level** — cue: "receptor" vs "post-receptor" vs "combined".
- Receptor = trichromatic (Young-Helmholtz). Post-receptor = opponent (Hering). Combined = stage (Ladd-Franklin).
- Trap: assigning opponent-process to the receptor level.

**Sheard vs Percival** — cue: criterion name.
- Sheard = fusional reserve ≥ **2× the phoria** (for symptomatic). Percival = phoria in the **middle third** of the vergence range (independent of phoria).
- Trap: swapping the two definitions.

**Worth-4-dot fusion grade** — cue: simultaneous / fusion / stereo.
- Grade 1 = simultaneous macular perception, Grade 2 = flat fusion, Grade 3 = stereopsis.
- Trap: calling stereopsis "Grade 2."

**Physiologic diplopia** — cue: near vs far target.
- Far object behind fixation = **uncrossed (homonymous)**; near object = **crossed**.
- Trap: reversing crossed/uncrossed.

**AC/A** — cue: deviation worse at near vs distance.
- Worse at **near** = high AC/A (convergence excess); worse at **distance** = divergence excess.
- Trap: matching "convergence insufficiency" to a high AC/A — CI is **low** AC/A.
