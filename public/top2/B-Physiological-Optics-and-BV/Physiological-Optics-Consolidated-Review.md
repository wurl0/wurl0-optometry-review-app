## 1. Definitions and Scope

**Physiology** — the branch of biology that deals with the normal functions of living organisms and their parts.

**Optics** — the branch of physics that deals with the properties and behavior of light and its interactions with matter.

**Physiological Optics** — the visual science that studies the structure of the eye and its functions as affected by the behavior of light, specifically:
- Refraction
- Absorption
- Reflection
- Dispersion

**Visual Optics** — covers fundamental knowledge based on scientific principles of light and its effects on the eye, and the application of these principles in optometric care and practice.

> HIGH-YIELD: Physiological optics sits at the intersection of anatomy, physics, and physiology. Board questions often test whether you know which structure does what optically, and what happens when it goes wrong.

## 2. Stimulus-Response Phenomenon

The body adapts to its environment through three types of specialized cells.

### Receptors
- The **real sense organs** of the body
- Made of neuro-epithelial or sensory epithelial cells
- Highly specialized — each receptor responds to only one type of stimulus
- The photoreceptors of the eye respond only to light, not to sound or chemical stimuli

### Effectors
- The **responding organ** — the subject of change brought about by receptor stimulation
- Reacts in two ways:
 - **Reflex action** — automatic, involuntary response (e.g., pupillary light reflex)
 - **Learned reaction** — conditioned response requiring CNS processing (e.g., reading)

**Transmission difference:** Reflex actions bypass the cortex and loop through the spinal cord or brainstem. Learned reactions always involve the cortex.

### Physiological Connectors
Nerves connecting receptors to effectors:
- **Sensory (afferent)** — carries impulse from receptor to CNS
- **Motor (efferent)** — carries impulse from CNS to effector

### Accessory Organs for Vision
1. Organs that **protect** the receptor (eyelids, orbit, brow)
2. Organs that **convey stimulus** to the receptor (cornea, lens, vitreous — the optical media)
3. Organs that **nourish** the receptor (choroid, RPE, retinal vasculature)

## 3. Anatomy of the Visual System

### 3.1 The Bony Orbit

- Paired pyramidal skeletal cavity in the skull, on either side of the nasal root
- Apex directed posteriorly; base open anteriorly (quadrangular opening)
- **Functions:**
 - Protects the globe from mechanical injury
 - Anchors muscles and ligaments
 - Provides a window for neurovascular structures
 - Orbital fat surrounds EOMs and globe as cushion, facilitating eye movement
 - Orbital septum acts as anterior barrier preventing orbital infection spread

### 3.2 Eyelids (Palpebra)

- Two thin movable folds that protect the eye through closure
- Upper eyelid is larger and more mobile; elevated by the **levator palpebrae superioris**

**Physiological roles:**
- Spreads tear film across the ocular surface with each blink
- Prevents corneal desiccation during sleep
- Blink reflex protects against foreign bodies

**Three lines of defense:**

| Line | Structure | Function |
|------|-----------|----------|
| 1st | Eyebrows (supercilia) and cilia | Deflect debris before it reaches the eye |
| 2nd | Glandular secretions (meibomian glands) | Lipid layer of tear film; prevents evaporation |
| 3rd | Muscular (levator palpebrae superioris) | Opens and closes the eye voluntarily/involuntarily |

### 3.3 The Eyeball

A spherical structure approximately 24mm in diameter. Composed of three concentric coats:

**Coat 1 — Fibrous (Sclero-Corneal) Coat**
- Provides structural support and shape
- **Sclera** — opaque posterior 5/6; insertion point for EOMs
- **Cornea** — transparent anterior 1/6; primary refracting surface
- **Limbus** — transition zone between sclera and cornea; site of the angle

**Coat 2 — Uveal (Vascular) Coat**
- Provides nutrition to the avascular structures
- **Choroid** — vascular layer between sclera and retina; nourishes the outer retina
- **Ciliary body** — produces aqueous humor; contains ciliary muscle for accommodation
- **Iris** — controls pupil size (sphincter and dilator pupillae)

**Coat 3 — Nervous (Functional) Coat — The Retina**
- Converts light energy into neural signals
- Derived from the neural tube; part of the CNS

### 3.4 The Retina

**Two parts:**
1. **Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)** — dark pigment layer
 - Absorbs stray light that passes through the receptor layer
 - Reduces light scatter and image distortion
 - Supplies glucose and ions to the neural retina
 - Stores vitamin A and resynthesizes photopigment
 - Removes shed outer segment disks

2. **Neural Retina** — contains five neuron types:
 - Visual receptor cells (rods and cones)
 - Horizontal cells
 - Bipolar cells
 - Amacrine cells
 - Retinal ganglion cells

**The Fovea**
- Central fixation point — where central visual field is focused
- Fewer retinal layers so light reaches photoreceptors with minimal obstruction
- Maximum cone density: ~150,000 cones/mm²
- Rod-free zone
- Surrounding **macula** is thicker and contains neurons receiving foveal signals

**The Optic Disc**
- Formed by retinal ganglion cell axons exiting the eye
- Located **nasal** to the fovea
- No photoreceptors — structural basis of the physiologic blind spot
- Located in the **temporal hemifield** of the visual field

## 4. Dioptrics of the Eye

**Dioptrics** — the science dealing with the refraction of light through transparent media; specifically, the study of refraction by the ocular media.

**Diopter** — unit of refracting power; the reciprocal of the focal length in meters (D = 1/f).

### 4.1 Factors Affecting Refractive Power

Two key determinants:

1. **Index of Refraction (n)** — a measure of how much light slows down in a medium compared to a vacuum. Higher n = greater bending of light at an interface.

 - Light slows down when it enters a denser medium; at the interface, it bends toward the normal
 - The greater the difference in n between two media, the greater the refraction

2. **Radius of Curvature (r)** — the radius of the sphere that best fits the curved surface
 - Shorter radius = more curved surface = greater refracting power (P = (n2 - n1) / r)

**Formula: Surface Refracting Power**

```
P = (n2 - n1) / r
```

- **P** = refracting power in diopters
- **n2** = index of the second medium (the one light is entering)
- **n1** = index of the first medium
- **r** = radius of curvature in meters (positive if center of curvature is to the right of the surface)

Why this form: It captures both the refractive index step and the geometry of the surface in one value. A flatter surface (larger r) or a smaller index difference gives less power. A steeper surface or greater index step gives more.

Physical analogy: Like the banking of a road curve — a sharper curve (smaller radius) forces more directional change.

Cross-subject connections:
- Contact lens prescribing uses this principle — fitting a steep lens on a flat cornea creates a plus tear lens
- Gullstrand's model uses this for each of the six surfaces of the eye

### 4.2 The Schematic Eye

Because the real eye has multiple refracting surfaces, simplified models are used to calculate image formation.

**Gullstrand's Schematic Eye (1924)** — 6-surface model using actual anatomical dimensions. Most accurate for clinical optics.

**Emsley's Reduced Eye (1952)** — simplified single-surface model. Useful for quick calculations.

### 4.3 Gullstrand's Constants

**Indices of Refraction:**

| Structure | Index (n) |
|-----------|-----------|
| Cornea | 1.376 |
| Aqueous humor | 1.336 |
| Lens cortex | 1.386 |
| Lens nucleus | 1.406 |
| Vitreous humor | 1.336 |

| Surface | Radius (mm) |

**Axial Distances (from corneal apex):**

| Corneal thickness | 0.5 |
| Anterior lens surface | 3.6 |
| Posterior lens surface | 7.2 |

| Nodal Point | Location from corneal apex (mm) |
|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Primary | 7.08 |
| Secondary | 7.33 |

### 4.4 The Cornea

- Contributes approximately **2/3 of total ocular refraction**
- Normal power range: **36 to 50 D** (average: **43 D**)
- Index of refraction: **1.376**
- Most of its power comes from the **air-tear interface** (the largest index step in the eye)

### 4.5 The Tear Film

- The **first refracting surface** encountered by incoming light
- Index: **1.336** (same as aqueous humor)
- Clinically important for contact lens fitting:
 - **Steep contact lens** over a flatter cornea = positive tear lens (more plus power)
 - **Flat contact lens** over a steeper cornea = negative tear lens (more minus power)

### 4.6 The Crystalline Lens

- Located **3.6 mm behind the corneal apex**
- Contributes approximately **1/3 of total refraction** (~19 D unaccommodated)
- Changes shape during accommodation to increase power
- Has a gradient index — nucleus (1.406) is denser than cortex (1.386)

## 5. Refractive Status

**Definition:** The optical condition of the eye determined by the relationship between its total refracting power and its axial length.

**Two determining factors:**
1. Total refracting power of the eye (cornea + lens)
2. Axial length (front-to-back length of the eye)

### 5.1 Emmetropia

Parallel rays of light from infinity focus exactly on the retina at rest (no accommodation).

### 5.2 Ametropia

Any condition where parallel rays of light do not focus on the retina at rest.

---

### 5.3 Myopia (Nearsightedness)

**Definition:** Parallel rays from infinity focus **anterior** to the retina at rest.

**Classification by Amount:**
| Class | Diopter Range |
|-------|---------------|
| Low | < -3.00 D |
| Moderate | -3.00 to -6.00 D |
| High | > -6.00 D |

**Classification by Origin:**

| Type | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| Axial myopia | Eye is longer than normal |
| Curvature myopia | Cornea or lens is too steep |
| Index myopia | Increased refractive index of lens (e.g., nuclear sclerosis) |
| Positional myopia | Lens displaced anteriorly |

**Classification by Onset:**

| Type | Age of Onset |
|------|--------------|
| Congenital | Present at birth |
| Youth-onset | Before age 20 |
| Early adult-onset | 20-40 years |
| Late adult-onset | After 40 years |

**Symptoms:** Blurred distance vision, asthenopia, squinting at distance, better near vision

**Signs:** Posterior staphyloma, lattice degeneration, longer axial length, peripapillary crescent, tigroid fundus (in high myopia)

---

### 5.4 Hyperopia (Farsightedness)

**Definition:** Parallel rays from infinity focus **posterior** to the retina at rest (or would if the eye could not accommodate).

**Classification:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Total hyperopia** | Full amount = manifest + latent |
| **Manifest hyperopia** | Can be measured by refraction without cycloplegia |
| - Facultative | Overcome by accommodation; correctable with accommodation |
| - Absolute | Cannot be overcome even with maximum accommodation; blur at all distances |
| **Latent hyperopia** | Masked by tonic ciliary muscle tone; only revealed with cycloplegia |

**Causes:** Short axial length (most common), flat cornea, small/flat lens, posterior lens dislocation

**Symptoms:** Blurred near vision (in low-moderate), headaches, asthenopia, esophoria tendency, accommodative esotropia in children

**Signs:** Small eye, farsighted fundus (crowded optic disc, small foveal reflex)

---

### 5.5 Astigmatism

**Definition:** A condition where the refracting surfaces of the eye have different curvatures in different meridians, causing light from a point source to form two focal lines rather than a single point.

**Principal Meridians:** The two meridians of greatest and least power, perpendicular to each other.

**Causes:**
- Corneal: irregular corneal surface (most common cause of astigmatism)
- Lenticular: tilted lens, lens subluxation, irregular lens surfaces

**Axis Classification:**

| Type | Meridian Configuration | Most Common Axis |
|------|------------------------|------------------|
| With-the-Rule (WTR) | Steeper vertical meridian | 90° (±20°) |
| Against-the-Rule (ATR) | Steeper horizontal meridian | 180° (±20°) |
| Oblique | Steeper at oblique axis | 45° or 135° |

**Resultant Types (Based on Focus Relative to Retina):**

| Type | Abbreviation | Description |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Compound Hyperopic Astigmatism | CHA | Both focal lines behind retina |
| Simple Hyperopic Astigmatism | SHA | One focal line on retina, one behind |
| Compound Myopic Astigmatism | CMA | Both focal lines in front of retina |
| Simple Myopic Astigmatism | SMA | One focal line on retina, one in front |
| Mixed Astigmatism | -- | One focal line in front, one behind retina |

**Regular vs. Irregular:**
- **Regular** — two principal meridians, perpendicular to each other, correctable with cylinders
 - Symmetric: equal magnitude each side of 90/180
 - Asymmetric: unequal magnitude
- **Irregular** — meridians not perpendicular, not correctable with standard cylinders (e.g., keratoconus, corneal scars)

### 5.6 Refractive Status Summary Table

| Condition | Focal Point | Far Point | Near Point |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Emmetropia | On retina (at rest) | Infinity | Near (with accommodation) |
| Myopia | In front of retina | Finite (in front of eye) | Very near |
| Hyperopia | Behind retina | Virtual (behind eye) | Farther than normal |

## 6. Accommodation

**Definition:** The process by which the eye changes its refracting power to focus on objects at different distances by altering the shape of the crystalline lens.

**Mechanism:** Ciliary muscle contraction loosens zonular tension, allowing the lens to become more convex (increase in power).

**Primary stimulus:** Blur (specifically, hyperopic defocus on the retina)

**Secondary stimuli:** Convergence, chromatic aberration, proximity

**Accommodation response types:**
- **Lag of accommodation** — response is less than the accommodative demand (under-response); most common in everyday use
- **Lead of accommodation** — response is more than the demand (over-response); less common

**Amplitude of accommodation** decreases with age (presbyopia begins around age 40-45).

## 7. Process of Seeing

Vision involves four sequential processes:

| Step | Process | Where It Happens |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Physical** | Light enters the eye and is refracted by the optical media |
| 2 | **Photochemical** | Light energy triggers photopigment breakdown in photoreceptors |
| 3 | **Neuromuscular** | Neural signals travel the visual pathway to the brain |
| 4 | **Psychological** | The brain interprets the neural signal as a visual percept |

## 8. Visual Pathway and Visual Field

### 8.1 Anatomy of the Visual Pathway

1. **Retina** — rods and cones convert light to neural signals
2. **Optic nerve (CN II)** — transmits signals from each eye independently; fibers from the nasal and temporal retina travel together
3. **Optic chiasma** — nasal fibers **cross** (decussate); temporal fibers stay ipsilateral
4. **Optic tract** — carries combined nasal (contralateral) and temporal (ipsilateral) fibers from the same visual hemifield
5. **Lateral Geniculate Body (LGB)** — thalamic relay; 6 laminae
6. **Optic radiations (geniculocalcarine tract)** — fan out to the occipital cortex
7. **Visual cortex / Area 17 (Broadman area / Area Striata)** — primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe

**80% of perceptual input comes from the visual system.**

### 8.2 Normal Visual Field Extent (Monocular)

| Direction | Extent |
|-----------|--------|
| Superior | 50° |
| Nasal | 60° |
| Inferior | 70° |
| Temporal | 90° |

### 8.3 Traquair's Island of Vision

Harry Moss Traquair (Scottish ophthalmologist and perimetrist) described the visual field as:
- An **island of vision in a sea of blindness**
- The highest point of the island = the point of fixation (fovea)
- The bottomless pit = the blind spot (optic disc)

**Hill of Vision** — 3D representation of differential retinal light sensitivity; peaks at the fovea, slopes peripherally. Isopters are contour lines connecting points of equal sensitivity on the hill.

**Isopter** — a line connecting all points in the visual field with the same threshold for a given stimulus.

### 8.4 Visual Field Testing

| Test | Type | What It Tests |
|------|------|---------------|
| Confrontation | Screening | Peripheral VF |
| Amsler Grid | Screening | Central VF (within 10°) |
| Tangent Screen (Bjerrum Screen) | Quantitative manual | Central 30°; uses pigment targets, 7 ft-candles illumination |
| Perimetry (arc perimeter) | Quantitative automated | Full hill of vision; used for glaucoma monitoring |

Both manual and automated perimetry can be performed **kinetically** (moving target) or **statically** (fixed target, varying brightness).

**Tangent Screen details:**
- Black felt background; circular stitching every 5°; radial stitching every 22.5° starting at 180°
- Tests out to 30° at 1 meter
- Targets: colored pigments

### 8.5 VF Defect Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Threshold** | Weakest stimulus seen 50% of the time at a given location |
| **Suprathreshold** | Stimulus seen 95% of the time |
| **Infrathreshold** | Stimulus seen only 5% of the time (low intensity) |
| **Sensitivity** | Most subtle stimulus visible at a specific point |
| **Fixation** | VF area corresponding to the fovea centralis |

**VF Defect Descriptors:**
- **Density** — Absolute (no perception even to maximum stimulus) vs. Relative (reduced sensitivity but present to stronger stimuli)
- **Area** — General (diffuse) or Local (focal)
- **Shape** — Sectorial/hemianopic vs. non-sectorial (scotoma)
- **Extent** — Total or Partial
- **Position** — Temporal, Nasal, Superior, Inferior; Right or Left
- **Laterality** — Monocular vs. Binocular
- **Equalness** — Congruous (same shape/size in both eyes) vs. Incongruous
- **Awareness** — Positive (patient sees the defect) vs. Negative (not subjectively noticed)

**Location classification:**
- Central: within 5° of fixation
- Paracentral: 5° to 30°
- Peripheral: beyond 30°

### 8.6 Lesion Localization and Visual Field Defects

> HIGH-YIELD: This is a frequent board topic. Know the rule: before the chiasm = same-side defect; at or after the chiasm = cross-referencing applies.

| Lesion Location | Visual Field Defect |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Right optic nerve / retina | Right eye total field loss (monocular blindness) |
| Left optic nerve / retina | Left eye total field loss |
| Optic chiasm (crossed fibers) | Bitemporal heteronymous hemianopsia |
| Optic chiasm (uncrossed fibers) | Binasal heteronymous hemianopsia |
| Right optic tract | Left homonymous hemianopsia (incongruous) |
| Left optic tract | Right homonymous hemianopsia (incongruous) |
| LGB / optic radiations | Homonymous hemianopsia (more congruous) |
| Right occipital lobe (Area 17) | Left homonymous hemianopsia with macular sparing |
| Left occipital lobe (Area 17) | Right homonymous hemianopsia with macular sparing |

**Memory rules:**
- Retina and optic nerve = same side total VF defect
- Chiasm crossed lesion = exotropia-like heteronymous defect (temporal fields lost)
- Chiasm uncrossed lesion = esotropia-like heteronymous defect (nasal fields lost)
- Optic tract to Area 17 = opposite side homonymous defect

**Congruity rule:** The more posterior the lesion (closer to Area 17), the more congruous (symmetric) the homonymous defect.

### 8.7 Common Causes of VF Defects

| Type | Common Causes |
|------|---------------|
| Central VF loss | Optic neuropathy, macular degeneration, macular hole, cone dystrophies, Best's disease, Stargardt's |
| Peripheral VF loss | Retinitis pigmentosa, chorioretinitis, glaucoma, retinal detachment, Leber's optic atrophy |

### 8.8 Errors in Visual Field Testing

- Inadequate patient instruction
- Inattentive or uncooperative patient (fixation losses, unreliable responses)
- Fellow eye not properly patched
- Improper stimulus selection (size, brightness)
- Incorrect lens position or wrong corrective lens power
- Obstructing lens rim, droopy lid, or prominent brow (produces artificial field loss)
- Pupil too small — restricts light reaching the retina and mimics sensitivity loss

## 9. Scotoma

**Scotoma** — a localized area of absent or depressed visual sensitivity within the visual field, surrounded by normal (or relatively normal) vision.

| Classification | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Absolute** | No visual response even to maximum stimulus (e.g., optic disc blind spot) |
| **Relative** | Visible to bright/large stimuli but not to dim/small ones |
| **Negative** | Patient is unaware of it (discovered on testing) — e.g., physiologic blind spot |
| **Positive** | Patient is subjectively aware of a dark or bright spot |
| **Central** | Located within the central fixation area |
| **Peripheral** | Located in peripheral field |
| **Scintillating** | Shimmering/flickering border; migraine aura |
| **Arcuate (Bjerrum)** | Arc-shaped defect from the blind spot; classic in glaucoma |
| **Nasal step** | Step defect in nasal field at the horizontal raphe; glaucoma |
| **Centro-cecal** | Connects fixation point to the blind spot; toxic/nutritional optic neuropathy |

## 10. The Pupil

### 10.1 Basic Definitions

- **Miosis** — pupil constriction
- **Mydriasis** — pupil dilation
- **Entrance pupil** — the virtual image of the pupil as seen through the cornea from the front
- **Exit pupil** — the virtual image of the pupil as seen through the lens from the retinal side

Both pupils serve as **conjugate foci** in the normal state (equal in size and reactions).

### 10.2 Pupillary Reflexes

| Reflex | Stimulus | Response |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **Direct light reflex** | Light in one eye | Constriction of that same eye |
| **Consensual (indirect) light reflex** | Light in one eye | Constriction of the opposite eye |
| **Accommodation-near reflex** | Near fixation demand | Miosis + convergence + accommodation |
| **Trigeminal reflex** | Touching cornea | Bilateral blink + miosis |
| **Psychic reflex** | Fear, pain, emotion | Mydriasis |

### 10.3 Pupillary Pathway (Photo-Pupil Reflex)

**Afferent limb:**
Retina → Optic nerve → Optic chiasma → Optic tract → Pre-tectal nucleus (dorsal midbrain)

**Efferent limb:**
Edinger-Westphal nucleus → Pre-ganglionic fibers (CN III) → Ciliary ganglion → Post-ganglionic fibers → Sphincter pupillae (and ciliary muscle)

> Because afferent fibers from each eye project to BOTH pre-tectal nuclei bilaterally, light in one eye produces bilateral constriction (direct + consensual). Damage to the afferent limb of one eye impairs both the direct and consensual reflex from that eye.

### 10.4 Pupillary Lesion Effects

**RAPD (Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect):**
- Afferent defect — one optic nerve or retina is damaged
- On swinging flashlight test: when light moves to the affected eye, both pupils dilate (the afferent signal is weaker)
- Test: Swinging flashlight test (Marcus Gunn pupil)

**REPD (Relative Efferent Pupillary Defect):**
- Efferent limb damaged (CN III pathway or ciliary ganglion)
- Affected eye cannot constrict when light is shone in either eye, but the fellow eye responds normally

### 10.5 Pupil Abnormalities

| Condition | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Anisocoria** | Unequal pupil sizes |
| **Dyscoria** | Irregular pupil shape |
| **Polycoria** | Multiple pupil openings |
| **Corectopia** | Displaced pupil from normal central position |
| **Iridoplegia** | Paralysis of the iris |
| **Iridodialysis** | Separation of iris from ciliary body at root |
| **Hippus** | Rhythmic oscillation of pupil size |
| **Amaurotic pupil** | Blind eye shows no direct reflex but consensual remains |

### 10.6 Specific Pupil Syndromes

**Adie's Tonic Pupil (Holmes-Adie Syndrome)**
- Dilated pupil with very slow/absent light reaction
- Slow, tonic response to near stimuli ("light-near dissociation")
- Due to damage to the ciliary ganglion or short ciliary nerves
- Hypersensitive to 0.1% pilocarpine (denervation supersensitivity)
- Often associated with absent deep tendon reflexes (Adie's syndrome)

**Argyll-Robertson Pupil**
- Small, irregular pupils
- No reaction to light, but brisk constriction to near (light-near dissociation)
- Classic for **tertiary syphilis**
- Lesion: dorsal midbrain (pre-tectal area)
- Mnemonic: "Argyll-Robertson pupils are like prostitutes — they accommodate but don't react"

**Horner's Syndrome**
- Triad: ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis
- Due to interruption of the sympathetic pathway to the eye
- Sympathetic pathway: hypothalamus → ciliospinal center (C8-T2) → superior cervical ganglion → dilator pupillae
- Anhidrosis localizes the lesion: face only (postganglionic); face + body (preganglionic/central)
- Test: cocaine drops (both pupils dilate in normal; affected eye doesn't dilate in Horner's) and hydroxyamphetamine (post vs. pre-ganglionic differentiation)

## 11. Photoreceptors and Visual Adaptation

### 11.1 Rods vs. Cones

| Feature | Rods | Cones |
|---------|------|-------|
| Number | ~120 million | ~6-7 million |
| Location | Peripheral retina (max at 20° from fovea) | Concentrated in fovea |
| Photopigment | Rhodopsin | Photopsin (3 types) |
| Sensitivity | High (single photon in optimal conditions) | Low (requires bright light) |
| Adaptation | Slower | Faster |
| Visual resolution | Poor | High |
| Color sensitivity | No | Yes |
| Scotopic/Photopic | Scotopic (dark-adapted) | Photopic (light-adapted) |
| Functions | Peripheral vision, motion detection, dark vision | Central vision, color, fine detail |

> No rods at: (1) the fovea, (2) the optic disc.
> No cones at: the optic disc.

### 11.2 Rod and Cone Distribution

- Maximum rod density: ~150,000/mm² at **20° peripheral to the fovea**
- Maximum cone density: ~150,000/mm² at the **fovea**
- Only 4% of all retinal cones are in the fovea; 96% are in the periphery

### 11.3 Cone Photopigments

| Cone Type | Photopigment | Wavelength Range | Peak (nm) | Perceived Color |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-----------|-----------------|
| S cone (short) | Cyanolabe | Short | 430 | Blue/blue-violet |
| M cone (middle) | Chlorolabe | Middle | 535 | Green |
| L cone (long) | Erythrolabe | Long | 565 | Red |

> There are **no S-cones in the fovea**.
> All cones respond to a broad range of wavelengths; the names reflect the peak of their absorption spectra.

### 11.4 Visual Adaptation (Duplicity Theory)

**Light (bright) adaptation** — quicker; cone photopigment regenerates rapidly.

**Dark adaptation** — slower; rod photopigment (rhodopsin) regenerates more slowly (up to 20-30 minutes for full rod adaptation).

- Rhodopsin is more sensitive to light than cone pigments
- In very dim light, only rod signals remain — producing achromatic (colorless) vision

**Purkinje Effect** — as light decreases, the visual system shifts from cone-dominated to rod-dominated, and peak sensitivity shifts from ~555nm (photopic) to ~507nm (scotopic). Objects that appear brighter in photopic conditions may appear dimmer in scotopic conditions (and vice versa).

### 11.5 Retinal Image vs. Optical Image

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Optical image** | Image formed by the eye's optical system; clearly focused but may fall in front of or behind the retina |
| **Retinal image** | Image actually formed on the retina; may be sharply focused or a blur circle |

**When optical image = retinal image:** emmetropia, or when the refractive error is fully corrected.

**Blur circle size is determined by:**
1. Distance of optical image from the retina — farther away = larger blur circle
2. Size of the pupil — smaller pupil = smaller blur circle
3. Depth of focus/field

**Depth of Focus** — how far the image can be displaced from the retina and still appear clear.
**Depth of Field** — how far an object can be moved in space and still remain in clear focus without changing lens power.

**Retinal image size:**
- Varies **directly** with object size
- Varies **inversely** with object distance

**Vision in the visual field center:**
- Photopic conditions; highest acuity and color sensitivity
- 10x better acuity than peripheral field

**Peripheral vision:**
- Scotopic conditions; sensitive to dim light
- Poor color and poor acuity; better motion detection

## 12. Color Vision

### 12.1 What Is Color Vision?

- The ability to discriminate light based on differences in **wavelength composition**
- Depends on the three cone types and subsequent neural processing
- Visible spectrum: approximately **390 nm to 700 nm**

Spectral color = a color evoked by a single wavelength or narrow band. The traditional names are red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet.

**Color is described by three dimensions:**
- **Hue** — the color appearance (red, green, blue, etc.)
- **Saturation** — purity; saturated = single dominant wavelength; desaturated = mixed/diluted with white
- **Brightness/Luminance** — apparent light intensity

### 12.2 Theories of Color Vision

**1. Trichromatic Theory (Young-Helmholtz)**
- Based on three types of cones with overlapping but distinct absorption spectra
- Each location in the visual field produces three signals (tristimulus values) based on the proportion of stimulation of S, M, and L cones
- Explains additive color mixing and primary colors
- In dim light, cones are understimulated and only rods respond, giving colorless vision

**2. Opponent Color Theory (Ewald Hering)**
- Cones do not process color independently — their outputs are fed into opponent channels
- Three opponent channels:
 - Red vs. Green
 - Blue vs. Yellow
 - Black vs. White (luminance)
- Explains why "reddish-green" and "yellowish-blue" are perceptually impossible — they are processed on the same opponent channel

Both theories are correct at different levels: trichromacy operates at the cone level; opponency operates at the ganglion cell and lateral geniculate level.

**Color Constancy**
- The brain's ability to recognize an object as the same color under different lighting conditions
- Achieved via double-opponent cells in the primary visual cortex
- **Retinex Theory (Edwin H. Land)** — both the eye and brain contribute to color constancy

### 12.3 Color Vision Anomalies

**Classification:**

```
Normal: Trichromacy (three normal cone types)

Anomalous Trichromacy: Three cones present, one shifted
 - Protanomaly: red cone shifted (red weakness)
 - Deuteranomaly: green cone shifted (most common CVD, X-linked)
 - Tritanomaly: blue cone shifted (rare)

Dichromacy: One cone type absent
 - Protanopia: no red cones (most common dichromacy)
 - Deuteranopia: no green cones
 - Tritanopia: rarest dichromacy (1 in 1 million)

Monochromacy (Achromatopsia):
 - Cone monochromacy (incomplete): only one cone type; VA normal
 - S-cone, M-cone, or L-cone monochromacy
 - Rod monochromacy (complete achromatopsia): no functional cones
 - Not sex-linked; occurs in 0.003%
 - Symptoms: total color blindness, reduced VA, nystagmus, hemeralopia, central scotoma

Tetrachromacy: Four cone types (rare, found in some women)
```

**Detailed Anomalous Trichromacy:**

| Type | Affected Cone | Common Name | Incidence |
|------|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| Deuteranomaly | Green cone (shifted) | Green weakness | ~5% of males; most common CVD overall |
| Protanomaly | Red cone (shifted) | Red weakness | Less common than deuteranomaly |
| Tritanomaly | Blue cone (shifted) | Blue weakness | Very rare (<0.01%) |

**Detailed Dichromacy:**

| Type | Missing Cone | What They Confuse |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Protanopia | Red (L) | Red/green; also reds appear very dark |
| Deuteranopia | Green (M) | Red/green; similar presentation to protanopia |
| Tritanopia | Blue (S) | Yellow/blue; rarest (1 in 1 million) |

> X-linked inheritance — CVD is far more common in males (they only need one affected X chromosome).

**Non-standard color perception:**
- **Synesthesia** — perceiving numbers/letters as colors (grapheme-color) or music as colors (music-color)

### 12.4 Color Vision Tests

#### Pseudoisochromatic Plates

**Ishihara Color Test**
- Tests red-green color deficiencies only
- Available in 18, 24, or 38 plate versions
- Plates 1-17 scored clinically

**Procedure:**
- VA must be 20/200 or better
- Good illumination required
- Test distance: **75 cm**
- Viewing time: **3 seconds** (maximum 5 seconds)

**Grading:**
- Normal: 4 or fewer errors out of 17 plates
- Fail: 5 or more errors

**Plate Types:**

| Plate Type | Plate Numbers | Purpose |
|------------|---------------|---------|
| Demonstration | Plate 1 | Visible to all; used to verify understanding ("12") |
| Transformation | Plates 2-7 | CVD patients see a different number than normal |
| Vanishing | Plates 8-13 | Normal patients see numbers; CVD patients see nothing |
| Hidden digit | Plates 14-15 | Only CVD patients see the number |
| Diagnostic | Plates 16-17 | Distinguishes protanopia vs. deuteranopia and severity |

**If patient fails Plate 1 (Demonstration), suspect:** misunderstood instructions, malingering, reduced VA, intellectual disability.

---

**Hardy-Rand-Rittler (HRR)**
- 24 plates; produced in 1954
- Can classify ALL three types of CVD (protan, deutan, tritan)
- Unlike Ishihara, HRR tests for tritan defects as well

#### Arrangement Tests

**Farnsworth D15 Dichotomous Test**
- Modified from Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue Test
- 1 reference disc + 15 numbered discs forming an incomplete color circle
- Patient arranges discs in order
- Classifies defects along deutan, protan, or tritan axes
- **Dichotomous** because it separates subjects into two groups:
 - Strongly/moderately color deficient
 - Mildly deficient or normal
- Indeterminate defects possible in retinal toxicity

**Lanthony Desaturated D15**
- Uses unsaturated (pale) discs
- More sensitive — detects early or mild defects that the D15 misses
- Not appropriate for patients who already failed the D15

**Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue Test**
- Most sensitive arrangement test; 100 colored discs
- Scores the total color discrimination error

#### Lanterns

- Simulate colored signal lights; primarily used as **vocational tests**
- High practical value; direct assessment of color naming ability
- Limitation: do not reveal the nature or severity of the underlying CVD

| Lantern | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Holmes-Wright | Shows pairs of colored lights (2 greens, 2 reds, 1 white); patient names colors |
| Farnsworth Lantern (Falant) | US standard; passes patients with mild CVD |

#### Anomaloscope

- Most accurate test for distinguishing dichromats from anomalous trichromats and grading severity
- Based on:
 - **Rayleigh match** — patient mixes red + green to match a yellow target
 - **Moreland match** — blue-green mixture to test tritan defects
- Anomalous trichromats reject the normal match; their matching range indicates severity
- Protan deficiency: uses more red to match
- Deutan deficiency: uses more green to match

### 12.5 Color Mixing

| Type | Primary Colors | All Present | None Present |
|------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Additive** (light) | Red, Green, Blue | White | Black |
| **Subtractive** (pigment) | Cyan, Magenta, Yellow | Black | White |

**Additive combinations:**
- Red + Green = Yellow
- Red + Blue = Magenta
- Blue + Green = Cyan

Used in: television, computer monitors.

**Subtractive combinations:**
- Cyan + Yellow = Green (absorbs red + blue)
- Used in printing and painting

## 13. Entoptic Imagery and Special Visual Phenomena

### 13.1 Entoptic Phenomenon

Visual perceptions produced by the structures of the eye itself (either normal anatomic components or pathologic opacities).

| Phenomenon | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Purkinje figures** | Images of the retinal blood vessels made visible by oblique illumination |
| **Floaters** | Vitreous opacities casting shadows on the retina |
| **Blue field (Scheerer's) phenomenon** | Tiny bright dots moving along squiggly paths; caused by WBCs moving in retinal capillaries |
| **Moore's Lightning Streaks** | Flashes of light like lightning; associated with vitreous traction; stress-related |
| **Phosphene** | Perception of light without actual light entering the eye; from stimulation of retinal ganglion cells |

**Types of phosphene:**
- Pressure phosphene — applying pressure to the sclera, especially in the dark
- Flick phosphene — rapid eye movement produces a brief flash
- Mechanical phosphene — sneezing, coughing, laughing, blowing the nose
- Electrical phosphene — electrical stimulation of the visual cortex

### 13.2 Halos

- **Physiologic halo:** radial arrangement of lens fibers acts as a diffraction grating; colored halos around point light sources
- **Pathologic halo:** increased IOP (glaucoma), radiation keratopathy

### 13.3 Afterimage

| Type | Cause | Appearance |
|------|-------|-----------|
| **Negative** | Over-stimulation of cones | Opposite (complementary) color to original |
| **Positive** | Impulse transmission in dark-adapted eye | Same color as original |

### 13.4 Binocular Fusion and Depth Perception

**Binocular fusion** — the brain's ability to combine slightly different images from each eye into a single percept (based on Law of Retinal/Sensory Correspondence).

**Retinal image disparity** — the two retinal images are slightly different (disparate) due to the lateral separation of the eyes. This disparity provides stereopsis (depth perception).

When disparity is too great, fusion fails:
- Brain suppresses one image, or
- Develops ARC (Anomalous Retinal Correspondence)
- Uncorrected strabismus from birth may cause amblyopia

**Monocular cues for depth perception:**

| Cue | How It Works |
|-----|-------------|
| Relative size | Larger objects perceived as closer |
| Motion parallax | Closer objects appear to move faster during observer movement |
| Aerial perspective | Distant objects appear hazy/blurred |
| Linear perspective | Parallel lines converge toward distance |
| Overlap/interposition | Partially obscured object perceived as farther away |

### 13.5 Pulfrich Phenomenon

- A psychophysical percept where lateral motion of an object is perceived as having a depth component
- Binocular — a pendulum swinging in a straight left-right path appears to swing in an ellipse
- Caused by latency disparity between the two visual pathways (one eye's signal is delayed)
- Named for German physicist Carl Pulfrich (1922)
- Clinically: usually induced by a dark filter over one eye; also occurs spontaneously with:
 - Cataract
 - Optic neuritis
 - Multiple sclerosis
- Symptom: difficulty judging paths of oncoming cars

### 13.6 Brightness Acuity Test (BAT)

Used to assess functional visual acuity under glare conditions.

**Indications:** Cataract, corneal opacities, posterior capsular opacification, vitreous opacities

**Three brightness settings:**

| Setting | Luminance | Equivalent Condition |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| High | 400 ft-lamberts | Direct overhead sunlight (10,000 ft-candles); white concrete/sand |
| Medium | 100 ft-lamberts | Indirect sunlight (2,500 ft-candles); partly cloudy day |
| Low | 12 ft-lamberts | Bright fluorescent indoor lighting (300 ft-candles) |

### 13.7 Macular Photostress Test

Uses the BAT to photostress the retina when maculopathy is suspected.

**Recovery time after photostress:**

| Recovery Time | Interpretation |
|---------------|----------------|
| 0-30 seconds | Normal |
| 30-60 seconds | Marginal/borderline prolongation |
| > 60 seconds | Definite maculopathy |

### 13.8 Contrast Sensitivity

- **Contrast** — degree of difference between target luminance and background luminance
- **Contrast threshold** — smallest contrast detectable for a given stimulus
- **Contrast sensitivity** — the reciprocal of contrast threshold (higher = more sensitive)
- **Liminal threshold** — the minimum energy needed to stimulate the retina and create a visual sensation

Test: **Pelli-Robson Contrast Sensitivity Chart**

## 14. Clinical Manifestations of Retinal Dysfunction

### 14.1 Vitamin A Deficiency

- Vitamin A is oxidized to all-trans retinal, which is critical for photopigment synthesis
- Cannot be synthesized by the body — must be ingested
- Deficiency leads to: night blindness (nyctalopia), xerophthalmia, and eventually permanent blindness from photoreceptor loss

### 14.2 Retinitis Pigmentosa

- Inherited disorder; progressive failure of photoreceptor cells
- Classic form: defective opsin prevents rhodopsin formation
- Rods fail first — leading to night blindness and peripheral field loss
- Cones may remain functional initially (central vision preserved)
- Other forms progress to cone involvement, destroying central vision

**Characteristic symptoms and signs:**
- Night blindness
- Concentric (ring-shaped) peripheral visual field loss
- Bone spicule pigmentation in the peripheral retina
- Arteriolar attenuation
- Waxy disc pallor

### 14.3 Macular Degeneration

- Leading cause of blindness in the elderly

| Type | Mechanism | Result |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Dry AMD** | Intraocular cell proliferation in the macular area | Slow, progressive central vision loss |
| **Wet AMD** | Choroidal neovascularization invades the macular area | Rapid, severe central vision loss |

Both types produce central visual field loss.

### 14.4 Retinal Detachment

- Neural retina separates from the RPE
- Vision lost in the area of detachment
- Neural retina depends on RPE for: 11-cis retinal, glucose, ions, outer segment support, and photopigment resynthesis
- Treatment: laser photocoagulation to weld the edges (detached area becomes functionally blind), scleral buckle, vitrectomy
- Symptom: flashes (photopsia), floaters, "curtain" across vision

### 14.5 Diabetic Retinopathy

- Pathological process: microaneurysms and punctate hemorrhages in the retina
- Swollen/leaking blood vessels in the underlying choroid damage receptors and neurons
- Produces localized scotomas (often peripheral temporal field in early stages)
- Treatment: laser photocoagulation to seal leaking vessels
- Perimetry finding: peripheral scotoma in the affected eye's temporal hemifield (early presentation)
