B1-01 — Visual System and Ocular Optics

The Simple Version

The eye is both a camera and a signal processor. As a camera, it refracts light through six cardinal optical points and focuses it on the retina. As a signal processor, it has two detector types — rods for dark/peripheral sensing and cones for bright/color/detail — operating on different wavelengths and adapting to light changes through chemical, migratory, and electrical mechanisms. The entire system can be described using three axes (for eye movement), four key angles (for visual alignment), and a characteristic dark adaptation curve that shows exactly how sensitivity rebuilds over 30–40 minutes in the dark. Understanding these foundations links anatomy directly to clinical optometry: why a patient fumbles entering a dark theater, why a faint star is easier to see off-center, and why two eyes are better than one for threshold tasks.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Why can you see a dim star better by looking slightly to the side of it?" The answer explains rod distribution, convergence, and averted vision in one question.

But Why?

Why do rods reach maximum density at ~20° from the fovea instead of at the fovea itself?

Because the fovea is specialized for cones — all rods are excluded from the central 0.35mm. The peripheral retina is rod territory, and rods peak at ~20° eccentricity.

Why does rod sensitivity keep improving for 30–40 minutes in the dark while cone sensitivity plateaus at 7 minutes?

Because rhodopsin (rod pigment) regenerates much more slowly than cone pigments. Cone pigments reform in ~7 minutes; rhodopsin regeneration is a long enzymatic process requiring vitamin A and takes the full 30–40 minutes.

Why does the Purkinje shift happen?

Because photopic vision depends on cones (peak 555nm), while scotopic vision depends on rods (peak 507nm). As light fades and cones become less effective, the system shifts to rod-dominated sensing — and rods are more sensitive to shorter (blue-green) wavelengths.

Why is the anteroposterior axis the longest at 24mm while the other two are 22mm?

Because the AP axis runs the full geometric depth of the eyeball (front to back), which is the longest dimension. The horizontal and vertical axes cross at the center of rotation within the eye, so they are shorter.

Retinal changes on light stimulation (MCE):

Practice Questions

1. During Hirschberg testing, the corneal light reflex falls approximately 1mm temporal to the corneal center in both eyes. Cover test reveals no movement. What is the most likely explanation?

  • A. Bilateral exotropia
  • B. Positive angle kappa
  • C. Negative angle kappa
  • D. Large angle alpha
Answer

B. Positive angle kappa.

Positive angle kappa exists when the center of the entrance pupil is nasal to the visual axis. Because the pupil is nasal, the corneal light reflex appears displaced temporally — simulating exotropia on Hirschberg. The negative cover test confirms no real deviation is present. This is pseudo-exotropia due to positive angle kappa. Negative kappa would produce a nasal reflex displacement (pseudo-esotropia). Angle alpha is measured at the nodal point and does not produce this clinical finding directly.

2. A patient is tested in a dark room after 40 minutes of adaptation. At what wavelength of monochromatic light would their visual threshold be lowest?

  • A. 440 nm
  • B. 507 nm
  • C. 535 nm
  • D. 555 nm
Answer

B. 507 nm.

After 40 minutes of dark adaptation, the visual system is operating in scotopic mode — driven entirely by rods. Rods have maximum sensitivity at approximately 507 nm (blue-green). The 555 nm peak applies to photopic (cone-driven) conditions. 440 nm is the S-cone peak (cyanolabe), relevant in photopic color vision. 535 nm is the M-cone (chlorolabe) peak. In scotopic conditions, all three cone types are essentially inactive, and rod sensitivity dominates.

3. A patient's rhodopsin cannot regenerate after light exposure. Which step in the visual cycle is most likely impaired?

  • A. Photoisomerization of 11-cis retinal in the outer segment
  • B. Transport of all-trans retinal from photoreceptors to RPE
  • C. Re-isomerization of all-trans retinal to 11-cis retinal in the RPE
  • D. Release of glutamate at the synaptic terminal in the dark
Answer

C. Re-isomerization of all-trans retinal to 11-cis retinal in the RPE.

Rhodopsin regeneration depends on recycling spent chromophore (all-trans retinal) back to its usable form (11-cis retinal). This enzymatic re-isomerization occurs in the RPE, not in the photoreceptor outer segment. After light hits rhodopsin, 11-cis retinal photoisomerizes to all-trans (Step A — this is the TRIGGER, not the regeneration step). All-trans retinal exits the photoreceptor and enters the RPE where the RPE65 enzyme converts it back to 11-cis retinal (Step C). RPE65 mutations cause Leber congenital amaurosis — a genetic cause of severe visual impairment. Option B (transport) is a prerequisite for C but not the regeneration step itself. Option D (glutamate release) is the dark-current synapse activity, unrelated to regeneration.

Extended Topics: Accommodation Mechanism, Ametropia Intro, Entoptic Phenomena, and Flicker

Accommodation — Full Coverage

Scheiner's Experiment (1619): A card with two pinholes (separated less than the pupil diameter) is held in front of the eye. An object NOT focused on the retina appears DOUBLE through the two pinholes. An object IN FOCUS on the retina appears SINGLE. This experiment proved accommodation EXISTS and that the eye actively adjusts its focus — it was the first objective proof of accommodation.

Purkinje-Sanson Images: Reflections (catoptric images) formed by the cornea and lens surfaces. There are THREE pairs:

Image 1: Corneal front surface → upright, virtual, bright
 (used in keratometry — measures corneal curvature)
Image 2: Lens front surface → upright, virtual, dim
 (moves forward during accommodation as anterior lens becomes more convex)
Image 3: Lens back surface → inverted, real, smallest
 (moves backward slightly during accommodation)

During accommodation: Image 2 moves FORWARD and becomes SMALLER (lens front surface steepens). Image 3 moves BACKWARD slightly. Image 1 is unchanged (cornea does not change shape).

Clinical use: Helmholtz (1855) used Purkinje images to prove the lens changes shape during accommodation, not the cornea or axial length.

Theories of Accommodation:

HELMHOLTZ THEORY (1855) — accepted as correct:
 At REST: Zonules are TAUT (stretched by ciliary ring diameter)
 Lens is FLAT and thin (held in its thinnest form by tension)
 During ACCOMMODATION (near):
 Ciliary muscle CONTRACTS (annular action, ring tightens)
 Zonules RELAX (tension released)
 Lens springs to natural spherical shape (MORE CONVEX, more power)
 Pupil constricts, eyes converge (the near triad)

Why the lens springs: The elastic lens capsule is always trying to make the lens spherical.
The taut zonules prevent this at rest. Relaxing the zonules frees the capsule → sphere.

Near Point, Far Point, and Range of Accommodation:

FAR POINT (punctum remotum) — the point at infinity (∞) that the eye can see clearly
 Emmetrope: far point = infinity (∞)
 Myope: far point is at a FINITE distance IN FRONT of the eye
 Far point distance = 100 / degree of myopia
 Example: −3.00D myope → far point = 100/3 = 33cm in front
 Hyperope: far point is VIRTUAL (behind the eye)
 
NEAR POINT (punctum proximum) — the closest point the eye can clearly focus (with maximum accommodation)
 Near point distance = 100 / (far point power + AOA)
 Normal 20-year-old (AOA ≈ 10D): near point ≈ 10cm

RANGE OF ACCOMMODATION — distance between near point and far point
 Emmetrope: from ∞ to near point
 Myope: from far point to near point (both finite)
 
AMPLITUDE OF ACCOMMODATION (AOA) — vergence change from far point to near point
 AOA = Far point power − Near point power (in diopters)
 Decreases with age (Hofstetter: Ave = 18.5 − 0.30 × age)

Ametropia — Introductory (Syllabi 3.0)

Classification by mechanism:

AXIAL AMETROPIA: Abnormal axial length; refractive power is normal
 Myopia → eye TOO LONG → focal point in front of retina
 Hyperopia → eye TOO SHORT → focal point behind retina

REFRACTIVE AMETROPIA: Normal axial length; refractive power abnormal
 High n (aqueous, cornea, lens nucleus) → myopia
 Low n (lens cortex, vitreous) → hyperopia
 Changed corneal curvature → any ametropia

LAW OF SILO (Index Ametropia):
 Applies when the ametropia is due to the index of refraction of the various media:
 Crystalline lens cortex: Lower n than average → nucleus: Higher n than average
 Changes in lens index with disease (e.g., cataract) → can cause myopic shift

BRACHYCEPHALIA vs DOLICHOCEPHALIA:
 Brachycephalia (short, wide skull) → associated with hyperopia
 Dolichocephalia (long, narrow skull) → associated with myopia
 (Skull shape affects orbital depth and axial length development)

Flicker and Critical Fusion Frequency (Syllabi 8.0)

Repetitive stimulation and flicker:

  • When a light is turned on and off rapidly, it appears to flicker at low frequencies
  • As the frequency increases, the flickering eventually disappears and the light appears steady
  • The frequency at which flickering merges into steady illumination = Critical Fusion Frequency (CFF)
CFF NORMAL VALUES:
 Photopic (daylight): ~60 Hz
 Scotopic (dim): ~15–20 Hz
 Fovea: higher CFF than periphery (faster temporal resolution)

FERRY-PORTER LAW: CFF increases linearly with log luminance
TALBOT'S LAW: When flicker rate exceeds CFF, perceived brightness = average of on/off intensities

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
 Decreased CFF → early sign of optic nerve disease, glaucoma
 CFF testing: used to detect subclinical optic nerve dysfunction before visual field loss
 Video/film: 24 fps appears continuous because it exceeds the CFF for those conditions

Optical Illusions (Syllabi 17.0)

Optical illusions demonstrate that perception is not a direct recording of reality — the brain actively processes and sometimes misinterprets visual information.

Categories:

1. GEOMETRIC ILLUSIONS — size, length, or angle appear different than measured:
 Müller-Lyer illusion: lines with arrows in vs. out appear different lengths
 Ponzo illusion: parallel lines appear different lengths on converging railroad tracks
 Horizontal-vertical illusion: vertical line appears longer than equal horizontal line

2. AMBIGUOUS FIGURES — two interpretations exist simultaneously:
 Necker cube: 3D cube flips orientation
 Rubin's vase: vase or two faces alternate

3. AFTERIMAGE-BASED ILLUSIONS:
 Stare at a color → look away → see the complementary color (opponent process)
 Red fatigue → look at white → see green (red afterimage)

4. MOTION ILLUSIONS:
 Static images appear to move due to high-contrast gradients
 
BOARD RELEVANCE:
 Illusions demonstrate: retinal adaptation (afterimages), opponent process (complementary colors),
 figure-ground perception (ambiguous figures), context effects on size/distance judgment.

Phototransduction Cascade — The Full Mechanism

Why it matters: The board tests the specific molecular steps, where Vitamin A fits, and why its deficiency causes night blindness first.

The cascade — rods (rhodopsin system):

STEP 1 — AT REST (DARK)
 11-cis retinal + opsin → RHODOPSIN (intact photopigment, purple-red)
 cGMP-gated Na+ channels are OPEN in the dark
 → constant Na+ influx → photoreceptor is DEPOLARIZED (dark current)
 → continuous glutamate release onto bipolar cells

STEP 2 — LIGHT STRIKES RHODOPSIN
 Photon energy → PHOTOISOMERIZATION
 11-cis retinal flips → ALL-TRANS RETINAL
 Rhodopsin → METARHODOPSIN II (activated form)
 Called "photo-bleaching" — rhodopsin loses its purple color → appears pale/yellow

STEP 3 — TRANSDUCIN ACTIVATION (G-protein cascade)
 Metarhodopsin II activates TRANSDUCIN (a G-protein)
 1 rhodopsin → activates ~500 transducin molecules
 (amplification factor x500 — explains extreme sensitivity of rods to single photons)

STEP 4 — PHOSPHODIESTERASE (PDE) ACTIVATION
 Transducin activates PDE (phosphodiesterase)
 PDE hydrolyzes cGMP → 5'-GMP (inactive)
 cGMP levels FALL rapidly inside the photoreceptor

STEP 5 — CHANNEL CLOSURE → HYPERPOLARIZATION
 cGMP-gated channels CLOSE (no cGMP to keep them open)
 Na+ entry STOPS → membrane potential goes from −40mV → −70mV
 Photoreceptor HYPERPOLARIZES in response to LIGHT
 (Key: light causes hyperpolarization — opposite of all other neurons)

STEP 6 — SIGNAL OUTPUT CHANGE
 Hyperpolarized photoreceptor → DECREASED glutamate release
 Bipolar cells detect this decrease → signal passes to ganglion cells → optic nerve

Critical rule: Photoreceptors are the only sensory neurons that HYPERPOLARIZE in response to their stimulus. Every other neuron depolarizes to fire a signal. This is routinely tested.

Extended Topics: Optical Aberrations of the Eye

The Plain Version First

A perfect lens takes every ray from an object point and converges them all to the exact same image point. Real lenses — including the eye — don't do this perfectly. Some rays end up converging at a slightly different point than others. The result is a blurred or distorted image even when there is no refractive error. These imperfections are called optical aberrations.

There are two categories: monochromatic aberrations (happen with single-wavelength light — geometry problem) and chromatic aberrations (happen because different wavelengths bend differently — a wavelength problem).

Monochromatic Aberrations — Seidel's Five

These are named after Ludwig Seidel (1857) who mathematically described the 5 types. They occur because real optical surfaces are not mathematically perfect.

Think of it this way: rays hitting the center of a lens and rays hitting the edge don't focus at exactly the same point — that mismatch is what all five aberrations describe in different ways.

ABERRATION 1: SPHERICAL ABERRATION
 What it is: Peripheral rays focus at a DIFFERENT point than central (paraxial) rays
 
 Plain version: The edge of the lens is a slightly different power than the center.
 Peripheral rays → converge closer to the lens (short focal point)
 Central rays → converge farther (long focal point)
 
 Result: A point source becomes a blurred circle ("blur circle") on the retina
 
 Clinical relevance:
 - LARGER PUPIL = MORE spherical aberration (more peripheral rays enter)
 - Pupil constriction REDUCES spherical aberration (limits entry of peripheral rays)
 - This is why vision is slightly sharper in bright light (miotic pupil)
 - Aspheric lens designs compensate by flattening the peripheral curve

ABERRATION 2: CHROMATIC ABERRATION
 What it is: DIFFERENT WAVELENGTHS focus at DIFFERENT points
 
 Plain version: Blue light bends more than red light because it has a higher index of refraction.
 Blue → shorter focal length → focuses IN FRONT of retina
 Red → longer focal length → focuses BEHIND retina
 
 Types:
 LONGITUDINAL chromatic aberration (LCA): focal points differ along the axis
 Blue (short λ) → focuses ~2D more powerfully than red
 Measured in diopters; approximately 2.5D total range in the human eye
 
 TRANSVERSE chromatic aberration (TCA): colored fringes at the edge of objects
 Different wavelengths hit different retinal locations
 Seen as colored halos around high-contrast edges
 
 Clinical relevance:
 - BICHROME TEST (duochrome test) uses LCA:
 Red and green backgrounds → if image is on retina, red/green letters are equal clarity
 Letters clearer on RED → over-minused (push lens to red/less minus)
 Letters clearer on GREEN → under-minused (pull lens to green/more minus)
 - Abbe value (v-number): measures how much a lens material disperses light
 HIGH Abbe = LOW chromatic aberration = better image quality
 LOW Abbe = HIGH chromatic aberration = more color fringing
 Abbe value of human cornea: ~57; crystalline lens: ~47

ABERRATION 3: COMA
 What it is: Off-axis point sources produce comet-shaped blur (not round)
 
 Plain version: When you look at a point of light that is off to the side,
 the peripheral rays from different parts of the pupil focus at different heights.
 The result is a smeared, comet-tail distortion — wider toward the edges of the image.
 
 Key feature: Coma is worst for oblique rays (off-axis objects)
 The "tail" of the comet always points toward the center of the image
 Board clue: Coma = off-axis comet shape

ABERRATION 4: ASTIGMATISM OF OBLIQUE INCIDENCE (Oblique Astigmatism)
 What it is: Oblique rays form two focal lines (like regular astigmatism)
 but this is purely a lens geometry problem, not a corneal problem
 
 Plain version: When light hits a lens at an angle (not straight on),
 the lens acts like it has two different powers — a vertical focus and a horizontal focus.
 This happens even with a perfect spherical lens for off-axis targets.
 
 Distinguish from regular astigmatism:
 Regular astigmatism = due to corneal/lens toric surface
 Oblique astigmatism = due to angle of incidence at any surface
 
 Clinical relevance: Large minus lenses produce oblique astigmatism at the lens periphery
 → "swim" phenomenon in strong minus glasses when looking off-center

ABERRATION 5: CURVATURE OF FIELD (Petzval Field Curvature)
 What it is: The sharpest focus of a lens lies on a CURVED surface, not a flat plane
 
 Plain version: Imagine focusing a projector onto a flat wall — but the sharpest image
 actually exists on a curved surface. The center is sharp but the edges are blurry, or
 if you focus the edges, the center goes blurry.
 
 In the eye: The retina is curved, which partially compensates for this aberration.
 The eye's curved retina actually HELPS reduce the blur from curvature of field.
 Board clue: Curvature of field is REDUCED by the curved retina of the eye

ABERRATION 6: DISTORTION
 What it is: Magnification varies across the image — straight lines appear bent
 
 Plain version: Objects at the edge of the image appear larger or smaller than
 they should be relative to objects at the center.
 
 BARREL DISTORTION: edges appear compressed inward (like a barrel)
 → seen with minus lenses (diverging)
 PINCUSHION DISTORTION: edges appear expanded outward (like a pincushion)
 → seen with plus lenses (converging)
 
 Board clue: "Barrel = minus; Pincushion = plus"

Chromatic Aberration — The Bichrome Test (Clinical Application)

This is the most board-tested aberration because it has a direct clinical test:

BICHROME TEST PRINCIPLE:
 Background: Red and green (wavelengths flanking yellow focus point)

 → Add minus (or reduce plus) — "pull toward green"
 
 Mnemonic: "RAM GAP"
 R = Red → Add Minus
 G = Green → Add Plus
 (Or: Red = Reduce minus; Green = Give more minus)

B2-01 — Visual Perception and Sensory Organization

The Simple Version

Visual perception is what the brain does with what the eyes send. The retinal signal travels through six relay stations to reach the visual cortex, but "seeing" doesn't happen at the retina — it happens at the cortex and beyond. Beyond Area 17 (the primary visual cortex), two parallel streams process what objects are (the ventral "what" pathway) and where they are (the dorsal "where" pathway). The LGB organizes input from both eyes into distinct layers before passing it along. The key clinical insight: a patient can have perfect retinal and optical function yet still fail to perceive what they see — because perception is brain function, not just eye function.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Can a person have 20/20 acuity and still not be able to recognize their mother's face?" Yes — prosopagnosia, a ventral pathway lesion. That answer separates sight from perception.

But Why?

Why does the LGB have separate layers for each eye?

Because the brain maintains eye-of-origin information even at this relay stage — it is the last point where the two eyes' signals are segregated. This segregation is important for binocular rivalry and for understanding amblyopia (where one eye's cortical representation is suppressed at the LGB level).

Why are cortical areas 18 and 19 needed when area 17 already receives the signal?

Because area 17 only registers basic features — orientation, contrast, spatial frequency. Making sense of those features (recognizing a face, tracking a moving object, reading a word) requires further processing in areas 18 and 19 and beyond.

Practice Questions

1. A patient with multiple sclerosis has early optic neuritis. Which of the following visual functions would be affected earliest?

  • A. Motion perception
  • B. Form perception
  • C. Color perception
  • D. Spatial contrast sensitivity
Answer

C. Color perception.

Color is the most vulnerable visual function — it is selectively affected early in optic nerve disease because color perception depends on intact, well-myelinated, high-frequency nerve fiber conduction. Even subtle demyelination (as in early optic neuritis) preferentially disrupts color transmission before form or motion are noticeably impaired. This is the basis of the "red desaturation" test — patients with early optic neuritis report that a bright red target appears washed-out or pink in the affected eye compared to the normal eye. Motion is the most resilient function and is typically preserved until late disease.

2. A researcher doubles the intensity of a light stimulus from 100 to 200 units. According to Weber's Law, what must be true about the just noticeable difference (JND)?

  • A. The JND stays constant regardless of the background intensity
  • B. The JND doubles because the background intensity doubled
  • C. The JND stays a fixed fraction (k) of the new background intensity
  • D. The JND decreases because more light is available
Answer

C. The JND stays a fixed fraction (k) of the new background intensity.

Weber's Law states: ΔI/I = k. The ratio of the just noticeable difference (ΔI) to the background intensity (I) is a constant (k) — not ΔI itself. When the background doubles from 100 to 200, the new JND = k �� 200, which is twice the original JND. The absolute threshold goes up, but the ratio stays the same. This is the clinical basis for contrast sensitivity: in dim conditions (low I), a smaller ΔI is needed; in bright conditions (high I), a larger ΔI is needed for detection. Option A is wrong — the absolute ΔI does change with intensity. Option B correctly states the ΔI doubles but the reasoning "because the background doubled" misses the point — it is the constant ratio, not a direct doubling, that defines Weber's Law.

3. An ipRGC (intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cell) is stimulated by sustained bright light. Which of the following is the PRIMARY function being activated?

  • A. High-acuity pattern vision at the fovea
  • B. Motion detection in the peripheral visual field
  • C. Circadian rhythm entrainment via the suprachiasmatic nucleus
  • D. ON-pathway activation feeding into the LGB parvocellular layers
Answer

C. Circadian rhythm entrainment via the suprachiasmatic nucleus.

ipRGCs express melanopsin and project primarily to the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus via the retinohypothalamic tract — the master circadian clock pathway. They also project to the pretectal area (pupillary light reflex) and intergeniculate leaflet. They are non-image-forming cells — they do NOT contribute to pattern vision, motion detection, or LGB parvocellular/magnocellular pathways. Their slow, sustained response to ambient light level makes them ideal for signaling time-of-day (circadian entrainment) rather than detecting rapid changes in the scene. Disrupting this system (e.g., blue-light exposure at night) suppresses melatonin and disrupts sleep cycles.

Types of Visual Acuity (Syllabi 16.0)

Visual acuity is not a single entity — it has five distinct forms, each measuring a different aspect of the eye's resolving ability.

1. Minimum Visible (Detection Acuity)

  • The smallest object that can be DETECTED (not identified) against a background
  • Does not require resolution — only light/dark detection
  • Threshold: can detect a line ~0.5 arc seconds wide (single photoreceptor width)
  • Measured by: preferential looking tests, detection gratings (LEA gratings for infants)
  • Board clue: lowest threshold of all VA types — seeing that something is there, not what it is

2. Minimum Separable (Resolution Acuity)

  • The smallest gap between two objects that can be resolved as SEPARATE
  • Requires two adjacent points to be resolved as distinct
  • Normal: 1 arc minute (Snellen 20/20 = 1 arc minute minimum separable angle)
  • Measured by: Snellen chart, ETDRS chart, Landolt C, Tumbling E
  • Limited by: cone spacing at fovea, optical aberrations, neural summation
  • Board clue: most commonly tested VA type; Snellen charts measure this

3. Minimum Discriminable (Discrimination Acuity)

  • The smallest detectable DIFFERENCE between two stimuli
  • E.g., detecting a slight change in orientation, color, or size
  • Includes: orientation discrimination, contrast discrimination

4. Minimum Legible (Recognition Acuity)

  • The smallest print or symbol that can be IDENTIFIED (not just detected or separated)
  • Requires letter/symbol recognition — involves higher cortical processing
  • Standard optometric exam VA: reading letters on Snellen chart = minimum legible
  • Requires intact: optics + retina + optic nerve + visual cortex + cognitive processing

5. Vernier Acuity (Hyperacuity)

  • The ability to detect the OFFSET of two aligned line segments
  • Normal: 2–8 arc SECONDS (much finer than minimum separable = 1 arc MINUTE)
  • Threshold is below the diameter of a single cone — hence called "hyperacuity"
  • Why possible: the brain uses spatial interpolation across multiple photoreceptors
  • Measured by: Vernier acuity tests; laser interferometry
  • Preserved longer in disease than resolution acuity → used to predict potential acuity
  • Board clue: finest of all acuity types; below the anatomical limit of single photoreceptors

Contrast Sensitivity and the Contrast Sensitivity Function (CSF)

What is contrast sensitivity?

  • Visual acuity measures resolution at HIGH contrast (black letters on white)
  • Contrast sensitivity measures the ability to detect DIFFERENCES IN LUMINANCE across the visual scene
  • Real-world vision depends heavily on contrast sensitivity — most of what we see is not high-contrast black-on-white
CONTRAST THRESHOLD: Smallest amount of contrast needed to detect a stimulus
CONTRAST SENSITIVITY (CS): Reciprocal of contrast threshold
 CS = 1 / Contrast threshold
 Higher CS = can detect lower contrast = better

The Contrast Sensitivity Function (CSF):

A graph of contrast sensitivity vs. spatial frequency (cycles per degree)
 Spatial frequency: how many cycles (dark-light pairs) per degree of visual angle
 Low SF: large, coarse objects (like large fuzzy blobs)
 High SF: fine detail (like sharp edges, fine print)
 
NORMAL CSF SHAPE: Bandpass (inverted U)

 Falls off at:
 Low SF: less sensitive to very coarse patterns (surround inhibition)
 High SF: less sensitive to very fine detail (optical + neural limits)

 Patient with normal Snellen but reduced CSF → difficulty with:

 Early glaucoma (affects CSF before Snellen VA)

Extended Topics: Retinal Sensitivity Laws, Nyctalopia vs Hemeralopia, and Retinal Neuron Types

The Plain Version First

Imagine your eye as a light meter. When light is very dim, your meter needs to accumulate more light before it registers — you need either a bigger light or a longer exposure. When the light is bright, even a tiny amount triggers a reading. The eye obeys the same logic, and two scientists — Weber and Fechner — put numbers to this behavior.

Weber discovered: the smallest change in stimulus you can detect is always a fixed fraction of the starting intensity. Double the background brightness, and you need double the change to notice a difference. This fraction is called the Weber fraction or just noticeable difference (JND). The clinical term for this property is Weber's Law.

Fechner took Weber's Law further and said: the sensation you experience doesn't grow linearly — it grows as the logarithm of the stimulus intensity. This is called Fechner's Law (also called the Weber-Fechner Law). In plain terms: each time you multiply the stimulus by 10, you only add a fixed unit of sensation.

Weber's Law

PLAIN VERSION:
 The minimum change you can detect = a constant fraction of the current stimulus

FORMULA:
 ΔI / I = k (Weber fraction)

 Where:
 ΔI = the minimum detectable change in intensity (just noticeable difference, JND)
 I = the background stimulus intensity
 k = Weber fraction (a constant for a given sensory system)

EXAMPLE IN VISION:
 You are in a room with 100 units of light.
 The Weber fraction for brightness ≈ 0.02 (2%)
 → You can detect a change only if ΔI ≥ 2 units (2% of 100)
 
 Now the room is 1000 units of light.
 → You need ΔI ≥ 20 units to notice a change

CLINICAL MEANING:
 The eye's ability to detect contrast depends on the background luminance.
 In bright conditions, you need a bigger absolute difference to see contrast.
 In dim conditions, even small absolute differences are detectable.
 This is why a candle flame is easily visible in a dark room but invisible in sunlight.

The clinical term for the minimum detectable change: Just Noticeable Difference (JND) or Difference Limen (DL).

Fechner's Law (Weber-Fechner Law)

PLAIN VERSION:
 Your perception of brightness doesn't grow at the same rate as the actual light increase.
 Every time you multiply the physical stimulus by the same amount,
 you only add a fixed increment to your sensation.

FORMULA:
 S = k × log(I)

 Where:
 S = perceived sensation (subjective brightness)
 I = physical stimulus intensity
 k = constant

EXAMPLE:
 Light intensity: 1 unit → sensation = 0 (log 1 = 0)
 Light intensity: 10 units → sensation = k × 1
 Light intensity: 100 units → sensation = k × 2
 Light intensity: 1000 units → sensation = k × 3
 
 Each 10× increase in actual light adds only 1 unit of perceived sensation.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
 This is why a log scale is used to measure light, sound, and other sensory stimuli.
 It explains why the photopic range (100,000:1 light intensity range)
 feels like a manageable range of brightness to us — because perception is logarithmic.
 
CLINICAL CONNECTION:
 Log MAR charts (Bailey-Lovie, ETDRS) are based on Fechner's principle —
 each line adds equal perceptual steps rather than equal physical steps in letter size.

The clinical term for this relationship: Fechner's Law / Weber-Fechner Law.

These are the specific cell types that the board tests under retinal perception. They explain WHY the eye is so good at different tasks in different conditions.

B3-01 — Visual Field

The Simple Version

The visual field is the total area a person can see while fixating straight ahead. It extends further temporally (100°) than nasally (60°) because the nose blocks nasal vision, and further inferiorly (75°) than superiorly (60°) because the brow blocks the upper field. The two eyes' fields overlap in the center (120° binocular zone), with a 30° monocular crescent on each side. Different diseases affect different parts of the field in predictable patterns — and mapping those patterns with the right test reveals where in the visual pathway the problem is. Goldmann perimetry uses stimulus size and brightness to plot isopters (sensitivity contour lines), while automated perimetry uses static stimuli to build a threshold map.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Why does glaucoma cause peripheral field loss while macular degeneration causes central field loss?" The answer maps retinal topography to disease location.

But Why?

Why is the temporal field larger (100°) than the nasal field (60°)?

Because the nasal retina receives the temporal field — and nasal retina extends further than temporal retina due to the nose blocking peripheral nasal visual input. The temporal field of view is unobstructed.

Why does color fail before form and motion in visual field loss?

Because color perception requires the most precisely functioning cone pathways. Small lesions or early dysfunction preferentially knock out color sensitivity before the coarser form/motion channels are affected. This is why color is tested for early disease detection.

Why does Goldmann perimetry use kinetic stimuli while automated perimetry uses static?

Because kinetic testing (moving the target in) maps the boundary of sensitivity efficiently but misses patchy losses within the field. Static testing (fixed position, varying intensity) detects threshold at specific locations, catching scotomas the kinetic method would cross over without detection.

B4-01 — Color Perception

The Simple Version

Color vision works through three cone types, each sensitive to a different wavelength range. Their combined output is processed by opponent channels in the brain — red vs green, blue vs yellow — which is why those color pairs cannot be perceived simultaneously (you cannot see a reddish-green). Most color deficiency is genetic, X-linked, and affects males more than females. The board tests color theory names, color mixing rules, specific color test criteria, and the nomenclature of color deficiency. Knowing the tests cold — which plate is for everyone (Ishihara plate 1), which test uses lanterns (FALANT), and which is most comprehensive (FM-100) — covers the clinical side completely.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Why can't you see a reddish-green color?" The answer explains opponent process theory, which explains why Hering proposed a different theory than Young-Helmholtz.

But Why?

Why do we need three cone types and not two or four?

Because color discrimination depends on comparing the relative outputs of cone types. Two cones would only give a single ratio (one color dimension). Three cones give three ratios — enough to distinguish millions of colors. Four would add marginal benefit for most purposes.

Why do red-green defects occur more frequently than blue-yellow defects?

Because the genes for red (OPN1LW) and green (OPN1MW) cones are both on the X chromosome and are arranged in tandem — making them prone to unequal crossover during meiosis. Blue cone genes are on chromosome 7 and are much less susceptible to this recombination error.

Why does color perception fail earlier than motion in optic nerve disease?

Because color signals depend on the sustained (parvocellular) pathway, which uses small, high-frequency nerve fibers. These are selectively more vulnerable to demyelination and ischemia than the larger transient (magnocellular) fibers that carry motion signals.

B5-01 — Binocular Vision and Fusion

The Simple Version

Binocular vision is the coordinated use of both eyes to produce a single, three-dimensional mental image. It requires alignment (both eyes pointing at the same target), sensory fusion (the brain merging two slightly different images), and motor fusion (the eye muscles maintaining alignment). When alignment breaks down or fusion fails, the brain adapts — either by seeing double (diplopia), suppressing one eye's image, or developing an abnormal retinal correspondence where the deviated eye recalibrates its directional sense. Understanding BV anomalies requires three things: knowing where the deviation is (distance, near, or both), knowing the AC/A ratio (how much convergence accompanies each diopter of accommodation), and knowing which criteria tell you if the fusion is sufficient (Sheard's, Percival's).

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Why does a child with uncorrected high hyperopia often develop esotropia?" The answer explains AC/A ratio, accommodative esotropia, and why glasses alone can straighten the eyes.

But Why?

Why does a high AC/A ratio cause convergence excess?

Because AC/A tells you how much convergence is triggered per diopter of accommodation. If this ratio is abnormally high, every time the child accommodates for near — which requires +4D if they are +4D hyperopic — they generate massive convergence (4 × high AC/A). The eyes cross at near, but are straight at distance because convergence demand at distance is zero.

Why does suppression develop in strabismus?

Because the deviated eye projects its image to a different visual direction from the fixating eye, causing diplopia. The brain resolves the conflict by actively inhibiting the signal from the non-fixating eye — suppression. Without this adaptation, constant diplopia would be intolerable.

Why is NRC normal in heterophoria but ARC develops in strabismus?

Because in heterophoria, binocular vision is maintained most of the time and the foveas retain their common visual direction (NRC). In constant strabismus with a fixed angle of deviation, the brain has time to rewire the retinal correspondence — the non-foveal area of the deviated eye that consistently receives the fixation target's image gradually takes over the fovea's visual direction (ARC).

Why is Sheard's criterion a 2:1 ratio?

Because research shows that patients are comfortable when their fusional reserve is at least twice the demand placed on their vergence system. If the demand exceeds half the reserve, the system is working at maximum capacity and symptoms (asthenopia, diplopia) result.

AC/A ratio — the engine of accommodative vergence:

Normal AC/A: 4:1 to 6:1 (4–6 PD of convergence per 1D of accommodation)

Formula (Gradient method): AC/A = (phoria at near – phoria at distance) ÷ accommodative stimulus
Formula (Calculated): AC/A = IPD (cm) + (near phoria – far phoria) ÷ near power (D)

HIGH AC/A (>6:1) → excess convergence for accommodation
 → Divergence excess: exo at far, ortho/less exo at near (high AC/A)
 → Convergence excess: ortho/eso at far, high eso at near (high AC/A)
 → Accommodative esotropia: hyperope who crosses at near

LOW AC/A (<4:1) → insufficient convergence for accommodation
 → Convergence insufficiency: exo at near, ortho at far (low AC/A)
 → Divergence insufficiency: eso at far, ortho/less eso at near (low AC/A)

NORMAL AC/A (4–6:1) → equal deviation at distance and near
 → Basic exophoria, basic esophoria, FVD

Sheard's criterion: Fusional reserve must be at least 2× the demand (phoria).

  • If compensating reserve < 2× phoria → symptomatic (cannot maintain fusion comfortably)
  • Example: Exophoria of 8PD; Positive Fusional Convergence (PFC) must be ≥16PD to pass

Percival's criterion: The phoria must fall within the middle third of the fusional range.

  • Take the full vergence range (NFC to PFC)
  • Patient is comfortable if phoria is in the middle ⅓

Fixation disparity criterion (Morgan's correlates): Used to assess whether BV is comfortable under natural conditions — not dissociated like phoria testing.

Practice Questions

1. A 12-year-old patient reports headaches and blurred vision after 20 minutes of reading. Distance acuity is 20/20 OU. Cover test at distance shows orthophoria; cover test at near reveals 12PD exophoria. NPC breaks at 14 cm and recovers at 22 cm. Positive Fusional Convergence at near is 8/10/6 (break/recovery). What is the most likely diagnosis and what does Sheard's criterion indicate?

  • A. Basic exophoria; passes Sheard's
  • B. Convergence insufficiency; fails Sheard's
  • C. Divergence excess; passes Sheard's
  • D. Convergence insufficiency; passes Sheard's
Answer

B. Convergence insufficiency; fails Sheard's criterion.

The pattern — orthophoria at distance + significant exophoria at near + receded NPC (>10cm) + near symptoms — defines convergence insufficiency (low AC/A). Sheard's criterion requires positive fusional convergence ≥ 2× the near exophoria: 2 × 12PD = 24PD needed. The PFC blur/break is only 10PD — well below the 24PD required. The patient fails Sheard's, which explains the symptoms (the system is working near capacity). Management: plus adds for near to reduce accommodative convergence demand, plus vision therapy (convergence exercises — pencil push-ups, Brock string).

2. A child with right esotropia is examined with the Hering-Bielschowsky after-image test. The after-image from the right eye appears displaced to the left of the after-image from the left eye, indicating a non-symmetric result. What does this finding indicate?

  • A. Normal retinal correspondence with strabismus
  • B. Anomalous retinal correspondence (harmonious)
  • C. Anomalous retinal correspondence (unharmonious)
  • D. Suppression of the right eye
Answer

B. Anomalous retinal correspondence (harmonious ARC).

In the Hering-Bielschowsky after-image test with NRC, the after-images would appear as a symmetric cross (equal displacement matching the angle of strabismus). Displacement in a non-symmetric pattern that exactly matches the angle of strabismus = harmonious ARC, where the anomalous neutral point has fully adapted to equal the angle of deviation. The brain has rewired — the extrafoveal area of the right eye now shares the same visual direction as the left fovea. Unharmonious ARC would show the neutral point NOT matching the deviation. Suppression would prevent the after-image from being seen at all.

Answer

C. Infantile nystagmus syndrome (congenital nystagmus) with null point.

The key features: (1) present since birth — congenital onset; (2) pendular in primary position (equal velocity both ways — no fast/slow phase); (3) dampens with convergence — this is the diagnostic hallmark of infantile nystagmus syndrome (INS), also called congenital nystagmus. Patients often adopt a head turn to bring gaze to their null point where the nystagmus is least intense. The convergence dampening explains why some patients read with the book held very close — the convergence demand reduces the nystagmus. Acquired pendular nystagmus (option A) has a known adult onset (MS, brainstem lesion) and does not dampen with convergence. Jerk nystagmus (option B) has fast/slow phases. Gaze-evoked (option D) only appears at eccentric gaze, not in primary position.

4. During binocular rivalry testing, a patient views a vertical grating with one eye and a horizontal grating with the other. The expected percept is:

  • A. A diagonal grating (fusion of both)
  • B. A plaid pattern (superimposition)
  • C. Alternating suppression of each grating
  • D. Permanent suppression of the non-dominant eye's grating
Answer

C. Alternating suppression of each grating.

Binocular rivalry occurs when the two eyes are shown incompatible images that cannot be fused. The brain cannot fuse a vertical and horizontal grating into a single stable percept. Instead, it alternates — one eye's image is perceived for a few seconds, then the other takes over, back and forth. This alternating suppression is the defining feature of binocular rivalry. It is NOT the same as permanent suppression (which occurs in constant strabismus with deep amblyopia — option D). It is also not fusion (option A/B) because the stimuli are too dissimilar for the disparity detectors to merge them.

Sheard's Criterion (Detailed)

SHEARD'S CRITERION:
 The compensating fusional reserve must be AT LEAST TWICE the demand (phoria)

Formula:
 Compensating reserve ≥ 2 × phoria

For exophoria: PFC (positive fusional convergence) must be ≥ 2 × exophoria
For esophoria: NFC (negative fusional convergence) must be ≥ 2 × esophoria

Example:
 Exophoria 8Δ at near; Near PFC blur = 10Δ
 Sheard's requirement: PFC ≥ 2 × 8 = 16Δ
 Patient has 10Δ PFC → FAILS Sheard's → symptomatic

Prism needed to restore Sheard's:
 Prism = (2/3) × phoria − (1/3) × compensating reserve
 = (2/3)(8) − (1/3)(10) = 5.33 − 3.33 = 2.0Δ BI

Percival's Criterion

PERCIVAL'S CRITERION:
 The phoria must fall within the MIDDLE THIRD of the total fusional vergence range

Method:
 1. Find the full vergence range: add NFC + PFC (total fusional range)
 2. The middle third = between 1/3 and 2/3 of the total range
 3. Patient is comfortable if their phoria is within this zone

Example:
 Distance: PFC = 20Δ, NFC = 10Δ → Total range = 30Δ
 Middle third = between 10Δ and 20Δ from the NFC end
 If phoria is 5Δ exo → phoria falls in the outer third → FAILS Percival's

When to apply Percival's vs Sheard's:
 Sheard's: best for EXOPHORIC patients (tests positive fusional reserve)
 Percival's: best for ESOPHORIC or high AC/A patients; also used when both NFC and PFC are reduced

Neumuller's (Neumueller's) Criterion

NEUMULLER'S CRITERION:
 Applies specifically to ESOPHORIC patients

Rule: The prism prescribed should equal HALF the difference between:
 Phoria at near − Phoria at distance

Formula: Prism = (near eso − distance eso) / 2

Example:
 Distance: orthophoria (0Δ); Near: 8Δ esophoria
 Prism = (8 − 0) / 2 = 4Δ BI
 
This is used when the near eso is significantly larger than distance eso (high AC/A),
suggesting the near esophoria is the primary clinical problem.

Types (recap): Strabismic, Anisometropic, Deprivation, Ametropic, Meridional

Management hierarchy:

STEP 1: CORRECT THE CAUSE
 Prescribe full refractive correction (glasses/contact lens)

DEFINITION:
 Fixation not occurring at the fovea — the patient uses a non-foveal retinal area for fixation

CAUSES:
 Long-standing uncorrected refractive error
 Deep amblyopia with foveal suppression
 Strabismus (especially constant unilateral esotropia)
 Macular disease destroying foveal function

ASSESSMENT:
 Visuoscopy — examiner shines a target (star pattern) onto the patient's fundus
 while the patient fixates a small target
 Normal: star projects onto the fovea
 Eccentric: star projects onto extrafoveal area → measured in degrees/prism diopters

CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
 Eccentric fixation is a marker of DEEP AMBLYOPIA
 Prognosis for full VA recovery is worse with eccentric fixation
 Management: same amblyopia treatment, but harder to improve
 
 Angle of eccentric fixation ≠ Angle of strabismus (these can differ)
 The degree of eccentricity is a poor prognostic factor for final VA

 Measured in degrees from the fovea and direction (nasal, temporal, superior, inferior)
 E.g., "2° nasal eccentric fixation" = patient fixates 2° nasal to fovea

Hering's Law and Sherrington's Law (law breakdown):

HERING'S LAW (Binocular — VERSIONS):
 Equal and simultaneous innervation to YOKE MUSCLES of both eyes
 
 What it means: When you look right, R LR and L MR get exactly equal nerve impulses
 Why: Ensures both eyes move the same amount at the same time (conjugate gaze)
 Clinical use: In a CN VI palsy (R LR paretic), the gaze command still fires equally

 left together (the secondary deviation = same angle as primary deviation)

 When an agonist contracts, the antagonist relaxes by an equal and opposite amount

 Why: Prevents muscles from fighting each other; allows smooth, graded movement
 Clinical use: Explains why a tight IR (e.g., after thyroid eye disease) restricts SR
 elevation — even if SR is healthy, the co-contracted IR resists it

Horopter and Panum's Fusional Area — Full Coverage

The Horopter — what it is:

The horopter is the curved surface in visual space through the fixation point that contains all object points whose images fall on corresponding retinal elements of both eyes at a given fixation distance.

  • Introduced by: Francisco de Aguilonius (Aguilonius), 1613 — term comes from Greek horos (boundary) + opter (observer)
  • Clinically: Objects on the horopter are seen as single. Objects off the horopter (too near or too far) are seen as double (physiological diplopia) — unless they fall within Panum's area.

Two types of horopter:

GEOMETRIC HOROPTER = VIETH-MÜLLER CIRCLE
 The theoretical circle passing through the fixation point and the nodal points
 of both eyes
 Based purely on geometric optics (corresponding retinal points = exact symmetry)
 
EMPIRICAL HOROPTER
 What is actually measured in a real human eye
 It is NOT the same as the Vieth-Müller circle
 
HILLEBRAND DEVIATION:
 The empirical horopter deviates from the Vieth-Müller circle in a predictable way:
 - At NEAR distances: empirical horopter falls INSIDE (in front of) the Vieth-Müller circle
 - At FAR distances: empirical horopter falls OUTSIDE (behind) the Vieth-Müller circle
 - The deviation = because corresponding retinal points are not perfectly geometric
 
ABATHIC DISTANCE:
 The distance at which the empirical horopter most closely matches the Vieth-Müller circle
 (the "true" geometric horopter)
 Approximately 1–2 meters

Panum's Fusional Area:

Named after Peter Ludvig Panum (1858). The zone around the horopter within which objects produce DISPARATE (non-identical) retinal images yet are still FUSED into a single percept — not diplopia.

DEFINITION:
 The zone of single binocular vision immediately surrounding the horopter
 Objects within Panum's area → single vision (despite some retinal disparity)
 Objects outside Panum's area → physiological diplopia

SIZE OF PANUM'S AREA:
 At the FOVEA: narrowest → 6–10 arcmin (very small; precise binocular alignment needed)
 At 12° eccentricity: ~30–40 arcmin (wider; more tolerance for misalignment)
 Panum's area INCREASES with distance from fixation (peripheral = more tolerant)

WHY IT GETS WIDER PERIPHERALLY:
 Peripheral retina has lower spatial resolution (larger receptive fields)
 A larger amount of disparity is tolerated before the visual system detects mismatch
 This is why you can fuse peripheral objects easily even if not perfectly aligned

THREE FUNCTIONS OF THE PERIPHERAL EXPANSION:
 1. Allows binocular fusion of large objects (wide disparity in periphery tolerated)
 2. Provides a "safety margin" for vergence errors without inducing diplopia
 3. Supports the perception of a continuous single visual world despite small deviations

Physiological diplopia — the full mechanism:

FIXATION POINT (on horopter) → retinal images on corresponding elements → SINGLE VISION

OBJECT BEYOND THE HOROPTER (farther than fixation):
 Image falls on NASAL retina of both eyes
 Nasal retina → projects to TEMPORAL visual field direction
 Both eyes "point" the object temporally → appears on same side (homonymous)
 RESULT: UNCROSSED (homonymous) diplopia
 Memory: "FAR = UNCROSSED" / FAR and UNcrossed both have 3 letters ending in consonants

OBJECT NEARER THAN THE HOROPTER (closer than fixation):
 Image falls on TEMPORAL retina of both eyes
 Temporal retina → projects to NASAL visual field direction
 Both eyes "point" the object nasally (but from opposite sides) → cross-pattern
 RESULT: CROSSED (heteronymous) diplopia
 Memory: "NEAR = CROSSED"

Extended Topics: Retinal Rivalry, Fusion Amplitude, and Nystagmus

Retinal Rivalry (Binocular Rivalry)

Plain version first: Close one eye and look at a vertical striped pattern. Open the other eye and show it a horizontal striped pattern. Your brain cannot fuse these — they are too different to merge into one image. Instead of seeing both at once, your brain flips between them: you see vertical stripes for a moment, then horizontal stripes, then vertical again. You do not control the switching. This is called retinal rivalry (also called binocular rivalry).

DEFINITION:
 When the two eyes receive completely incompatible images (too different to fuse),
 the visual cortex alternates between the two images rather than superimposing them.
 Neither image is permanently suppressed — they take turns dominating perception.

WHEN IT OCCURS:
 Images presented to each eye are so dissimilar that sensory fusion is impossible.
 Examples: different colors, different shapes, different orientations in each eye.
 
 Contrast with suppression in strabismus:
 SUPPRESSION (strabismus): one image is constantly inhibited (same image repeatedly lost)
 RIVALRY: alternation happens — both images get perceived, just not simultaneously

MECHANISM:
 The dominant eye's image suppresses the other for ~1–5 seconds
 Then the other eye's image breaks through
 The switch is involuntary and roughly periodic
 Rate of switching: influenced by attention, fatigue, image strength
 
CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:
 Used as a research tool to study visual consciousness and cortical dominance
 Anaglyph tests (red-green glasses with different images to each eye) use rivalry
 Rivalry is more likely with high-contrast, detailed, or moving images
 Slower alternation may indicate neurological or visual pathway disease
 
BOARD RULE:
 "Rivalry = alternation (not permanent suppression)"
 If both images are too different to fuse → rivalry
 If one image chronically dominates → suppression (strabismic)

Amplitude of Fusion (Fusional Vergence Range)

Plain version first: Your eyes can converge or diverge a certain amount while still maintaining a single fused image. Push them too far in either direction and the image splits into two (diplopia). The total range — from where the image breaks going inward to where it breaks going outward — is the amplitude of fusion, also called the fusional vergence range or fusional reserve.

DEFINITION:
 The total range of vergence (in prism diopters) over which a person maintains
 single binocular vision (fusion) without diplopia.

COMPONENTS:
 POSITIVE FUSIONAL CONVERGENCE (PFC) — base-out prism tolerance
 How much convergence demand the system can handle while still fusing
 Measured with base-out (BO) prism until:
 Blur point: when accommodation/vergence can no longer keep up
 Break point: image splits into diplopia
 Recovery point: when fusion is re-established as prism is reduced
 
 NEGATIVE FUSIONAL CONVERGENCE (NFC) — base-in prism tolerance
 How much divergence demand the system can handle while still fusing
 Measured with base-in (BI) prism until blur/break/recovery

NORMAL VALUES (Morgan's norms at distance):
 PFC (BO): Blur 9Δ | Break 19Δ | Recovery 10Δ
 NFC (BI): Blur 7Δ | Break 11Δ | Recovery 8Δ
 
NORMAL VALUES (Morgan's norms at near):
 PFC (BO): Blur 17Δ | Break 21Δ | Recovery 11Δ
 NFC (BI): Blur 13Δ | Break 21Δ | Recovery 13Δ

CLINICAL USE:
 Reduced PFC + exophoria → convergence insufficiency
 Reduced NFC + esophoria → divergence excess or accommodative esotropia
 Reduced BOTH NFC and PFC with normal phorias → fusional vergence dysfunction (FVD)
 Applied to Sheard's and Percival's criteria to determine if reserves are adequate

BOARD RULE:
 "Amplitude of fusion = the vergence range that maintains single vision"
 Larger amplitude = more flexible binocular system = better tolerance of phorias
 Smaller amplitude = symptomatic even with small phorias

Nystagmus is an involuntary, rhythmic oscillation of the eyes — the eyes drift or jerk repeatedly without the person choosing to move them. Think of it like a car windshield wiper that won't stop: the eyes keep moving back and forth, and the person cannot hold a steady gaze voluntarily.

Physiological Nystagmus Types

These are normal responses — the eye is working correctly.

1. END-POINT NYSTAGMUS
 What it is: Fine jerk nystagmus that appears when the eye is held at extreme lateral gaze
 Plain version: Hold your eyes all the way to the side and they start to drift back — the
 brain corrects with a jerk. This is normal.
 Key rule: Normal only at extreme gaze. Nystagmus in PRIMARY GAZE = always abnormal.

2. OPTOKINETIC NYSTAGMUS (OKN)
 What it is: Alternating slow pursuit + fast saccadic return when watching a moving
 repetitive pattern (like a train going by, or striped drum)
 Plain version: Your eyes follow a stripe (slow pursuit), then snap back to catch the next
 stripe (fast saccade). Over and over.
 Clinical use:
 - Tests integrity of pursuit system (slow phase) + saccade system (fast phase)
 - Used to CONFIRM visual acuity in pre-verbal infants — if OKN is present, child can see
 - Asymmetric OKN = parietal lobe lesion (drum moving toward lesion = absent/reduced OKN)
 Direction convention: Named by direction of the FAST (saccadic) phase

3. CALORIC NYSTAGMUS
 What it is: Nystagmus induced by irrigation of the external ear canal with warm or cold water
 Plain version: Water changes the temperature of the horizontal semicircular canal, creating
 artificial endolymph convection — tricks the brain into thinking the head is turning
 Cold water → nystagmus AWAY from irrigated ear (fast phase contralateral)
 Warm water → nystagmus TOWARD irrigated ear (fast phase ipsilateral)
 Mnemonic: COWS — Cold Opposite, Warm Same
 Clinical use: Tests brainstem/vestibular integrity in comatose patients

B6-01 — Stereopsis and Depth Perception

The Simple Version

Stereopsis is the brain's ability to extract depth from the slight difference between the two retinal images — because the eyes are ~62.5mm apart, each sees a scene from a slightly different angle. That difference (retinal disparity) encodes depth information that the visual cortex converts into the perception of 3D space. It is the highest grade of binocular vision (Worth Grade 3) and requires functioning binocular vision. Normal stereoacuity is 20–40 seconds of arc — the minimum retinal disparity detectable. Monocular cues (perspective, shadow, overlap) provide depth information for patients with only one eye; they just cannot achieve the fine stereoacuity of binocular disparity.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Can a monocular patient judge depth?" Yes — through monocular cues. Then ask what they cannot do — measure stereoacuity. That distinction separates depth perception from stereopsis.

But Why?

Why does having two eyes (separated by ~62.5mm) give us depth perception that one eye cannot achieve?

Because each eye sees the world from a slightly different angle. An object that is close to you shifts position more between your two eyes' views than an object far away. The brain measures this shift — called retinal disparity — and converts it into a depth signal. One eye has no shift to compare, so it cannot do this calculation. This is the specific mechanism called stereopsis, and it is the most precise depth cue available.

Why does stereoacuity have a threshold measured in arc seconds rather than millimeters?

Because the brain is measuring a difference between two retinal positions — and retinal resolution is angular, not linear. The smallest detectable retinal disparity is about 20 seconds of arc (~0.006°). This corresponds to physically detecting a depth difference of a few centimeters at arm's length. The clinical term for this threshold is stereoacuity.

Why does strabismus or amblyopia abolish stereopsis even if both eyes have good acuity individually?

Because stereopsis requires both eyes to send simultaneous matching signals to binocular cortical neurons in V1. In strabismus, the images from the two eyes are misaligned — they stimulate non-corresponding retinal points. In amblyopia, the suppressed eye's signal is actively inhibited. Either way, the binocular neurons that detect disparity never receive proper input, and the depth calculation cannot run. The individual monocular pathways may be intact, but the binocular computation is missing.

Practice Question

1. A patient with right amblyopia (VA 20/200, strabismic) undergoes stereopsis testing. The expected finding on Randot stereotest is:

  • A. Normal stereo (40 sec arc)
  • B. Reduced stereo (~200 sec arc)
  • C. No measurable stereopsis
  • D. Stereo unaffected because the left eye is normal
Answer

C. No measurable stereopsis.

In strabismus with deep suppression, the amblyopic eye's image is actively inhibited — binocular neurons are not stimulated normally. Without binocular cortical input, stereopsis cannot be generated regardless of monocular acuity in the dominant eye. Residual coarse stereopsis might persist with very mild or intermittent strabismus, but with deep constant amblyopia (20/200), stereopsis is essentially absent.

B7-01 — VIP, VPS, and Cognitive Visual Processing

The Simple Version

Visual Information Processing (VIP) is what happens after the image reaches the brain — how the brain interprets, organizes, stores, and acts on visual information. A person can have 20/20 acuity and still fail at VIP tasks. Visual Perceptual Skills (VPS) is the non-motor subset of VIP — the seven cognitive visual skills that let a person recognize shapes, recall sequences, and locate targets in busy backgrounds, without needing to use their hands or body to respond. These are assessed with three main tools: TVPS-3 (pure non-motor VPS), DTVP-2 (separates motor vs non-motor), and Beery VMI (visual-motor integration). Boards test the definitions, which tool measures what, and the seven VPS subtopics.

Teaching angle: Ask students — "Can a child fail to read not because of poor eyesight but because of poor visual processing?" Yes — and that distinction drives the referral decision between optometry and occupational therapy.

Practice Question

1. A 7-year-old has 20/20 acuity OU, no strabismus, and normal binocular vision. The teacher reports he cannot find objects on a busy worksheet and frequently loses his place while reading. Which assessment would best evaluate the most likely deficit?

  • A. Humphrey visual field testing
  • B. Ishihara color vision testing
  • C. TVPS-3
  • D. Beery VMI
Answer

C. TVPS-3.

The described difficulties — finding objects on a busy page (figure-ground), losing place while reading (visual sequential memory and figure-ground) — point to visual perceptual skills deficits. With normal acuity and binocular vision ruling out optical and alignment problems, the deficit is at the VPS level. TVPS-3 is the gold standard for assessing all 7 non-motor VPS subtopics in children ages 4–18. It requires only pointing, so motor output doesn't confound the results. Beery VMI would test visual-motor integration (copying shapes) — appropriate if handwriting or drawing difficulties were the chief complaint. VF testing and color testing are not relevant here.

2. A 6-year-old scores poorly on the Beery VMI. Before concluding she has a visual perceptual deficit, which assessment should be done first to separate perception from motor output?

  • A. Humphrey visual field test
  • B. TVPS-3
  • C. DTVP-2 motor-enhanced subtest
  • D. Snellen acuity testing
Answer

B. TVPS-3.

Beery VMI requires copying geometric shapes — a visual-motor task. A low score could reflect poor visual perception OR poor fine motor skills (shaky hands, poor pencil grip). TVPS-3 is the appropriate next step because it tests all 7 VPS subtopics with pointing responses only — no motor output is required. If TVPS-3 is normal, the Beery VMI deficit is motor (refer to OT for motor base work). If TVPS-3 is also poor, the deficit is perceptual (vision therapy targeting VPS). DTVP-2 (option C) also separates motor from non-motor but tests ages 4–10 and is less specific than TVPS-3 for identifying the perceptual subtype. Visual fields and acuity testing (A, D) do not assess VPS.
