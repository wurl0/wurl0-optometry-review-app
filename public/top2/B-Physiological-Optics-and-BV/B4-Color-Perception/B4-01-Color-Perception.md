## B4-01 — Color Perception

## The Simple Version

Color vision works through three cone types, each sensitive to a different wavelength range. Their combined output is processed by opponent channels in the brain — red vs green, blue vs yellow — which is why those color pairs cannot be perceived simultaneously (you cannot see a reddish-green). Most color deficiency is genetic, X-linked, and affects males more than females. The board tests color theory names, color mixing rules, specific color test criteria, and the nomenclature of color deficiency. Knowing the tests cold — which plate is for everyone (Ishihara plate 1), which test uses lanterns (FALANT), and which is most comprehensive (FM-100) — covers the clinical side completely.

**Teaching angle:** Ask students — "Why can't you see a reddish-green color?" The answer explains opponent process theory, which explains why Hering proposed a different theory than Young-Helmholtz.

---

## But Why?

**Why do we need three cone types and not two or four?**
Because color discrimination depends on comparing the relative outputs of cone types. Two cones would only give a single ratio (one color dimension). Three cones give three ratios — enough to distinguish millions of colors. Four would add marginal benefit for most purposes.

**Why do red-green defects occur more frequently than blue-yellow defects?**
Because the genes for red (OPN1LW) and green (OPN1MW) cones are both on the X chromosome and are arranged in tandem — making them prone to unequal crossover during meiosis. Blue cone genes are on chromosome 7 and are much less susceptible to this recombination error.

**Why does color perception fail earlier than motion in optic nerve disease?**
Because color signals depend on the sustained (parvocellular) pathway, which uses small, high-frequency nerve fibers. These are selectively more vulnerable to demyelination and ischemia than the larger transient (magnocellular) fibers that carry motion signals.

### How It Works — The Mechanism

**The three color theories:**

```
1. TRICHROMATIC THEORY (Young-Helmholtz)
   Level: Receptor (cone)
   Mechanism: Three cone types with different peak sensitivities
              L-cones (Erythrolabe) = Red ~750nm
              M-cones (Chlorolabe) = Green ~535nm
              S-cones (Cyanolabe) = Blue ~440nm
   Why it works: Different stimuli produce different ratios of cone activation
   Limitation: Cannot explain why red-green is impossible to perceive simultaneously
   No S-cones in fovea (but sufficient L and M for central color)

2. OPPONENT PROCESS THEORY (Hering)
   Level: Post-receptor (retinal ganglion cells, LGB, cortex)
   Mechanism: Signals processed in opposing pairs:
              Red ↔ Green (opponent — cannot coexist)
              Blue ↔ Yellow (opponent — cannot coexist)
              Black ↔ White (luminance channel)
   Why it works: Explains color after-images, simultaneous contrast, why reddish-green is impossible

3. STAGE THEORY (Ladd-Franklin)
   Evolutionary stages:
   Stage 1: Monochromat (all wavelengths = one signal — like blue-green)
   Stage 2: Dichromat (blue-yellow system evolves)
   Stage 3: Trichromat (red-green system evolves last — most recently evolved)
   Board clue: Red-green defects are more common because the red-green system is the most recently evolved (most vulnerable)
```

**Color mixing:**

```
ADDITIVE (light/mixing wavelengths):
R + G = Yellow
R + B = Magenta
G + B = Cyan
R + G + B = WHITE

SUBTRACTIVE (pigments absorb wavelengths):
C + M = Blue
C + Y = Green
M + Y = Red
C + M + Y = BLACK

Key: Primary colors differ between additive (RGB) and subtractive (CMY)
```

### Structure or Process Map

**Color deficiency classification:**

```
ANOMALOUS TRICHROMACY (3 cone types; one shifted)
  Protanomaly — weak/shifted L-cone (red weakness); most common sex-linked defect in males
  Deuteranomaly — weak/shifted M-cone (green weakness); most common overall
  Tritanomaly — weak/shifted S-cone (blue weakness); rare; autosomal

DICHROMACY (only 2 cone types; one absent)
  Protanopia — no L-cone (red blind); confuses red with dark green/gray
  Deuteranopia — no M-cone (green blind); confuses red with green; most common dichromat
  Tritanopia — no S-cone (blue blind); confuses blue-yellow; rare; autosomal

MONOCHROMACY (only 1 or 0 color channels)
  Rod monochromacy (achromatopsia) — no cones; completely color blind; photophobia; poor acuity
  Cone monochromacy — one cone type only; rare
```

**Sex-linked prevalence:**
- Males: ~8% of Caucasian males have red-green defect
- Females: ~0.5% (carrier females rarely symptomatic)
- Reason: OPN1LW (L-cone) and OPN1MW (M-cone) genes both on X chromosome

**Color vision tests:**

| Test | Fail Criteria | Stimulus | What it tests |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ishihara | >2 errors (note: plate 1 is "12" for EVERYONE including deficient) | Pseudoisochromatic plates | Red-green deficiency screening |
| FALANT (Farnsworth Lantern) | >2 errors at 8 feet | Colored light pairs (red, green, yellow, white) | Occupational color vision (aviation, navy) |
| D-15 (Farnsworth D-15) | >2 major transpositions | 15 colored caps + pilot cap | Screens mild from severe; type of defect |
| FM-100 Hue | High total error score | 85 hue-graded caps, 4 boxes | Most comprehensive; identifies axis of defect |
| HRR (Hardy-Rand-Rittler) | Miss symbols | Pseudoisochromatic; symbols not numbers | Tests both red-green AND blue-yellow |

> **Ishihara plate 1 = "12" — everyone sees this, including color-deficient patients.** It is a control plate. Failure here suggests malingering or low intelligence, not color deficiency.

### Comparisons That Matter

**Protanopia vs Deuteranopia — both confuse red and green but differently:**

| Feature | Protanopia | Deuteranopia |
|---|---|---|
| Missing cone | L-cone (red) | M-cone (green) |
| Neutral point | ~492 nm (blue-green appears white) | ~499 nm |
| Red brightness | Dark (red light looks dim because L-cones absent) | Normal |
| Most common in | Males; ~1% | Males; ~1% |
| Board clue | Protan = red is DIM | Deutan = red and green just look similar, no dimming |

**Anomalous trichromacy vs Dichromacy:**

| Feature | Anomalous trichromacy | Dichromacy |
|---|---|---|
| Cone types | 3 (one shifted) | 2 (one missing) |
| Severity | Mild–moderate defect | Severe defect |
| Most common | Deuteranomaly (green weakness) | Deuteranopia |
| Ishihara errors | Some errors | More errors |

### Memory Anchors

**Cone pigment names — "ECG":**
Erythrolabe (Red/750nm), Chlorolabe (Green/535nm), Cyanolabe (Blue/440nm)

**Cone deficiency names — "PDT" for protanopia, deuteranopia, tritanopia:**
Protan = Red problem; Deutan = Green problem; Tritan = Blue problem
Anomaly = shifted (partial); Anopia = absent (complete)

**Most common defects:**
- Most common anomalous: Deuteranomaly (green weakness)
- Most common dichromat: Deuteranopia
- Both are green (deutan) problems — green gene on X is most prone to crossover

**Additive = White; Subtractive = Black:**
Add light → get all wavelengths → white
Subtract (absorb) everything → no wavelengths reflected → black

**Color theories by level:**
- Trichromatic = receptor level (cones)
- Opponent process = post-receptor (retinal ganglion/LGB/cortex)
- Stage theory = evolutionary explanation

**Most comprehensive color test = FM-100:**
"Full Map" — 85 caps, plots the exact axis of color confusion.

## Board Traps

- **Trap 1:** "Plate 1 of the Ishihara is only seen by normal observers" — Wrong. Plate 1 ("12") is seen by EVERYONE including color deficient patients. It is the control plate.
- **Trap 2:** "Tritanopia is the most common color deficiency" — Wrong. Deuteranomaly (weak green) is the most common. Tritanopia is rare and autosomal.
- **Trap 3:** "Additive mixing of all colors produces black" — Wrong. Additive (light) mixing R+G+B = White. Subtractive (pigment) mixing C+M+Y = Black.
- **Trap 4:** "Protan defects cause no change in red brightness" — Wrong. Protanopia makes red appear DIM because L-cones are absent and red light doesn't stimulate M or S-cones effectively.
- **Trap 5:** "FM-100 uses numbers to identify errors" — Wrong. FM-100 uses hue-graded caps; the patient arranges them in order. Ishihara uses numbers (and symbols in HRR).
- **Trap 6:** "HRR only tests red-green deficiency" — Wrong. HRR tests BOTH red-green and blue-yellow deficiencies, unlike Ishihara (which only screens red-green).

## Practice Questions

**1.** A patient fails multiple Ishihara plates and is suspected of having a color vision deficiency. To determine the exact axis and severity of the defect, which test should be ordered?

- A. FALANT
- B. D-15
- C. FM-100 Hue test
- D. HRR

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. FM-100 Hue test.**

The FM-100 Hue test is the most comprehensive color vision assessment. Using 85 hue-graded colored caps arranged in four boxes, it plots a "total error score" and identifies the exact axis of color confusion (the angle of the error vector). This allows precise classification of both type and severity of color defect. D-15 is useful for screening mild vs severe defects and type, but is less detailed. FALANT is an occupational test, not a diagnostic one. HRR screens for both red-green and blue-yellow defects but does not quantify the defect as precisely as FM-100.

</details>
