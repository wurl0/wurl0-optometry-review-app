## E2 — Anterior Segment Disease
*Personal study notes | TOS: E Ocular Disease | Source: Ocular Disease + consolidated review*

---

## The Simple Version

The anterior segment is everything from the conjunctiva to the lens. Walk the light path inward:

- **Conjunctiva:** the four conjunctivitides (bacterial, viral, allergic, chlamydial), and the growths (pinguecula, pterygium).
- **Sclera/episclera:** episcleritis (mild) vs scleritis (severe, systemic).
- **Cornea:** keratoconus, the iron lines and pigments, dry eye, the herpes keratitides.
- **Iris/pupil/anterior chamber:** the named pupils (Adie, Argyll-Robertson, Horner, RAPD), and the injections (conjunctival vs ciliary).
- **Glaucoma:** POAG vs PACG, the secondary glaucomas, and the drug ladder.
- **Lens:** ectopia lentis, the cataract types, and surgery.
- **Anterior uveitis:** cells, flare, keratic precipitates, and the systemic links.

**Teaching angle:** the anterior segment is a museum of comparison tables. Master each two-column pair and you have most of the marks.

---

## But Why?

**Why does a pterygium threaten vision but a pinguecula does not?**
A pinguecula is a degenerative yellow deposit that stops at the limbus. A pterygium is a fibrovascular wing that crosses the limbus, destroys **Bowman's layer**, and drags onto the visual axis, inducing astigmatism. Crossing the limbus is the whole difference.

**Why is ciliary (circumcorneal) injection a danger sign but conjunctival injection usually is not?**
Conjunctival injection is superficial vessels reacting to surface irritation (conjunctivitis, allergy). Ciliary flush is the deep perilimbal vessels, which engorge only when the cornea, iris, or angle is in trouble: keratitis, uveitis, or acute glaucoma. Deep vessels, deep disease.

**Why does the pupil go mid-dilated and fixed in acute angle-closure?**
The pressure spike paralyzes the iris sphincter, and the iris, jammed against the trabecular meshwork, sits mid-dilated. That mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil with a cloudy cornea and a hard, painful eye is the emergency picture.

**Why does nuclear sclerosis give "second sight"?**
The hardening nucleus raises the lens index of refraction, adding minus power (myopic shift). A presbyope can suddenly read without glasses, briefly, before the cataract clouds vision. The same index change that helps near vision will eventually blur everything.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| Pterygium | Greek *pterygion* = little wing | Fibrovascular wing crossing the limbus; destroys Bowman's layer |
| Pinguecula | Latin *pinguis* = fat | Yellow degenerative limbal deposit; does not cross the limbus |
| Ciliary flush | perilimbal "flush" | Deep violet circumcorneal injection = keratitis/uveitis/acute glaucoma |
| Keratoconus | *kerato* = cornea + *konos* = cone | Bilateral non-inflammatory conical thinning |
| Hypopyon | *hypo* = under + *pyon* = pus | Layered white cells in the anterior chamber |
| Keratic precipitates | inflammatory cells on endothelium | Mutton-fat = granulomatous; fine stellate = viral/Fuchs |
| Synechiae | Greek *synecheia* = continuity | Iris adhesions: anterior (to cornea) or posterior (to lens) |
| Rubeosis iridis | Latin *rubeo* = redden | Iris neovascularization; closed angle without pupillary block |
| Ectopia lentis | *ec* = out + *topos* = place | Lens dislocation; Marfan up, homocystinuria down |
| Phacolytic | *phakos* = lens + *lysis* = loosening | Open-angle glaucoma from leaked hypermature lens protein |
| Phacomorphic | *phakos* + *morphe* = shape | Closed-angle glaucoma from an intumescent swollen lens |
| Arcus senilis | Latin = bow of old age | Peripheral stromal cholesterol ring with a lucid interval |

### Conjunctiva

**Pinguecula vs pterygium:** pinguecula = yellow limbal deposit, does **not** cross the limbus. Pterygium = wing crossing the cornea, destroys **Bowman's layer**, **Stocker iron line** ahead of the head, treat by excision.

**The four conjunctivitides:**

| Feature | Bacterial | Viral | Allergic | Chlamydial/Trachoma |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discharge | Mucopurulent | Watery | Stringy mucoid | Mucopurulent |
| Cells | Neutrophils (PMN) | Lymphocytes | Eosinophils | Lymphocytes + follicles |
| Follicles/papillae | Small papillae | Large follicles | Cobblestone papillae | Upper tarsal follicles, pannus |
| Cause | Staph, Strep, H. flu | Adenovirus, herpes | IgE | Chlamydia trachomatis |
| Treatment | Topical antibiotic | Self-limiting | Antihistamine, mast-cell stabilizer | Tetracycline + azithromycin |

**Trachoma** (Chlamydia A, B, C; leading infectious cause of world blindness): stages **I-A-C-S** (Inflammation follicular → Intense → Arlt's line scarring → Cicatricial entropion/trichiasis). **Pannus at the upper limbus**; Herbert pits when limbal follicles heal.

**Vernal keratoconjunctivitis:** young males, seasonal, **cobblestone papillae**, Trantas dots, **shield ulcer**. **GPC:** contact-lens related, upper tarsal giant papillae. **Phlyctenular:** hypersensitivity to TB/Staph antigen, limbal nodule.

**Injection compare:** conjunctival (peripheral, bright red, moves, blanches with epinephrine, = surface disease) vs **ciliary flush** (perilimbal, violet, fixed, does not blanch, = uveitis/keratitis/acute glaucoma).

### Sclera

**Episcleritis vs scleritis:** episcleritis is superficial, mild/no pain, benign (RA, gout). **Scleritis** is deep, **severe boring pain**, often bilateral, linked to **PTB, RA, SLE**, and can cause secondary glaucoma. **Staphyloma** = uveal-lined scleral ectasia (posterior type with high myopia). Blue sclera: osteogenesis imperfecta, Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos.

### Cornea

**Keratoconus** (bilateral non-inflammatory conical thinning): **Fleischer ring** (iron at cone base, earliest), Munson's sign (V-lid on downgaze), scissors reflex on retinoscopy, Vogt's striae, oil-droplet reflex. Treat with RGP lens, crosslinking, or penetrating keratoplasty.

**Opacity grades:** nebula (faint) < macula (grey) < leukoma (dense white).

**Iron lines (all epithelial) "HuFFeStO":** **Hu**dson-Stahli (aging, lower third), **F**leischer (keratoconus), **F**erry (next to bleb), **Sto**cker (ahead of pterygium).

**Corneal pigments by layer:**

| Pigment | Layer | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Iron lines | Epithelium | (above) |
| **Kayser-Fleischer ring** | **Descemet's** | **Wilson's disease** |
| Krukenberg spindle | Endothelium | Pigment dispersion syndrome |
| Argyrosis (silver) / Chrysiasis (gold) | Stroma | Silver / gold therapy |

**Pigment dispersion triad:** Krukenberg spindle + iris transillumination defects + Sampaolesi line → pigmentary (open-angle) glaucoma.

**Dry eye (KCS):** aqueous deficiency (**Sjogren's**), mucin deficiency (vitamin A, Stevens-Johnson, trachoma), lipid deficiency (MGD). Tests: **Schirmer <10 mm/5 min** (aqueous), **TBUT <10 sec** (stability), rose bengal/lissamine (devitalized cells).

**Arcus senilis:** peripheral stromal cholesterol with a lucid interval; if young (**arcus juvenilis**), screen for hyperlipidemia. **Band keratopathy:** calcium in Bowman's (chronic uveitis, hypercalcemia); treat with EDTA chelation.

**Herpes simplex keratitis:** recurrent **dendritic ulcer** (terminal bulbs, stains with fluorescein and rose bengal); disciform = immune stromal edema. Treat with topical acyclovir/trifluridine; **never topical steroids on epithelial disease**. **Herpes zoster ophthalmicus:** V1 reactivation; **Hutchinson's sign** (nose-tip vesicles = nasociliary = high ocular risk).

### Iris, Pupils, Anterior Chamber

**Named pupils:**

| Pupil | Finding | Cause | Test |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Adie's tonic** | Large, sluggish | Ciliary ganglion (benign) | Constricts to **0.1% pilocarpine** |
| **Argyll-Robertson** | Small, irregular, accommodates but no light reaction | Neurosyphilis (pretectal) | "Prostitute's pupil" |
| **Marcus Gunn (RAPD)** | Dilates on swinging light | Optic nerve/severe retinal disease | Swinging flashlight |
| **Horner's** | Ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis | Sympathetic lesion | Cocaine (no dilation) |

Both Adie's and Argyll-Robertson "accommodate but do not react"; Adie's is peripheral/unilateral/benign, Argyll-Robertson is central/bilateral/syphilis.

**Iris bombe** (posterior synechiae → pupillary block → iris bows forward → closed angle **with** pupillary block; treat laser iridotomy) vs **rubeosis iridis** (neovascular, closed angle **without** pupillary block; diabetes/CRVO; treat anti-VEGF/PRP). **Iridodialysis:** iris root torn by blunt trauma, D-shaped pupil.

### Glaucoma

Normal IOP **10-21 mmHg**; aqueous drains trabecular (80%) and uveoscleral (20%).

| Feature | POAG | PACG |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Gradual, painless ("silent thief") | Sudden, painful (emergency) |
| Angle | Open | Closed |
| Pupil | Normal | Mid-dilated, fixed |
| Cornea | Clear | Cloudy/edematous |
| Treatment | Medical | Surgical (laser iridotomy) |

**Drug ladder "MAPCO + B":** Miotics (open trabeculum), Alpha-2 agonists (less production + outflow), Prostaglandins (uveoscleral, first-line), CAIs (less production), Osmotics (acute), Beta blockers (less production). Detail in [[G4-01-Therapeutic-Ophthalmic-Agents]].

**Secondary glaucomas:** rubeosis (closed, no block), iris bombe (closed, with block), **phacolytic** (open, leaking hypermature lens protein), **phacomorphic** (closed, swollen lens), pigmentary (open), steroid (open), uveitic.

**Workup:** tonometry, ophthalmoscopy (C/D >0.6 or asymmetry >0.2), perimetry (arcuate/nasal step early), gonioscopy, pachymetry (thin <520 µm = risk).

### Lens and Cataract

**Ectopia lentis:** **Marfan = UP** (superotemporal, FBN-1, aortic aneurysm), **Homocystinuria = DOWN** (inferonasal, thromboembolism risk), Weill-Marchesani = spherophakia.

**Cataract types:** **nuclear sclerosis** (second sight/myopic shift), cortical (spokes), **posterior subcapsular** (most common age-related, most disabling, steroid-linked, near worse than distance), **diabetic** (snowflake, progresses in 72 h), galactosemia (oil droplet, reversible), traumatic (rosette).

**Maturation:** immature → intumescent (phacomorphic risk) → mature → **hypermature/Morgagnian** (phacolytic risk) → after-cataract (treat **YAG capsulotomy**).

**Surgery:** ICCE (whole lens, no capsule) / ECCE (keep posterior capsule) / **phacoemulsification** (ultrasound, keep posterior capsule). **Aphakia = +10 to +12 D hyperopia**; IOL power from **biometry**.

### Anterior Uveitis

**Symptoms:** photophobia, pain, blurred vision. **Signs:** ciliary flush, cells and flare, **keratic precipitates** (fine stellate = viral/Fuchs; **mutton-fat = granulomatous** sarcoid/TB/VKH), posterior synechiae, Busacca/Koeppe nodules, **hypopyon** (HLA-B27/Behcet, endophthalmitis).

**Systemic links:** **HLA-B27** (ankylosing spondylitis, Reiter, psoriatic, IBD), sarcoidosis (mutton-fat, bilateral), JIA (chronic, ANA+, asymptomatic). **Treat with topical steroid + cycloplegic** (prevent synechiae).

**Fuchs heterochromic cyclitis:** chronic unilateral, **lighter iris** on affected side, fine KP, **no posterior synechiae**, causes cataract; does not respond to steroids.

## Numbers and Values to Know

| Parameter | Value | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Normal IOP | 10–21 mmHg | Trabecular 80% + uveoscleral 20% outflow |
| Schirmer test | < 10 mm / 5 min | Aqueous tear deficiency |
| Tear break-up time (TBUT) | < 10 sec | Tear film instability |
| Cup-to-disc ratio | > 0.6 or asymmetry > 0.2 | Glaucomatous optic neuropathy |
| Central corneal thickness | thin < 520 µm | Independent glaucoma risk factor |
| Aphakic correction | +10 to +12 D | Hyperopia after lens removal |
| Diabetic cataract | progresses in 72 h | Snowflake type, acute |

## Visual Map

```
RED EYE — surface vs deep
─────────────────────────
CONJUNCTIVAL injection → surface (conjunctivitis/allergy); bright, mobile, blanches
CILIARY flush → DEEP danger: keratitis / uveitis / acute glaucoma; violet, fixed

GLAUCOMA — the angle splits the diagnosis
─────────────────────────────────────────
OPEN angle → POAG (silent, gradual) + phacolytic / pigmentary / steroid
CLOSED angle → PACG (acute emergency) + phacomorphic / iris bombe / rubeosis

CATARACT MATURATION
───────────────────
Immature → INTUMESCENT (phacomorphic risk) → Mature → HYPERMATURE / Morgagnian (phacolytic risk)
 → after-cataract → YAG capsulotomy
```

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** Kayser-Fleischer ring is iron in the epithelium. Wrong. It is **copper in Descemet's** (Wilson's disease).
- **Trap:** pinguecula destroys Bowman's layer. Wrong. The **pterygium** does; pinguecula stays at the limbus.
- **Trap:** Marfan dislocates the lens downward. Wrong. **Up** (superotemporal). Homocystinuria goes down.
- **Trap:** topical steroids are good for herpes dendritic keratitis. Dangerous. Steroids on epithelial herpes worsen it; use antivirals.
- **Trap:** PACG is treated medically first. Wrong. It is a surgical emergency (laser iridotomy); drops only temporize.
- **Trap:** phacolytic glaucoma is closed-angle. Wrong. It is **open-angle** (protein blocks the meshwork); phacomorphic is the closed one.
- **Trap:** the most common age-related cataract is nuclear. Common, but **posterior subcapsular** is described as the most common age-related and most visually disabling here.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A red eye has a violet ring of injection around the cornea that does not blanch with epinephrine. This points to:

- A. Allergic conjunctivitis
- B. A deep process (uveitis, keratitis, or acute glaucoma)
- C. Subconjunctival hemorrhage
- D. Pinguecula

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Ciliary (circumcorneal) flush reflects engorged deep perilimbal vessels, seen in uveitis, keratitis, or acute angle-closure. Surface conditions give bright, mobile conjunctival injection that blanches.
</details>

**2.** A green-gold ring is found in Descemet's membrane. The systemic disease is:

- A. Hemochromatosis
- B. Wilson's disease
- C. Diabetes
- D. Gout

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Wilson's disease.** The Kayser-Fleischer ring is copper deposition in Descemet's membrane in hepatolenticular degeneration.
</details>

**3.** A tall patient with long fingers and an aortic aneurysm has a lens dislocated superotemporally. The diagnosis and shift are:

- A. Homocystinuria, down
- B. Marfan syndrome, up
- C. Weill-Marchesani, spherophakia
- D. Trauma, variable

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Marfan, up.** Marfan dislocates the lens superotemporally (up); homocystinuria dislocates it inferonasally (down).
</details>

**4.** A hypermature cataract leaks lens protein, macrophages clog the trabecular meshwork, and IOP rises with an open angle. This is:

- A. Phacomorphic glaucoma
- B. Phacolytic glaucoma
- C. Rubeosis iridis
- D. Iris bombe

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Phacolytic glaucoma** (open angle). Phacomorphic is the closed-angle type from a swollen intumescent lens.
</details>

**5.** Mutton-fat keratic precipitates on the corneal endothelium indicate which kind of uveitis?

- A. Viral
- B. Fuchs heterochromic
- C. Granulomatous (sarcoid, TB)
- D. Allergic

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Granulomatous.** Large greasy mutton-fat KP suggest sarcoidosis, TB, VKH. Fine stellate KP suggest viral or Fuchs.
</details>
