## 1. Bony Orbit & Globe Anomalies

### Globe Size Anomalies

| Condition | Description | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Anophthalmos | Absent globe | No eye develops |
| Microphthalmos | Abnormally small globe | May be functional |
| Phthisis bulbi | Shrunken, disorganized globe | End-stage of severe ocular disease |
| Buphthalmos | Enlarged globe | Congenital glaucoma; corneoscleral stretching from raised IOP in infants |
| Hypertelorism | Abnormally wide distance between orbits | |
| Telecanthus | Wide distance between medial canthi only | Orbits are normal distance |

### Exophthalmos vs Proptosis
- Both = anterior protrusion of the globe. Proptosis refers to any direction of displacement; exophthalmos specifically means anterior.
- **Types of exophthalmos:**
 - **Active:** thyroid ophthalmopathy (Graves disease) — soft tissue inflammation
 - **Intermittent:** orbital varicosities; worse with Valsalva (bending forward)
 - **Pulsating:** carotid-cavernous fistula or orbital meningocele (transmitted vascular pulse)
 - **Unilateral:** usually neoplastic; proptosis beyond 21mm = pathologic
 - **Bilateral:** usually systemic (thyroid, leukemia, neuroblastoma)

### Orbital Cellulitis
- Most common cause: **ethmoiditis** (direct spread from ethmoid sinuses)
- Treatment: **Incision and drainage (I&D) + massive antibiotics**
- Must distinguish from preseptal/periorbital cellulitis (anterior to orbital septum, less serious)

### Orbital Tumors
- **Most common benign:** Cavernous hemangioma
- **Most common malignant:** Rhabdomyosarcoma (most common primary orbital malignancy in children; peak age 7-8 years)

## 2. Eyelids

### Congenital Conditions

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Coloboma of lid | Full-thickness defect, usually upper lid nasal area |
| Epicanthus | Vertical skin fold covering medial canthus; common in Asians |
| Blepharoptosis | Drooping of upper lid; see types below |
| Distichiasis | Extra row of lashes from meibomian gland orifices |
| Symblepharon | Adhesion of bulbar conjunctiva to palpebral conjunctiva; also caused by chemical burns |
| Blepharophimosis | Short horizontal palpebral fissure; ptosis + telecanthus + epicanthus inversus |

### Blepharoptosis (Ptosis) Types

| Type | Mechanism | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Neurogenic | CN III palsy or Horner's syndrome | Horner's triad: ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis |
| Myogenic | Myasthenia gravis (MG) | Fatiguable ptosis; treated with neostigmine; Tensilon (edrophonium) test positive |
| Aponeurotic | Levator aponeurosis disinsertion | Most common adult ptosis; thin lid, high crease |
| Mechanical | Weight of tumor or scar tissue | Lid heaviness |
| Congenital | Levator maldevelopment | Absent lid crease; amblyopia risk |

**Board trap:** Horner's syndrome triad is **ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis** (not mydriasis).

### Inflammatory Eyelid Conditions

| Condition | Feature | Key Distinction |
|---|---|---|
| Squamous blepharitis | Dry scales; seborrheic | No ulceration |
| Ulcerative blepharitis | Crusted ulcers at lash base | Staphylococcal; madarosis (lash loss) |
| Meibomitis | Meibomian gland inflammation | Foamy tear film; posterior blepharitis |
| **Hordeolum (Stye)** | **Acute painful abscess of lid glands** | External (Zeis/Moll) or internal (Meibomian); TENDER |
| **Chalazion** | **Chronic granuloma of meibomian gland** | NON-TENDER; lipogranuloma; excised if persistent |

**Trap:** Hordeolum = acute, tender, infected. Chalazion = chronic, non-tender, sterile lipid granuloma.

### Eyelid Position Abnormalities

**Entropion** (lid margin turns inward, lashes scratch cornea):
- Cicatricial: scarring (trachoma, chemical burns)
- Spastic: orbicularis spasm in elderly
- Atonic: lax lower lid
- Congenital: lower lid usually

**Ectropion** (lid margin turns outward, corneal exposure):
- Cicatricial: scar pulls lid out
- Senile/atonic: most common; lax tissue in elderly
- Paralytic: CN VII palsy (orbicularis paralysis)

**Lagophthalmos:** Inability to close the eye completely. Causes: facial nerve palsy, exophthalmos, scarring. Risk: exposure keratopathy.

### Orbicularis Disorders
- **Blepharoclonus:** Rapid repetitive blinking; benign
- **Myokymia:** Unilateral eyelid twitching; benign, usually stress/fatigue
- **Essential blepharospasm:** Forceful, bilateral, involuntary lid closure; treated with botulinum toxin

### Eyelid Tumors

| Tumor | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| **Basal cell carcinoma** | **Most common malignant eyelid tumor**; pearly margin; lower lid most common |
| Squamous cell carcinoma | Less common; faster growing; can metastasize |
| Milia | Tiny white epidermal inclusion cysts; benign |
| Xanthelasma | Yellow lipid deposits near medial canthi; associated with hyperlipidemia |
| Keratoacanthoma | Rapidly growing, then involutes; volcano crater appearance |
| Syringoma | Eccrine sweat duct origin; lower lids; young women |

## 3. Lacrimal System

### Lacrimal Gland Diseases

| Condition | Details |
|---|---|
| **Dacryoadenitis, acute** | Painful swelling upper outer orbit; S-shaped ptosis; bacterial (Staph, Strep) or viral (EBV, mumps) |
| **Dacryoadenitis, chronic** | Painless enlargement; sarcoidosis, TB, lymphoma |
| **Mikulicz syndrome** | Bilateral painless enlargement of lacrimal AND salivary glands; lymphoma, sarcoidosis, TB |

**Lacrimal Gland Tumors:**
- Most common benign: **Pleomorphic adenoma** (mostly males <35 years)
- Most common malignant: **Adenoid cystic carcinoma** (mostly >35 years; perineural invasion; "Swiss cheese" pattern; poor prognosis)

### Lacrimal Passages — Epiphora and Obstruction

**Epiphora** = overflow of tears down the cheek. Causes: increased tear production, decreased drainage.

**Tests for lacrimal drainage:**
- **Jones Test I (Primary dye test):** Fluorescein instilled; recovered from nostril with cotton tip = patent system. Positive = patent
- **Jones Test II (Secondary dye test):** If Jones I negative, irrigate with saline; if dye recovered = partial obstruction above the sac; if clear saline recovered = block at or below the sac

**Dacryocystitis** (infection of lacrimal sac):

| Type | Feature |
|---|---|
| Congenital bilateral | Newborn; Pneumococcus most common; mucocele of nasolacrimal duct |
| Adult unilateral | Unilateral; Staph, Strep, mixed flora; swelling at medial canthus; regurgitation of pus |
| Chronic | Blood on pressure over the sac = sign of tumor (squamous cell carcinoma of sac) |

Treatment for congenital: probing of NLD at 12-18 months if massage fails.
Treatment for adult: **DCR (Dacryocystorhinostomy)** for chronic obstruction.

## 4. Conjunctiva

### Conjunctival Lesions

| Lesion | Description |
|---|---|
| Chemosis | Edema of conjunctiva; accumulation of fluid in subconjunctival space |
| Subconjunctival hemorrhage | Bright red; spontaneous (Valsalva, trauma, hypertension); self-limiting |
| **Pinguecula** | Yellowish deposit nasal or temporal to limbus; does NOT invade cornea |
| **Pterygium** | Wing-shaped fibrovascular tissue growing onto cornea; **destroys Bowman's membrane**; Stocker line (iron deposit) ahead of head; treatment: surgical excision |

**Trap:** Pinguecula does not cross limbus. Pterygium does, and it destroys Bowman's layer.

### Conjunctivitis — Master Comparison Table

| Feature | Bacterial | Viral | Allergic | Chlamydial (Trachoma) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discharge | Mucopurulent | Watery | Mucoid, stringy | Mucopurulent |
| Cell type | PMN (neutrophils) | Lymphocytes | Eosinophils | Lymphocytes + follicles |
| Follicles | No | Yes (large) | No | Yes (upper tarsal) |
| Papillae | Yes (small) | Possible | Large (cobblestones) | Pannus |
| Laterality | Unilateral, then bilateral | Usually bilateral | Bilateral | Bilateral |
| Common cause | Staph, Strep, H.flu | Adenovirus, Herpes | IgE-mediated | Chlamydia trachomatis |
| Treatment | Topical antibiotics | Self-limiting | Antihistamines, mast cell stabilizers | Tetracycline + sulfonamide |

### Trachoma
- Cause: **Chlamydia trachomatis** (serotypes A, B, C)
- Leading infectious cause of blindness worldwide
- **4 Stages (IACS):**
 - Stage I (Inflammation, follicular): follicles on upper tarsal conjunctiva
 - Stage II (Inflammation, intense): intense hyperemia, immature follicles, limbal follicles (Herbert pits when healed)
 - Stage III (Scarring): Arlt's line — horizontal scarring of upper tarsal conjunctiva
 - Stage IV (Cicatricial): entropion, trichiasis, corneal scarring
- **Pannus** forms at **upper limbus** (not lower — this distinguishes from other causes)
- Treatment: **Tetracycline topically + sulfonamide/azithromycin orally**

### Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis (VKC)
- Bilateral, recurrent, seasonal (warm weather)
- Affects: young males predominantly
- Signs:
 - **Cobblestone papillae** on upper tarsal conjunctiva
 - Limbal follicles (Trantas dots)
 - **Shield corneal ulcer** (sterile, flat oval ulcer at superior cornea)
- Treatment: mast cell stabilizers (sodium cromoglycate), topical steroids in acute attacks

### Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (GPC)
- Contact lens wear (especially soft CL); sutures; ocular prosthesis
- Giant papillae on **upper tarsal** conjunctiva
- Treatment: discontinue CL, topical mast cell stabilizers

### Phlyctenular Keratoconjunctivitis
- Hypersensitivity reaction to microbial antigens (TB protein, Staph)
- Gray-white nodule at limbus
- Can migrate onto cornea with vascularization

### Conjunctival vs Ciliary Injection

| Feature | Conjunctival Injection | Ciliary (Circumcorneal) Injection |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Peripheral, away from limbus | Ring around limbus |
| Color | Bright red | Violet-red ("ciliary flush") |
| Moves with conjunctiva | Yes | No |
| Blanches with epinephrine | Yes | No |
| Associated with | Conjunctivitis, allergy | Uveitis, keratitis, acute glaucoma |

## 5. Sclera & Episclera

### Episcleritis vs Scleritis

| Feature | Episcleritis | Scleritis |
|---|---|---|
| Layer involved | Superficial episclera | Deep scleral tissue |
| Pain | Mild or no | Severe, boring, radiates |
| Laterality | Usually unilateral | Often bilateral |
| Sex | Women | Women (more severe) |
| Systemic associations | RA, gout, rosacea | PTB (tuberculosis), RA, SLE |
| Secondary glaucoma | No | Yes (secondary open angle) |
| Treatment | NSAIDs, steroids | Systemic NSAIDs, steroids; immunosuppression |

### Staphyloma
- Ectasia of sclera lined with uveal tissue
- Types by location: anterior (near limbus), ciliary (4mm from limbus), equatorial, posterior, intercalary
- Posterior staphyloma: associated with high myopia

### Congenital Blue Sclera
- Scleral thinning allows uveal pigment to show through
- Associations: osteogenesis imperfecta, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan syndrome

## 6. Cornea

### Keratoconus
- Bilateral non-inflammatory thinning and conical protrusion of the cornea
- Signs:
 - **Munson's sign:** V-shaped deformity of lower lid on downgaze
 - **Scissors reflex** on retinoscopy (irregular astigmatism)
 - **Fleischer ring:** iron deposits at base of cone (epithelial layer) — **earliest sign**
 - Vogt's striae: vertical stress lines in Bowman's layer
 - Oil droplet reflex on ophthalmoscopy
- Treatment: rigid GP contact lens; severe cases: **penetrating keratoplasty**; Intacs; corneal collagen crosslinking

### Corneal Opacities
- **Nebula:** Faint; barely visible; does not obstruct vision
- **Macula:** Moderate; greyish
- **Leukoma:** Dense white opacity; significantly reduces vision

### Corneal Iron Lines (All Epithelial Layer)

| Line | Condition |
|---|---|
| Hudson-Stahli line | Horizontal; normal aging; at lower 1/3 of cornea |
| **Fleischer ring** | Around base of cone in **keratoconus** |
| Ferry's line | Adjacent to filtering bleb |
| Stocker's line | Ahead of pterygium head |

**Memory:** "HuFFeStO" — Hudson-Stahli, Fleischer, Ferry, Stocker = all iron, all epithelial.

### Corneal Pigments

| Pigment | Color | Layer | Condition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Iron (all lines above) | Brown/gold | Epithelium | See table above |
| **Kayser-Fleischer ring** | Green-gold | **Descemet's membrane** | **Wilson's disease** (hepatolenticular degeneration) |
| Krukenberg spindle | Brown vertical | Endothelium | Pigment Dispersion Syndrome (PDS) |
| Argyrosis | Silver | Deep stroma | Prolonged silver nitrate use |
| Chrysiasis | Gold | Deep stroma | Gold therapy (rheumatoid arthritis) |

### Pigment Dispersion Syndrome (PDS) Triad
1. **Krukenberg spindle** (pigment on endothelium)
2. **Iris transillumination defects** (mid-peripheral radial slits)
3. **Sampaolesi's line** (pigment on Schwalbe's line)
— leads to pigmentary glaucoma (open angle type)

### Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca (KCS) / Dry Eye
Causes (think of layers of tear film):
- Aqueous deficiency: Sjogren's syndrome (most common immune cause), post-radiation, post-surgical
- Mucin deficiency: Vitamin A deficiency, Stevens-Johnson, trachoma, chemical burns
- Lipid deficiency: meibomian gland dysfunction, blepharitis

Tests:
- **Schirmer's test:** <10mm at 5 minutes = aqueous deficiency
- **Tear break-up time (TBUT):** <10 seconds = mucin/stability deficiency; evaluated after fluorescein staining with cobalt blue filter
- Rose bengal or lissamine green staining: stains dead/devitalized epithelial cells (interpalpebral zone)

### Arcus Senilis
- Lipid (cholesterol ester) deposition in peripheral corneal stroma
- Starts at superior then inferior, separated from limbus by lucid interval
- Benign in elderly (>60 years); if in young patients = **arcus juvenilis** (hyperlipidemia screen required)

### Band Keratopathy
- Calcium deposits in Bowman's layer; horizontal band across interpalpebral zone
- Causes: chronic uveitis, hypercalcemia (hyperparathyroidism, sarcoidosis), chronic corneal edema, renal failure
- Treatment: EDTA chelation

### Herpes Simplex Keratitis
- Primary: usually follicular conjunctivitis (lid vesicles)
- Recurrent: **dendritic ulcer** (pathognomonic); branching epithelial lesion with terminal bulbs; stains with fluorescein and rose bengal
- Disciform keratitis: immune stromal response; central disc of stromal edema
- Treatment: topical acyclovir or trifluridine (antivirals); NEVER use topical steroids for epithelial disease

### Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus
- VZV reactivation in ophthalmic branch (V1) of CN V
- **Hutchinson's sign:** vesicles on tip of nose = nasociliary involvement = high risk for ocular disease
- Complications: keratitis, uveitis, optic neuritis, CNIII palsy

## 7. Iris, Pupils & Anterior Chamber

### Pupillary Abnormalities

| Condition | Finding | Cause | Test/Clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Adie's tonic pupil** | Large, sluggish pupil; dilated in light | Ciliary ganglion damage (benign peripheral neuropathy) | Constricts to **0.1% pilocarpine** (denervation hypersensitivity) |
| **Argyll-Robertson pupil** | Accommodates but does NOT react to light; bilateral, small, irregular | Neurosyphilis (tabes dorsalis); pre-tectal lesion | "Prostitute's pupils" — accommodate but don't react |
| **Marcus Gunn / RAPD** | Paradoxical dilation when light swings to affected eye | Optic nerve lesion or severe retinal disease | Swinging flashlight test |
| **Horner's syndrome** | Ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis | Sympathetic pathway lesion | Cocaine test (no dilation) |

**"Accommodates but does not react":** Both Adie's and Argyll-Robertson.
**Difference:** Adie's is peripheral, unilateral, benign. Argyll-Robertson is central (pre-tectal), bilateral, syphilis.

### Iris Bombe vs Rubeosis Iridis

| Feature | Iris Bombe | Rubeosis Iridis |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Posterior synechiae + pupillary block → aqueous cannot pass pupil → iris bows forward | Neovascularization of iris (at pupillary margin first, then angle) |
| Result | Secondary closed-angle glaucoma WITH pupillary block | Secondary closed-angle glaucoma WITHOUT pupillary block |
| Associations | Uveitis, trauma | Diabetes mellitus, post-vitrectomy (central retinal vein occlusion) |
| Treatment | Laser iridotomy | Treat underlying cause; anti-VEGF; panretinal photocoagulation |

### Iridodialysis
- Separation of iris root from ciliary body
- Creates a dark biconvex area near the limbus
- Pupil appears D-shaped
- Caused by blunt trauma

### Heterochromia Iridis
- Difference in color between the two irises (binocular) or sectors of same iris
- Causes: Fuchs heterochromic cyclitis (lighter eye affected), siderosis (darker, iron deposits), Horner's syndrome congenital (lighter eye on affected side), nevus

## 8. Glaucoma

### IOP Physiology
- Normal IOP: **10-21 mmHg** (statistically)
- Aqueous humor produced by ciliary body epithelium → flows through pupil → drains via trabecular meshwork (80%) and uveoscleral route (20%) → Schlemm's canal → aqueous veins → episcleral veins
- Fluctuation: highest in morning, lowest at night; diurnal variation >5 mmHg = suspicious

### POAG vs PACG Comparison

| Feature | POAG | PACG |
|---|---|---|
| Nickname | "Silent thief of sight" | "Emergency glaucoma" |
| Onset | Gradual, painless | Sudden, painful |
| Angle | Open (trabecular block) | Closed (iris blocks meshwork) |
| Vision loss | Peripheral first | Blurred + halos around lights |
| Pupil | Normal | Mid-dilated, non-reactive |
| Cornea | Normal | Oedematous, cloudy |
| Ciliary injection | No | Yes |
| Nausea/vomiting | No | Yes (parasympathetic) |
| IOP | Gradually elevated | Acutely very elevated |
| Treatment | Medical (drops) | Surgical (laser iridotomy) |
| Optic disc | Cupping (C/D ratio >0.6) | Later cupping |

**Normal tension glaucoma:** Optic nerve damage + visual field loss at normal IOP (<21 mmHg). Treat with IOP-lowering agents even at normal baseline.

### Anti-Glaucoma Drugs — "MAPCO Lowers Pressure"

**M — Miotics (Parasympathomimetics)**
- Pilocarpine, Carbachol
- Mechanism: pupil constriction opens trabecular meshwork
- Use: POAG and PACG
- Side effects: induced myopia, brow ache, headache, miosis limits night vision

**A — Alpha-2 Agonists**
- Apraclonidine, Brimonidine
- Mechanism: decrease aqueous production + increase uveoscleral outflow
- Brimonidine: also neuroprotective; contraindicated in infants (CNS depression)

**P — Prostaglandin Analogues**
- Latanoprost, Travoprost, Bimatoprost (all "-prost")
- Mechanism: increase uveoscleral outflow
- Most effective; once-daily dosing
- Side effects: iris color darkening, lash growth, conjunctival hyperemia
- Do NOT use in uveitic glaucoma or active intraocular inflammation

**C — Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (CAI)**
- Acetazolamide (oral), Dorzolamide/Brinzolamide (topical)
- Mechanism: decrease aqueous production
- Acetazolamide side effects: paresthesia (most common), kidney stones, hypokalemia, **myopic shift** (ciliary body swelling)
- Contraindication: sulfonamide allergy

**O — Osmotic Agents** (for acute IOP lowering)

| Drug | Route | Key Note |
|---|---|---|
| Mannitol | IV only | Most commonly used; large MW stays in blood; C/I heart failure |
| Urea | IV only | C/I in renal disease |
| Glycerol | Oral | C/I in **diabetes** (raises blood glucose) |
| Isosorbide | Oral | **Safe in diabetes**; oral osmotic choice for diabetics |

**B — Beta-Blockers**
- Timolol (non-selective beta-blocker): most commonly used; C/I asthma, COPD, heart block, bradycardia
- Betaxolol (cardioselective beta-1 blocker): safer in pulmonary disease; slightly less effective

### Provocative Tests

**For POAG:**
- **Water drinking test (WDT):** Fasting, then 1L water in <5 minutes; positive = IOP rise >5 mmHg
- **Tonography:** Schiotz tonometer for 4 minutes; assesses outflow facility

**For PACG:**
- **Dark room test:** 1 hour in dark → mydriasis → angle narrows; positive = IOP rise **>8 mmHg**
- **Prone provocative test:** Face-down for 1 hour; same threshold

### Glaucoma Diagnostic Work-Up
1. **Tonometry** — measures IOP (Goldmann applanation = gold standard)
2. **Ophthalmoscopy** — optic disc cupping (C/D ratio >0.6 suspicious; asymmetry >0.2 between eyes)
3. **Perimetry** — visual field; Humphrey automated; early = arcuate defects, nasal step; advanced = tunnel vision
4. **Gonioscopy** — direct visualization of drainage angle (open vs. closed); essential for classification
5. **Pachymetry** — central corneal thickness (CCT); thin cornea (<520 microns) = independent risk factor for POAG

### Secondary Glaucomas

| Cause | Angle | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Rubeosis iridis | Closed WITHOUT pupillary block | NV occludes trabeculum |
| Iris bombe (synechiae) | Closed WITH pupillary block | Aqueous blocked at pupil |
| Phacolytic | Open | Leaking lens protein blocks trabeculum |
| Phacomorphic | Closed | Swollen lens pushes iris forward |
| PDS/Pigmentary | Open | Pigment granules block trabeculum |
| Corticosteroid-induced | Open | Trabecular blockage by steroids |
| Uveitic | Open or closed | Trabecular inflammation/synechiae |

## 9. Crystalline Lens & Cataract

### Ectopia Lentis

| Syndrome | Displacement | Gene/Defect | Other Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Marfan syndrome** | **UP (superotemporal)** | FBN-1, chr. 15q21 | Spider fingers (arachnodactyly), aortic aneurysm, dies mid-40s; autosomal dominant |
| **Homocystinuria** | **DOWN (inferonasal)** | Cystathionine beta-synthase deficiency | Thromboembolism risk; intellectual disability; methionine-restricted diet |
| **Weill-Marchesani** | Small, spherical lens (spherophakia) | ADAMTS10 | Myopia, microspherophakia → angle closure |

**Board trap:** Marfan = UP. Homocystinuria = DOWN.

### Lens-Induced Glaucomas

| Type | Mechanism | Angle Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Phacomorphic** | Swollen (intumescent) lens pushes iris forward | **Closed angle** |
| **Phacolytic** | Hypermature cataract leaks protein → macrophage blockade | **Open angle** |
| **Phacogenic/Phacoanaphylactic** | Ruptured lens capsule → immune reaction | Open angle |

### Cataract — Types and Features

| Type | Location | Key Clue |
|---|---|---|
| **Nuclear sclerosis** | Central nucleus | "Second sight" (senopia) — myopia increases; improved near vision without glasses |
| **Cortical** | Cortex | "Spokes of wheel"; VA not severely affected early |
| **Posterior subcapsular (PSC)** | Posterior capsule | **Most common age-related type**; most visually disabling; steroid-induced; near vision worse than distance |
| **Diabetic** | Ant + post subcapsular | **Snowflake opacity**; bilateral; **progresses in 72 hours** |
| **Galactosemia** | Cortex (oil droplet) | Dulcitol accumulates; reversible if galactose restricted early |
| **Traumatic** | PSC or rosette-shaped | Rupture of capsule; contusion; "Christmas tree" pattern also seen |
| **Steroid-induced** | Posterior subcapsular | Same as PSC; topical or systemic steroids |
| **Congenital rubella** | Central pearly opacity | Associated with deafness, heart defect (rubella triad) |

### Degrees of Cataract Maturation

1. **Immature** — some clear cortex remains; best for phacoemulsification
2. **Intumescent** — lens swells with fluid; risk of phacomorphic glaucoma
3. **Mature** — all fibers opaque
4. **Hypermature (Morgagnian)** — cortex liquefies, nucleus sinks; phacolytic glaucoma risk
5. **After-cataract** — posterior capsule opacification after ECCE; treated with **YAG laser capsulotomy**

### Cataract Surgery

| Procedure | What is Removed | Capsule Retained |
|---|---|---|
| **ICCE** (intracapsular) | Entire lens including both capsules | No capsule left |
| **ECCE** (extracapsular) | Lens + anterior capsule; posterior capsule retained | Yes (posterior capsule) |
| **Phacoemulsification** | Lens nucleus + cortex by ultrasound; posterior capsule retained | Yes (posterior capsule) |

- **Aphakia** = absence of lens = **severe hyperopia** (approximately +10 to +12D)
- IOL power calculated by **biometry** (measures corneal power + axial length)
- Corrective spectacles given **2-3 months post-op**

## 10. Uveal Tract & Uveitis

### Anatomy Quick Reference
- **Anterior uvea:** iris + ciliary body
- **Posterior uvea:** choroid
- **Uveitis classification (anatomic):**
 - Anterior uveitis = iritis, iridocyclitis (most common type overall)
 - Intermediate uveitis = pars planitis (snow balls, snow banking)
 - Posterior uveitis = choroiditis, chorioretinitis, retinitis
 - Panuveitis = all segments involved (e.g., sarcoidosis, Behcet's, VKH)

### Anterior Uveitis (Iritis/Iridocyclitis)

**Symptoms:** Photophobia, pain, blurred vision, lacrimation
**Signs:**
- Ciliary flush (circumcorneal injection)
- Cells and flare in anterior chamber (aqueous cell/flare)
- Keratic precipitates (KP) on endothelium:
 - **Fine (stellate) KP** = viral, Fuchs heterochromic cyclitis
 - **Mutton-fat (large greasy) KP** = granulomatous uveitis (sarcoidosis, TB, VKH, leprosy)
- Posterior synechiae (iris adhesions to lens)
- Busacca nodules (iris stroma) and Koeppe nodules (pupillary margin): granulomatous
- Hypopyon (pus in AC): HLA-B27 diseases (Behcet's), bacterial endophthalmitis

**Systemic Associations of Anterior Uveitis:**
- HLA-B27: ankylosing spondylitis, Reiter's syndrome, psoriatic arthritis, IBD
- Sarcoidosis: mutton-fat KP, bilateral
- JIA (juvenile idiopathic arthritis): chronic, insidious, anterior; often **ANA positive, asymptomatic**
- Herpes simplex/zoster: unilateral, with iris atrophy

**Treatment:** Topical steroids + cycloplegics (dilate pupil, prevent synechiae)

### Fuchs Heterochromic Cyclitis
- Chronic, low-grade unilateral uveitis
- Lighter-colored (hypochromic) iris on affected side
- Fine stellate KP; no posterior synechiae (distinguishing feature)
- Complications: cataract (most common), glaucoma
- No response to steroids; treat complications

### Posterior Uveitis

**Toxoplasmosis** (most common cause of posterior uveitis worldwide):
- Reactivation of Toxoplasma gondii cysts in retina
- "Headlight in fog" appearance: focal chorioretinitis with overlying vitritis
- Lesion at edge of old pigmented scar = reactivation
- Treatment: pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid (not folic acid); or TMP-SMX

**Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Retinitis:**
- In immunocompromised (HIV/AIDS, CD4 <50)
- "Pizza pie" or "brushfire" appearance: yellow-white patches with hemorrhage
- Treatment: ganciclovir (systemic or intravitreal implant), foscarnet

**Ocular Toxocariasis:**
- Children; contact with dog/cat roundworm (Toxocara)
- Peripheral granuloma or posterior pole granuloma
- Leukocoria (white reflex)

**Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) Syndrome:**
- Panuveitis + systemic features: vitiligo, alopecia, poliosis (white lashes), auditory changes, meningismus
- Treatment: high-dose systemic steroids

**Sympathetic Ophthalmia:**
- Bilateral panuveitis after penetrating trauma or intraocular surgery to one eye
- **Exciting eye** = injured eye; **sympathizing eye** = fellow eye
- Prevention: enucleation of severely injured eye within **10-14 days** (though evidence limited)
- Treatment: systemic steroids, immunosuppression

## 11. Vitreous & Retina

### Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD)
- Liquefaction of vitreous gel with age → separation of cortical vitreous from ILM
- Symptoms: floaters (vitreous opacities), photopsia (flashes from traction)
- **Weiss ring:** large floater = detached peripapillary vitreous
- Risk: retinal tear if posterior hyaloid tears from retina (see tobacco dust = RPE pigment in vitreous = retinal break until proven otherwise)

### Retinal Detachment (RD)

| Type | Mechanism | Key Feature | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rhegmatogenous** | Retinal break → fluid under retina | Horseshoe tear, dialysis; photopsia + floaters + curtain | Scleral buckle or PPV + gas |
| **Tractional** | Fibrous bands pull retina from RPE | DM, sickle cell, ROP; taut surface; no break | PPV |
| **Exudative** | Fluid from choroid without break | No break, retina shifts with position (shifting fluid); VKH, malignant HTN, posterior scleritis | Treat underlying cause |

**Tobacco dust (Shafer's sign):** Pigment cells in anterior vitreous = sign of retinal break. Emergency referral.

**Retinal breaks:**
- **Horseshoe (flap) tear:** vitreoretinal traction pulling retina; most likely to lead to RD
- **Round hole:** atrophic; low risk of RD
- **Retinal dialysis:** peripheral retina separates at ora serrata; trauma-related; most common in young males

**Symptoms of RD progression:** Photopsia (flashes) → floaters → "curtain coming down" (shadow in visual field).

### Diabetic Retinopathy (DR)

**Type 1 DM:** DR develops after 5-7 years; virtually 100% by 20 years.
**Type 2 DM:** May have DR at diagnosis (undetected DM for years).

#### Non-Proliferative DR (NPDR) Stages

| Stage | Findings |
|---|---|
| **Mild NPDR** | At least 1 microaneurysm |
| **Moderate NPDR** | Microaneurysms, dot-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots |
| **Severe NPDR** | "4-2-1 rule": ≥20 hemorrhages in all 4 quadrants OR venous beading in ≥2 quadrants OR IRMA in ≥1 quadrant |

**Features of NPDR:**
- **Microaneurysms:** earliest sign of DR; dot-like; leaks fluorescein on FFA
- **Dot-blot hemorrhages:** deep retinal hemorrhages; intraretinal
- **Hard exudates:** lipid/protein leakage; waxy yellow rings around leaking capillaries
- **Cotton wool spots:** nerve fiber layer infarcts; fluffy white patches; ischemia
- **IRMA:** Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities; shunt vessels; marker of ischemia
- **Venous beading:** vein irregularity; marker of severe ischemia

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME):**
- May occur at ANY stage of DR
- Clinically significant macular edema (CSME): hard exudates/thickening within 500 microns of foveal center
- Treatment: intravitreal anti-VEGF (first line); grid/focal laser photocoagulation; intravitreal steroids

#### Proliferative DR (PDR)
- New vessels at disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
- Complications: preretinal hemorrhage, vitreous hemorrhage, tractional RD, neovascular glaucoma (rubeosis iridis)
- Treatment: **panretinal photocoagulation (PRP)** = 1200-1600 laser spots outside posterior pole; destroys ischemic retina; reduces VEGF drive; intravitreal anti-VEGF as adjunct

### Hypertensive Retinopathy

**Keith-Wagener-Barker Classification:**

| Grade | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Grade I** | Arteriolar narrowing/tortuosity; increased arteriolar light reflex (copper wire) |
| **Grade II** | AV nicking (Gunn's sign, Salus's sign); silver wire arterioles |
| **Grade III** | Flame hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, hard exudates (macular star) |
| **Grade IV** | Grade III + papilledema = **malignant/accelerated hypertension** |

- **Copper wire arterioles:** arteriosclerosis, thickened wall
- **Silver wire arterioles:** denser opacity; near-total wall thickening
- **AV nicking/banking:** at crossing, thickened arteriole compresses venule
- **Macular star:** hard exudates in Henle's layer radiating from fovea
- **Elschnig's spots:** choroidal infarcts in severe hypertension

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (ARMD)

| Type | Key Features | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Dry (Atrophic)** | Drusen, geographic atrophy of RPE; gradual vision loss | AREDS2 vitamins; no cure; 85-90% of cases |
| **Wet (Neovascular)** | Choroidal neovascularization (CNV) → subretinal fluid/blood; rapid severe vision loss | **Intravitreal anti-VEGF** (ranibizumab, bevacizumab, aflibercept) — first line |

- **Drusen:** deposits under RPE; hard drusen (small, discrete) = lower risk; soft drusen (large, confluent) = higher risk of conversion to wet
- **Amsler grid distortion (metamorphopsia):** early symptom of CNV
- **Subretinal neovascular membrane:** on FFA shows early hyperfluorescence with late leakage

### Retinal Vascular Occlusions

**Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO):**
- Sudden, painless, monocular vision loss; **cherry-red spot** at fovea (choroidal flow preserved at fovea)
- Background retinal whitening (ischemic edema)
- RAPD present; afferent defect
- Treatment (within 4-6 hours): ocular massage, lowering IOP, hyperventilation; no proven treatment
- Rule out giant cell arteritis (elevated ESR/CRP), carotid emboli (Hollenhorst plaques)

**Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO):**
- Wedge-shaped area of whitening; partial visual field loss; Hollenhorst plaque visible at bifurcation

**Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO):**
- "Blood and thunder" fundus: diffuse flame hemorrhages all 4 quadrants; disc edema; dilated tortuous veins; cotton wool spots
- Types: **ischemic CRVO** (severe, RAPD, poor prognosis, neovascular glaucoma risk) vs **non-ischemic CRVO** (milder, better prognosis)
- Complications: macular edema, rubeosis iridis (3-month rule: neovascularization at 3 months)
- Treatment: treat macular edema (intravitreal anti-VEGF); PRP for ischemic type

**Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO):**
- Sector hemorrhage at AV crossing; superior temporal quadrant most common
- Treatment: treat macular edema

### Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP)
- Premature infants (<36 weeks gestation, <1500g); supplemental oxygen disrupts retinal vascularization
- Immature retinal blood vessels → abnormal neovascularization
- **Plus disease:** dilation + tortuosity of posterior retinal vessels = active disease
- **Stage 5:** total retinal detachment = leukocoria
- Screening: all infants <1500g or <32 weeks gestation
- Treatment: laser photocoagulation (current standard); intravitreal anti-VEGF; cryotherapy; scleral buckle/PPV for advanced stages

### Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP)
- Hereditary dystrophy of rods and cones; rods affected first
- Symptoms: night blindness (nyctalopia) → peripheral field loss → tunnel vision → central vision loss (late)
- Signs: **Bone spicule pigmentation** (perivenular), arteriolar attenuation, **waxy pale disc**
- ERG: reduced/extinguished rod response (b-wave reduction)
- Visual field: ring scotoma expanding outward
- Inheritance: autosomal dominant (most common and mildest), AR (severe), X-linked (severe in males)
- Associations: Usher syndrome (RP + sensorineural deafness), Laurence-Moon-Bardet-Biedl syndrome

## 12. Optic Nerve

### Papilledema
- Bilateral optic disc edema from raised intracranial pressure (ICP)
- Key feature: **bilateral**, no vision loss initially (enlarged blind spot on perimetry early)
- Causes: brain tumor, pseudotumor cerebri (IIH), meningitis, venous sinus thrombosis, malignant hypertension
- Stages: early → fully developed → chronic → atrophic
- Differentiate from **pseudopapilledema** (disc drusen, hyperopic disc)
- Treatment: treat cause; acetazolamide for IIH; optic nerve sheath fenestration if vision threatened

**Papilledema vs Papillitis (Optic Neuritis):**

| Feature | Papilledema | Papillitis |
|---|---|---|
| Laterality | **Bilateral** | **Unilateral** (usually) |
| Vision loss | Late (blind spot enlarged) | **Early and significant** |
| Pain | No | Yes (pain on eye movement) |
| RAPD | Absent (bilateral) | Present |
| ICP | Elevated | Normal |

### Optic Neuritis
- Inflammation of optic nerve; most common in young adult females
- Symptoms: unilateral vision loss over days; periocular pain (especially on movement)
- Signs: RAPD; central scotoma; dyschromatopsia (color vision loss, especially red desaturation)
- **Marcus Gunn pupil present**
- Associations: **Multiple sclerosis** (optic neuritis is first presentation in 25% of MS patients); neuromyelitis optica (NMO/Devic); post-viral; autoimmune
- MRI: shows demyelinating plaques; predicts MS risk
- Treatment: IV methylprednisolone accelerates recovery (does not change final visual outcome); oral prednisone alone may increase recurrence risk (ONTT study)

### Non-Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (NAION)
- Sudden, painless visual loss; usually upon waking (nocturnal hypotension)
- Altitudinal visual field defect (inferior more common)
- Pale, edematous disc with peripapillary splinter hemorrhages
- Risk factors: "small disc with small cup" (disc at risk), DM, HTN, hyperlipidemia, sleep apnea
- No proven treatment; manage vascular risk factors

### Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (AAION / Giant Cell Arteritis)
- Occurs in patients >55 years (mean age 70)
- Symptoms: severe headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, polymyalgia rheumatica
- Signs: tender, nodular, non-pulsatile temporal artery; elevated **ESR (>50) and CRP**
- Optic nerve: pale, chalky white edema
- **Emergency:** Vision loss can be bilateral if untreated; second eye affected in days/weeks
- Treatment: **high-dose IV corticosteroids immediately** (do not wait for biopsy); temporal artery biopsy confirms (skip lesions possible)

### Glaucomatous Optic Neuropathy
- Enlarged C/D ratio (>0.6 or asymmetry >0.2); progressive cupping
- Disc signs: notching (inferior > superior > nasal > temporal — ISNT rule violated in glaucoma), RNFL defects, disc hemorrhage (Drance hemorrhage), bayonetting of vessels, nasal shift of vessels

### Traumatic Optic Neuropathy (TON)
- Sudden vision loss after head/orbital trauma; RAPD; no external sign initially
- CT scan: optic canal fracture
- Treatment: IV methylprednisolone within 8 hours (controversial); surgical decompression in selected cases

## 13. Ocular Trauma

### Blunt Trauma Findings (Anterior to Posterior)

| Finding | Description |
|---|---|
| Corneal abrasion | Epithelial breach; fluorescein staining |
| Hyphema | Blood in anterior chamber; "fluid level"; 8-ball hyphema (total) |
| Vossius ring | Pigment imprint on anterior lens capsule; from iris compression against lens |
| Traumatic mydriasis | Dilated, poorly reactive pupil; sphincter tears |
| Iridodialysis | Iris root separation; D-shaped pupil |
| Traumatic cataract | PSC or rosette; rosette may resolve or progress |
| Angle recession | Tear in ciliary body face; risk of late glaucoma (monitor for years) |
| Commotio retinae | Gray retinal opacification; macula = cherry-red spot; resolves in 2-6 weeks |
| Choroidal rupture | White crescent concentric with disc; subretinal hemorrhage initially |
| Retinal dialysis | Peripheral detachment at ora serrata; most common in young males with blunt trauma |

### Blow-Out Orbital Floor Fracture
- Blunt force >5cm diameter (fist, ball) → orbital pressure → fracture at weakest point (orbital floor)
- **Signs:**
 - Infraorbital nerve anesthesia (lower lid, cheek, upper lip, upper teeth)
 - Diplopia (restricted upgaze and downgaze)
 - Enophthalmos (appears after edema resolves)
 - Subcutaneous emphysema
- **Best imaging:** CT scan with **coronal sections** (not axial)
- **Treatment:**
 - Conservative initially (ice, antibiotics, nasal decongestant, **NO nose blowing**)
 - Surgery if: >1/3 of floor involved, persistent diplopia, enophthalmos >2mm
 - Timing: repair within 2 weeks if indicated

### Intraocular Foreign Bodies (IOFB)

| Metal | Condition | Key Sign | ERG Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Iron (ferrous)** | **Siderosis** | Rust-colored iris, heterochromia, anterior capsular cataract | **B-wave attenuation** (progressive) |
| **Copper (high content)** | Endophthalmitis-like (acute) | Violent reaction; progresses to phthisis | — |
| **Copper alloy (brass/bronze)** | **Chalcosis** | Kayser-Fleischer ring + sunflower cataract | Golden retinal plaques; less toxic than iron |

- **MRI is contraindicated** with ferrous IOFB
- CT (axial + coronal) = best imaging for IOFB
- Localization: anywhere from AC to choroid

### Shaken Baby Syndrome
- Age: children <2 years
- **Most common feature: bilateral retinal hemorrhages** (may extend to ora serrata)
- Associated: subdural/subarachnoid hemorrhage; retinal folds; retinoschisis
- Mechanism: violent shaking → vitreoretinal traction + sudden increase in ICP
- Child abuse; mandatory reporting

## 14. Chemical & Radiation Injuries

### Chemical Burns

**Key rule:** Chemical burn is the ONLY ocular injury requiring treatment BEFORE full history and examination.

**Alkali vs Acid:**
- **Alkali** (ammonia, NaOH, lime) = MORE dangerous; penetrates deeper (liquefaction necrosis); no protective coagulum
- **Acid** (sulphuric, acetic, hydrofluoric) = less penetrating; forms coagulum that limits spread
- Exception: **Hydrofluoric acid** and **ammonia** penetrate deeply like alkali

**Roper-Hall Grading:**

| Grade | Cornea | Limbal Ischemia | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Clear (epithelial damage only) | None | Excellent |
| II | Hazy; iris details visible | <1/3 | Good |
| III | Total epithelial loss; iris details obscured | 1/3 to 1/2 | Guarded |
| IV | Opaque | >1/2 | Very poor |

**Emergency Treatment:**
1. **Copious irrigation immediately** (normal saline or Ringer's lactate for 30+ minutes; tap water if nothing else available) — most important prognostic factor
2. **Double-evert upper lid** — remove retained particulate matter from fornices
3. Measure pH; continue irrigation until pH is 7.0-7.4
4. Topical anesthetic before irrigation for cooperation
5. Debride necrotic epithelium

**Medical Treatment of Severe Burns:**
- Topical steroids: first 7-10 days (reduce inflammation but impair stromal healing; taper after day 10)
- Topical/systemic ascorbic acid (vitamin C): reverses scorbutic state; promotes collagen synthesis
- Topical/systemic citrate: chelates calcium, inhibits collagenase; inhibits second wave of PMN infiltration (day 7)
- Tetracycline (topical/oral): collagenase inhibitor; anti-neutrophil
- Acetylcysteine 10%: alternative anti-collagenase
- Symblepharon lysis: daily; glass rod or cotton bud
- Cycloplegics; antibiotics; IOP monitoring (acetazolamide if elevated)

**Radiation Injuries:**
- UV (arc welding, snow blindness, sun eclipse): photokeratitis — treatment: cool compresses, topical anesthetics, NSAIDs; heals in 24-48 hours
- Infrared: lenticular (glass-blower's cataract) — PSC opacity
- Ionizing radiation: radiation retinopathy (cotton wool spots, hemorrhages, macular exudates), dry eye, cataract, optic neuropathy

## 15. Aging Changes

### Sequence of Aging Changes — "ECAL-CILR"

**External and Anterior Segment:**
- **E** — Enophthalmos (loss of orbital fat)
- **C** — Corneal aging: arcus senilis (lipid at periphery), Hassall-Henle warts (endothelial blebs at periphery), band keratopathy risk, decreased corneal sensitivity, reduced endothelial cell count
- **A** — Aqueous humor decrease: ~25% per decade after age 30
- **L** — Lens: increased thickness and weight; decreased elasticity (presbyopia)

**Lens Changes with Age:**
- Axial thickness increases ~28% by age 70 (from ~3.5mm to ~5.0mm)
- Nuclear sclerosis (yellowing/brunescence)
- Presbyopia (decreased amplitude of accommodation): AoA = 18 - 1/3 age (Donders); minimum AoA = 1 (maximum near) = 100cm = +1.00D

**Vitreous and Posterior:**
- **C** — Cortical vitreous separates (PVD, syneresis)
- **I** — IOP: slight decrease after age 40 (ciliary body regression)
- **L** — RNFL thinning: 20-30% over lifetime; worst in superior quadrant
- **R** — Retinal sensitivity decreases (peripheral first); dark adaptation slows

**Anterior Chamber:**
- AC depth decreases from ~3.6mm (young) to ~3.0mm (elderly) — narrowing angle risk

**Other aging changes:**
- Conjunctiva: pinguecula formation; laxity (conjunctivochalasis)
- Lower lid: ectropion, entropion risk from lax tissues
- Schlemm's canal: decreased aqueous outflow facility
- Choroidal thinning (especially temporal peripapillary)

### Disease Organizer by Affected Structure

**Eyelid malignancy → Basal cell carcinoma**
**Corneal iron lines → Epithelium (HuFFeStO)**
**Corneal metal deposits → KF ring (Descemet's, copper, Wilson's); Krukenberg (endothelium, pigment, PDS)**
**White pupil (leukocoria) → RB, Toxocara, ROP, congenital cataract, persistent fetal vasculature**
**Cherry-red spot → CRAO, Tay-Sachs, Niemann-Pick, commotio retinae**
**Neovascular glaucoma → CRVO, PDR, CRAO (rare)**
**Night blindness → RP, Vit A deficiency, choroideremia**
**Bilateral retinal hemorrhages in child → Shaken baby syndrome**
**Pizza pie retina → CMV retinitis (immunocompromised)**
**Headlight in fog → Toxoplasma chorioretinitis**

### Clinical Action Sequences

**Acute PACG emergency:**
1. IV acetazolamide + topical beta-blocker + topical alpha-agonist
2. Pilocarpine to constrict pupil (once IOP lowered, as pilocarpine ineffective at very high IOP)
3. Mannitol IV if IOP does not respond
4. Laser iridotomy once cornea clears

**Chemical burn emergency:**
1. Irrigation IMMEDIATELY (do not wait for history)
2. Double evert upper lid, remove particles
3. Check pH; repeat irrigation until neutral
4. Then: topical anesthetic, antibiotics, steroids, ascorbate, cycloplegia

**Retinal detachment urgent referral signs:**
- New floaters + flashes + curtain in visual field = emergency referral to ophthalmologist same day
- Tobacco dust (Shafer's sign) = retinal break → urgent referral
