## E1 — Orbit, Adnexa, and Eyelids
*Personal study notes | TOS: E Ocular Disease | Source: Ocular Disease, Dr. Elmo Ado + consolidated review*

---

## The Simple Version

This note covers the disease of everything *around* the eyeball: the bony orbit, the eyelids, and the lacrimal (tear) system. Work front to back and outside in:

- **Orbit:** is the globe the wrong size, pushed forward (proptosis), or infected (cellulitis)?
- **Eyelids:** is the lid drooping (ptosis), turned in (entropion) or out (ectropion), lumpy (stye vs chalazion), or growing a tumor?
- **Lacrimal:** is the gland inflamed, or is the drain blocked (epiphora, dacryocystitis)?

**Teaching angle:** most of this note is paired opposites. Hordeolum vs chalazion, entropion vs ectropion, episcleritis vs scleritis. Learn the pairs, not the single facts, because the board tests the distinction.

---

## But Why?

**Why does orbital cellulitis come from the sinuses?**
The ethmoid sinus sits right against the medial orbital wall, separated only by paper-thin bone (lamina papyracea). Infection crosses easily, so **ethmoiditis** is the most common cause. The orbital septum is the firewall: infection in front of it (preseptal) is milder; behind it (orbital) threatens vision and the brain.

**Why is a stye tender but a chalazion is not?**
A hordeolum (stye) is an acute bacterial abscess, so it is hot, red, and painful. A chalazion is a chronic sterile lipogranuloma: the meibomian gland blocked and the trapped lipid triggered a slow granuloma. No bug, no acute inflammation, no pain.

**Why does the lower lid droop outward (ectropion) in CN VII palsy?**
The orbicularis muscle (closed by CN VII) holds the lid against the globe. Paralyze it and the lid falls away, exposing the eye. The same logic explains lagophthalmos (cannot close) and exposure keratopathy.

**Why does blood on pressure over the lacrimal sac suggest a tumor?**
A simple infected sac regurgitates pus. Blood means something is eroding vessels, classically a squamous cell carcinoma of the sac. The character of the regurgitation changes the diagnosis.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| Proptosis | Greek *pro* = forward + *ptosis* = falling | Forward globe displacement in any direction; pathologic beyond 21 mm |
| Exophthalmos | *ex* = out + *ophthalmos* = eye | Anterior globe protrusion specifically; Graves disease is the #1 cause |
| Buphthalmos | Greek *bous* = ox + *ophthalmos* = eye ("ox eye") | Enlarged infant globe from congenital glaucoma stretching cornea/sclera |
| Ptosis | Greek = falling | Drooping of the upper lid |
| Entropion | *en* = in + *trope* = turning | Lid margin turns inward; lashes abrade the cornea |
| Ectropion | *ec* = out + *trope* = turning | Lid margin turns outward; causes exposure and epiphora |
| Lagophthalmos | Greek *lagos* = hare + *ophthalmos* | Incomplete lid closure ("hare's eye"); leads to exposure keratopathy |
| Hordeolum | Latin *hordeum* = barley | Acute, tender lid-margin abscess (stye) |
| Chalazion | Greek *chalaza* = hailstone | Chronic, non-tender meibomian lipogranuloma |
| Madarosis | Greek *madaros* = bald | Loss of lashes/brows, classic for ulcerative blepharitis |
| Epiphora | Greek = sudden flow of tears | Tear overflow from drainage block or reflex tearing |
| Dacryoadenitis | *dakryon* = tear + *aden* = gland | Lacrimal gland inflammation; S-shaped ptosis |
| Dacryocystitis | *dakryon* + *kystis* = sac | Lacrimal sac infection; medial canthal swelling |
| Distichiasis | *di* = two + *stichos* = row | Accessory second row of eyelashes |
| Symblepharon | *syn* = together + *blepharon* = lid | Adhesion between palpebral and bulbar conjunctiva |

### 1. Orbit and Globe

**Globe size anomalies:**

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Anophthalmos | No globe develops |
| Microphthalmos | Abnormally small globe |
| Phthisis bulbi | Shrunken end-stage globe |
| **Buphthalmos** | Enlarged globe from **congenital glaucoma** (raised IOP stretches the infant cornea/sclera) |
| Hypertelorism | Orbits set too far apart |
| Telecanthus | Wide medial canthi only (orbits normal) |

**Exophthalmos / Proptosis** (globe protrusion; proptosis = any direction, exophthalmos = anterior). Pathologic beyond **21 mm**.
- **Active:** thyroid (Graves), the most common cause of both unilateral and bilateral proptosis in adults.
- **Intermittent:** orbital varices, worse with Valsalva.
- **Pulsating:** carotid-cavernous fistula.
- **Unilateral:** usually neoplastic. **Bilateral:** usually systemic (thyroid, leukemia, neuroblastoma).

**Orbital cellulitis:** most common cause **ethmoiditis**. Treat with incision/drainage + massive antibiotics. Distinguish from milder preseptal cellulitis (anterior to septum).

**Orbital tumors:** most common benign = **cavernous hemangioma**; most common primary malignancy in children = **rhabdomyosarcoma** (peak 7 to 8 years).

### 2. Eyelids

**Congenital:** coloboma (upper nasal), epicanthus (medial fold, common in Asians), distichiasis (extra lash row), symblepharon (conjunctival adhesion), blepharophimosis (short fissure + ptosis + telecanthus).

**Ptosis types:**

| Type | Cause | Clue |
|---|---|---|
| Neurogenic | CN III palsy or Horner's | Horner triad: ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis |
| Myogenic | Myasthenia gravis | Fatiguable; positive Tensilon test |
| Aponeurotic | Levator disinsertion | Most common adult; high crease |
| Mechanical | Tumor/scar weight | Lid heaviness |
| Congenital | Levator maldevelopment | Absent crease; amblyopia risk |

**Inflammatory:**

| Condition | Key |
|---|---|
| Squamous blepharitis | Dry seborrheic scales |
| Ulcerative blepharitis | Staph; crusted ulcers, madarosis (lash loss) |
| Meibomitis | Posterior; foamy tears |
| **Hordeolum (stye)** | Acute, **tender** abscess (external Zeis/Moll, internal meibomian) |
| **Chalazion** | Chronic, **non-tender** lipogranuloma of meibomian gland |

**Position abnormalities:**
- **Entropion** (turns in, lashes scratch): cicatricial (trachoma), spastic (elderly), atonic, congenital.
- **Ectropion** (turns out, exposure): senile/atonic (most common), cicatricial, paralytic (**CN VII**).
- **Lagophthalmos:** incomplete closure → exposure keratopathy.

**Orbicularis:** myokymia (benign twitch, stress), essential blepharospasm (forceful bilateral closure, treat with botulinum).

**Eyelid tumors:** **basal cell carcinoma** is the most common malignancy (pearly margin, lower lid). Xanthelasma (yellow lipid, hyperlipidemia), keratoacanthoma (volcano crater, involutes), milia, syringoma.

### 3. Lacrimal System

**Gland disease:**
- Acute dacryoadenitis: painful upper-outer swelling, **S-shaped ptosis**; bacterial or viral (mumps, EBV).
- Chronic dacryoadenitis: painless; sarcoid, TB, lymphoma.
- **Mikulicz syndrome:** bilateral painless lacrimal AND salivary gland enlargement.
- Tumors: benign = **pleomorphic adenoma**; malignant = **adenoid cystic carcinoma** (perineural invasion, painful, poor prognosis).

**Drainage (epiphora = tear overflow):**
- **Jones I:** fluorescein recovered from the nose = patent system.
- **Jones II:** if Jones I negative, irrigate; dye recovered = partial block above the sac; clear saline = block at/below the sac.

**Dacryocystitis** (sac infection):
- Congenital: bilateral, newborn, pneumococcus, mucocele of NLD. Probe at 12 to 18 months if massage fails.
- Adult: unilateral, medial canthal swelling, pus regurgitation. Treat chronic with **DCR** (dacryocystorhinostomy).
- Blood on pressure over the sac = suspect **squamous cell carcinoma** of the sac.

## Numbers and Values to Know

| Parameter | Value | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Pathologic proptosis | > 21 mm | Anterior protrusion beyond this is abnormal |
| Normal exophthalmometry (Hertel) | 12–21 mm | Side-to-side asymmetry > 2 mm is abnormal |
| Rhabdomyosarcoma peak age | 7–8 years | Most common primary pediatric orbital malignancy |
| Congenital NLD probing | 12–18 months | Probe if conservative massage fails by this age |
| Cavernous hemangioma | adults | Most common benign orbital tumor overall |

## Memory Anchors

- **Buphthalmos = big infant eye = congenital glaucoma.**
- **Horner triad = ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis** (small pupil, not big).
- **Stye = Sore (tender, acute). Chalazion = Chronic, Cool (non-tender).**
- **EntropioN = iN; ectropion = out** (ectropion with CN VII).
- **Orbital cellulitis = Ethmoiditis.**
- **BCC = most common lid malignancy** (pearly, lower lid).
- **Jones I to the nose; Jones II to localize.**
- **Blood from the sac = sac tumor.**

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** Horner's causes a dilated pupil. Wrong. **Miosis** (small). The dilated-pupil ptosis is CN III palsy.
- **Trap:** chalazion is a painful infection. Wrong. It is a chronic, non-tender, sterile lipogranuloma. The painful one is the hordeolum.
- **Trap:** ectropion scratches the cornea with lashes. Wrong. That is **entropion** (turns in). Ectropion turns out and causes exposure.
- **Trap:** the most common cause of proptosis is a tumor. Usually thyroid (Graves) is most common overall; unilateral proptosis is the one that is more often neoplastic.
- **Trap:** pus regurgitating from the sac means cancer. No, pus = infection; **blood** is the tumor sign.

## Practice Questions

**1.** An infant has an enlarged, cloudy cornea and tearing. The enlarged globe is termed:

- A. Microphthalmos
- B. Buphthalmos
- C. Phthisis bulbi
- D. Anophthalmos

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Buphthalmos.** Raised IOP from congenital glaucoma stretches the infant's distensible cornea and sclera, enlarging the globe.
</details>

**2.** A painful, tender, red nodule erupts at the lid margin over a few days. Most likely:

- A. Chalazion
- B. Hordeolum
- C. Xanthelasma
- D. Basal cell carcinoma

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Hordeolum (stye).** Acute, tender bacterial abscess. A chalazion is chronic and non-tender.
</details>

**3.** A patient with Bell's palsy (CN VII) develops a lower lid that falls away from the globe. This is:

- A. Entropion
- B. Ectropion
- C. Lagophthalmos only
- D. Distichiasis

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Ectropion** (paralytic). Orbicularis paralysis lets the lid evert. Lagophthalmos (incomplete closure) often coexists, but the everted lid is ectropion.
</details>

**4.** Fluorescein placed in the eye is not recovered from the nose; after saline irrigation the dye is recovered. This indicates:

- A. A fully patent system
- B. Partial obstruction above the sac
- C. Complete block below the sac
- D. Lacrimal gland tumor

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** A negative Jones I with dye recovered on Jones II irrigation indicates partial (functional) obstruction proximal to the sac.
</details>

**5.** Chronic dacryocystitis with blood regurgitating on pressure over the sac should raise suspicion for:

- A. Pneumococcal infection
- B. Squamous cell carcinoma of the sac
- C. Allergic reaction
- D. Canalicular stenosis

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Pus suggests infection; blood suggests a sac tumor, classically squamous cell carcinoma.
</details>
