E1 — Orbit, Adnexa, and Eyelids

The Simple Version

This note covers the disease of everything around the eyeball: the bony orbit, the eyelids, and the lacrimal (tear) system. Work front to back and outside in:
  • Orbit: is the globe the wrong size, pushed forward (proptosis), or infected (cellulitis)?
  • Eyelids: is the lid drooping (ptosis), turned in (entropion) or out (ectropion), lumpy (stye vs chalazion), or growing a tumor?
  • Lacrimal: is the gland inflamed, or is the drain blocked (epiphora, dacryocystitis)?

Teaching angle: most of this note is paired opposites. Hordeolum vs chalazion, entropion vs ectropion, episcleritis vs scleritis. Learn the pairs, not the single facts, because the board tests the distinction.

But Why?

Why does orbital cellulitis come from the sinuses?

The ethmoid sinus sits right against the medial orbital wall, separated only by paper-thin bone (lamina papyracea). Infection crosses easily, so ethmoiditis is the most common cause. The orbital septum is the firewall: infection in front of it (preseptal) is milder; behind it (orbital) threatens vision and the brain.

Why is a stye tender but a chalazion is not?

A hordeolum (stye) is an acute bacterial abscess, so it is hot, red, and painful. A chalazion is a chronic sterile lipogranuloma: the meibomian gland blocked and the trapped lipid triggered a slow granuloma. No bug, no acute inflammation, no pain.

Why does the lower lid droop outward (ectropion) in CN VII palsy?

The orbicularis muscle (closed by CN VII) holds the lid against the globe. Paralyze it and the lid falls away, exposing the eye. The same logic explains lagophthalmos (cannot close) and exposure keratopathy.

Why does blood on pressure over the lacrimal sac suggest a tumor?

A simple infected sac regurgitates pus. Blood means something is eroding vessels, classically a squamous cell carcinoma of the sac. The character of the regurgitation changes the diagnosis.

Exophthalmos / Proptosis (globe protrusion; proptosis = any direction, exophthalmos = anterior). Pathologic beyond 21 mm.

  • Active: thyroid (Graves), the most common cause of both unilateral and bilateral proptosis in adults.
  • Intermittent: orbital varices, worse with Valsalva.
  • Pulsating: carotid-cavernous fistula.
  • Unilateral: usually neoplastic. Bilateral: usually systemic (thyroid, leukemia, neuroblastoma).

Orbital cellulitis: most common cause ethmoiditis. Treat with incision/drainage + massive antibiotics. Distinguish from milder preseptal cellulitis (anterior to septum).

Orbital tumors: most common benign = cavernous hemangioma; most common primary malignancy in children = rhabdomyosarcoma (peak 7 to 8 years).

2. Eyelids

Congenital: coloboma (upper nasal), epicanthus (medial fold, common in Asians), distichiasis (extra lash row), symblepharon (conjunctival adhesion), blepharophimosis (short fissure + ptosis + telecanthus).

Position abnormalities:

  • Entropion (turns in, lashes scratch): cicatricial (trachoma), spastic (elderly), atonic, congenital.
  • Ectropion (turns out, exposure): senile/atonic (most common), cicatricial, paralytic (CN VII).
  • Lagophthalmos: incomplete closure → exposure keratopathy.

Orbicularis: myokymia (benign twitch, stress), essential blepharospasm (forceful bilateral closure, treat with botulinum).

Eyelid tumors: basal cell carcinoma is the most common malignancy (pearly margin, lower lid). Xanthelasma (yellow lipid, hyperlipidemia), keratoacanthoma (volcano crater, involutes), milia, syringoma.

3. Lacrimal System

Gland disease:

  • Acute dacryoadenitis: painful upper-outer swelling, S-shaped ptosis; bacterial or viral (mumps, EBV).
  • Chronic dacryoadenitis: painless; sarcoid, TB, lymphoma.
  • Mikulicz syndrome: bilateral painless lacrimal AND salivary gland enlargement.
  • Tumors: benign = pleomorphic adenoma; malignant = adenoid cystic carcinoma (perineural invasion, painful, poor prognosis).

Drainage (epiphora = tear overflow):

  • Jones I: fluorescein recovered from the nose = patent system.
  • Jones II: if Jones I negative, irrigate; dye recovered = partial block above the sac; clear saline = block at/below the sac.

Dacryocystitis (sac infection):

  • Congenital: bilateral, newborn, pneumococcus, mucocele of NLD. Probe at 12 to 18 months if massage fails.
  • Adult: unilateral, medial canthal swelling, pus regurgitation. Treat chronic with DCR (dacryocystorhinostomy).
  • Blood on pressure over the sac = suspect squamous cell carcinoma of the sac.

Practice Questions

1. An infant has an enlarged, cloudy cornea and tearing. The enlarged globe is termed:

  • A. Microphthalmos
  • B. Buphthalmos
  • C. Phthisis bulbi
  • D. Anophthalmos
Answer

B. Buphthalmos. Raised IOP from congenital glaucoma stretches the infant's distensible cornea and sclera, enlarging the globe.

2. A painful, tender, red nodule erupts at the lid margin over a few days. Most likely:

  • A. Chalazion
  • B. Hordeolum
  • C. Xanthelasma
  • D. Basal cell carcinoma
Answer

B. Hordeolum (stye). Acute, tender bacterial abscess. A chalazion is chronic and non-tender.

3. A patient with Bell's palsy (CN VII) develops a lower lid that falls away from the globe. This is:

  • A. Entropion
  • B. Ectropion
  • C. Lagophthalmos only
  • D. Distichiasis
Answer

B. Ectropion (paralytic). Orbicularis paralysis lets the lid evert. Lagophthalmos (incomplete closure) often coexists, but the everted lid is ectropion.

4. Fluorescein placed in the eye is not recovered from the nose; after saline irrigation the dye is recovered. This indicates:

  • A. A fully patent system
  • B. Partial obstruction above the sac
  • C. Complete block below the sac
  • D. Lacrimal gland tumor
Answer

B. A negative Jones I with dye recovered on Jones II irrigation indicates partial (functional) obstruction proximal to the sac.

5. Chronic dacryocystitis with blood regurgitating on pressure over the sac should raise suspicion for:

  • A. Pneumococcal infection
  • B. Squamous cell carcinoma of the sac
  • C. Allergic reaction
  • D. Canalicular stenosis
Answer

B. Pus suggests infection; blood suggests a sac tumor, classically squamous cell carcinoma.

E2 — Anterior Segment Disease

The Simple Version

The anterior segment is everything from the conjunctiva to the lens. Walk the light path inward:
  • Conjunctiva: the four conjunctivitides (bacterial, viral, allergic, chlamydial), and the growths (pinguecula, pterygium).
  • Sclera/episclera: episcleritis (mild) vs scleritis (severe, systemic).
  • Cornea: keratoconus, the iron lines and pigments, dry eye, the herpes keratitides.
  • Iris/pupil/anterior chamber: the named pupils (Adie, Argyll-Robertson, Horner, RAPD), and the injections (conjunctival vs ciliary).
  • Glaucoma: POAG vs PACG, the secondary glaucomas, and the drug ladder.
  • Lens: ectopia lentis, the cataract types, and surgery.
  • Anterior uveitis: cells, flare, keratic precipitates, and the systemic links.

Teaching angle: the anterior segment is a museum of comparison tables. Master each two-column pair and you have most of the marks.

But Why?

Why does a pterygium threaten vision but a pinguecula does not?

A pinguecula is a degenerative yellow deposit that stops at the limbus. A pterygium is a fibrovascular wing that crosses the limbus, destroys Bowman's layer, and drags onto the visual axis, inducing astigmatism. Crossing the limbus is the whole difference.

Why is ciliary (circumcorneal) injection a danger sign but conjunctival injection usually is not?

Conjunctival injection is superficial vessels reacting to surface irritation (conjunctivitis, allergy). Ciliary flush is the deep perilimbal vessels, which engorge only when the cornea, iris, or angle is in trouble: keratitis, uveitis, or acute glaucoma. Deep vessels, deep disease.

Why does the pupil go mid-dilated and fixed in acute angle-closure?

The pressure spike paralyzes the iris sphincter, and the iris, jammed against the trabecular meshwork, sits mid-dilated. That mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil with a cloudy cornea and a hard, painful eye is the emergency picture.

Why does nuclear sclerosis give "second sight"?

The hardening nucleus raises the lens index of refraction, adding minus power (myopic shift). A presbyope can suddenly read without glasses, briefly, before the cataract clouds vision. The same index change that helps near vision will eventually blur everything.

Trachoma (Chlamydia A, B, C; leading infectious cause of world blindness): stages I-A-C-S (Inflammation follicular → Intense → Arlt's line scarring → Cicatricial entropion/trichiasis). Pannus at the upper limbus; Herbert pits when limbal follicles heal.

Vernal keratoconjunctivitis: young males, seasonal, cobblestone papillae, Trantas dots, shield ulcer. GPC: contact-lens related, upper tarsal giant papillae. Phlyctenular: hypersensitivity to TB/Staph antigen, limbal nodule.

Injection compare: conjunctival (peripheral, bright red, moves, blanches with epinephrine, = surface disease) vs ciliary flush (perilimbal, violet, fixed, does not blanch, = uveitis/keratitis/acute glaucoma).

Sclera

Episcleritis vs scleritis: episcleritis is superficial, mild/no pain, benign (RA, gout). Scleritis is deep, severe boring pain, often bilateral, linked to PTB, RA, SLE, and can cause secondary glaucoma. Staphyloma = uveal-lined scleral ectasia (posterior type with high myopia). Blue sclera: osteogenesis imperfecta, Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos.

Cornea

Keratoconus (bilateral non-inflammatory conical thinning): Fleischer ring (iron at cone base, earliest), Munson's sign (V-lid on downgaze), scissors reflex on retinoscopy, Vogt's striae, oil-droplet reflex. Treat with RGP lens, crosslinking, or penetrating keratoplasty.

Opacity grades: nebula (faint) < macula (grey) < leukoma (dense white).

Iron lines (all epithelial) "HuFFeStO": Hudson-Stahli (aging, lower third), Fleischer (keratoconus), Ferry (next to bleb), Stocker (ahead of pterygium).

Pigment dispersion triad: Krukenberg spindle + iris transillumination defects + Sampaolesi line → pigmentary (open-angle) glaucoma.

Dry eye (KCS): aqueous deficiency (Sjogren's), mucin deficiency (vitamin A, Stevens-Johnson, trachoma), lipid deficiency (MGD). Tests: Schirmer <10 mm/5 min (aqueous), TBUT <10 sec (stability), rose bengal/lissamine (devitalized cells).

Arcus senilis: peripheral stromal cholesterol with a lucid interval; if young (arcus juvenilis), screen for hyperlipidemia. Band keratopathy: calcium in Bowman's (chronic uveitis, hypercalcemia); treat with EDTA chelation.

Herpes simplex keratitis: recurrent dendritic ulcer (terminal bulbs, stains with fluorescein and rose bengal); disciform = immune stromal edema. Treat with topical acyclovir/trifluridine; never topical steroids on epithelial disease. Herpes zoster ophthalmicus: V1 reactivation; Hutchinson's sign (nose-tip vesicles = nasociliary = high ocular risk).

Lens and Cataract

Ectopia lentis: Marfan = UP (superotemporal, FBN-1, aortic aneurysm), Homocystinuria = DOWN (inferonasal, thromboembolism risk), Weill-Marchesani = spherophakia.

Cataract types: nuclear sclerosis (second sight/myopic shift), cortical (spokes), posterior subcapsular (most common age-related, most disabling, steroid-linked, near worse than distance), diabetic (snowflake, progresses in 72 h), galactosemia (oil droplet, reversible), traumatic (rosette).

Maturation: immature→intumescent (phacomorphic risk)→mature→hypermature/Morgagnian (phacolytic risk)→after-cataract (treat YAG capsulotomy).

Surgery: ICCE (whole lens, no capsule) / ECCE (keep posterior capsule) / phacoemulsification (ultrasound, keep posterior capsule). Aphakia = +10 to +12 D hyperopia; IOL power from biometry.

Anterior Uveitis

Symptoms: photophobia, pain, blurred vision. Signs: ciliary flush, cells and flare, keratic precipitates (fine stellate = viral/Fuchs; mutton-fat = granulomatous sarcoid/TB/VKH), posterior synechiae, Busacca/Koeppe nodules, hypopyon (HLA-B27/Behcet, endophthalmitis).

Systemic links: HLA-B27 (ankylosing spondylitis, Reiter, psoriatic, IBD), sarcoidosis (mutton-fat, bilateral), JIA (chronic, ANA+, asymptomatic). Treat with topical steroid + cycloplegic (prevent synechiae).

Fuchs heterochromic cyclitis: chronic unilateral, lighter iris on affected side, fine KP, no posterior synechiae, causes cataract; does not respond to steroids.

Practice Questions

1. A red eye has a violet ring of injection around the cornea that does not blanch with epinephrine. This points to:

  • A. Allergic conjunctivitis
  • B. A deep process (uveitis, keratitis, or acute glaucoma)
  • C. Subconjunctival hemorrhage
  • D. Pinguecula
Answer

B. Ciliary (circumcorneal) flush reflects engorged deep perilimbal vessels, seen in uveitis, keratitis, or acute angle-closure. Surface conditions give bright, mobile conjunctival injection that blanches.

2. A green-gold ring is found in Descemet's membrane. The systemic disease is:

  • A. Hemochromatosis
  • B. Wilson's disease
  • C. Diabetes
  • D. Gout

B. Wilson's disease. The Kayser-Fleischer ring is copper deposition in Descemet's membrane in hepatolenticular degeneration.

E3 — Posterior Segment and Retina

The Simple Version

The posterior segment is the vitreous, retina, choroid, and optic nerve. The board hits five big themes:
  • Vitreoretinal interface: PVD and the three retinal detachments.
  • Vascular retina: diabetic and hypertensive retinopathy, and the artery/vein occlusions.
  • Macula: age-related macular degeneration.
  • Inherited retina: retinitis pigmentosa.
  • Optic nerve: papilledema vs optic neuritis, and the ischemic neuropathies.

Teaching angle: most posterior-segment items are recognized by a single signature image. Cherry-red spot (CRAO), blood-and-thunder fundus (CRVO), bone spicules (RP), cotton-wool spots (ischemia). Anchor on the picture, then reason out the cause.

But Why?

Why is there a cherry-red spot in CRAO?

The inner retina goes white and opaque when its artery is blocked, but the fovea is thin and fed by the choroid underneath, so the intact red choroid shows through the pale surroundings as a cherry-red dot. The spot is not diseased tissue; it is the one healthy window.

Why do diabetic vessels leak and then grow?

High glucose kills pericytes, so capillary walls weaken (microaneurysms, hemorrhages, exudates). As capillaries close, the retina starves, releases VEGF, and grows fragile new vessels (proliferative disease). Leak first, then grow, all driven by ischemia, which is why we destroy ischemic retina with laser to cut the VEGF signal.

Why does optic neuritis lose vision fast and hurt, while papilledema does not?

Optic neuritis is inflammation in the nerve, so the fibers stop conducting (early, severe vision loss) and the inflamed sheath hurts on eye movement. Papilledema is back-pressure from raised intracranial pressure pushing on the disc from behind; the fibers still conduct, so vision is spared early. One is a nerve problem, the other a plumbing problem.

Why must giant cell arteritis be treated before the biopsy result returns?

The inflamed arteries can occlude the second eye within days, causing permanent bilateral blindness. Steroids are started immediately on suspicion (raised ESR/CRP, jaw claudication, age >55); the biopsy can be done up to a couple of weeks later. You treat the risk, not the confirmation.

Diabetic Retinopathy

Retinal Vascular Occlusions

  • CRAO: sudden painless monocular loss, cherry-red spot, retinal whitening, RAPD. Within 4 to 6 h: ocular massage, lower IOP. Rule out giant cell arteritis. Emboli = Hollenhorst plaques.
  • CRVO: "blood and thunder" fundus (diffuse hemorrhages, dilated tortuous veins, disc edema). Ischemic (RAPD, neovascular glaucoma at 3 months) vs non-ischemic. Treat macular edema with anti-VEGF; PRP if ischemic.
  • BRAO: wedge whitening, Hollenhorst plaque at bifurcation. BRVO: sector hemorrhage at an AV crossing, superotemporal most common.

Retinopathy of Prematurity

Premature, low birth weight, supplemental oxygen. Plus disease (dilation + tortuosity) = active. Stage 5 = total RD with leukocoria. Screen <1500 g or <32 weeks. Treat with laser or anti-VEGF.

Retinitis Pigmentosa

Rod-cone dystrophy, rods first. Nyctalopia → ring scotoma → tunnel vision. Signs: bone-spicule pigment, attenuated arterioles, waxy pale disc. ERG: reduced/extinguished rod b-wave. AD (mildest), AR/X-linked (severe). Usher = RP + deafness.

Posterior Uveitis

  • Toxoplasmosis (most common worldwide): "headlight in fog," reactivation at edge of old scar. Treat pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid.
  • CMV retinitis (HIV, CD4 <50): "pizza pie" hemorrhage. Treat ganciclovir/foscarnet.
  • Toxocariasis (children, roundworm): granuloma, leukocoria.
  • VKH: panuveitis + vitiligo, poliosis, alopecia, meningismus. High-dose steroids.
  • Sympathetic ophthalmia: bilateral panuveitis after penetrating trauma; injured = exciting eye, fellow = sympathizing eye. Prevent by enucleating a blind injured eye within 10 to 14 days.

E4 — Ocular Emergencies

The Simple Version

An ocular emergency is a problem where minutes to hours decide whether the eye survives. The disease detail lives in E1 to E3; this note is about recognition and what to do first. Sort emergencies into three tiers:
  • Treat before you even finish the history: chemical burn (irrigate now).
  • Same-hour sight-or-life threats: acute angle-closure, CRAO, giant cell arteritis, open globe, endophthalmitis, orbital cellulitis.
  • Urgent, refer today: retinal detachment, hyphema, blow-out fracture, traumatic optic neuropathy.

Teaching angle: for each emergency, lock one trigger sign and one first action. The exam rewards the action, not just the name.

But Why?

Why is a chemical burn the only injury treated before history and exam?

Because the chemical keeps destroying tissue every second it sits on the eye. Irrigation removes the agent and is the single biggest factor in the outcome, so it cannot wait for paperwork. Everything else can pause for a two-minute history; a burning alkali cannot.

Why is alkali worse than acid?

Acid coagulates surface proteins, and that coagulum forms a barrier that limits how deep it goes. Alkali causes liquefactive necrosis: it dissolves cell membranes and drives deeper and deeper, into the stroma and anterior chamber. No barrier forms, so alkali keeps penetrating.

Why does acute angle-closure cause a hard, painful eye with a mid-dilated pupil?

The iris jams the drainage angle shut, aqueous cannot leave, and pressure spikes fast. The high pressure paralyzes the sphincter (mid-dilated, fixed pupil) and edemates the cornea (cloudy), while the stretched globe and nausea reflex cause the pain and vomiting. It is a plumbing blockage with a pressure explosion.

Why patch nothing and shield a suspected open globe?

Any pressure on a ruptured globe can extrude intraocular contents. So you place a rigid shield (not a pad), avoid pressure, give nothing by mouth (possible surgery), and never measure IOP or instill drops that require pressure.

Tier 1 — Treat Before History: Chemical Burn

  • The rule: irrigate immediately, before history and exam.
  • Alkali (ammonia, lye, lime): liquefactive necrosis, penetrates deep, worse. Acid: coagulative, self-limiting, except hydrofluoric acid and ammonia, which penetrate like alkali.
  • First actions: topical anesthetic for cooperation → copious irrigation (saline/Ringer's, 30+ min) → double-evert the upper lid to remove particles → check pH, irrigate until pH 7.0 to 7.4 → debride necrotic epithelium.
  • Roper-Hall grading is by corneal clarity and limbal ischemia (the prognostic key); grade IV = opaque cornea, >1/2 limbal ischemia, very poor.
  • Medical: ascorbate and citrate (collagen support, anticollagenase), tetracycline (collagenase inhibitor), topical steroid for the first 7 to 10 days then taper, cycloplegic, daily symblepharon lysis.

Tier 2 — Same-Hour Threats

Acute angle-closure glaucoma: sudden painful red eye, mid-dilated fixed pupil, cloudy cornea, halos, nausea/vomiting, rock-hard globe. First: lower IOP medically (topical beta blocker, alpha-2, pilocarpine to pull the iris open, oral/IV CAI or osmotic), then definitive laser peripheral iridotomy.

CRAO: sudden painless monocular loss, cherry-red spot, RAPD. Within 4 to 6 h: ocular massage, lower IOP (anterior chamber paracentesis, IV acetazolamide), consider rebreathing. Always exclude giant cell arteritis.

Giant cell arteritis (AAION): >55 y, headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, raised ESR/CRP, chalky-white swollen disc. Immediate high-dose corticosteroids before biopsy to save the second eye.

Open globe / penetrating injury: distorted pupil, shallow chamber, prolapsed uvea, positive Seidel. Shield (do not patch), nothing by mouth, no IOP, no pressure, systemic antibiotics, urgent surgery.

Endophthalmitis: post-surgical or post-trauma; pain, hypopyon, decreasing vision, vitritis. Intravitreal antibiotics (and vitreous tap); emergency.

Orbital cellulitis: proptosis, painful restricted motility, fever (vs the painless, full-motility preseptal). Most common cause ethmoiditis. IV antibiotics + drainage; CT to exclude abscess.

Tier 3 — Urgent, Refer Today

Retinal detachment: photopsia→floaters→curtain/shadow; tobacco dust signals a break. Refer for buckle/PPV. (Detail in E3-01-Posterior-Segment-and-Retina.)

Blow-out orbital floor fracture: blunt force, infraorbital nerve numbness, diplopia on up/down gaze, enophthalmos, orbital emphysema. Best imaging CT coronal. No nose blowing; surgery if >1/3 floor, persistent diplopia, or enophthalmos >2 mm (within ~2 weeks).

Hyphema: blood in the anterior chamber after blunt trauma. Risk: rebleed (days 3 to 5) and IOP rise. Rest, head elevation, shield, avoid aspirin; screen for sickle cell (worse outcomes). 8-ball = total hyphema.

Other blunt findings: Vossius ring (lens pigment imprint), traumatic mydriasis, iridodialysis (D-pupil), angle recession (late glaucoma), commotio retinae (gray retina, resolves), choroidal rupture.

Shaken baby syndrome: <2 y, bilateral retinal hemorrhages + subdural hemorrhage. Child abuse, mandatory reporting.

Radiation: UV photokeratitis (welding, snow blindness) heals in 24 to 48 h (cool compress, NSAID); infrared = glassblower's cataract.

Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue→rule→trap`.

Paired opposites — cue: tender/chronic, in/out, mild/severe.

  • Stye = tender/acute; chalazion = non-tender/chronic. Entropion = turns in (lashes scratch); ectropion = turns out (CN7). Episcleritis = mild; scleritis = boring pain.
  • Trap: ectropion "scratches the cornea" — that's entropion.

Signature fundus → diagnosis — cue: the described picture.

  • Cherry-red = CRAO; blood-and-thunder = CRVO; bone spicules = RP; macular star = HTN III.
  • Trap: "cherry-red spot is diseased tissue" — it's the spared choroid-fed fovea.

Glaucoma type — cue: open vs closed angle.

  • Open = silent, medical; closed = acute emergency, laser iridotomy. Phacolytic = open; phacomorphic = closed.
  • Trap: treating angle-closure medically first / calling phacolytic "closed-angle."

"Earliest" vs "most common/classic" — cue: the word "earliest".

  • Earliest DR sign = microaneurysm; earliest keratoconus = Fleischer ring; earliest chloroquine = ↓VA/color (bull's-eye is late).
  • Trap: picking the classic late sign when the stem says "earliest."

PH blindness vs low vision — cue: which one the stem asks.

  • Blindness → cataract leads. Low vision → refractive error leads.
  • Trap: using the blindness order for a low-vision question.
