## E3 — Posterior Segment and Retina
*Personal study notes | TOS: E Ocular Disease | Source: Ocular Disease, Dr. Elmo Ado + consolidated review*

---

## The Simple Version

The posterior segment is the vitreous, retina, choroid, and optic nerve. The board hits five big themes:

- **Vitreoretinal interface:** PVD and the three retinal detachments.
- **Vascular retina:** diabetic and hypertensive retinopathy, and the artery/vein occlusions.
- **Macula:** age-related macular degeneration.
- **Inherited retina:** retinitis pigmentosa.
- **Optic nerve:** papilledema vs optic neuritis, and the ischemic neuropathies.

**Teaching angle:** most posterior-segment items are recognized by a single signature image. Cherry-red spot (CRAO), blood-and-thunder fundus (CRVO), bone spicules (RP), cotton-wool spots (ischemia). Anchor on the picture, then reason out the cause.

---

## But Why?

**Why is there a cherry-red spot in CRAO?**
The inner retina goes white and opaque when its artery is blocked, but the **fovea is thin and fed by the choroid underneath**, so the intact red choroid shows through the pale surroundings as a cherry-red dot. The spot is not diseased tissue; it is the one healthy window.

**Why do diabetic vessels leak and then grow?**
High glucose kills pericytes, so capillary walls weaken (microaneurysms, hemorrhages, exudates). As capillaries close, the retina starves, releases VEGF, and grows fragile new vessels (proliferative disease). Leak first, then grow, all driven by ischemia, which is why we destroy ischemic retina with laser to cut the VEGF signal.

**Why does optic neuritis lose vision fast and hurt, while papilledema does not?**
Optic neuritis is inflammation **in** the nerve, so the fibers stop conducting (early, severe vision loss) and the inflamed sheath hurts on eye movement. Papilledema is back-pressure from raised intracranial pressure pushing on the disc from behind; the fibers still conduct, so vision is spared early. One is a nerve problem, the other a plumbing problem.

**Why must giant cell arteritis be treated before the biopsy result returns?**
The inflamed arteries can occlude the second eye within days, causing permanent bilateral blindness. Steroids are started immediately on suspicion (raised ESR/CRP, jaw claudication, age >55); the biopsy can be done up to a couple of weeks later. You treat the risk, not the confirmation.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| PVD | posterior vitreous detachment | Age-related vitreous separation; floaters + Weiss ring |
| Shafer's sign | "tobacco dust" | Pigment in anterior vitreous = retinal break until proven otherwise |
| Rhegmatogenous | Greek *rhegma* = break/rent | Retinal detachment caused by a full-thickness break |
| Microaneurysm | *micro* + *aneurysma* = dilation | Earliest visible sign of diabetic retinopathy |
| Cotton-wool spot | nerve fibre layer infarct | Ischemic swelling of axons, not an exudate |
| IRMA | intraretinal microvascular abnormality | Shunt vessels in ischemic retina; severe NPDR marker |
| Drusen | German = nodule | Sub-RPE deposits; hallmark of dry ARMD |
| Metamorphopsia | *meta* = change + *morphe* = shape | Wavy distortion on Amsler grid; early CNV warning |
| Nyctalopia | Greek *nyx* = night + *alaos* = blind | Night blindness; first symptom of retinitis pigmentosa |
| Bone-spicule pigment | perivascular pigment migration | Classic mid-peripheral sign of retinitis pigmentosa |
| Papilledema | *papilla* + *oidema* = swelling | Bilateral disc swelling from raised intracranial pressure |
| RAPD | relative afferent pupillary defect | Marcus Gunn pupil; optic nerve or massive retinal disease |

### Vitreous and Retinal Detachment

**PVD:** age-related vitreous liquefaction and separation. Floaters + photopsia; **Weiss ring** = detached peripapillary ring. **Tobacco dust (Shafer's sign)** = pigment in anterior vitreous = retinal break until proven otherwise.

**Retinal detachment (three types):**

| Type | Mechanism | Clue | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rhegmatogenous** | A break lets fluid under the retina | Horseshoe tear; photopsia + floaters + curtain | Scleral buckle or PPV + gas |
| **Tractional** | Fibrous bands pull the retina | Diabetes, ROP, sickle; taut, no break | PPV |
| **Exudative** | Fluid from choroid, no break | Shifting fluid; VKH, malignant HTN | Treat the cause |

Breaks: **horseshoe tear** (traction, highest RD risk), round hole (atrophic, low risk), dialysis (ora serrata, young trauma). Progression: flashes → floaters → curtain.

### Diabetic Retinopathy

- Type 1: appears 5 to 7 years after onset. Type 2: may be present at diagnosis.
- **NPDR signs:** microaneurysm (earliest), dot-blot hemorrhage, hard exudate (lipid), cotton-wool spot (NFL infarct), IRMA, venous beading.
- **Severe NPDR "4-2-1 rule":** hemorrhages in all 4 quadrants, OR venous beading in 2, OR IRMA in 1.
- **PDR:** new vessels (NVD/NVE). Risk: vitreous hemorrhage, tractional RD, neovascular glaucoma. Treat with **panretinal photocoagulation** + anti-VEGF.
- **Diabetic macular edema:** can occur at any stage; CSME within 500 µm of fovea. First-line: intravitreal anti-VEGF.

### Hypertensive Retinopathy (Keith-Wagener-Barker)

| Grade | Finding |
|---|---|
| I | Arteriolar narrowing, copper wire |
| II | AV nicking (Gunn/Salus), silver wire |
| III | Flame hemorrhages, cotton-wool spots, **macular star** |
| IV | Grade III + papilledema = **malignant hypertension** |

Macular star = hard exudates in Henle's layer. Elschnig's spots = choroidal infarcts.

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration

| Type | Features | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Dry (atrophic)** | Drusen, geographic atrophy; 85 to 90% | AREDS2 vitamins |
| **Wet (neovascular)** | Choroidal neovascularization, rapid loss | **Intravitreal anti-VEGF** |

Soft confluent drusen = higher conversion risk. **Amsler grid metamorphopsia** = early CNV warning.

### Retinal Vascular Occlusions

- **CRAO:** sudden painless monocular loss, **cherry-red spot**, retinal whitening, RAPD. Within 4 to 6 h: ocular massage, lower IOP. **Rule out giant cell arteritis.** Emboli = Hollenhorst plaques.
- **CRVO:** "**blood and thunder**" fundus (diffuse hemorrhages, dilated tortuous veins, disc edema). Ischemic (RAPD, neovascular glaucoma at **3 months**) vs non-ischemic. Treat macular edema with anti-VEGF; PRP if ischemic.
- **BRAO:** wedge whitening, Hollenhorst plaque at bifurcation. **BRVO:** sector hemorrhage at an AV crossing, superotemporal most common.

### Retinopathy of Prematurity

Premature, low birth weight, supplemental oxygen. **Plus disease** (dilation + tortuosity) = active. **Stage 5** = total RD with leukocoria. Screen <1500 g or <32 weeks. Treat with laser or anti-VEGF.

### Retinitis Pigmentosa

Rod-cone dystrophy, **rods first**. Nyctalopia → ring scotoma → tunnel vision. Signs: **bone-spicule pigment**, attenuated arterioles, **waxy pale disc**. ERG: reduced/extinguished rod b-wave. AD (mildest), AR/X-linked (severe). Usher = RP + deafness.

### Posterior Uveitis

- **Toxoplasmosis** (most common worldwide): "**headlight in fog**," reactivation at edge of old scar. Treat pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid.
- **CMV retinitis** (HIV, CD4 <50): "**pizza pie**" hemorrhage. Treat ganciclovir/foscarnet.
- **Toxocariasis** (children, roundworm): granuloma, leukocoria.
- **VKH:** panuveitis + vitiligo, poliosis, alopecia, meningismus. High-dose steroids.
- **Sympathetic ophthalmia:** bilateral panuveitis after penetrating trauma; injured = **exciting eye**, fellow = **sympathizing eye**. Prevent by enucleating a blind injured eye within **10 to 14 days**.

### Optic Nerve

**Papilledema vs optic neuritis (papillitis):**

| Feature | Papilledema | Optic neuritis |
|---|---|---|
| Laterality | Bilateral | Unilateral |
| Vision loss | Late (blind spot enlarges) | Early, severe |
| Pain | No | Yes (on movement) |
| RAPD | Absent | Present |
| ICP | Raised | Normal |

- **Optic neuritis:** young women, RAPD, central scotoma, red desaturation. Linked to **multiple sclerosis** (first sign in 25%). IV methylprednisolone speeds recovery (ONTT).
- **NAION:** painless, on waking, **altitudinal** defect, "disc at risk" (small cup), vascular risk factors.
- **AAION / giant cell arteritis:** >55 y, headache, **jaw claudication**, raised **ESR/CRP**, chalky-white disc. **Emergency: immediate high-dose steroids before biopsy.**
- **Glaucomatous:** cupping, **ISNT rule violated**, notching, Drance hemorrhage.
- **Traumatic optic neuropathy:** RAPD after trauma, optic canal fracture on CT.

## Memory Anchors

- **Signature pictures:** cherry-red = CRAO; blood-and-thunder = CRVO; bone spicules = RP; macular star = HTN grade III; headlight in fog = toxoplasmosis; pizza pie = CMV.
- **Diabetic order:** leak (microaneurysm) → starve (cotton wool, IRMA) → grow (NVD/NVE). 4-2-1 = severe.
- **PVD floaters + tobacco dust = look for a retinal break.**
- **Optic neuritis: unilateral, painful, RAPD, MS. Papilledema: bilateral, painless, raised ICP.**
- **GCA = treat first, biopsy later.**
- **RP: rods first = night blindness first.**

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** the cherry-red spot is diseased tissue. Wrong. It is the **healthy** choroid-fed fovea showing through pale ischemic retina (CRAO; also Tay-Sachs).
- **Trap:** papilledema causes early vision loss. Wrong. Vision is spared early (enlarged blind spot); **optic neuritis** loses vision early.
- **Trap:** in giant cell arteritis, wait for biopsy before steroids. Dangerous. Start steroids immediately to protect the second eye.
- **Trap:** cotton-wool spots are exudates. No. They are **nerve fiber layer infarcts** (ischemia); hard exudates are lipid.
- **Trap:** tractional RD has a retinal break. Wrong. Only **rhegmatogenous** has a break; tractional and exudative do not.
- **Trap:** wet ARMD is the common type. Wrong. **Dry** is 85 to 90%; wet is the aggressive minority treated with anti-VEGF.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Sudden painless loss of vision in one eye shows a pale retina with a cherry-red fovea and an RAPD. The diagnosis and first step:

- A. CRVO; observe
- B. CRAO; ocular massage and lower IOP, rule out GCA
- C. Optic neuritis; IV steroids
- D. Retinal detachment; surgery

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. CRAO.** The pale retina with cherry-red fovea is classic. Attempt ocular massage and IOP lowering within the window, and exclude giant cell arteritis.
</details>

**2.** A diabetic has hemorrhages in all four quadrants and venous beading in two quadrants, but no new vessels. This is:

- A. Mild NPDR
- B. Moderate NPDR
- C. Severe NPDR (4-2-1)
- D. Proliferative DR

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Severe NPDR.** The 4-2-1 rule is met (4-quadrant hemorrhage and 2-quadrant venous beading). New vessels would make it proliferative.
</details>

**3.** A 30-year-old woman has unilateral vision loss over days, pain on eye movement, red desaturation, and an RAPD. The key systemic association is:

- A. Diabetes
- B. Multiple sclerosis
- C. Hypertension
- D. Giant cell arteritis

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Multiple sclerosis.** This is optic neuritis, the first presentation of MS in about 25% of patients. GCA affects older patients and is painless arteritic ischemia.
</details>

**4.** A 72-year-old man has sudden vision loss, jaw pain when chewing, scalp tenderness, and ESR of 80. The immediate action is:

- A. Observe and repeat ESR
- B. Temporal artery biopsy, then treat
- C. Start high-dose corticosteroids immediately
- D. Anti-VEGF injection

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Start high-dose corticosteroids immediately.** Giant cell arteritis can blind the second eye within days; treat on suspicion and biopsy afterward.
</details>

**5.** Night blindness, bone-spicule pigmentation, and a waxy pale disc with an extinguished rod ERG describe:

- A. Diabetic retinopathy
- B. Retinitis pigmentosa
- C. Age-related macular degeneration
- D. CRVO

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Retinitis pigmentosa.** Rod-first dystrophy: nyctalopia, bone spicules, attenuated vessels, waxy disc, reduced rod b-wave.
</details>
