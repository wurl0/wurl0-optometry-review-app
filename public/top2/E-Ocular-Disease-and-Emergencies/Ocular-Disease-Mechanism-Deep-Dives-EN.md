## Gullstrand Baseline (Reference Card)

Gullstrand's schematic eye is the benchmark. When disease occurs, one or two of these constants shift — and that shift creates every sign and symptom downstream.

| Parameter | Normal Value |
|---|---|
| Cornea front radius | 7.7 mm |
| Cornea back radius | 6.8 mm |
| Net corneal power | ~43.05 D (2/3 of total) |
| Corneal thickness | 0.5 mm |
| Corneal refractive index (n) | 1.376 |
| ACD (corneal apex to lens front) | 3.6 mm |
| Lens front radius | 10.0 mm |
| Lens back radius | 6.0 mm |
| Lens power (unaccommodated) | ~19.11 D (1/3 of total) |
| Lens cortex n | 1.386 |
| Lens nucleus n | 1.406 |
| Aqueous / vitreous n | 1.336 |
| Total eye power | ~58.64 D |
| Secondary focal point F₂ | 24.38 mm (on retina = emmetropia) |
| Primary focal length | 15.70 mm |

**Core rule:** If the image falls in front of the retina — myopia. If behind — hyperopia. Any ocular disease that changes the shape, position, or refractive index of any structure moves the focal point. That shift is where all refractive consequences begin.

## 1. Keratoconus

### What Changes in Gullstrand?
Normal cornea front radius: **7.7 mm**. In keratoconus, the central cornea thins and bulges forward. The radius decreases — a tighter curve, a steeper surface.

Steeper radius = higher local power (F = (n₂ − n₁) / R).

But the change is not uniform. Only one focal zone of the cornea bulges. This means the surface is **irregular** — unlike regular astigmatism, which a cylinder lens can correct. Two focal points form in the same eye, and neither sits cleanly on the retina.

### Why It Happens

Think of a balloon with a weak spot. When pressure pushes from inside, that weak spot stretches forward while the rest stays back. The corneal stroma in keratoconus has defective collagen crosslinks and abnormal proteoglycan breakdown. The structural support fails at one zone, and normal intraocular pressure continuously pushes that zone outward — forming a cone.

The cone tip has a smaller radius of curvature than 7.7 mm — higher local power at the apex than the rest of the cornea. Different meridians now have wildly different powers. The result: **progressive myopia and irregular astigmatism**.

### Cascade of Clinical Signs

| Gullstrand change | Consequence | Clinical sign |
|---|---|---|
| Corneal radius ↓ at cone apex | Higher local power at the tip | Increasing myopia at each follow-up visit |
| Non-uniform radius change across meridians | Irregular astigmatism — no two meridians are equal cylinders | **Scissors reflex** on retinoscopy |
| Cone protrudes anteriorly | Lower lid mechanically indented by the cone on downgaze | **Munson's sign** (V-shaped lid deformity on downgaze) |
| Epithelium slides repetitively over an irregular base | Hemosiderin deposits accumulate at the base of the cone in the epithelium | **Fleischer ring** (iron, epithelial layer) — earliest sign |
| Bowman's layer under tension from cone | Vertical stress lines in the stroma visible on slit lamp | **Vogt's striae** |

**Why soft contact lenses fail:** A soft lens conforms to the irregular corneal surface — it takes the shape of the cone. No correction is gained. A **rigid GP lens** carries its own anterior surface, replacing the irregular cornea with a smooth refracting surface.

**Board hook:** Fleischer ring is the **earliest sign** of keratoconus. It is an iron deposit in the **epithelial layer only** — not stroma, not Bowman's, not Descemet's.

## 2. Glaucoma — POAG

### What Changes in Gullstrand?
No direct Gullstrand optical parameter changes in POAG. The problem is **aqueous dynamics and optic nerve pressure tolerance**, not refraction. Use the fluid dynamics framework here.

### Why It Happens

Picture the eye as a sink. Aqueous humor is the water. The ciliary body is the tap. The trabecular meshwork is the drain.

Normal eye: production rate = drainage rate. Pressure stays between 10 and 21 mmHg.

POAG: the drain clogs slowly. Not suddenly — gradually. The ciliary body keeps making aqueous. Outflow decreases. Pressure climbs over months to years.

The optic nerve head sits at the weakest part of the sclera — the **lamina cribrosa** (a sieve-like plate with holes for nerve fiber bundles). As IOP rises, the lamina is compressed backward. The nerve fiber bundles passing through are squeezed and begin to die.

The death is not uniform. **Superior and inferior arcuate fibers** are the most vulnerable — these originate from the upper and lower poles of the optic disc. So the first visual field loss in POAG is **arcuate scotoma and nasal step**.

### Cascade of Clinical Signs

| Mechanism | Clinical Sign |
|---|---|
| TM blockage → IOP ↑ | Tonometry: IOP >21 mmHg (note: 1/3 of POAG patients have normal IOP — normal tension glaucoma) |
| IOP compresses lamina cribrosa → disc cupping | C/D ratio >0.6; asymmetric C/D (>0.2 difference between eyes) |
| Superior/inferior arcuate fibers die first | Arcuate scotoma → nasal step on perimetry |
| Total arcuate loss → tunnel vision | Only central island remains in advanced POAG |
| RNFL progressively thins | OCT shows RNFL defects — worst in the superior quadrant |
| Vessels bend at the margin of the excavating disc | Bayonetting of disc vessels; nasal shift |

**Why there is no pain in POAG:** The IOP rise is slow — the eye adjusts. No acute pressure spike triggers the pain-nausea response seen in PACG.

**Board hook:** "Silent thief of sight." Up to 90% of optic nerve fibers may already be lost before a patient notices a visual field deficit.

## 3. Glaucoma — PACG

### What Changes in Gullstrand?
ACD (normal: **3.6 mm**) decreases. In PACG, the anterior chamber is shallower than average — anatomically predisposed. Hyperopic eyes with a short axial length, a thicker lens, and a shallow ACD are most at risk.

### Why It Happens

Think of a drain in a narrow corner. Even before the drain clogs, something can block the path to the drain.

In PACG, the iris physically blocks the trabecular meshwork — not trabecular disease, but **physical closure of the drainage angle by the iris**.

**Pupillary block mechanism (most common):**
- Aqueous from the ciliary body must pass through the pupil into the anterior chamber, then drain via the trabecular meshwork
- When the pupil is mid-dilated (in dim light, emotional arousal, or certain drugs), the iris touches the anterior lens surface without fully breaking contact
- Aqueous is trapped in the posterior chamber — it cannot pass through the pupil
- Posterior chamber pressure builds and bows the iris forward (like a sail catching wind)
- The bowed iris closes the angle — **iris bombe posture**
- Mid-dilation is the worst position: fully dilated iris separates cleanly from the lens; fully constricted iris is too thin to block. Mid-dilation creates the tightest contact.

**Plateau iris mechanism:**
- A large ciliary body pushes the peripheral iris forward, creating a shallow angle structurally — angle closes on dilation even without pupillary block

### Cascade of Clinical Signs

| Mechanism | Clinical Sign |
|---|---|
| Acute angle closure → IOP spikes (may reach 60–70 mmHg) | Severe ocular pain; headache |
| Very high IOP overwhelms the corneal endothelial pump | Corneal edema — cloudy, steamy cornea |
| Edematous cornea refracts light irregularly | Halos around lights (iridescent vision) |
| IOP rise → ciliary spasm + vagal stimulation | Nausea and vomiting |
| Iris sphincter ischemic at very high IOP — pupil stuck | Fixed, mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil |
| Anterior uveal congestion | Ciliary flush (circumcorneal injection — violet-red ring) |

**Iris bombe vs rubeosis iridis:**
- **Iris bombe:** Aqueous cannot pass the pupil → pushes iris forward → closed angle **WITH pupillary block**
- **Rubeosis iridis:** New vessels grow directly over the trabecular meshwork angle → closed angle **WITHOUT pupillary block** (fluid moves freely through the pupil; the block is at the TM level)

**Board hook:** Dark room test — patient sits in darkness for 1 hour → pupil dilates → angle narrows → IOP rises >8 mmHg = positive for PACG.

## 4. Cataract — Mechanism per Type

### What Changes in Gullstrand?
Lens constants change. Three optical pathways:
1. **Refractive index n increases** → lens power increases → acquired myopia
2. **Radius of curvature decreases** → lens swells → steeper → phacomorphic glaucoma risk
3. **Lens transparency decreases** → light scatter → blur regardless of optical power change

---

### Nuclear Sclerosis Cataract

**Why it happens:**
Lens fibers are continuously produced at the equator and pushed inward. Old fibers are never discarded — they are compacted into the nucleus over decades. As they compress, their density rises and the **refractive index of the nucleus increases** beyond the normal value of 1.406.

Higher n at the same radius = higher lens power (from the lensmaker's equation). The natural ~19.11 D of the unaccommodated lens increases — the lens acts as if it is accommodating even when relaxed.

**The "Second Sight" (Senopia) paradox:**
- Acquired myopia from elevated lens power shifts the near point closer
- A patient who previously needed reading glasses suddenly reads without them — not because vision improved, but because the focal point moved
- This is a clinical sign of advancing nuclear cataract, not recovery

**Board hook:** Second sight = nuclear sclerosis = **increasing myopia** = Gullstrand's lens nucleus n↑.

---

### Diabetic Cataract (Snowflake)

**Why it happens:**
In prolonged hyperglycemia, excess glucose enters the lens. Inside the lens cells, **aldose reductase** converts glucose to **sorbitol**. Sorbitol cannot exit lens cells (it is a polar molecule, blocked by the lipid membrane).

Sorbitol accumulates inside lens fibers → osmotic pressure builds → water is drawn in to dilute it → lens fibers swell and collapse → **snowflake opacity** (minute anterior and posterior subcapsular dots).

**Why it is bilateral and progresses in 72 hours:**
Both eyes share the same systemic glucose environment. A diabetic crisis exposes both lenses simultaneously. The osmotic process reaches visible opacity within 72 hours of severe hyperglycemia.

**Board hook:** Diabetic cataract = bilateral, snowflake, anterior + posterior subcapsular, progresses in 72 hours.

---

### Hypermature Cataract → Phacolytic Glaucoma

**Why it happens:**
In a hypermature (Morgagnian) cataract, the cortex liquefies and the nucleus sinks to the bottom of the capsular bag. The liquefied cortical proteins slowly leak through microscopic pores in the intact lens capsule into the anterior chamber.

These leaked proteins are large molecules. Macrophages in the aqueous engulf them — and the **macrophage-protein complexes physically clog the trabecular meshwork**. Aqueous cannot drain → IOP rises.

Critically: the drainage angle is **open** — the block is inside the trabecular meshwork, not at the angle level. This is **open-angle secondary glaucoma**.

---

### Phacomorphic Glaucoma (Intumescent Lens)

**Why it happens:**
Normal lens thickness: ~3.6 mm (matching the ACD of 3.6 mm — a physical coincidence that matters here). In an intumescent cataract, fluid clefts open inside the lens cortex and the lens swells. Thickness increases beyond 3.6 mm.

The thicker lens pushes the iris-lens diaphragm forward → **ACD decreases** → the peripheral iris crowds against the trabecular meshwork → angle narrows and closes → **closed-angle secondary glaucoma with pupillary block element**.

**Key distinction:**
- Phacolytic → hypermature lens, leaking protein → **open-angle** (TM blocked by debris)
- Phacomorphic → swollen lens, mechanical push → **closed-angle** (iris pushed against TM)

## 5. Corneal Edema

### What Changes in Gullstrand?
Corneal thickness: normal **0.5 mm**. Corneal n: **1.376**. In edema, water enters the stroma and thickness increases. The collagen lamellae, which must be precisely spaced to remain transparent, are pushed apart.

### Why It Happens

Corneal transparency depends on two things:
1. **Regular spacing of collagen fibrils** — when spacing is less than the wavelength of light, light passes through without scattering
2. **Endothelial pump function** — the endothelium (a single-cell layer) actively pumps water out of the stroma, keeping it at ~78% water content (relative dehydration). When the pump is overwhelmed — by very high IOP (acute PACG), endothelial disease (Fuchs dystrophy), trauma, or contact lens hypoxia — water floods in, collagen spacing becomes irregular, and light scatters in all directions.

**Why PACG and Fuchs cause the same corneal appearance:**
- PACG: IOP crushes the endothelial pump → edema. Temporary if IOP is lowered.
- Fuchs dystrophy: pump fails intrinsically → edema. Permanent; requires keratoplasty.

**Board hook:** Corneal edema always begins in the epithelium first (bedewing), then stroma (Descemet's folds), then bullae (bullous keratopathy) in severe cases.

## 6. Ectopia Lentis — Marfan vs Homocystinuria

### What Changes in Gullstrand?
Normal lens: centered on the optical axis, 3.6 mm behind the corneal apex. In subluxation, the lens shifts off the optical axis. One portion of the pupil has the lens in front of it (lenticular zone — with full lens power contribution). Another portion is aphakic (no lens contribution). Two different focal systems exist in the same eye → **monocular diplopia and lenticular astigmatism**.

### Why Marfan Goes Up, Homocystinuria Goes Down

**Marfan syndrome:**
- Zonular fibers are made of **fibrillin**, a connective tissue protein
- Mutation in **FBN-1 (chromosome 15)** → defective fibrillin → zonular fibers are weak
- The inferior zonules bear the most gravitational and tensile load — they fail first
- Without inferior zonular support, the lens shifts **superotemporal** (upward — gravity wins at the top; nothing holds the bottom)

**Homocystinuria:**
- The problem is not fibrillin — it is **cystathionine beta-synthase deficiency**
- Without this enzyme, homocysteine accumulates systemically. Homocysteine is toxic to collagen crosslinks — including zonular fiber collagen
- The superior zonules are more affected → lens falls **inferonasal** (downward)

**Memory anchor:** Marfan = UP. Homocystinuria = DOWN.

## 7. Retinal Detachment — Three Types

### Background: Why the Retina Stays in Place

The retina is not permanently bonded to the RPE — it is held in contact by:
1. **Vitreous pressure** pushing the retina against the RPE
2. **RPE pump** actively removing subretinal fluid to keep the space dry
3. **Interdigitation** of photoreceptor outer segments with RPE microvilli

When any of these fail — or when a break develops — the retina lifts.

---

### Rhegmatogenous RD ("rhegma" = break)

**Why it happens:**
1. Vitreous liquefies in the center (normal aging, myopia, trauma) → cortical vitreous peels away from the retina (posterior vitreous detachment — PVD)
2. At zones of strong vitreoretinal adhesion (lattice degeneration, retinal tufts), the peeling vitreous tugs the retina → **retinal tear or horseshoe break**
3. Liquid vitreous seeps through the tear into the subretinal space → RPE pump cannot keep up → fluid spreads under the retina → detachment

**Key sign:** Tobacco dust (Shafer's sign) = RPE pigment cells floating in the anterior vitreous = retinal break until proven otherwise. Emergency referral.

---

### Tractional RD

**Why it happens:**
In proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR), the ischemic retina releases **VEGF**, signaling for new blood vessel growth. These new vessels are abnormal — they grow out of the retinal surface and into the vitreous body, accompanied by fibrous scaffolding.

As the fibrous tissue matures, it contracts — like a scar pulling tight. The contraction pulls the retinal surface **upward**, away from the RPE, without creating a break.

**Key distinction from rhegmatogenous:** No subretinal fluid level that shifts with head position. Taut, tent-like surface. No break on examination.

---

### Exudative (Serous) RD

**Why it happens:**
The RPE pump keeps the subretinal space dry. When choroidal fluid production overwhelms the pump — from malignant hypertension, choroidal tumor, posterior scleritis, or VKH — fluid collects under the retina from below, not from a break above.

**No break. No traction.** The retina stays attached at several points but bulges with fluid underneath. As head position changes, the fluid shifts (gravity) and the detachment area moves — **shifting fluid sign** is pathognomonic for exudative RD.

---

## 8. Diabetic Retinopathy — The VEGF Cycle

### Root Cause: Pericyte Loss

Everything in diabetic retinopathy begins with **pericyte death**. Pericytes are support cells wrapped around retinal capillaries — they regulate blood flow and maintain the inner blood-retinal barrier.

In prolonged hyperglycemia:
1. Excess glucose → aldose reductase → **sorbitol** accumulates in pericytes → toxic → pericyte death
2. Without pericytes, capillary walls weaken → **microaneurysms** (first sign of DR) → plasma proteins leak → **hard exudates**
3. Capillaries close progressively → **capillary non-perfusion zones** → surrounding retinal cells become ischemic
4. Ischemic retina releases **VEGF** (vascular endothelial growth factor) as a distress signal
5. VEGF drives abnormal new vessel growth (neovascularization) — fragile, leaky vessels prone to hemorrhage
6. Vitreous hemorrhage → fibrous proliferation → tractional RD → **end-stage blindness**

**Why almost all advanced DR treatment targets VEGF:** VEGF is the single driver behind DME, PDR, rubeosis iridis, and neovascular glaucoma. Cut VEGF, and you cut the cascade at the source.

### The 4-2-1 Rule (Severe NPDR Threshold)

This is the boundary where the ischemic load is so high that VEGF-driven PDR is imminent:
- **4 quadrants** with ≥20 dot/blot hemorrhages, **OR**
- **2 quadrants** with venous beading, **OR**
- **1 quadrant** with IRMA (intraretinal microvascular abnormalities)

Meeting any one criterion = Severe NPDR. One more step = PDR.

## 9. CRAO — Why the Cherry-Red Spot

### Why the Fovea Stays Red

The central retinal artery supplies the **inner retinal layers** (nerve fiber layer down to the inner nuclear layer). The **outer retinal layers** (photoreceptors, RPE) are fed by the **choroidal circulation** — an entirely separate system.

In CRAO:
- The CRA is occluded (embolus, thrombosis, vasospasm)
- Inner retina loses its blood supply → ischemia → **cytotoxic edema** (cells swell with fluid) → the inner retina turns white and opaque
- The entire posterior pole appears diffusely white from this ischemic swelling

**At the fovea specifically:** The fovea has a structural pit (foveal pit). The inner retinal layers — which are white and swollen — are absent or very thin at the foveal center. The choroid behind the fovea is still perfused and visible through the thin foveal tissue → the fovea appears **red against the surrounding white ischemic retina**.

This red spot against a white background = **cherry-red spot**.

**Same mechanism in storage diseases:** In Tay-Sachs and Niemann-Pick disease, abnormal material deposits in ganglion cells (inner retina) → those cells turn pale/white. The fovea, lacking ganglion cells at its center, remains red. Same cherry-red spot, different cause.

## 10. Uveitis — Why the Signs Look the Way They Do

### Background: The Blood-Ocular Barrier

Two barriers protect the inside of the eye:
1. **Blood-aqueous barrier** (ciliary body and iris vessels) — prevents proteins and cells from entering the anterior chamber
2. **Blood-retinal barrier** (inner: retinal vessels; outer: RPE) — prevents leakage into the vitreous and subretinal space

Uveitis breaks down these barriers. Vascular permeability increases → proteins and white blood cells pour through.

### Why There Are Cells and Flare

**Cells:** White blood cells that cross the broken blood-aqueous barrier and float in the aqueous humor. Cell type identifies the etiology:
- Neutrophils (PMN) → acute bacterial inflammation
- Lymphocytes → viral or chronic inflammation
- Eosinophils → allergic or parasitic

**Flare:** Protein (mainly albumin) that leaks into the aqueous. When the slit-lamp beam crosses the anterior chamber, scattered light becomes visible — just like a flashlight beam through smoke. This is the **Tyndall effect in the aqueous**. More protein = brighter flare.

### Why KPs Collect in Arlt's Triangle

The endothelium is constantly exposed to aqueous convection currents. Warmer aqueous rises in the center of the anterior chamber and cooler aqueous descends at the periphery (thermal convection pattern). WBCs and macrophages flowing with the aqueous settle at the inferior corneal endothelium as they descend.

The result: keratic precipitates (KPs) cluster in the **inferior 1/3 of the cornea**, forming a triangular distribution — **Arlt's triangle**.

**Fine (stellate) KPs** = small lymphocytes = non-granulomatous uveitis (viral, Fuchs heterochromic cyclitis)
**Mutton-fat KPs** = large macrophages and epithelioid cells = **granulomatous uveitis** (sarcoidosis, TB, VKH, leprosy)

### Why Posterior Synechiae Form and Why They Lead to Iris Bombe

Inflammatory mediators → iris anterior surface becomes inflamed → touches the anterior lens capsule during incomplete dilation → the contact becomes fibrotic = **posterior synechiae**.

When synechiae extend 360° around the pupil (seclusio pupillae) → aqueous is completely blocked from passing through the pupil → pressure builds in the posterior chamber → iris bows forward = **iris bombe** → secondary closed-angle glaucoma.

## 11. Chemical Burns — Why Alkali Is More Dangerous

### The Chemistry of Tissue Damage

**Acid burns:**
- Acids cause **coagulation necrosis** — surface proteins are denatured and clump together
- The coagulated protein layer acts as a **physical barrier** that slows further penetration
- Self-limiting — the acid consumes itself building the barrier
- Exception: hydrofluoric acid penetrates deeply (like alkali); sulphuric acid causes thermal damage from exothermic reaction

**Alkali burns:**
- Alkalis (NaOH, ammonia, lime) cause **liquefaction necrosis** — tissue proteins dissolve
- No protective coagulum forms
- Alkali penetrates freely: corneal epithelium → Bowman's layer → stroma → Descemet's membrane → endothelium → anterior chamber → lens → ciliary body
- Ciliary body damage matters because it produces **ascorbate** — a substrate required for corneal stromal healing. When the ciliary body is destroyed, corneal repair becomes impaired.

**Gullstrand connection:** In severe alkali burns, structural protein denaturation changes corneal architecture — but the clinical emergency is limbal stem cell destruction and corneal perforation risk, not refraction.

### Why Irrigation Is the Most Important Treatment

You cannot know the depth of injury at first contact — but every additional second of alkali contact deepens the injury. **Copious, immediate irrigation** dilutes the alkali and normalizes pH. Speed and volume of irrigation directly determine the long-term outcome.

**Board hook:** Chemical burn is the only ocular emergency that requires treatment **before** taking a full history or complete examination. Irrigate first. Everything else waits.

## 12. Sympathetic Ophthalmia — Why the Fellow Eye Is Affected

### Why It Happens

The eye is an **immune-privileged site** — the blood-ocular barrier keeps uveal antigens (melanin, melanin-binding proteins, S-antigen from photoreceptors) hidden from the immune system throughout life. The immune system has never encountered these antigens and does not recognize them as "self."

After penetrating trauma to one eye (the **exciting eye**):
1. Uveal antigens are exposed to the systemic immune system for the first time
2. T-lymphocytes become sensitized to these antigens — recognizing them as foreign
3. Sensitized T-cells circulate through the bloodstream
4. Because **both eyes share identical uveal antigens**, the immune system attacks the **fellow eye** (the **sympathizing eye**) as well
5. Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis develops

**Why the 10–14 day window for enucleation:**
This is the approximate time for T-cell sensitization and trafficking to the fellow eye to initiate damage. Enucleation of the exciting eye within this window may prevent sympathetic ophthalmia. After this window, the sensitized T-cells are already active — removing the exciting eye no longer stops the process.

## 13. Trachoma — Why Pannus Forms at the Upper Limbus

### Why Chlamydia Affects the Upper Tarsal Conjunctiva

*Chlamydia trachomatis* is an obligate intracellular pathogen. It targets epithelial cells with high turnover. The **upper tarsal conjunctiva** is one of the densest lymphoid and epithelial areas of the ocular surface. Infectious material (from discharge, contaminated hands, flies) contacts the superior fornix most directly during hand-to-eye transmission. This is why follicular response begins in the upper tarsal plate (Stage I and II trachoma).

### Why Pannus Starts at the Superior Limbus

Normal corneal healing: limbal stem cells at the corneal periphery generate new epithelial cells that migrate centripetally to resurface the cornea.

In trachoma, chronic inflammation from the upper tarsal conjunctiva extends downward to the **superior limbus** — the 12-o'clock limbal zone that directly underlies the upper tarsal tissue. This repeated inflammatory contact damages superior limbal stem cells. In response, the limbal region generates **pannus** — fibrovascular tissue that grows centripetally from the superior limbus over the corneal surface.

The inferior limbus is not in direct contact with the site of inflammation, so pannus starts and is most prominent superiorly.

**Board hook:** Trachoma pannus = **upper limbus**. Contrast with pterygium, which grows from the **nasal or temporal limbus** (interpalpebral fissure UV exposure).

## 14. Retinitis Pigmentosa — Why Rods Die First

### Why Night Blindness Comes Before Central Vision Loss

RP is a hereditary photoreceptor dystrophy — rods are primarily affected, cones secondarily.

**Why rods are lost first:**
- Rods outnumber cones 120 million to 6–7 million
- Rods are concentrated in the **peripheral retina** — outside the central 5–10° that cones dominate
- The RP mutation (commonly in rhodopsin, or photoreceptor outer segment cycling proteins) causes rod outer segment discs to fail recycling → accumulation of toxic byproducts → rod death
- Peripheral rod loss first → **nyctalopia (night blindness)** + **peripheral field loss** → expanding ring scotoma
- Central cone-dominant fovea is preserved until late stages → **central VA maintained late into disease**

**Why bone spicule pigmentation forms:**
As peripheral rods die, the RPE cells in the peripheral retina become disorganized and migrate anteriorly toward the inner retina — following degenerating photoreceptors. The RPE carries melanin. This melanin deposits in a pattern around the **perivascular spaces** (retinal vessels act as scaffolding for RPE migration) → the characteristic **bone spicule (perivascular) pigmentation**.

**ERG finding:** b-wave attenuation (photoreceptor → bipolar cell signal is lost). Note: siderosis from iron IOFB also shows **b-wave attenuation** — they share this ERG pattern despite entirely different etiologies.

## 15. Aging Changes — Gullstrand Perspective

### How Gullstrand Constants Shift with Age

| Parameter | Young Adult | Elderly | Clinical Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Corneal front radius | ~7.7 mm | Slightly flatter | Minor reduction in corneal power |
| ACD | 3.6 mm | ~3.0 mm | Narrower angle → PACG risk increases |
| Lens axial thickness | ~3.5 mm | ~5.0 mm (+28%) | Lens displaces iris forward; presbyopia; myopic shift |
| Lens nucleus n | ~1.406 | Higher | Nuclear sclerosis → acquired myopia (senopia) |
| Maximum accommodated lens power | Up to ~32 D total | Decreasing toward resting value | Presbyopia |
| RNFL | Full thickness | Thins 20–30% over lifetime | Visual field sensitivity decreases peripherally |

### Presbyopia — Why It Happens

Accommodation requires the ciliary muscle to contract → zonular fiber tension decreases → the lens, being naturally elastic, becomes more convex (steeper on all surfaces) → lens power increases → F₂ moves forward → near object comes into focus.

With aging: the lens crystallins crosslink and harden — the lens loses elasticity. The ciliary muscle may still contract normally, but the lens no longer changes shape in response. Accommodative amplitude drops approximately **0.3 D per year after age 40**.

**Minimum AoA formula (Hofstetter):** 15 − (1/4 × age)
- At age 40: 15 − 10 = **5 D** (borderline presbyopia)
- At age 60: 15 − 15 = **0 D** (fully presbyopic)

**Board hook:** Presbyopia is not a disease — it is normal age-related loss of lens elasticity. The far point of an emmetrope does not change. Only near focus is affected.
