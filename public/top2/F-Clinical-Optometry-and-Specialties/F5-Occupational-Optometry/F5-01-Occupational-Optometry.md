## The Simple Version

Occupational optometry is about ensuring workers can see safely and efficiently in their specific work environment. It covers three things: understanding the visual demands of different jobs, identifying ocular hazards in the workplace, and prescribing the right protective eyewear and corrective devices for those demands. Environmental optometry is the broader principle — how the visual system interacts with any environment, including the workplace.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "A welder, an office worker on a computer all day, and a surgeon all have different visual demands. What do they share that makes them all occupational optometry patients?"

## The Details

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Occupational Optometry | Portion of optometric practice concerned with efficient and safe visual functioning within the work environment |
| Environmental Optometry | Discipline concerned with the interaction of the visual system with the environment (home, school, work, recreation, transport) |
| Industrial Vision | Devoted to protection of the eye in the workplace from flying objects, radiation, and lasers |
| Occupational (Industrial) Hygiene | Art and science dedicated to anticipation, recognition, evaluation, and control of workplace environmental stressors |

### Regulatory Framework

**International:**
- **WHO (World Health Organization)** — collaborates with member states on occupational health surveys
- **ILO (International Labour Organization)** — prevention of occupational injuries; promotion of safety and well-being
- **ICOH (International Commission on Occupational Health)** — specialist committees on specific hazards

**Philippines (Local):**
- **DOLE (Department of Labor and Employment)** — implements labor laws and policies
- **OSHS (Occupational Safety and Health Standards)** — conducts research on workplace safety; monitors working environments; conducts medical examinations for workers exposed to hazardous substances
- **SSS (Social Security System) — RA 8282** — protection against sickness, disability, maternity, old age, death
- **GSIS (Government Service Insurance System)** — same as SSS for government employees

### Ocular Hazards in the Workplace

| Hazard Type | Examples | Ocular Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanical / Particulate** | Grinding, drilling, carpentry, construction | Corneal foreign body; penetrating injury |
| **Radiation — UV** | Welding arc, solar exposure, tanning booths | Photokeratitis (welder's flash); pterygium; cataract |
| **Radiation — Infrared (IR)** | Glassblowing, furnace work, foundry | Thermal damage to cornea and lens; IR cataract |
| **Radiation — Visible light** | Laser use, arc welding | Retinal photocoagulation; macular burn |
| **Chemical splash** | Laboratories, agriculture, manufacturing | Chemical burns (acid or alkali) |
| **Computer / Digital display** | Office work, programming, data entry | Computer vision syndrome (CVS): eye strain, dry eye, headaches |

**Priority in chemical burns:** Irrigate FIRST with water or saline for at least 20–30 minutes. Take history AFTER. This is a strict rule — alkali burns penetrate deeper and faster.

### Computer Vision Syndrome (CVS) / Digital Eye Strain

**Definition:** A cluster of eye and vision problems related to prolonged use of computers and digital devices.

**Symptoms:** Eye strain, blurred near vision, dry eyes, headaches, neck and shoulder pain

**Causes:**
- Reduced blink rate during screen use (normal: 15–20/min; screen: ~5/min)
- Accommodative demand at screen distance (usually 50–70 cm)
- Glare from screen or overhead lighting
- Uncorrected or under-corrected refractive error
- Poor workstation ergonomics

**Management — 20-20-20 rule:**
Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds.

**Prescribing for screen users:**
- Single-vision near lenses for presbyopes who use progressive and struggle at screen distance
- Anti-reflection coating to reduce glare
- Screen distance add: typically 0.50–1.00D less than full near add
- Blue-light filtering lenses (optional; evidence is limited but patient-acceptable)

### Protective Eyewear

**OSHS standards for protective eyewear apply in the Philippines.**

**Types of protection:**

| Protection Type | Indication |
|---|---|
| Safety glasses (with side shields) | Moderate impact hazard; grinding, chipping |
| Goggles (sealed) | Chemical splash; fine dust; severe impact |
| Face shield | Full-face chemical splash protection; in addition to goggles, not instead |
| Welding helmet / filtered goggles | UV and IR radiation from welding arc |
| Laser safety goggles | Specific wavelength-blocking for laser type used |

**Lens standards (ANSI Z87.1 / Philippines equivalent):**
- Impact resistance: polycarbonate is preferred (highest impact resistance)
- Side protection required for most industrial environments
- UV protection required for outdoor and welding work

**Shade number system for welding filters:**
- Shade number increases with arc intensity
- Higher shade = darker lens = more protection from the brighter arc
- Shade 10–13 typically used for arc welding

### Vision Standards for Specific Occupations

| Occupation | Vision Requirement |
|---|---|
| Drivers (commercial) | Minimum VA with correction; adequate peripheral fields; color vision |
| Pilots | Strict VA and color vision standards (Civil Aviation Authority) |
| Seafarers (MARINA standards) | Specified distance VA; color vision (Ishihara/Farnsworth D-15) |
| Police / Military | VA and color vision standards per agency |
| Industrial workers (hazardous) | Pre-employment eye exam; periodic occupational exams |

---

### Ocular Safety Plan — Key Components

An optometrist developing an ocular safety plan for a workplace should include:
1. Hazard identification and risk assessment
2. Selection and provision of appropriate protective eyewear
3. Employee training on eye safety and PPE use
4. Pre-employment and periodic vision examinations
5. Emergency protocols (eye wash stations, chemical spill procedures)
6. Record-keeping and reporting of occupational eye injuries

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** A face shield replaces goggles in chemical splash situations — Wrong. A face shield is always in addition to, not a replacement for, splash goggles. The face shield protects the face; the sealed goggles protect the eyes.
- **Trap:** GSIS covers private sector employees — Wrong. GSIS is for government employees; SSS is for private sector.
- **Trap:** Maternity leave is 60 days under current law — Wrong. RA 11210 (2019) expanded it to 105 days.
- **Trap:** Chemical burns — history first, then irrigate — Wrong. Irrigate immediately. The cause matters less than stopping ongoing damage.
- **Trap:** All occupational eye exams are the same — Wrong. The exam is tailored to the job's visual demands — a driver needs fields and color vision assessed; a computer worker needs accommodative function assessed.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A worker splashes a chemical solvent into their eye. What is the immediate correct action?

- A. Identify the chemical first, then call poison control before irrigating
- B. Apply a bandage contact lens to protect the cornea
- C. Irrigate with clean water or saline for at least 20–30 minutes immediately
- D. Instill proparacaine and refer to the emergency room without irrigation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** Irrigation is the first and most critical action regardless of the chemical involved. Alkali burns (especially) continue to penetrate deeper every second without irrigation. History and identification are done AFTER starting irrigation.

</details>

---

**2.** A graphic designer working 10 hours daily on a computer complains of eye strain, headache, and blurred vision at the end of the day. Which of the following is NOT part of the management?

- A. Prescribing anti-reflection coated lenses
- B. Advising the 20-20-20 rule
- C. Prescribing a slightly reduced near add for screen distance if presbyopic
- D. Prescribing cycloplegic drops for daily use to relax the ciliary muscle

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**D.** Cycloplegic drops are diagnostic agents — never prescribed for daily home use. They abolish accommodation, which would make near work impossible and cause photophobia. Management of CVS includes AR coating, the 20-20-20 rule, workstation optimization, and possibly task-specific lenses.

</details>
