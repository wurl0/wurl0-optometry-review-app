## F8 — Ocular Prosthesis and Absorptive Lenses

## The Simple Version

This subtopic covers two separate but related areas. Ocular prosthesis is for patients who have lost an eye — the goal is cosmetic rehabilitation, socket health, and psychological support. Absorptive lenses are for patients who need selective filtering of light — either to protect the eye from UV/IR radiation or to improve functional vision in patients with photophobia and low contrast sensitivity. The board tests prosthesis types and care schedules, and absorptive lens prescribing matched to specific diagnoses.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "A patient whose eye was removed 6 months ago is now having daily irritation and discharge. What are the three most likely causes and how do you manage each?"

---

## Part A — Ocular Prosthesis

### Why It Is Needed

After loss of an eye (enucleation, evisceration) or severe disfigurement (phthisis bulbi, anophthalmos), an ocular prosthesis:
- Maintains the volume of the orbit (prevents socket collapse)
- Provides cosmetic rehabilitation
- Maintains fornix depth (prevents contracted socket)
- Supports psychological well-being

**Surgical procedures:**
- **Enucleation:** Complete removal of the globe; orbital implant placed; prosthesis over the implant
- **Evisceration:** Intraocular contents removed; scleral shell preserved; better motility
- **Exenteration:** Orbital contents removed (radical; for malignancy) — full prosthesis or orbital prosthesis required

---

### Types of Ocular Prostheses

| Type | Description | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Stock (ready-made) | Pre-manufactured; standard sizes; generic iris | Temporary; resource-limited settings |
| Custom prosthesis (PMMA) | Hand-crafted; individually fitted by ocularist; PMMA material | Standard of care for long-term rehabilitation |
| Scleral shell | Thin custom shell over an existing shrunken globe (phthisis) | Phthisis bulbi; microphthalmos |
| Conformer | Clear plastic shell placed immediately post-surgery | Prevents socket contraction during healing; replaced by definitive prosthesis at 6–8 weeks |

**Material:** Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA / acrylic) — same material used for some CL lenses and ophthalmic lens materials.

---

### Care and Maintenance Schedule

| Task | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Daily cleaning | Remove; rinse with saline; reinsert |
| Socket hygiene | Flush socket with sterile saline each time |
| Polishing by ocularist | Every 6–12 months |
| Replacement (adults) | Every 5–7 years |
| Replacement (children) | More frequently — growing orbit requires larger prosthesis |
| Sleeping | Most patients keep prosthesis in; remove only on ocularist's advice |

**What to use:** Sterile saline, mild soap. Never alcohol, detergents, or tap water for soaking (Acanthamoeba risk from tap water).

**Signs to see the ocularist:**
- Increased discharge
- Socket redness / irritation
- Visible scratches on prosthesis
- Reduced motility
- Poor cosmetic match (indicates socket volume change)

---

### Contracted Socket

- Complication of poor prosthetic care or prolonged absence of prosthesis
- Fornices shrink → socket volume decreases → cannot retain prosthesis
- **Prevention:** Consistent prosthesis wear; immediate conformer placement post-surgery

## Part B — Absorptive Lenses

### The Electromagnetic Spectrum — Board-Relevant Wavelengths

| Range | Wavelength | Ocular Risk |
|---|---|---|
| UV-C | 100–280 nm | Blocked by atmosphere; minimal clinical concern outdoors |
| UV-B | 280–315 nm | Photokeratitis; climatic droplet keratopathy; cataract |
| UV-A | 315–400 nm | Cataract; possible macular damage |
| HEV / Blue light | 400–500 nm | Implicated in retinal phototoxicity; ARMD |
| Visible | 400–700 nm | Normal photopic vision |
| Infrared (IR) | > 700 nm | Thermal damage; IR cataract (glassblowers, furnace workers) |

**Standard clear lenses** (CR-39, poly, hi-index) block UV up to ~380 nm. Polycarbonate blocks UV up to 385 nm inherently.

---

### Photochromic Lenses

- **Mechanism:** Silver halide crystals (silver chloride/bromide in glass) or organic dye molecules (plastic) that darken reversibly when exposed to UV
- Activate outdoors (UV present); clear indoors
- **Limitation:** Do not activate inside a car — windshield blocks UV
- Darkening time: 30–60 seconds; clearing time: 2–5 minutes

---

### Corning CPF (Contrast Preservation Filters)

Longpass filters designed for patients with photosensitivity and reduced contrast sensitivity (low vision, cataract, RP, AMD).

| Filter | Cutoff Wavelength | Color | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| CPF 450 | Cuts below 450 nm | Very light yellow | Minimal photophobia; early lens changes |
| CPF 511 | Cuts below 511 nm | Yellow-orange | Moderate photophobia; nuclear cataract |
| CPF 527 | Cuts below 527 nm | Orange | RP; glaucoma; macular disease |

**Wratten filter numbers:** Used in occupational and research contexts; higher number = longer cutoff wavelength = more blocking of short-wave light. Examples: Wratten #12 = yellow; Wratten #15 = deep yellow; Wratten #22 = orange; Wratten #29 = red.

---

### Diagnosis-Linked Absorptive Lens Prescribing

| Diagnosis | Recommended Lens |
|---|---|
| Albinism | Deep absorptive (orange/red tint); 80–90% transmission blocking; UV 400 essential |
| Aniridia | High-transmission absorptive; photochromic or fixed tint; pupil replica lens (cosmetic) |
| Achromatopsia (rod monochromacy) | Red or deep orange tint; eliminates short-wavelength stimulation of remaining cone photoreceptors; CPF 527 or darker |
| Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP) | Yellow or amber (CPF 511/527); reduces blue-light scatter; protects remaining rods; photochromic acceptable |
| Age-Related Macular Degeneration | Yellow/amber tint; AREDS2 lutein and zeaxanthin (dietary); CPF 450–511 |
| Cataract (pre-surgical) | CPF 511 or 527 to reduce glare from light scatter in the lens |
| Glaucoma | Absorptive to manage photophobia; avoid heavy tints that reduce available light for already-restricted fields |
| Cone dystrophy | Red-blocking; similar to achromatopsia prescription |

## Memory Anchors

**Prosthesis types — SCSC:** Stock, Custom, Scleral shell, Conformer

**CPF numbers = cutoff in nm:** CPF 450 cuts at 450 nm; CPF 511 at 511 nm; CPF 527 at 527 nm — the number IS the cutoff.

**RP + achromatopsia tint rule:** "Red goes to zero rods, orange helps the rest" — achromatopsia patients need to eliminate short-wave stimulation entirely (deep orange/red); RP patients still have some rods, so amber/yellow (CPF 511/527) improves contrast without blocking everything.

---

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** A scleral shell is used after enucleation — Wrong. A scleral shell fits over an existing (but shrunken) globe (phthisis bulbi, microphthalmos). After enucleation, the globe is gone — a custom prosthesis is used over the orbital implant.
- **Trap:** Photochromic lenses work in a car — Wrong. Car windshields block UV, which is what activates photochromic lenses. They remain clear while driving.
- **Trap:** CPF 527 is appropriate for all photophobia — Wrong. CPF 527 (orange, cuts below 527 nm) is for RP, macular disease, and glaucoma. For mild photophobia or early lens changes, CPF 450 (light yellow) is more appropriate.
- **Trap:** Tap water is safe for soaking a prosthesis — Wrong. Tap water carries Acanthamoeba risk and is not sterile. Use sterile saline.
- **Trap:** A conformer is the definitive prosthesis — Wrong. A conformer is a temporary clear placeholder placed immediately after surgery to maintain socket shape. The custom PMMA prosthesis is fitted at 6–8 weeks once healing is complete.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A patient with achromatopsia (complete rod monochromacy) is extremely photophobic and reports glare and difficulty in any lit environment. Which absorptive lens is most appropriate?

- A. Light yellow (CPF 450)
- B. Gray (neutral density)
- C. Deep orange or red tint (CPF 527 or darker)
- D. Green tint for general outdoor use

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** Achromatopsia patients have non-functional cones and rely entirely on rods. Rods are most sensitive in the mid-spectrum (peak ~507 nm) and are overwhelmed by bright light. A deep orange or red longpass filter (CPF 527 or darker) eliminates the short and mid-wavelength stimulation that causes overwhelming photophobia and allows the remaining rod function to operate comfortably. Light yellow (CPF 450) is not sufficient. Gray reduces all wavelengths equally without targeted relief.

</details>

---

**2.** A patient's eye was surgically removed 6 months ago. They are now wearing a stock prosthesis and report daily discharge and socket irritation. What is the next step?

- A. Prescribe topical antibiotics and reassure
- B. Refer for a custom PMMA prosthesis fitted by a certified ocularist
- C. Switch to a scleral shell
- D. Instruct the patient to stop wearing the prosthesis to allow the socket to heal

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** The stock (ready-made) prosthesis is a poor fit — it cannot conform to the individual socket, which leads to chronic discharge and irritation. The standard of care is a custom PMMA prosthesis fitted by a certified ocularist. Six months is sufficient healing time for proper fitting. Stopping prosthesis wear risks contracted socket, not healing.

</details>

## Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue → rule → trap`.

**Dilation drug** — cue: with vs without cycloplegia.
- Dilate **without** cycloplegia = phenylephrine (α1). Dilate **with** cycloplegia = tropicamide / cyclopentolate (M3 block).
- Trap: "phenylephrine causes cycloplegia" — it has none.

**Pregnancy dilation** — cue: pregnant patient needs dilation.
- Tropicamide (shortest, least exposure) + nasolacrimal occlusion.
- Trap: phenylephrine 10% (cardiovascular) in pregnancy.

**CN3 pupil rule** — cue: third-nerve palsy + pupil.
- Pupil-**involving** = posterior communicating artery aneurysm (neurosurgical emergency). Pupil-**spared** = ischemic.
- Trap: observing a pupil-involving CN3 palsy.

**Vertical deviation 3-step** — cue: a hyperdeviation worse on tilt.
- Parks-Bielschowsky 3-step localizes a CN4 (superior oblique) palsy; head tilts **away**, chin down.
- Trap: tilting the head toward the affected side.

**Absorptive/UV** — cue: UV band or filter.
- UVC highest energy (blocked by ozone); UV400 standard; CPF filters cut blue for specific diagnoses.
- Trap: calling UVA the highest-energy band.
