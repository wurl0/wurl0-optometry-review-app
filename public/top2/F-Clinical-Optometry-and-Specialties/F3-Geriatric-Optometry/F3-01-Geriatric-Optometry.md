## The Simple Version

Geriatric optometry is about understanding how aging changes every part of the eye and visual system, how systemic diseases common in the elderly (diabetes, hypertension, thyroid disease) manifest in the eye, and how to manage these patients including ophthalmic correction for their special needs. The most tested areas are AMD, diabetic retinopathy, hypertensive retinopathy, and glaucoma in the elderly — because these are the leading causes of irreversible vision loss in the aging population.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "If your patient is 75 years old and has both diabetes and glaucoma, where do you start the eye exam and why?" This forces students to think about systemic-ocular links and prioritization.

---

## But Why?

**Why does the lens yellow and harden with age?**
Lens proteins (crystallins) undergo progressive cross-linking and oxidation throughout life. There is no turnover of lens fiber cells — every fiber laid down from embryonic development remains. Over decades, these proteins denature, yellow (from accumulation of UV-generated chromophores), and become less elastic. The loss of elasticity is presbyopia; the yellowing and opacity formation is nuclear cataract.

**Why does diabetic retinopathy cause vision loss through neovascularization?**
Chronic hyperglycemia damages retinal capillary endothelial cells and pericytes. Pericyte loss destabilizes vessel walls → microaneurysms and non-perfusion. Non-perfused retina secretes VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor) → signals for new vessel growth. But new vessels (NVD/NVE) are fragile, structurally defective, and grow into the vitreous → vitreous hemorrhage, traction retinal detachment → severe vision loss.

## The Details

### Age-Related Anatomical Changes in the Eye

| Structure | Change with Aging | Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Cornea | Arcus senilis (lipid deposition at periphery); decreased sensitivity; flatten | Reduced corneal reflex; increased susceptibility to infection |
| Anterior chamber | Shallows as lens thickens | Increased risk of angle closure |
| Iris | Atrophy of dilator muscle; iris rigidity | Miosis (small pupil); poor dilation; reduced low-light vision |
| Lens | Yellowing, nuclear sclerosis, decreased elasticity | Nuclear cataract; presbyopia; increased short-wavelength absorption |
| Vitreous | Syneresis (liquefaction) and posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) | Floaters; risk of retinal tear if vitreous pulls on a retinal vessel |
| Retina | Rod loss (peripheral); cone changes at macula; decreased sensitivity | Reduced contrast sensitivity; slower dark adaptation |
| Optic nerve | Decreased axon count; increased C:D ratio | Must distinguish from glaucomatous damage |
| Tear film | Reduced lacrimal gland secretion; altered lipid layer | Dry eye disease — most common complaint in geriatric patients |

### Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

*From Gap Lecture 13 — full content*

**Classification:**

| Type | Frequency | Pathology | Vision Loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dry AMD | ~85% | Drusen accumulation; RPE atrophy; geographic atrophy | Slow, progressive |
| Wet AMD (neovascular) | ~15% | Choroidal neovascularization (CNV) through Bruch's membrane | Rapid, severe |

**Drusen types:**
- Hard drusen (small, discrete) = lower risk
- Soft drusen (large, confluent, indistinct borders) = high risk for progression

**Stages of Dry AMD:**
1. Early: small drusen (<63 µm), no pigmentary changes, VA usually normal
2. Intermediate: medium drusen (63–124 µm) or pigmentary abnormality
3. Advanced GA (Geographic Atrophy): large RPE/photoreceptor loss, severe central vision loss

**Wet AMD key signs:**
- Sudden metamorphopsia or central vision loss
- Subretinal fluid, hemorrhage, macular edema (on OCT)
- Disciform scar = end-stage (Junius-Kuhnt)

**Management:**

| Stage | Management |
|---|---|
| Early / intermediate dry | AREDS2 supplements (25% risk reduction); Amsler grid monitoring; annual review |
| Advanced GA | Low vision assessment; rehabilitation; no proven treatment to reverse atrophy |
| Wet AMD (CNV) | Anti-VEGF intravitreal injections (bevacizumab / ranibizumab / aflibercept); urgent referral |

**AREDS2 Formula (daily):**
- Vitamin C: 500 mg
- Vitamin E: 400 IU
- Lutein: 10 mg
- Zeaxanthin: 2 mg
- Zinc: 80 mg
- Copper: 2 mg

**Anti-VEGF agents:**
- Bevacizumab (Avastin) — off-label, least expensive
- Ranibizumab (Lucentis) — FDA-approved for wet AMD
- Aflibercept (Eylea) — FDA-approved; less frequent injections required

### Diabetic Retinopathy (DR)

**Pathophysiology:** Chronic hyperglycemia → pericyte loss → microaneurysms → retinal ischemia → VEGF release → neovascularization

**Classification:**

| Stage | Features |
|---|---|
| Non-Proliferative DR (NPDR) — Mild | Microaneurysms only |
| NPDR — Moderate | Microaneurysms + dot/blot hemorrhages + hard exudates + soft exudates (cotton wool spots) |
| NPDR — Severe (4-2-1 rule) | Hemorrhages in 4 quadrants; venous beading in 2 quadrants; IRMA in 1 quadrant |
| Proliferative DR (PDR) | Neovascularization (NVD or NVE); vitreous hemorrhage; traction retinal detachment |

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME):**
- Most common cause of vision loss in DR (not neovascularization)
- Fluid accumulation in the macula → blurred central vision
- Clinically significant macular edema (CSME): edema within 500 µm of fovea, or hard exudates with adjacent thickening within 500 µm, or thickening ≥ one disc area within one disc diameter of fovea

**Management:**
| Stage | Management |
|---|---|
| Mild–moderate NPDR | Optimize glycemic control; 6–12 month review |
| Severe NPDR / early PDR | Pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP) |
| PDR with vitreous hemorrhage or TRD | Vitrectomy |
| DME | Anti-VEGF (first line); focal/grid laser (second line) |

**Optometric role:** Screen annually; detect early DR; refer when moderate NPDR or worse, or any DME.

### Hypertensive Retinopathy (HTR)

**Pathophysiology:** Chronic hypertension → arteriole wall thickening (arteriolosclerosis) → reduced lumen → AV nicking → flame hemorrhages → cotton wool spots → papilledema (in severe hypertensive crisis)

**Classification (Scheie / Keith-Wagener-Barker grading):**

| Grade | Features |
|---|---|
| Grade 1 | Mild narrowing of arterioles; increased arteriovenous (AV) ratio |
| Grade 2 | Moderate narrowing + AV nicking (arteriovenous crossing changes) |
| Grade 3 | Grade 2 + flame hemorrhages + cotton wool spots + hard exudates |
| Grade 4 | Grade 3 + papilledema (optic disc swelling) — hypertensive emergency |

**Normal AV ratio:** 2:3 (artery:vein). In HTR: ratio narrows (arteries thin, veins compressed).

**AV nicking (Gunn's sign / Salus sign):** Arteriole crosses over vein and compresses it → vein appears indented or deflected at the crossing — sign of arteriolosclerosis.

**Management:** Refer for blood pressure management. Grade 4 = medical emergency.

---

### Geriatric Binocular Vision Anomalies

Common in elderly:
- **Exophoria at near** increases as accommodative convergence decreases with presbyopia
- **Esotropia** can develop in patients with long-standing exo who decompensate
- **Vertical deviations** from skew deviation in brainstem ischemia
- **Diplopia from CN palsies** — microvascular (DM/HTN) is the most common cause

---

### Ophthalmic Correction for Elderly Special Needs

| Need | Solution |
|---|---|
| Reading and near work | Progressive lenses or bifocals with appropriate add |
| Low light / night driving | High-transmission lenses; anti-reflection coating |
| Photophobia (cataract, AMD) | Absorptive lenses (CPF filters); polarized lenses |
| Dry eye | Lubricating drops; optimize CL wear schedule |
| Low vision | High-add spectacles, magnifiers, telescopes (see F7) |
| Depth perception issues | Caution with progressive for stairs; may need separate reading glasses |

## Comparisons That Matter

| Feature | Dry AMD | Wet AMD |
|---|---|---|
| Frequency | 85% | 15% |
| Pathology | Drusen; RPE atrophy; GA | CNV; subretinal fluid; hemorrhage |
| Onset | Gradual | Sudden |
| Severity | Moderate | Severe (rapid if untreated) |
| Treatment | AREDS2 supplements | Anti-VEGF injections |
| Board clue | Drusen on fundus | Metamorphopsia + sudden vision loss |

| Feature | DR | HTR |
|---|---|---|
| Vessels | Microaneurysms; neovascularization | Arteriolosclerosis; AV nicking |
| Exudates | Hard exudates in rings (circinate) | Hard exudates in macular star pattern |
| Specific sign | Cotton wool spots, NVD, NVE | AV nicking; grade 4 = papilledema |
| Key cause | Diabetes | Hypertension |

## Memory Anchors

**AREDS2 vitamins:** "C-E-LZ" → Vitamin C, Vitamin E, Lutein, Zeaxanthin (+ Zinc and Copper)

**Wet AMD treatment:** "B-R-A" → Bevacizumab, Ranibizumab, Aflibercept (anti-VEGF agents)

**HTR grades 1–4:** "Narrow, Nick, Flame, Papilledema" — each grade adds the next finding.

**DR 4-2-1 rule for severe NPDR:** Hemorrhages in **4** quadrants; venous beading in **2** quadrants; IRMA in **1** quadrant → any ONE of these = severe NPDR, high risk for PDR.

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** Dry AMD is the most common cause of severe vision loss in AMD — Wrong. Wet AMD (only 15% of cases) causes the majority of severe vision loss; dry AMD progresses slowly.
- **Trap:** AREDS2 supplements treat AMD — Wrong. They reduce the risk of progression from intermediate to advanced; they do not treat existing disease or reverse damage.
- **Trap:** Grade 4 hypertensive retinopathy is characterized by AV nicking — Wrong. AV nicking = Grade 2. Grade 4 = papilledema (hypertensive emergency, referring sign).
- **Trap:** The most common cause of vision loss in DR is PDR (neovascularization) — Wrong. Diabetic macular edema (DME) is the most common cause of vision loss in DR.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 68-year-old patient with a 10-year history of diabetes presents with best corrected VA of 20/50 in the right eye. Fundus shows thickening of the retina within 500 µm of the fovea with adjacent hard exudates. What is the diagnosis and appropriate management?

- A. Moderate NPDR; observe and recheck in 12 months
- B. Clinically significant macular edema (CSME); urgent referral for anti-VEGF treatment
- C. Proliferative DR; pan-retinal photocoagulation
- D. Dry AMD with macular drusen; AREDS2 supplementation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** The findings (retinal thickening within 500 µm of fovea + hard exudates with thickening within 500 µm) meet the criteria for clinically significant macular edema (CSME). This requires urgent referral for treatment (anti-VEGF is first-line). This is not PDR (no neovascularization), not AMD (hard exudates in diabetic pattern), and not a watch-and-wait situation.

</details>

---

**2.** A 72-year-old patient reports sudden onset of central distortion (wavy lines) in the right eye over 2 days. Fundus exam shows subretinal fluid and hemorrhage near the fovea. What is the most likely diagnosis?

- A. Dry AMD with geographic atrophy
- B. Wet AMD with choroidal neovascularization
- C. Diabetic macular edema
- D. Hypertensive retinopathy grade 3

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Sudden onset of metamorphopsia (wavy lines) + subretinal fluid and hemorrhage at the macula = wet AMD with choroidal neovascularization until proven otherwise. This is urgent — refer for anti-VEGF injection. Dry AMD progresses gradually; DME is associated with diabetes and has a different fundus picture; HTR grade 3 shows flame hemorrhages and cotton wool spots but not subretinal hemorrhage.

</details>
