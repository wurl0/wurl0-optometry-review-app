## 1. Definition and Scope

**Pediatrics:** The specialty of medical science concerned with the physical, mental, and social health of children from birth to young adulthood.

**Pediatric Optometry:** A subspecialty of optometry concerned with eye diseases, visual development, and care in children. It focuses on childhood eye misalignment and disorders, as well as adult eye movement problems.

**Behavioral / Functional Optometrist:** Specializes in how the visual system functions and how behavior affects vision (and vice versa). Also called a "developmental optometrist."

**Optometric Vision Therapy:** Drug-free, non-invasive treatment to improve visual skills (tracking, focusing, eye teaming). Not the same as strengthening muscles — it rewires the visual pathway through repetitive stimulation.

- **Orthoptics:** A related field focused on strengthening eye muscles (practiced by orthoptists)

**The core clinical challenge:** Children cannot reliably answer "which is clearer, one or two?" All tests must be adapted to developmental level. The key diseases are **amblyopia and strabismus** — early detection determines outcome because the visual cortex has a critical period.

**Critical period of visual plasticity:**
- Peak sensitivity: birth to age 3
- Functional window: birth to approximately age 7-8
- After closure: minimal response to amblyopia treatment

## 2. Visual Development — Embryology and Milestones

### Embryological Timeline

| Structure | Month of Development |
|---|---|
| Orbit | 2nd month |
| Optic nerve | 3rd month |
| Choroid | 3rd month |
| Sclera and extraocular muscles | 3rd to 4th month |
| Cornea | 4th month |
| Anterior chamber | 9-10 weeks |
| Vitreous | 4th month |
| Lids and lacrimal apparatus | 5th month |
| Iris and ciliary body | 6th month |
| Retina | 6th month |
| Lens | 7th month (onset) |
| Blood vessels | 8th month |

**ROP and RLF context:**
- **Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP):** Occurs when abnormal blood vessels grow and spread throughout the retina. These fragile vessels can leak, scar the retina, and cause retinal detachment. Cause: prematurity + supplemental oxygen.
- **Retrolental fibroplasia (RLF):** Older term for the same condition. Fibrous tissue behind the lens in premature infants given high oxygen concentrations. Can cause blindness.
- Suck reflex begins at 32 weeks gestation; fully developed by 36 weeks. Premature babies may lack it.

| 3 months | Binocular vision and eye coordination developing; follows light or face; foveal maturation begins |

| 6 months (6/30 = 20/200) | Reaches accurately for toys; convergence and depth perception present |
| 9 months | Looks for hidden toys (object permanence) |
| 1 year (6/15 = 20/50) | VA approximately 20/50-20/70 |
| 2 years | Picture matching |
| 3 years (6/6 = 20/20) | Letter matching (Sheridan Gardiner); VA reaches adult level |
| 5 years | Snellen chart by matching or naming |

| Function | When Develops |
|---|---|
| Pupillary light reaction | 30 weeks gestation |
| Blink response to visual stimuli | 2-5 months |
| Fixation | 2 months |
| Smooth pursuit | 6-8 weeks |

**Emmetropization:** Neonates are typically hyperopic (+2.00 to +3.00D). The eye grows to match its optical power over childhood, progressively reducing refractive error toward emmetropia. Most hyperopia resolves by age 4.

## 3. Primitive Reflexes

### What They Are and Why They Matter

Primitive reflexes are reflex actions originating in the CNS that appear in normal infants but not in neurologically intact adults. They emerge during gestation and integrate (disappear) as the CNS matures — typically within the first months of life.

**If a reflex persists beyond its expected integration age:** signals incomplete CNS maturation. This can contribute to ADD/ADHD, autism, learning disabilities, developmental delay, sensory integration disorders, and visual problems.

**If a reflex is absent when it should be present:** signals a neurological problem.

Assessment of primitive reflexes is part of vision rehabilitation.

### Types of Primitive Reflexes

**1. Fear Paralysis Reflex**
- Emerge: 5 gestational weeks (GW)
- Integrate: 9-12 GW (inside the womb)
- Type: Withdrawal reflex (inside womb)
- Residual effects if persistent: deeply rooted anxieties, fears, OCD tendencies; difficulty integrating the Moro reflex

**2. Babkin Palmomental Reflex**
- Emerge: 9 GW; Integrate: 3 months
- Baby opens mouth and rotates head when palms are pressed
- Residual effects: moves mouth when writing, sticks tongue when catching ball, often fists hands, person is tense

**3. Root Reflex**
- Emerge: 36 weeks gestation (birth); Integrate: 3-4 months
- Trigger: stroke corner of baby's mouth
- Response: head turns toward stimulus, mouth opens
- Purpose: helps find breast/bottle for feeding
- Residual effects: feeding problems, mouth movements when writing

**4. Suck Reflex**
- Emerge: 32 weeks gestation; fully developed by 36 weeks; Integrate: 2-4 months
- Trigger: something touching roof of baby's mouth
- Response: sucking
- Premature babies may lack this reflex (born before 32 weeks)
- Thumb-sucking beyond expected age can be a sign of autism or developmental issues (along with tip-toeing)

**5. Moro Reflex (Startle Reflex)**
- Emerge: 8-9 weeks in utero; Integrate: 5-6 months
- Trigger: sudden loud sound or movement
- Response: arms and legs thrown out, then pulled back in; crying
- Clinically tests hearing ability and environmental responsiveness
- Residual effects if persistent: ADHD, sensory processing difficulties, hypersensitivity

**6. Asymmetric Tonic Neck Reflex (ATNR)**
- Also called "fencing reflex" or "fencing position"
- Emerge: 18 weeks in utero; Integrate: 6-7 months
- Trigger: head is turned to one side while lying on back
- Response: arm and leg on the face side extend; opposite arm bends up to elbow ("fencing position")
- Clinical use: assesses head-trunk-extremity coordination
- Residual effects if persistent: difficulty crossing midline, poor fine/gross motor skills, disrupts fixation and tracking (every head turn triggers arm movement, can mimic convergence insufficiency)

**7. Symmetric Tonic Neck Reflex (STNR)**
- Emerge: 6-9 months; Integrate: 9-11 months
- Head flexion → arms flex + legs extend; head extension → arms extend + legs flex
- Residual effects if persistent: poor eye-hand coordination, difficulty copying tasks, poor posture, near vision problems

**8. Grasp Reflex (Palmar Grasp Reflex)**
- Emerge: 26 weeks gestation; Integrate: 2-3 months (some sources: 5-6 months)
- Trigger: stroke baby's palm
- Response: fingers close in a grasp
- Stronger in premature babies
- Absent grasp reflex = neurological problem
- Persistent grasp: may cause swallowing problems and delayed speech

**9. Babinski Reflex (Plantar Reflex)**
- Emerge: 18 weeks in utero; Integrate: by 2 years of age
- Trigger: firm stroke along sole of foot from heel upward
- Normal response in infant: big toe bends toward top of foot (dorsiflexion); other toes fan out
- **Board trap:** In adults, Babinski response (toe fanning) is pathological. In children under 2 years, it is normal.
- Purpose: preparation for walking

**10. Step (Walking/Dance) Reflex**
- Integrate: 3-4 months
- Trigger: hold baby upright with feet touching surface
- Response: stepping movements (like walking or dancing)
- Together with Babinski = preparation for walking

**11. Tonic Labyrinthine Reflex (TLR)**
- Integrate: approximately 4 months postnatally
- Head tilted back (supine) → back stiffens and arches, legs extend, arms flex; head forward (prone) → opposite
- Residual effects if persistent: slouched posture, low muscle tone, balance problems, dislike of physical activity, visual-perceptual difficulties

**12. Galant Reflex**
- Emerge: 20 weeks in utero; Integrate: 9 months
- Trigger: touch side of child's spine while lying on stomach
- Response: trunk/hips rotate toward the stimulated side
- Purpose: aides crawling and walking movements
- Residual effects if persistent: bedwetting, fatigue, attention difficulties, difficulty sitting still, posture problems, short-term memory difficulties

**13. Landau Reflex**
- Emerge: 3-10 weeks postnatal; Integrate: 1-3 years
- Trigger: lift infant horizontally → back arches and head raises
- If absent: indicates motor development issue (generalized intellectual impairment, cerebral palsy)
- Residual effects if not integrated: short-term memory problems, poor balance, low muscle tone

**14. Crawling (Bauer) Reflex**
- Trigger: place infant on stomach, apply pressure to sole of foot
- Response: crawling-like motion
- Disappears within weeks to months after birth

### Summary Table

| Reflex | Integration Age | Key Clinical Point |
|---|---|---|
| Fear Paralysis | 9-12 GW | Inside womb; anxiety/OCD if persistent |
| Root | 3-4 months | Feeding; head turns to stimulus |
| Suck | 2-4 months | Absent in very premature babies (<32 wks) |
| Moro (Startle) | 5-6 months | Tests hearing; ADHD if persistent |
| ATNR (Fencing) | 6-7 months | Disrupts reading/writing if persistent |
| STNR | 9-11 months | Near vision problems if persistent |
| Grasp | 5-6 months | Stronger in premature babies |
| Babinski | 2 years | Normal in infants; pathological in adults |
| Step | 3-4 months | Preparation for walking |

| Landau | 1-3 years | If absent = motor development issue |

## 4. Areas of Child Development

Seven areas that must develop together for healthy visual and cognitive function:

| Area | Description |
|---|---|
| **Perceptual (Sensorial Skills)** | Processing sensory input: vision, touch, hearing, smell, vestibular, taste. How we interpret what we see |
| **Sensorimotor (Tool Using)** | Movements and motor responses to sensory input. Comparing perceptual input to construct spatial understanding |
| **Conceptual (Thinking)** | Cognitive development; understanding that objects exist independently. Logical thinking, comprehension |
| **Representational (Symbolic)** | Ability to grasp that a sound or picture can represent something (e.g., picture of a dog = the word "dog"). Short-term to long-term memory conversion |
| **Communicative (Linguistic)** | Learning how words are used — when to speak, listen, understand. Language is as much cognitive as verbal |
| **Social (Interactive)** | Ability to take turns in conversation; mingle and make friends; interactive skills develop before first words |
| **Expressive (Emotive)** | Ability to express emotions; emotions change with age |
| **Self-Regulatory (Coping)** | Coping with stresses of everyday life; lasts first three years |

**Gross Motor Development:** Maturational process is **top-down** (cephalocaudal) — head control first, then shoulder/arms, then trunk, then hips, then legs. Balance and coordination are part of gross motor skills.

**Pediatric Age Ranges:**
- 0-2 years: Infants and toddlers
- 3-5 years: Pre-schoolers
- 6-10 years: Grade-schoolers (school-age extends to 18 years in clinical definitions)

## 5. Pediatric Eye Examination

### When and How to Examine

**Timing:**
- Best time: when infant is **awake and attentive**
- Do NOT feed just before examination (infants become drowsy after feeding)
- VA and ocular motility: assess in **alert, attentive** state
- Direct ophthalmoscopy: easier when infant is **drowsy**, ideally while being fed

**Environment:**
- Non-threatening; child-friendly
- Bright room; pleasant cartoons for decoration
- Examiner should NOT wear a white coat
- Decorations should not be overdone (avoid distracting from test stimuli)

**Recommended Examination Frequency (American Optometric Association):**

| Age Group | At-Risk | Low-Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Birth to 24 months | At 6 months | At 6 months |
| 2 to 5 years | At 3 years | At 3 years |
| 6 to 18 years | Annually | Before 1st grade, then every 2 years |

### Factors Placing a Child at Significant Risk

- Prematurity, low birth weight, prolonged supplemental oxygen
- Family history of retinoblastoma, congenital cataracts, or metabolic/genetic disease
- Maternal infection during pregnancy
- Difficult or assisted labor
- Strabismus or anisometropia
- Known or suspected CNS dysfunction

### Signs of Eye and Vision Problems

**Infants:**
- Excessive tearing → blocked tear ducts
- Red, crusty eyelids → eye infection
- Eyes not moving together → eye muscle control problem
- Cloudiness in eyes or extreme sensitivity to light → elevated IOP
- White reflex (leukocoria) → possible eye cancer (retinoblastoma)

**Pre-schoolers:** Sitting close to TV, squinting, tilting head, rubbing eyes, short attention span, sensitivity to light, turning of eye in or out.

**School-aged:** Frequent eye rubbing or blinking, avoiding reading, covering one eye, tilting head, holding materials close, seeing double, losing place when reading, difficulty remembering what was read.

### Components of the Pediatric Eye Examination (AOA)

1. Patient history
2. Visual acuity
3. Refraction
4. Binocular vision and ocular motility
5. Ocular health assessment and systemic health screening
6. Supplemental testing

### By Age Group

**Infants (0 to 2 years):**
- VA: Fixation preference test, preferential looking (Lea paddle), OKN drum, Teller Acuity Cards
- Refraction: Cycloplegic retinoscopy, static retinoscopy
- BV/Motility: Cover test, version test, NPC, Hirschberg, Krimsky, Bruckner
- Ocular health: Anterior segment, posterior segment, pupillary responses, visual field screening
- Supplemental: Electronic diagnostic testing (VEP, ERG)

**Pre-schoolers (3 to 5 years):**
- VA: Lea symbols, HOTV, Allen figures, Sheridan Gardiner, broken wheel chart
- Refraction: Cycloplegic retinoscopy, static retinoscopy, subjective if cooperation allows
- BV/Motility: Cover test, version test, NPC, positive/negative fusional vergences, stereopsis, MEM retinoscopy
- Ocular health: Anterior + posterior + pupillary + visual field + color vision
- Supplemental: Denver Developmental Screening Test (DDST), Developmental Test of Visual Motor Integration (DTVMI)

**School-age (6 to 18 years):**
- VA: Snellen chart; HOTV, Tumbling E, Broken Wheel if needed
- Refraction: Cycloplegic if indicated; static/dynamic retinoscopy; subjective refraction
- BV/Motility: Cover test, version test, NPC, fusional vergences, stereopsis, MEM retinoscopy, accommodative amplitude and facility
- Ocular health: All preschool components + IOP measurement
- Supplemental: Directionality testing, visual motor integration

## 6. Visual Acuity Tests for Children

### Four Types of Visual Acuity (Weymouth and Riggs)

1. **Detection Acuity** — minimum size visible; presence or absence of target. Tests: Catford drum, Stycar balls
2. **Resolution Acuity** — minimum separation between grating elements. Tests: Preferential looking cards, Lea gratings
3. **Recognition Acuity** — minimum size for identifying letters, pictures, or orientation of symbols. Tests: Letter or picture charts
4. **Localization (Vernier) Acuity** — ability to detect lateral displacement of lines placed end-to-end

### Age-Specific VA Tests

| Method | Age | Principle and Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Visually Evoked Potentials (VEP)** | Any age (infants) | Brain response to reversing checkerboard pattern; objective, no cooperation needed |
| **Optokinetic Nystagmus (OKN) drum** | 0-3 months | Involuntary nystagmus when moving stripes pass; present = sees |
| **Fixation Preference Test** | 0-3 years | Indirect assessment; covers each eye; child objects if better eye is covered |
| **Teller Acuity Cards / Forced Choice Preferential Looking (FCPL)** | Newborn to 2 years | Infant looks toward patterned vs. blank card; spatial frequency at threshold = VA. Range: 6/240 (newborn) to 6/60 (3 months) to 6/6 (36 months) |
| **Cardiff Acuity Cards** | 1-3 years | Pictures on cards; child looks toward picture side |
| **Catford Drum** | Infants | Small dot moves; tests for fixation and following |
| **Allen Figures** | 2-3 years | Seven cards with familiar objects (car, cake, bird); child names or points. Test distance: 3 meters |
| **Sheridan Gardiner HOTV Test** | 2-5 years | Four letters (H, O, T, V); matching task with keycard; child points to matching letter. Test at 6 meters (3 meters if fails 6/60) |
| **Lea Symbol Chart** | 2-5 years | Five symbols (apple, house, circle, square, etc.); matching task. Distance: 3 meters. Uses crowding phenomenon |
| **Kay Picture Test** | 2-3 years | Single picture per card based on Snellen principle; matching with keycard at 6 meters |
| **Stycar Visual Acuity Test** | Toddlers | Pictures of eating items or toys; matching task at 10-20 feet |
| **Sjogren Hand Test** | Toddlers | Isolated figure of a hand replaces the E; alternative to Tumbling E |
| **Broken Wheel Chart** | 2.5-5 years | Child identifies which wheel has a gap; simple, minimal verbal |
| **Landolt C Chart** | 3-6 years | Broken circles in different orientations; child points direction of gap; based on LogMAR |
| **Tumbling E Chart** | 3-6 years | Letter E in four directions; child shows direction with fingers |
| **Snellen Chart** | 5 years and older | Standard; requires knowing the alphabet |

**Candy Beads Test:** Near VA estimate at 40cm using candy beads of progressively smaller sizes.

**Dot Test:** Isolated dots in progressively smaller sizes; near VA at 40cm.

### Key numbers:
- VA at birth: 6/240 (20/800)
- VA at 1 month: 6/120 (20/400)
- VA at 6 months: 6/30 (20/200)
- VA at 1 year: 6/15 (20/50)
- VA at 3 years: 6/6 (20/20)

## 7. Refraction in Children

### Age Norms for Refractive Error (Grounds; Rushen and Speedwell)

| Age | Average Rx |
|---|---|
| 3 months | +3.00 D |
| 6 months | +2.50 D |
| 9 months | +2.25 D |
| 1 year | +2.00 D |
| 18 months | +1.50 D |
| 2 years | +1.25 D |
| 3 years | +1.00 D |
| 4 years | +0.50 D |
| 5 years | +0.50 D |
| 6 years | +0.50 D |

- Refractive error in infants is predominantly hyperopic; very few are myopic at birth.
- Most hyperopia resolves by age 2; substantially gone by age 4 (emmetropization).

### Refraction Methods for Children

**Cycloplegic Retinoscopy** — most important refraction method for children. Paralysis of the ciliary muscle (using tropicamide or cyclopentolate) eliminates accommodation and reveals full hyperopic error.

Indicated for:
- Unexplained poor VA
- Poor stereopsis
- Esophoria or manifest esotropia (presence or suspicion of squint)
- Over- or underactive accommodation
- Children of parents with high hyperopia or squint
- Strabismus or significant refractive error present

**Nott / Distance Retinoscopy (Static Retinoscopy)** — distance fixation with accommodation relaxed. May require assistant to keep child fixating.

**Dark Room Retinoscopy (Mohindra Technique)** — non-cycloplegic; totally dark room + dimmed retinoscope light; accommodation relaxes to tonic level; **add -1.25D to final result** to convert to manifest refraction.

**Dynamic Retinoscopy** — patient is actively accommodating to a near target attached to the retinoscope; evaluates the accommodative system rather than measuring refractive error.

### Prescribing Thresholds

**Significant refractive error in children >12-18 months (prescription indicated):**

| Error Type | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Hyperopia | >+3.50 D in any meridian |
| Hyperopia (age 4+) | >+2.00 D |
| Persistent astigmatism | >1.50 D |
| Persistent anisometropia | >1.00 D |
| Myopia | Any amount |

**Significant refractive error in children over 2 years:**
- Hyperopia: +3.00D or greater
- Myopia: -0.75D or greater
- Astigmatism (without hyperopia/myopia): 2.50D or greater

**Important prescribing principles:**
- Use partial correction of hyperopia on first prescription
- Correct anisometropia fully (both eyes)
- Reassess every 4 weeks during active amblyopia therapy
- Lens prescription is objective at first; refine with subjective responses as acuity improves

## 8. Binocular Vision Assessment

### MEM Retinoscopy (Monocular Estimated Method)

- Alternative method to measure lag or lead of accommodation at near
- Patient looks at near target attached to retinoscope
- **"With" movement = lag of accommodation** (positive lag = under-accommodating)
- **"Against" movement = lead of accommodation (negative lag)**
- **Normal value: +0.50 to +0.75 D**

| Finding | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|
| High lag (+0.75 to +2.50 D) | Esophoria at near; accommodative insufficiency/infacility/paresis; over-minused; uncorrected hyperope |
| High lag (+2.50 D) | Significantly uncorrected or over-minused; patient not actually accommodating |
| Low lag/lead (±0.25 D) | Exophoria at near; spasm of accommodation (pseudomyopia); uncorrected myope |

### Binocular Vision Assessment Tools by Age

**Infants:**
- **Hirschberg Test:** Penlight at 33-40cm; assess corneal light reflex position. Normal: centered symmetrically. 1mm displacement = ~7 prism diopters. Assesses presence/absence of strabismus.
- **Krimsky Test:** Prism placed over deviating eye until reflexes are symmetric. Measures exact amount of deviation when cover test cooperation is unavailable.
- **Bruckner Test:** Direct ophthalmoscope into both pupils simultaneously at 1 meter. Normal: equal red reflex brightness. Abnormal: brighter, whiter reflex in deviating or amblyopic eye.
- **Cover test:** Determines presence/absence of phoria or tropia.
- **Version test:** Tests conjugate eye movements (EOM function).
- **NPC:** Measures maximum convergence ability.

**Pre-school and School-age additions:**
- Positive and negative fusional vergences
- **Lang Stereotest:** Random dot stereogram on a card; no glasses needed; child must see embedded images to pass; confirms stereopsis and fusion
- **Accommodative amplitude and facility:** Push-up technique; flipper lens testing
- MEM retinoscopy

### Accommodation Types

**Accommodative Insufficiency:** Difficulty with amplitude of accommodation.
**Accommodative Infacility:** Difficulty with flexibility (changing focus distance to distance or distance to near).
**Accommodative Spasm/Excess:** Difficulty releasing accommodation; can cause pseudomyopia.

### Binocularity Components

1. **Sensory fusion:** Corresponding retinal areas are stimulated simultaneously
2. **Motor fusion:** Physical movement of eyes to achieve single image so sensory fusion can be maintained (vergence)

### Vision Therapy Filters

**Polaroid filters:**
- Used with vectographic targets and bar readers
- Right eye sees image not visible to left eye (and vice versa)
- Right eye → left target = BO demand (crossed fusion)
- Right eye → right target = BI demand (uncrossed fusion)

**Red/Green filters (Anaglyphs):**
- One eye = red filter; other eye = green/blue filter
- Red + Green = Black (when viewed through appropriate filter)
- Eye with red filter sees the green target; eye with green filter sees the red target

| Feature | Polaroid | Red/Green |
|---|---|---|
| Viewing conditions | More natural | Less natural |
| Shadow | May see shadow | No shadow |
| Head position | Head tilt influences | Not important |
| Stimulus to fusion | Stronger | Weaker |
| Depth perception | More vivid | Less vivid |
| Anisometropia risk | No | May induce |

**SILO ("Small In, Large Out"):** Perceived changes in size and location when vergence changes. Things appear smaller as they come closer (BI demand) and larger as they move away (BO demand). Used in vision therapy to reveal and treat suppression.

## 9. Visual Information Processing

The visual cognitive skills that allow processing and interpreting of meaning from visual information. Three broad components of the visual system:

1. **Visual Integrity** — eye health and refractive status; normal development of VA and ocular health
2. **Visual Efficiency** — oculomotor system, accommodation, vergence, sensory fusion. The input channel for visual information to the brain.
3. **Visual Information Processing** — visual perceptual skills, visual motor development, visual integration skills

### Eye Movement Types

**Fixation:** Directing the eye precisely at the object of regard; accurate fixation and fixation stability keep the world clear.

**Pursuits:** Slow, smooth eye movements that follow a moving object. Required for visually guided movement and sustained attention.

**Saccades:** Rapid eye movements jumping from one fixation point to another. Primary stimulus is a shift in attention. Peripheral awareness helps calculate the size of the jump needed.

**Signs of Ocular Motility Dysfunction:**
- Moving head excessively when reading
- Skipping lines, omitting or transposing words
- Losing place when reading; needing a finger/marker
- Confusion during return sweep
- Illusory text movement
- Poor ball-playing skills

### Visual Spatial Skills

**Bilateral Integration:** Awareness and use of both sides of the body separately and simultaneously (e.g., typing, riding a bicycle, multitasking). Tests: Body knowledge and control, Chalkboard circles.

**Laterality:** Self-awareness of two sides of the body as different from one another. Poor laterality = not using non-dominant hand, body rotating to avoid crossing midline. Tests: Piaget Right-Left Awareness Test, Stickman Test.

**Directionality:** Projecting right/left/up/down directions into external space. Important for reading (all letters must be directionally correct). Tests: Gardner Reversals Frequency Test, Jordan Left-Right Reversal Test.

**Signs of visual-spatial orientation skill deficiency:**
- Delayed gross motor development
- Confusion of right and left
- Letter reversals (b/d, p/q)
- Decreased coordination and balance
- Difficulty crossing midline
- Inconsistent dominant handedness

### Visual Perceptual Skills (Seven Types)

| Skill | Definition | Problems if Deficient |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual Discrimination** | Recognizing similarities and differences in shapes, size, colors, objects and patterns | Letter reversal, can't distinguish b/d; difficulty matching/sorting |
| **Visual Memory** | Remembering what has been seen; storing visual details in short-term memory | Difficulty copying from board, mixing upper/lowercase, poor sight word retention |
| **Visual Sequential Memory** | Remembering a series of forms in order (important for spelling and reading) | Cannot sequence letters in words, forgets steps in a task, poor spelling |
| **Form Constancy** | Recognizing forms regardless of size, orientation, or color | Can't recognize letters in different fonts; confusion between p/q/b/d |
| **Figure-Ground** | Selecting and processing an object from a busy background | Can't focus on a word on a page; distracted by surroundings; skips words |
| **Visual Closure** | Identifying an incomplete shape or object (filling in missing information) | Leaves out parts of words, cannot complete dot-to-dot, spelling errors |
| **Visual Spatial Relations** | Determining that one form or part of a form is in a different direction than others | Difficulty with "in, out, on, under"; loses place on a page; reversal errors |

### Skeffington's 4 Circles of Vision

Vision is at the core of the organism. Four overlapping circles:
1. **Antigravity** — ability to develop upright posture and gain balance. "Where am I?"
2. **Centering** — locating oneself in space using eyes and body. "Where is it?"
3. **Identification** — analyzing information through eyes and senses. "What is it?"
4. **Speech-Language Aspect** — communicating what is seen. "What about it?"

## 10. Amblyopia

### Definition

Decreased best-corrected VA in one or both eyes due to abnormal visual experience during the critical period, without structural pathology to account for the reduction. The visual cortex simply never received adequate stimulation from that eye during the critical period.

The critical question for management: **Is the visual cortex still plastic?**
- Yes (before age 7-8): treat aggressively — recovery is possible
- Partial (age 7-12): some improvement still possible with treatment
- No (after ~age 12): minimal improvement expected

### Types of Amblyopia

| Type | Cause | Key Feature | Board Clue |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strabismic** | Constant unilateral strabismus; brain suppresses deviating eye | Most common in clinical practice | Suppression on sensory testing |
| **Anisometropic** | Significant refractive difference (≥1.50D sphere or cyl) between eyes; blurred image in one eye | Often found at school screening | Normal alignment; found at VA screening |
| **Meridional** | Uncorrected astigmatism; one meridian consistently blurred | Specific orientation of VA reduction | Astigmatism in history |
| **Deprivation** | Media opacity (cataract, ptosis, corneal scar) blocks image | Worst prognosis; most urgent treatment | Dense amblyopia despite normal-looking exam |

**Combined Anisostrabismic Amblyopia:** When strabismus and anisometropia are both present, the strabismic eye generally has the higher refractive error.

### Amblyopia Depth
- Shallow: best-corrected VA 20/30 to 20/60
- Moderate: 20/80 to 20/200
- Deep: worse than 20/200

### Amblyopia Treatment Sequence

**Stage 1: Initial Therapy**

**Step 1 — Correct the refractive error (full correction of ametropia)**
- Wear full spectacle correction for **12-16 weeks** before adding occlusion
- Correct anisometropia fully (both eyes)
- Some anisometropic amblyopes improve with spectacles alone
- Reassess every 4 weeks; change lens if significant improvement occurs

**Step 2 — Occlusion (Patching)**

When to use:
- Direct occlusion: foveal fixators and unsteady eccentric fixators
- Inverse occlusion: steady eccentric fixators

Schedule:
- Constant/deep amblyopia (constant strabismus): full-time patching of fellow eye
- Intermittent strabismus: part-time patching
- Inefficient heterophoria: part-time

Duration per day:
- Mild amblyopia: 2 hours/day
- Moderate to severe: full-time (all waking hours)
- Monitor VA every 4-8 weeks

Modifications:
- If no improvement after 4 weeks of direct occlusion with unsteady eccentric fixation → switch to inverse
- If no improvement after 4 weeks in deep amblyopia → treat as constant strabismus; use full-time

**Step 3 — Atropine Penalization** (alternative to patching; better compliance for some families)
- 1% atropine in the fellow eye blurs near vision; forces use of amblyopic eye for near tasks
- Particularly useful when patching compliance is poor

**Step 4 — Active Vision Therapy**
- Added to lens + occlusion to accelerate improvement
- Home activities: looking at TV, reading books (large to small print), fast-pointing, eye-hand coordination games
- As acuity improves, progressively smaller targets are used to stimulate conscious effort
- Office-based therapy added in Stage 2 when initial home program shows compliance

**Dichoptic Treatment (emerging):** Video games or apps designed to stimulate both eyes simultaneously at different contrast levels; combines sensory suppression treatment with active stimulation.

### Amblyopia Treatment Goals

1. Improvement of VA (primary objective)
2. Elimination of eccentric fixation and abnormal localization
3. Resolution of the binocular anomaly producing the amblyopia

**Critical point:** If the binocular anomaly (strabismus/anisometropia) is not resolved, VA may improve during monocular therapy but will not be permanent. The underlying cause must be treated.

### Eccentric Fixation
- The fovea is not used for fixation; the eye fixates with a parafoveal locus instead
- Most often found in strabismic amblyopic eyes (Brock and Giver 1952)
- Managed with inverse occlusion (cover the amblyopic eye to force foveal fixation development)

## 11. Strabismus in Children

### Infantile Esotropia (Congenital ET)
- Onset: before 6 months of age
- Deviation: large angle (>30 prism diopters)
- Usually alternating fixation
- Associated: dissociated vertical deviation (DVD) and inferior oblique overaction
- Treatment: surgery (align before age 2 to establish binocularity window)

### Accommodative Esotropia
- Onset: 2-3 years (when accommodation demand increases as the child begins to look at near targets)
- Associated with: hyperopia (moderate to high)
- The hyperope must accommodate excessively → increased accommodative convergence → esotropia
- Treatment: **full cycloplegic refraction (full plus correction)** is the first and often definitive treatment; surgery only if residual deviation remains after maximal optical correction

### Pseudostrabismus
- Eyes appear crossed due to wide epicanthal folds or flat nasal bridge
- **Hirschberg test: reflexes are symmetric** (both reflexes centered) — no true deviation
- Treatment: none needed; resolves as nasal bridge develops
- Reassure parents

### Cover Test Interpretation
- **Cover-uncover test:** phoria (latent deviation — appears only when fusion is disrupted)
- **Unilateral cover test:** tropia (manifest deviation — present without disruption of fusion)

### Hirschberg Quantitative Estimates

| Reflex Position | Estimated Deviation |
|---|---|
| 1 mm nasal displacement | ~7 prism diopters |
| Edge of pupil (~2 mm) | ~15 prism diopters |
| Midway between pupil and limbus (~4 mm) | ~30 prism diopters |
| At the limbus (~6 mm) | ~45 prism diopters |

### Board Traps

| Trap | Correct Answer |
|---|---|
| Pseudostrabismus needs treatment | No. Hirschberg reflexes are symmetric; resolves as nasal bridge develops; no treatment needed |
| Patching is always the first step in amblyopia | No. Cycloplegic refraction + full spectacle wear (12-16 weeks) first; patching added only if VA not normalized |
| Amblyopia cannot be treated after age 7 | Partially wrong. Still some improvement possible up to ~age 12; minimal after that |
| Accommodative esotropia requires surgery | No. Full cycloplegic correction (full plus Rx) is first treatment; surgery only for residual deviation |
| Babinski toe fanning = pathological | Normal in infants under 2 years. Pathological only in adults. |
| MEM "with" movement = lead of accommodation | No. "With" = lag (under-accommodating). "Against" = lead (over-accommodating) |
| Suck reflex appears at birth | No. Begins at 32 weeks gestation; fully developed at 36 weeks; may be absent in very premature babies |
| The VA at 1 year should be 20/20 | No. 20/50 at 1 year. 20/20 not expected until age 3. |
| Moro reflex should integrate by 3 months | No. Moro integrates at 5-6 months. |
| Direct occlusion for all amblyopia | No. Inverse occlusion for steady eccentric fixators. Direct occlusion for foveal fixators and unsteady eccentric fixators. |
| Only strabismus causes amblyopia | No. Also: anisometropia, meridional (uncorrected astigmatism), and deprivation (cataract, ptosis, media opacity) |
| Worst prognosis amblyopia | Deprivation amblyopia (requires urgent removal of the opacity) |
