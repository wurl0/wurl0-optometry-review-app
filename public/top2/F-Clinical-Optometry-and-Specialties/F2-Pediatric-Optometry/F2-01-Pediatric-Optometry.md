## The Simple Version

Pediatric optometry is about understanding how a child's visual system develops from birth, what can go wrong at each stage, and how to examine and manage a child who cannot reliably answer "which is clearer, one or two?" The key challenge is that children cannot self-report well, so tests must be adapted to their developmental level. The key diseases are amblyopia and strabismus — and early detection is what determines outcome.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "If a 2-year-old cannot read a Snellen chart, how do we know if they can see?" This question opens the entire topic of pediatric VA testing.

---

## But Why?

**Why is early detection of amblyopia critical?**
The visual cortex has a critical period of plasticity (birth to approximately age 7–8, with peak sensitivity in years 1–3). During this window, the brain can be rewired — a deprived or suppressed eye can recover full function with the right treatment. After the critical period closes, the same treatment produces minimal improvement.

**Why does patching work in amblyopia?**
By covering the good eye, you force the brain to use the amblyopic eye. Repetitive stimulation during the critical period strengthens the visual cortex connections for that eye, recovering cortical representation and improving VA.

**Why does strabismus cause amblyopia?**
A misaligned eye projects a confused or diplopic image. The brain suppresses the deviating eye to avoid diplopia. Constant suppression means the visual cortex of the suppressed eye is never adequately stimulated → cortical cells for that eye atrophy → amblyopia develops.

## The Details

### Visual Development Milestones

| Age | Visual Milestone |
|---|---|
| Birth | No coordinated eye movement; responds to light; VA ~20/400–20/800 |
| 1 month | Fixes briefly on a face; irregular fixation |
| 2 months | Follows large objects; tracks horizontally |
| 3 months | Fovea fully formed; fixes and follows well |
| 3–4 months | Eyes should be straight (any persistent deviation after this age is abnormal) |
| 6 months | Fixes for 1–2 minutes; develops convergence and depth perception |
| 1 year | VA ~20/70 |
| 3 years | VA ~20/20; accommodation fully functional |
| 7–12 years | Age of emmetropization |

**Refraction at birth:** Neonates are typically hyperopic (+2.00 to +3.00D). This gradually resolves through emmetropization.

---

### Pediatric VA Testing Methods

| Method | Age | Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Visually Evoked Potentials (VEP) | Any age (infants) | Brain response to reversing checkerboard pattern — objective, no verbal response needed |
| Preferential Looking (PL) / Teller Acuity Cards | Newborn to 2 years | Infant preferentially looks at patterned vs. blank stimulus — spatial frequency at threshold = VA |
| Optokinetic Nystagmus (OKN) drum | 0–3 months | Involuntary nystagmus when moving stripes pass before the eyes — present = seeing |
| Cardiff Acuity Cards | 1–3 years | Pictures on cards; child looks toward the picture |
| HOTV / Lea Symbols | 2–5 years | Matching task — child matches letter or symbol without naming |
| Allen figures | 2–3 years | Familiar pictures (car, cake, bird); child names or points |
| Snellen chart | 5 years and older (when alphabet is known) | Standard |

### Primitive Reflexes (Board-Relevant)

Primitive reflexes are present at birth and integrate (disappear) by specific ages. Their persistence beyond the expected integration age or absence when expected to be present both signal developmental problems.

| Reflex | Emerges | Integrates | If Persistent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fear Paralysis Reflex | 5 GW | 9–12 GW | Anxieties, OCD tendencies |
| Root Reflex | Birth | 3–4 months | Feeding problems |
| Suck Reflex | 32 GW | 2–4 months | Premature infants may lack it |
| Moro (Startle) | Birth | 5–6 months | ADHD, sensory processing difficulties |
| ATNR (Tonic Neck) | Birth | 6–7 months | Difficulty with reading/writing if not integrated |
| STNR | 6–9 months | 9–11 months | Posture, near vision problems |

**Visual significance of unintegrated reflexes:** ATNR links head turning to arm extension — if unintegrated, every head turn triggers arm movement, disrupting fixation and tracking. This can mimic convergence insufficiency or tracking disorders.

### Amblyopia

**Definition:** Decreased best-corrected VA in one or both eyes due to abnormal visual experience during the critical period, without structural pathology to explain the reduction.

**Types:**

| Type | Cause | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Strabismic amblyopia | Constant unilateral strabismus; brain suppresses deviating eye | Most common in clinical practice |
| Anisometropic amblyopia | Significant refractive difference between eyes; blurred image in one eye | Often discovered during school screening |
| Meridional amblyopia | Uncorrected astigmatism; one meridian consistently blurred | Specific orientation of VA reduction |
| Deprivation amblyopia | Media opacity (cataract, ptosis, corneal scar) blocking image | Worst prognosis if not treated urgently |

**Diagnosis:**
- VA worse in one eye (or both in bilateral deprivation) with best correction
- No pathology to account for the reduction
- History of amblyogenic factor during critical period

**Treatment:**
1. Correct the refractive error first — full time spectacle wear for 12–16 weeks before starting occlusion
2. **Patching** of the fellow eye: 2 hours/day (mild) to full-time (severe) depending on depth
3. **Atropine penalization** (1% atropine in the fellow eye): blurs the good eye for near — alternative to patching
4. **Dichoptic treatment** (binocular stimulation): emerging; video games/apps designed to stimulate both eyes simultaneously

**Critical period:** Best results before age 7; still some improvement up to age 12; minimal after.

---

### Strabismus — Pediatric

**Infantile esotropia (congenital ET):**
- Onset before 6 months
- Large-angle esotropia (>30 pd)
- Alternating fixation pattern
- Associated with dissociated vertical deviation (DVD) and inferior oblique overaction
- Treatment: surgery after age 6 months (most before 2 years) to establish binocularity

**Accommodative esotropia:**
- Onset 2–3 years (when accommodation demand increases)
- Associated with hyperopia
- Correcting the hyperopia with full plus correction reduces or eliminates the deviation
- Treatment: full cycloplegic refraction → spectacles → check if deviation resolves

**Pseudostrabismus:**
- Eyes appear crossed due to wide epicanthal folds or negative angle kappa — but Hirschberg test shows symmetric reflexes
- Reassure parents; no treatment needed; resolves as the nasal bridge develops

---

### Pediatric Examination Techniques

**Hirschberg corneal reflex test:**
- Shine penlight at equal distance between eyes
- Normal: both reflexes centered symmetrically in each pupil
- Abnormal: reflex displaced from center → strabismus
- 1 mm of displacement = approximately 7 prism diopters (or 15 pd depending on source)

**Brückner test:**
- Shine direct ophthalmoscope into both pupils simultaneously from 1 meter
- Normal: equal red reflex brightness in both eyes
- Abnormal: brighter reflex in deviating or amblyopic eye

**Krimsky test:**
- Prism placed over the deviating eye until the corneal reflexes are symmetric
- Measures deviation when the patient will not cooperate with the cover test

**Lang stereotest:**
- Random dot stereograms on a card; no glasses needed
- Child must see the embedded images to pass → confirms stereopsis → confirms fusion

|---|---|---|---|
| Cause | Constant unilateral strabismus | Refractive difference ≥ 1.50D sphere or 1.50D cylinder | Media opacity (cataract, ptosis) |
| Eye appearance | Deviated eye | Normal alignment | May look normal or have lid/cornea problem |
| Depth | Moderate | Mild to moderate | Severe (worst prognosis) |

| Board clue | Suppression on sensory testing | Found on school screening | Dense amblyopia despite normal ocular exam |

## Memory Anchors

**DEVSS:** Types of amblyopia — **D**eprivation, **E**xotropic (any strabismus), **V**isual form deprivation (same as D), **S**trabismic, **S**ensory

**"3–4 months rule":** Eyes must be straight by 3–4 months. Any persistent deviation after this is pathological.

**Patching sequence:** Full Rx first → then patch. Never patch without correcting refractive error first — you may be patching the wrong eye if the Rx changes the preferred eye.

---

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** Pseudostrabismus needs treatment — Wrong. It resolves as the nasal bridge widens; no treatment needed. Hirschberg shows symmetric reflexes.
- **Trap:** Amblyopia cannot be treated after age 7 — Partially wrong. Treatment is less effective after age 7, but improvement is still possible up to ~age 12; after that, minimal gains.
- **Trap:** Patching is always the first step in amblyopia treatment — Wrong. Cycloplegic refraction and full spectacle wear for 12–16 weeks comes first. Patching is added only if spectacles alone don't normalize VA.
- **Trap:** Accommodative esotropia requires surgery — Wrong. Full cycloplegic refraction (full plus correction) is the first treatment. Surgery is only needed if residual deviation remains after maximal optical correction.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 4-year-old with a best corrected VA of 20/80 in the left eye and 20/20 in the right eye is found to have a 3.50D hyperopic difference between the eyes. What is the most likely diagnosis and first treatment step?

- A. Strabismic amblyopia; start immediate patching of the right eye
- B. Anisometropic amblyopia; prescribe full cycloplegic correction and observe for 12–16 weeks
- C. Deprivation amblyopia; refer for surgical removal of media opacity
- D. Pseudoamblyopia; reassure and retest in 1 year

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Anisometropic amblyopia — significant refractive difference (> 1.50D) between eyes, reduced VA in the more ametropic eye, normal alignment. First step is always cycloplegic refraction and full spectacle correction. Patching is added only if VA does not normalize after 12–16 weeks of spectacle wear.

</details>

---

**2.** A parent brings in a 2-year-old whose eyes appear to cross. On Hirschberg testing, both corneal reflexes are centered symmetrically. What is the most likely diagnosis?

- A. Infantile esotropia — refer for surgical alignment
- B. Accommodative esotropia — prescribe full plus correction
- C. Pseudostrabismus — reassure; no treatment needed
- D. Strabismic amblyopia — patch the preferred eye

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** Symmetric Hirschberg reflexes rule out true strabismus. The appearance of crossed eyes with normal reflexes is pseudostrabismus, caused by wide epicanthal folds. It resolves as the nasal bridge develops. No treatment is needed.

</details>
