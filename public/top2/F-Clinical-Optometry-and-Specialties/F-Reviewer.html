F1 — Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agents (DPA)

The Simple Version

DPAs are drugs that optometrists are legally authorized to use under RA 8050 to examine the eye more thoroughly. They dilate the pupil, paralyze accommodation, stain the cornea, numb the surface, or constrict the pupil — depending on which agent you use and why. Five agents are approved. Each one has a specific purpose, a receptor mechanism, and a set of conditions where you must not use it.

Teaching angle: Ask: "If DPAs are just eye drops, why do we need a law to authorize their use?" Because these drugs affect the autonomic nervous system and can cause systemic reactions — they are pharmaceutical agents with real risks, not just saline.

But Why?

Why does tropicamide dilate the pupil?

Because it blocks the M3 muscarinic receptor on the sphincter pupillae.

Why does blocking M3 cause dilation?

Because the sphincter is tonically innervated by the parasympathetic system (CN3 → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves → M3). Block M3 → sphincter stops contracting → dilator pupillae (sympathetically innervated, unopposed) pulls the iris open.

Why does cycloplegia accompany some mydriatics?

The ciliary muscle that controls accommodation is also innervated by M3. The same muscarinic blockade that dilates the pupil also paralyzes accommodation — the ciliary muscle relaxes, zonules tighten, lens flattens, and accommodation is abolished.

Why does phenylephrine dilate without cycloplegia?

Phenylephrine is an α1 agonist — it stimulates the dilator pupillae directly (α1 receptor) without touching the parasympathetic pathway. The ciliary muscle has no α1 receptors; it is purely under parasympathetic (M3) control. So phenylephrine produces mydriasis with zero effect on accommodation.

The Five Approved DPAs

A — Mydriatic WITHOUT Cycloplegia

1. Phenylephrine 2.5%

  • Class: α1-adrenergic agonist (sympathomimetic)
  • Receptor: α1 on dilator pupillae
  • Action: contracts dilator → mydriasis; no effect on ciliary muscle (no cycloplegia)
  • Onset: 15–20 minutes; duration 4–6 hours
  • Use: dilation for fundus exam when accommodation must be preserved (e.g., elderly, glaucoma suspect)
  • Contraindication: narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma (dilation can push iris against the trabecular meshwork, blocking aqueous outflow → acute angle-closure crisis)
  • Systemic risk: hypertension, tachycardia (α1 systemic vasoconstriction) — use 2.5%, not 10%

B — Mydriatics WITH Cycloplegia

2. Tropicamide 0.5% / 1%

  • Class: anticholinergic (competitive muscarinic antagonist)
  • Receptor: M3 on sphincter pupillae AND ciliary muscle
  • Action: blocks M3 → sphincter relaxes → mydriasis; ciliary muscle relaxes → cycloplegia
  • Onset: 20–30 minutes; duration 4–6 hours
  • The preferred DPA in pregnancy (FDA Category C, but tropicamide is chosen because of shortest duration and least systemic absorption among cycloplegics)
  • Use: dilated fundus exam, cycloplegic refraction (especially in pseudomyopia)
  • Contraindication: same as phenylephrine — narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma

3. Cyclopentolate 0.5% / 1% / 2%

  • Class: anticholinergic
  • Receptor: M3
  • Stronger and longer cycloplegia than tropicamide
  • Onset: 30–60 minutes; duration 12–24 hours
  • Use: cycloplegic refraction in children (more reliable cycloplegia needed); suspected pseudomyopia
  • Contraindication: narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma
  • Pediatric caution: can cause CNS effects in young children (hallucinations, behavioral changes) — more common with 2% in infants; monitor for 30 minutes post-instillation

C — Local Anesthetic Agents

4. Proparacaine 0.5%

  • Class: ester-type local anesthetic
  • Mechanism: blocks sodium channels on corneal sensory nerve endings → topical anesthesia
  • Onset: 30 seconds; duration ~15 minutes
  • Use: tonometry, foreign body removal, corneal staining, contact lens fitting procedures requiring manipulation
  • Never prescribed for home use — chronic use prevents pain sensation → patients unknowingly damage the cornea (neurotrophic keratopathy)
  • Alternative esters: oxybuprocaine, benoxinate (0.4%)

D — Diagnostic Dye

5. Fluorescein strips

  • Class: xanthene dye
  • Mechanism: fluoresces green-yellow under cobalt blue light (465 nm) — accumulates where epithelial cells are missing
  • Use: corneal abrasion detection, contact lens fluorescein pattern assessment, applanation tonometry (with proparacaine)
  • Strips preferred over drops (less contamination risk)
  • Do not use with soft CLs in place — fluorescein permanently stains hydrogel CLs; must remove CLs first

The DPA Clinical Examination Workflow

Step 1: Take full history
 — Confirm no contraindications (narrow angle, allergy, systemic disease)

Step 2: Baseline assessment BEFORE instillation
 — Anterior chamber depth (oblique flashlight / Van Herick method)
 — Undilated fundus check if possible
 — Document pupil size and reactivity

Step 3: Instill DPA
 — Lower conjunctival fornix
 — Tip does not touch eye or skin
 — Apply nasolacrimal occlusion (NLO) immediately after instillation

Step 4: Nasolacrimal Occlusion (NLO)
 — Press the inner corner of the eye (lacrimal sac area) for 2 minutes
 — Prevents the drug from draining into the nasolacrimal duct and reaching systemic circulation
 — Most important step for minimizing systemic side effects

Step 5: Wait for onset
 — Tropicamide: 20–30 min
 — Cyclopentolate: 30–60 min
 — Phenylephrine: 15–20 min

Step 6: Confirm dilation and perform examination

Step 7: Document
 — Drug used, concentration, time instilled, eye(s) treated, patient response
 — Inform patient: blurred near vision and photosensitivity until drug wears off
 — Never drive immediately after dilation

DPA in Pregnancy (Gap Lecture 08 Summary)

  • All approved DPAs are FDA Category C in pregnancy (risk cannot be ruled out)
  • If dilation is clinically necessary during pregnancy: tropicamide is the preferred agent
  • Rationale: shortest duration of action (4–6 hours vs. 24 hours for cyclopentolate); least systemic exposure; most data available
  • Always apply NLO technique to minimize systemic absorption

Practice Questions

1. A 32-year-old pregnant patient needs a dilated fundus exam. Which DPA is most appropriate?

  • A. Cyclopentolate 1%
  • B. Phenylephrine 10%
  • C. Tropicamide 0.5%
  • D. Proparacaine 0.5%
Answer

C. Tropicamide 0.5%. All DPAs are FDA Category C in pregnancy. When dilation is clinically necessary, tropicamide is the preferred agent because it has the shortest duration of action and least systemic exposure. Phenylephrine 10% is avoided due to its systemic cardiovascular risks. Cyclopentolate has a longer duration and more systemic exposure. Proparacaine is a local anesthetic, not a mydriatic.

2. Which of the following best explains why phenylephrine produces mydriasis without cycloplegia?

  • A. It acts on M3 receptors selectively on the sphincter pupillae only
  • B. It acts on α1 receptors on the dilator pupillae; the ciliary muscle has no α1 receptors
  • C. It blocks acetylcholinesterase, preventing ACh from contracting the ciliary muscle
  • D. It directly relaxes the ciliary muscle through a non-receptor mechanism
Answer

B. Phenylephrine is an α1 agonist that stimulates the dilator pupillae. The ciliary muscle is under purely parasympathetic (M3) control — it has no α1 receptors. Therefore, phenylephrine produces mydriasis without any effect on accommodation. Options A and D are incorrect mechanisms; option C describes indirect cholinomimetics, not phenylephrine.

F2 — Pediatric Optometry

The Simple Version

Pediatric optometry is about understanding how a child's visual system develops from birth, what can go wrong at each stage, and how to examine and manage a child who cannot reliably answer "which is clearer, one or two?" The key challenge is that children cannot self-report well, so tests must be adapted to their developmental level. The key diseases are amblyopia and strabismus — and early detection is what determines outcome.

Teaching angle: Ask: "If a 2-year-old cannot read a Snellen chart, how do we know if they can see?" This question opens the entire topic of pediatric VA testing.

But Why?

Why is early detection of amblyopia critical?

The visual cortex has a critical period of plasticity (birth to approximately age 7–8, with peak sensitivity in years 1–3). During this window, the brain can be rewired — a deprived or suppressed eye can recover full function with the right treatment. After the critical period closes, the same treatment produces minimal improvement.

Why does patching work in amblyopia?

By covering the good eye, you force the brain to use the amblyopic eye. Repetitive stimulation during the critical period strengthens the visual cortex connections for that eye, recovering cortical representation and improving VA.

Why does strabismus cause amblyopia?

A misaligned eye projects a confused or diplopic image. The brain suppresses the deviating eye to avoid diplopia. Constant suppression means the visual cortex of the suppressed eye is never adequately stimulated → cortical cells for that eye atrophy → amblyopia develops.

Amblyopia

Definition: Decreased best-corrected VA in one or both eyes due to abnormal visual experience during the critical period, without structural pathology to explain the reduction.

Types:

Diagnosis:

  • VA worse in one eye (or both in bilateral deprivation) with best correction
  • No pathology to account for the reduction
  • History of amblyogenic factor during critical period

Treatment:

  1. Correct the refractive error first — full time spectacle wear for 12–16 weeks before starting occlusion
  2. Patching of the fellow eye: 2 hours/day (mild) to full-time (severe) depending on depth
  3. Atropine penalization (1% atropine in the fellow eye): blurs the good eye for near — alternative to patching
  4. Dichoptic treatment (binocular stimulation): emerging; video games/apps designed to stimulate both eyes simultaneously

Critical period: Best results before age 7; still some improvement up to age 12; minimal after.

Strabismus — Pediatric

Infantile esotropia (congenital ET):

  • Onset before 6 months
  • Large-angle esotropia (>30 pd)
  • Alternating fixation pattern
  • Associated with dissociated vertical deviation (DVD) and inferior oblique overaction
  • Treatment: surgery after age 6 months (most before 2 years) to establish binocularity

Accommodative esotropia:

  • Onset 2–3 years (when accommodation demand increases)
  • Associated with hyperopia
  • Correcting the hyperopia with full plus correction reduces or eliminates the deviation
  • Treatment: full cycloplegic refraction → spectacles → check if deviation resolves

Pseudostrabismus:

  • Eyes appear crossed due to wide epicanthal folds or negative angle kappa — but Hirschberg test shows symmetric reflexes
  • Reassure parents; no treatment needed; resolves as the nasal bridge develops

Pediatric Examination Techniques

Hirschberg corneal reflex test:

  • Shine penlight at equal distance between eyes
  • Normal: both reflexes centered symmetrically in each pupil
  • Abnormal: reflex displaced from center → strabismus
  • 1 mm of displacement = approximately 7 prism diopters (or 15 pd depending on source)

Brückner test:

  • Shine direct ophthalmoscope into both pupils simultaneously from 1 meter
  • Normal: equal red reflex brightness in both eyes
  • Abnormal: brighter reflex in deviating or amblyopic eye

Krimsky test:

  • Prism placed over the deviating eye until the corneal reflexes are symmetric
  • Measures deviation when the patient will not cooperate with the cover test

Lang stereotest:

  • Random dot stereograms on a card; no glasses needed
  • Child must see the embedded images to pass → confirms stereopsis → confirms fusion

Practice Questions

1. A 4-year-old with a best corrected VA of 20/80 in the left eye and 20/20 in the right eye is found to have a 3.50D hyperopic difference between the eyes. What is the most likely diagnosis and first treatment step?

  • A. Strabismic amblyopia; start immediate patching of the right eye
  • B. Anisometropic amblyopia; prescribe full cycloplegic correction and observe for 12–16 weeks
  • C. Deprivation amblyopia; refer for surgical removal of media opacity
  • D. Pseudoamblyopia; reassure and retest in 1 year
Answer

B. Anisometropic amblyopia — significant refractive difference (> 1.50D) between eyes, reduced VA in the more ametropic eye, normal alignment. First step is always cycloplegic refraction and full spectacle correction. Patching is added only if VA does not normalize after 12–16 weeks of spectacle wear.

2. A parent brings in a 2-year-old whose eyes appear to cross. On Hirschberg testing, both corneal reflexes are centered symmetrically. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • A. Infantile esotropia — refer for surgical alignment
  • B. Accommodative esotropia — prescribe full plus correction
  • C. Pseudostrabismus — reassure; no treatment needed
  • D. Strabismic amblyopia — patch the preferred eye
Answer

C. Symmetric Hirschberg reflexes rule out true strabismus. The appearance of crossed eyes with normal reflexes is pseudostrabismus, caused by wide epicanthal folds. It resolves as the nasal bridge develops. No treatment is needed.

F3 — Geriatric Optometry

The Simple Version

Geriatric optometry is about understanding how aging changes every part of the eye and visual system, how systemic diseases common in the elderly (diabetes, hypertension, thyroid disease) manifest in the eye, and how to manage these patients including ophthalmic correction for their special needs. The most tested areas are AMD, diabetic retinopathy, hypertensive retinopathy, and glaucoma in the elderly — because these are the leading causes of irreversible vision loss in the aging population.

Teaching angle: Ask: "If your patient is 75 years old and has both diabetes and glaucoma, where do you start the eye exam and why?" This forces students to think about systemic-ocular links and prioritization.

But Why?

Why does the lens yellow and harden with age?

Lens proteins (crystallins) undergo progressive cross-linking and oxidation throughout life. There is no turnover of lens fiber cells — every fiber laid down from embryonic development remains. Over decades, these proteins denature, yellow (from accumulation of UV-generated chromophores), and become less elastic. The loss of elasticity is presbyopia; the yellowing and opacity formation is nuclear cataract.

Why does diabetic retinopathy cause vision loss through neovascularization?

Chronic hyperglycemia damages retinal capillary endothelial cells and pericytes. Pericyte loss destabilizes vessel walls → microaneurysms and non-perfusion. Non-perfused retina secretes VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor) → signals for new vessel growth. But new vessels (NVD/NVE) are fragile, structurally defective, and grow into the vitreous → vitreous hemorrhage, traction retinal detachment → severe vision loss.

F4 — Neuro-Optometry and Rehabilitation

The Simple Version

Neuro-optometry is about understanding what happens to vision when the brain or cranial nerves are damaged. The board tests three main areas: cranial nerve palsies (which muscle is affected and how to localize the lesion), nystagmus (classify the waveform and identify the cause), and cortical visual impairment (the brain cannot process what the intact eye sends). Mastery here depends on knowing the anatomy of each nerve, because the presentation tells you exactly where the lesion is.

Teaching angle: Ask: "If a patient walks in with one eye drifting inward and double vision that is worse at distance, which nerve is affected and how do you know?" Walking through this builds the entire neuro-optometry framework.

But Why?

Why does CN4 palsy cause vertical diplopia on downgaze?

The superior oblique's primary action in the adducted position is depression. When the eye looks down and in (as in reading), the SO is the main depressor. If CN4 is paretic, this depression fails → the eye stays high → vertical diplopia, worst on downgaze toward the nose. This is why patients tilt their head to the opposite shoulder — to rotate the affected eye into a position where it sees straight without the SO.

Why is CN6 called a "false localizing sign"?

CN6 has the longest intracranial course of the ocular motor nerves, traveling along the clivus before entering the cavernous sinus. Elevated intracranial pressure stretches the nerve anywhere along this course, not just at the actual source of the ICP rise. So a CN6 palsy doesn't tell you where the lesion is — it tells you the ICP is elevated.

The Details

CN4 — Trochlear Nerve Palsy

What it innervates: Superior oblique (SO) only — one nerve, one muscle.

Why CN4 is special:

  • Only CN to exit the dorsal (posterior) brainstem
  • Longest intracranial course = most vulnerable to trauma
  • CN4 nucleus is contralateral to the muscle it innervates (right CN4 nucleus → left SO)

Clinical triad:

SignMechanism
Hypertropia (affected eye higher)SO underacts → eye rides up; contralateral IO compensates poorly
Head tilt toward opposite shoulderCompensatory — keeps eyes aligned without using the paretic meridian
Chin depressionAvoids downgaze where SO is most needed

Parks-Bielschowsky 3-Step Test:

  1. Which eye is hypertropic in primary gaze? → Identifies the hypertropic eye
  2. Does hypertropia worsen in right gaze or left gaze? → Identifies which pair of vertically acting muscles is involved
  3. Bielschowsky head tilt test — tilt toward the hypertropic eye: if hypertropia worsens → CN4 palsy confirmed

Why Bielschowsky works: Tilting toward the hypertropic eye reflexively intorts that eye. The SO handles this intorsion. If SO is paretic, the superior rectus takes over intorsion — but also elevates the eye → hypertropia worsens.

Causes:

  • Congenital: most common overall (look for old photos with head tilt)
  • Trauma: most common acquired cause (vulnerable at tentorial edge)
  • Microvascular: DM, HTN — resolves in 3–6 months
  • Bilateral CN4 palsy → suspect raised ICP or severe head trauma

Management: Prism (base-down over hypertropic eye); observation for microvascular; surgery for persistent cases.

CN6 — Abducens Nerve Palsy

What it innervates: Lateral rectus (LR) only.

Cardinal sign: Esotropia of the affected eye (unopposed MR action when LR is paretic)

Clinical features:

FeatureDescription
EsotropiaWorse at distance than near; incomitant
Failure of abductionCannot cross midline toward temple
Horizontal diplopiaWorse on ipsilateral gaze
Face turn toward paretic sideCompensatory; moves eye away from its action field

False localizing sign: CN6 palsy with elevated ICP — nerve is compressed along its long clivus course regardless of lesion location.

Causes:

  • Microvascular (most common in adults): DM, HTN — resolves in 3–4 months
  • Raised ICP: false localizing
  • Trauma: basilar skull fracture
  • Gradenigo's syndrome: CN6 palsy + ipsilateral facial pain + otitis media → petrous apex
  • Children: rule out pontine glioma (urgent MRI)

Key distinction from accommodative esotropia:

  • CN6 palsy: acquired, incomitant, restricted abduction
  • Accommodative ET: comitant, full ductions, associated with hyperopia

Management: Patch or Fresnel prism; observation; botulinum toxin to MR; surgery for permanent cases.

CN3 — Oculomotor Nerve Palsy (Brief)

What it innervates: SR, MR, IR, IO (4 recti + IO), levator palpebrae, pupillary sphincter (parasympathetic)

Complete CN3 palsy:

  • Ptosis (levator paralysis)
  • Eye down and out (SO and LR unopposed)
  • Fixed dilated pupil (mydriasis — parasympathetic involved)
  • Diplopia

Pupil-involving vs. pupil-sparing:

  • Pupil-involved + painful = posterior communicating artery aneurysm (PCA aneurysm) — neurosurgical emergency
  • Pupil-sparing = microvascular (DM, HTN) — monitor and resolve in 3 months

Board pearl: Any third nerve palsy with pupil involvement = aneurysm until proven otherwise. This is a neurosurgical emergency.

Nystagmus

Definition: Involuntary, rhythmic oscillation of the eyes.

  • Onset < 6 months
  • Horizontal in all gaze positions (uniplanar)
  • Dampens with convergence
  • Null point → abnormal head position (AHP) is therapeutic
  • Associated: albinism, congenital cataract, aniridia, achromatopsia

  • Only appears or worsens when one eye is covered
  • Fast phase toward the viewing eye
  • Associated with infantile esotropia

Cortical Visual Impairment (CVI)

Definition: Vision loss from retrogeniculate pathway damage (optic radiations / visual cortex) with structurally normal eyes (or vision worse than ocular pathology explains).

Most common cause of visual impairment in children in developed countries.

Causes:

  • Perinatal hypoxia-ischemia (HIE, periventricular leukomalacia) — most common
  • Premature birth (white matter injury)
  • Hydrocephalus, TBI, CNS infections, metabolic disorders

Roman-Kay 10 Behavioral Features of CVI:

  1. Preference for color (especially red and yellow)
  2. Need for movement to sustain attention
  3. Visual latency (delayed visual response)
  4. Visual field preference (lower fields often impaired)
  5. Difficulty with visual complexity (crowded/cluttered environments)
  6. Light-gazing; non-purposeful gaze
  7. Difficulty with distance viewing
  8. Atypical visual reflexes
  9. Difficulty with novel visual stimuli
  10. Absence of visually guided reach (severe cases)

Diagnosis: Clinical (history + Roman-Kay features) + MRI (retrogeniculate injury) + VEP (delayed)

Management: No cure; multidisciplinary rehabilitation; environmental modification (reduce visual clutter; use bright colors; single objects); functional vision training.

F5 — Occupational and Environmental Optometry

The Simple Version

Occupational optometry is about ensuring workers can see safely and efficiently in their specific work environment. It covers three things: understanding the visual demands of different jobs, identifying ocular hazards in the workplace, and prescribing the right protective eyewear and corrective devices for those demands. Environmental optometry is the broader principle — how the visual system interacts with any environment, including the workplace.

Teaching angle: Ask: "A welder, an office worker on a computer all day, and a surgeon all have different visual demands. What do they share that makes them all occupational optometry patients?"

F6 — Sports Vision

The Simple Version

Sports vision is the branch of optometry that evaluates and enhances an athlete's visual performance. The eyes are the primary input system for sport — if the visual input is inaccurate, delayed, or incomplete, motor output (catching, hitting, throwing) suffers. Sports vision goes beyond measuring 20/20 acuity. It tests dynamic acuity, depth perception, reaction time, eye-hand coordination, and peripheral awareness — the visual skills specific to athletic performance.

Teaching angle: Ask: "A baseball batter with 20/20 VA still can't hit a fastball consistently. What visual skill is likely deficient and how would you test it?"

The Details

Key Definitions

Sports VisionProgram addressing all visual needs of an athlete — assessment, correction, protection, and enhancement
SightAbility to see (sensory input; organ = eye)
VisionAbility to gain meaning from what the eyes see (brain integration)
Dynamic Visual AcuityVA for a moving target or while the observer is moving
Static Visual AcuityVA for a stationary target
Basic premise: Eyes input→brain integrates + other senses→body responds (arms, legs, balance). If the eye's message is inaccurate, incomplete, or delayed→performance suffers.

Protective Eyewear in Sports

Why it matters: Sports account for a significant proportion of eye injuries in young people. Most are preventable with proper protective eyewear.

ASTM International Standards (F803):

  • Racquet sports (squash, racquetball, tennis): require ASTM F803-certified polycarbonate lenses
  • Hockey: requires full face shield or certified hockey visor
  • Baseball/softball: batting helmets with polycarbonate face guards

Key material: Polycarbonate lenses — most impact resistant; absorb UV inherently; 30–40% thinner than CR-39 for the same power.

Contact lenses in sports:

  • Soft CLs: appropriate for most sports; no protective function; daily disposables preferred for sports use (hygiene, cost of loss)
  • RGP: may dislodge; not ideal for contact sports
  • CLs do not provide UV or impact protection — protective eyewear is still required

F7 — Low Vision and Rehabilitation

The Simple Version

Low vision is vision loss that cannot be fully corrected with standard glasses, contact lenses, or medical treatment, yet leaves some usable vision. The goal of low vision care is not to restore 20/20 — it is to maximize the patient's functional vision for their specific daily tasks. This requires choosing the right magnification device, training the patient to use eccentric viewing if the fovea is damaged, and modifying the environment to support whatever vision remains.

Teaching angle: Ask: "A patient with 20/200 VA wants to read the newspaper. What magnification do they need, and where does that number come from?"

The Details

Key Definitions

WHO definition of Blindness: VA ≤ 20/200 (or equivalent) in the better eye after best correction; OR visual field < 10° from fixation

Legal Blindness (US standard, also used in PH exams): VA ≤ 20/200 with best correction in the better eye; OR visual field ≤ 20° in the widest meridian

WHO definition of Low Vision: VA < 20/70 (6/18) but > 20/200 in the better eye after best correction; OR visual field < 20° from fixation, but some usable vision remains

Functional definition (most clinically relevant): A person with low vision cannot fully remediate their vision impairment with conventional spectacles, CLs, or medical intervention, and this causes restriction in daily life.

Moderate< 20/60 – 20/200
Severe< 20/200 – 20/400
Blindness< 20/400 (worse than 3/60)

Common Causes of Low Vision

Adults over 50:

  1. Cataract (62.6%) — most common globally; avoidable
  2. Refractive error (19.7%)
  3. Glaucoma (5.8%)
  4. Posterior segment disorders (4.7%)
  5. Corneal opacity (0.9%)

Children:

  1. Refractive error (33.3%)
  2. Congenital anomalies (25%)
  3. Retinal degeneration (16.7%)
  4. Vitamin A deficiency (8.3%)

Global: 285 million visually impaired worldwide; 39 million blind; 246 million low vision; 80% of blindness is avoidable.

Low Vision Rehabilitation Concepts

Rehabilitation vs. Habilitation:

  • Rehabilitation: Re-teaching a skill to someone who had it and lost it (acquired vision loss)
  • Habilitation: Teaching a skill to someone who never had it (congenital vision loss)

The rehabilitation chain:

Visual disorder→visual impairment (functional limitation)→visual disability (skill reduction)→visual handicap (psychosocial/economic consequence)

The goal of rehabilitation is to break the chain before it reaches handicap — maximize remaining function at the impairment level.

Magnification — Principles and Calculation

Why magnification works: Enlarging the retinal image allows it to be processed by remaining functioning photoreceptors outside the damaged fovea.

Four types of magnification:

TypeHow It WorksExample
Relative sizeMake the object physically largerLarge-print books, enlarged font on phone
Relative distanceMove closer to the objectBring reading material from 40 cm to 10 cm
AngularUse a lens or optical device to enlarge the visual angleMagnifiers, telescopes
ElectronicDigitally enlarge and enhanceCCTV, tablet with zoom

Calculating required magnification:

M = Required VA / Present Near VA

Or using dioptric method:
M = Required add power / 4
(Reference standard: 4D = 1x at 25 cm)

Example:

  • Patient best near VA: N24 at 40 cm
  • Task demand: N8
  • M = 24/8 = 3×
  • Required add: 3 × 4 = +12.00D
  • Working distance: 100/12 = 8.3 cm

Eccentric Viewing (EV) Training

When the fovea is damaged (AMD, macular scar, macular dystrophy), the patient must learn to use a healthier peripheral retinal area as the preferred retinal locus (PRL).

Training methods:

  • Steady eye strategy: teach patient to hold their eye steadily on a peripheral fixation point
  • Clock face method: describe where the scotoma is on a clock face; teach patient to look "off-center" by a specific amount and direction
  • Amsler grid: monitor the scotoma size and location

Referral and Follow-Up in Low Vision

When to refer:

  • VA worse than 20/70 after best correction that cannot be further improved medically
  • Patient has a task they cannot perform with current correction

Low vision team:

  • Low vision optometrist: device prescription and training
  • Orientation and mobility specialist: safe travel training
  • Occupational therapist: activities of daily living
  • Social worker: psychosocial and financial support
  • Teacher of the visually impaired: literacy and education

F8 — Ocular Prosthesis and Absorptive Lenses

The Simple Version

This subtopic covers two separate but related areas. Ocular prosthesis is for patients who have lost an eye — the goal is cosmetic rehabilitation, socket health, and psychological support. Absorptive lenses are for patients who need selective filtering of light — either to protect the eye from UV/IR radiation or to improve functional vision in patients with photophobia and low contrast sensitivity. The board tests prosthesis types and care schedules, and absorptive lens prescribing matched to specific diagnoses.

Teaching angle: Ask: "A patient whose eye was removed 6 months ago is now having daily irritation and discharge. What are the three most likely causes and how do you manage each?"

Part A — Ocular Prosthesis

Why It Is Needed

After loss of an eye (enucleation, evisceration) or severe disfigurement (phthisis bulbi, anophthalmos), an ocular prosthesis:

  • Maintains the volume of the orbit (prevents socket collapse)
  • Provides cosmetic rehabilitation
  • Maintains fornix depth (prevents contracted socket)
  • Supports psychological well-being

Surgical procedures:

  • Enucleation: Complete removal of the globe; orbital implant placed; prosthesis over the implant
  • Evisceration: Intraocular contents removed; scleral shell preserved; better motility
  • Exenteration: Orbital contents removed (radical; for malignancy) — full prosthesis or orbital prosthesis required

Memory Anchors

Prosthesis types — SCSC

Stock, Custom, Scleral shell, Conformer

CPF numbers = cutoff in nm

CPF 450 cuts at 450 nm; CPF 511 at 511 nm; CPF 527 at 527 nm — the number IS the cutoff.

RP + achromatopsia tint rule

"Red goes to zero rods, orange helps the rest" — achromatopsia patients need to eliminate short-wave stimulation entirely (deep orange/red); RP patients still have some rods, so amber/yellow (CPF 511/527) improves contrast without blocking everything.

Board Traps

A scleral shell is used after enucleation
A scleral shell fits over an existing (but shrunken) globe (phthisis bulbi, microphthalmos). After enucleation, the globe is gone — a custom prosthesis is used over the orbital implant.
Photochromic lenses work in a car
Car windshields block UV, which is what activates photochromic lenses. They remain clear while driving.
CPF 527 is appropriate for all photophobia
CPF 527 (orange, cuts below 527 nm) is for RP, macular disease, and glaucoma. For mild photophobia or early lens changes, CPF 450 (light yellow) is more appropriate.
Tap water is safe for soaking a prosthesis
Tap water carries Acanthamoeba risk and is not sterile. Use sterile saline.
A conformer is the definitive prosthesis
A conformer is a temporary clear placeholder placed immediately after surgery to maintain socket shape. The custom PMMA prosthesis is fitted at 6–8 weeks once healing is complete.

Practice Questions

1. A patient with achromatopsia (complete rod monochromacy) is extremely photophobic and reports glare and difficulty in any lit environment. Which absorptive lens is most appropriate?

  • A. Light yellow (CPF 450)
  • B. Gray (neutral density)
  • C. Deep orange or red tint (CPF 527 or darker)
  • D. Green tint for general outdoor use
Answer

C. Achromatopsia patients have non-functional cones and rely entirely on rods. Rods are most sensitive in the mid-spectrum (peak ~507 nm) and are overwhelmed by bright light. A deep orange or red longpass filter (CPF 527 or darker) eliminates the short and mid-wavelength stimulation that causes overwhelming photophobia and allows the remaining rod function to operate comfortably. Light yellow (CPF 450) is not sufficient. Gray reduces all wavelengths equally without targeted relief.

2. A patient's eye was surgically removed 6 months ago. They are now wearing a stock prosthesis and report daily discharge and socket irritation. What is the next step?

  • A. Prescribe topical antibiotics and reassure
  • B. Refer for a custom PMMA prosthesis fitted by a certified ocularist
  • C. Switch to a scleral shell
  • D. Instruct the patient to stop wearing the prosthesis to allow the socket to heal
Answer

B. The stock (ready-made) prosthesis is a poor fit — it cannot conform to the individual socket, which leads to chronic discharge and irritation. The standard of care is a custom PMMA prosthesis fitted by a certified ocularist. Six months is sufficient healing time for proper fitting. Stopping prosthesis wear risks contracted socket, not healing.

Fast Picks — Exam Shortcuts

Read the options first, kill wrong values, then settle the flip. `cue→rule→trap`.

Dilation drug — cue: with vs without cycloplegia.

  • Dilate without cycloplegia = phenylephrine (α1). Dilate with cycloplegia = tropicamide / cyclopentolate (M3 block).
  • Trap: "phenylephrine causes cycloplegia" — it has none.

Pregnancy dilation — cue: pregnant patient needs dilation.

  • Tropicamide (shortest, least exposure) + nasolacrimal occlusion.
  • Trap: phenylephrine 10% (cardiovascular) in pregnancy.

CN3 pupil rule — cue: third-nerve palsy + pupil.

  • Pupil-involving = posterior communicating artery aneurysm (neurosurgical emergency). Pupil-spared = ischemic.
  • Trap: observing a pupil-involving CN3 palsy.

Vertical deviation 3-step — cue: a hyperdeviation worse on tilt.

  • Parks-Bielschowsky 3-step localizes a CN4 (superior oblique) palsy; head tilts away, chin down.
  • Trap: tilting the head toward the affected side.

Absorptive/UV — cue: UV band or filter.

  • UVC highest energy (blocked by ozone); UV400 standard; CPF filters cut blue for specific diagnoses.
  • Trap: calling UVA the highest-energy band.
