## F4 — Neuro-Optometry and Rehabilitation
*Personal study notes | TOS: F.4 Neuro-Optometry | 20 items*

---

## The Simple Version

Neuro-optometry is about understanding what happens to vision when the brain or cranial nerves are damaged. The board tests three main areas: cranial nerve palsies (which muscle is affected and how to localize the lesion), nystagmus (classify the waveform and identify the cause), and cortical visual impairment (the brain cannot process what the intact eye sends). Mastery here depends on knowing the anatomy of each nerve, because the presentation tells you exactly where the lesion is.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "If a patient walks in with one eye drifting inward and double vision that is worse at distance, which nerve is affected and how do you know?" Walking through this builds the entire neuro-optometry framework.

---

## But Why?

**Why does CN4 palsy cause vertical diplopia on downgaze?**
The superior oblique's primary action in the adducted position is depression. When the eye looks down and in (as in reading), the SO is the main depressor. If CN4 is paretic, this depression fails → the eye stays high → vertical diplopia, worst on downgaze toward the nose. This is why patients tilt their head to the opposite shoulder — to rotate the affected eye into a position where it sees straight without the SO.

**Why is CN6 called a "false localizing sign"?**
CN6 has the longest intracranial course of the ocular motor nerves, traveling along the clivus before entering the cavernous sinus. Elevated intracranial pressure stretches the nerve anywhere along this course, not just at the actual source of the ICP rise. So a CN6 palsy doesn't tell you where the lesion is — it tells you the ICP is elevated.

## The Details

### CN4 — Trochlear Nerve Palsy

**What it innervates:** Superior oblique (SO) only — one nerve, one muscle.

**Why CN4 is special:**
- Only CN to exit the dorsal (posterior) brainstem
- Longest intracranial course = most vulnerable to trauma
- CN4 nucleus is contralateral to the muscle it innervates (right CN4 nucleus → left SO)

**Clinical triad:**

| Sign | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Hypertropia (affected eye higher) | SO underacts → eye rides up; contralateral IO compensates poorly |
| Head tilt toward opposite shoulder | Compensatory — keeps eyes aligned without using the paretic meridian |
| Chin depression | Avoids downgaze where SO is most needed |

**Parks-Bielschowsky 3-Step Test:**
1. Which eye is hypertropic in primary gaze? → Identifies the hypertropic eye
2. Does hypertropia worsen in right gaze or left gaze? → Identifies which pair of vertically acting muscles is involved
3. Bielschowsky head tilt test — tilt toward the hypertropic eye: if hypertropia worsens → CN4 palsy confirmed

**Why Bielschowsky works:** Tilting toward the hypertropic eye reflexively intorts that eye. The SO handles this intorsion. If SO is paretic, the superior rectus takes over intorsion — but also elevates the eye → hypertropia worsens.

**Causes:**
- Congenital: most common overall (look for old photos with head tilt)
- Trauma: most common acquired cause (vulnerable at tentorial edge)
- Microvascular: DM, HTN — resolves in 3–6 months
- Bilateral CN4 palsy → suspect raised ICP or severe head trauma

**Management:** Prism (base-down over hypertropic eye); observation for microvascular; surgery for persistent cases.

### CN6 — Abducens Nerve Palsy

**What it innervates:** Lateral rectus (LR) only.

**Cardinal sign:** Esotropia of the affected eye (unopposed MR action when LR is paretic)

**Clinical features:**

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Esotropia | Worse at distance than near; incomitant |
| Failure of abduction | Cannot cross midline toward temple |
| Horizontal diplopia | Worse on ipsilateral gaze |
| Face turn toward paretic side | Compensatory; moves eye away from its action field |

**False localizing sign:** CN6 palsy with elevated ICP — nerve is compressed along its long clivus course regardless of lesion location.

**Causes:**
- Microvascular (most common in adults): DM, HTN — resolves in 3–4 months
- Raised ICP: false localizing
- Trauma: basilar skull fracture
- Gradenigo's syndrome: CN6 palsy + ipsilateral facial pain + otitis media → petrous apex
- Children: rule out pontine glioma (urgent MRI)

**Key distinction from accommodative esotropia:**
- CN6 palsy: acquired, incomitant, restricted abduction
- Accommodative ET: comitant, full ductions, associated with hyperopia

**Management:** Patch or Fresnel prism; observation; botulinum toxin to MR; surgery for permanent cases.

### CN3 — Oculomotor Nerve Palsy (Brief)

**What it innervates:** SR, MR, IR, IO (4 recti + IO), levator palpebrae, pupillary sphincter (parasympathetic)

**Complete CN3 palsy:**
- Ptosis (levator paralysis)
- Eye down and out (SO and LR unopposed)
- Fixed dilated pupil (mydriasis — parasympathetic involved)
- Diplopia

**Pupil-involving vs. pupil-sparing:**
- Pupil-involved + painful = posterior communicating artery aneurysm (PCA aneurysm) — neurosurgical emergency
- Pupil-sparing = microvascular (DM, HTN) — monitor and resolve in 3 months

**Board pearl:** Any third nerve palsy with pupil involvement = aneurysm until proven otherwise. This is a neurosurgical emergency.

### Nystagmus

**Definition:** Involuntary, rhythmic oscillation of the eyes.

**Classification by waveform:**

| Type | Description | Localization |
|---|---|---|
| Jerk nystagmus | Slow drift + fast corrective saccade; named for FAST phase | Vestibular, cerebellar, CNS |
| Pendular nystagmus | Equal velocity in both directions; no distinct fast phase | MS (acquired); infantile (INS) |

**Congenital / Infantile Nystagmus Syndrome (INS):**
- Onset < 6 months
- Horizontal in all gaze positions (uniplanar)
- Dampens with convergence
- Null point → abnormal head position (AHP) is therapeutic
- Associated: albinism, congenital cataract, aniridia, achromatopsia

**Latent / Manifest-Latent Nystagmus:**
- Only appears or worsens when one eye is covered
- Fast phase toward the viewing eye
- Associated with infantile esotropia
- Clinical trap: use +5.00 lens over "occluded" eye (fogs without triggering reflex) to get true VA

**Acquired Nystagmus — Key Types:**

| Type | Direction | Localization | Cause |
|---|---|---|---|
| Downbeat | Fast phase down | Craniocervical junction | Arnold-Chiari, cerebellar atrophy |
| Upbeat | Fast phase up | Medulla/pons | Brainstem infarct, Wernicke's |
| See-saw | One eye rises and intorts while other falls | Parasellar | Craniopharyngioma; bitemporal hemianopsia |
| Periodic alternating (PAN) | Reverses direction every ~90 sec | Cerebellum (nodulus) | Drug-induced (phenytoin), cerebellar lesion |
| Vestibular | Horizontal + torsional | Peripheral: labyrinth; Central: brainstem | BPPV vs. stroke |
| Gaze-evoked | Appears only in eccentric gaze | Cerebellar / drug | Anticonvulsants, cerebellar disease |

**Alexander's Law:** In jerk nystagmus, amplitude increases when gazing in the direction of the fast phase.

### Cortical Visual Impairment (CVI)

**Definition:** Vision loss from retrogeniculate pathway damage (optic radiations / visual cortex) with structurally normal eyes (or vision worse than ocular pathology explains).

**Most common cause of visual impairment in children in developed countries.**

**Causes:**
- Perinatal hypoxia-ischemia (HIE, periventricular leukomalacia) — most common
- Premature birth (white matter injury)
- Hydrocephalus, TBI, CNS infections, metabolic disorders

**Roman-Kay 10 Behavioral Features of CVI:**
1. Preference for color (especially red and yellow)
2. Need for movement to sustain attention
3. Visual latency (delayed visual response)
4. Visual field preference (lower fields often impaired)
5. Difficulty with visual complexity (crowded/cluttered environments)
6. Light-gazing; non-purposeful gaze
7. Difficulty with distance viewing
8. Atypical visual reflexes
9. Difficulty with novel visual stimuli
10. Absence of visually guided reach (severe cases)

**Diagnosis:** Clinical (history + Roman-Kay features) + MRI (retrogeniculate injury) + VEP (delayed)

**Management:** No cure; multidisciplinary rehabilitation; environmental modification (reduce visual clutter; use bright colors; single objects); functional vision training.

```
  +---> VERTICAL DIPLOPIA (worse on downgaze)

  |         → Parks-Bielschowsky 3-step → confirms SO
  |
  +---> PTOSIS + EYE DOWN AND OUT
  |         → CN3 PALSY
  |         → Pupil involved? → ANEURYSM (emergency)
  |         → Pupil spared? → Microvascular (observe 3 months)
  |
  +---> NYSTAGMUS
            → Horizontal, present since birth, dampens on convergence → INS
            → Downbeat → craniocervical junction
            → See-saw + bitemporal VF loss → parasellar lesion
            → Appears on covering one eye → Latent nystagmus
```

## Comparisons That Matter

| Feature | CN4 Palsy | CN6 Palsy | CN3 Palsy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Muscle affected | Superior oblique | Lateral rectus | SR, MR, IR, IO, levator |
| Diplopia direction | Vertical (diagonal) | Horizontal | Oblique / any direction |
| Eye position | Hypertropia | Esotropia | Down and out |
| Head posture | Tilt away, chin down | Turn toward paretic side | May have no compensation |
| Pupil involvement | No | No | Yes (mydriasis) if compressive |
| Worst on | Downgaze (reading, stairs) | Distance fixation, ipsilateral gaze | All directions |
| Emergency flag | Bilateral → raised ICP | Any CN6 in child | Pupil-involved = aneurysm |

## Memory Anchors

**CN palsies — "SO4, LR6, AO3":** Superior Oblique = CN4; Lateral Rectus = CN6; All Others = CN3

**CN4 head posture:** "Away and down" — head tilts toward the opposite shoulder, chin down

**Bielschowsky:** "Tilt toward the high eye — if it gets higher, that eye's SO is paretic"

**CN3 pupil rule:** "Pupil down = put your phone down and call neurosurgery" — pupil-involving CN3 = aneurysm = emergency

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** CN4 palsy is named for the fast phase of nystagmus — Wrong. This is the naming rule for jerk nystagmus, not CN palsies.
- **Trap:** CN6 palsy always localizes to the pons — Wrong. CN6 is the false localizing sign; it can be compressed anywhere along its long course, especially with raised ICP.
- **Trap:** Congenital nystagmus is always vertical — Wrong. INS is characteristically horizontal in all gaze positions. Vertical nystagmus is always acquired.
- **Trap:** CVI patients have abnormal ocular findings — Wrong. The eyes are structurally normal in CVI; the vision loss is cortical. Pupils react normally; OCT shows normal retina.
- **Trap:** Latent nystagmus is tested by occluding one eye with a patch — Wrong. Occlusion triggers it, worsening VA. Use a +5.00 lens to fog the eye without triggering the reflex.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 45-year-old diabetic patient reports vertical diplopia that is worst when going down stairs and when reading. On examination, the right eye is hypertropic and the patient tilts the head to the left. Bielschowsky head tilt test worsens the hypertropia when tilted right. What is the diagnosis?

- A. Right CN6 palsy
- B. Right CN3 palsy
- C. Right CN4 palsy
- D. Left CN4 palsy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Right CN4 palsy.** Vertical diplopia worst on downgaze + right hypertropia + head tilt to the left (away from affected side) + positive Bielschowsky when tilted toward the hypertropic (right) eye = right superior oblique palsy = right CN4 palsy.

</details>

---

**2.** A 2-year-old child is referred for poor visual tracking. The parents note that she uses one eye more than the other and has difficulty looking at complex images. MRI shows periventricular leukomalacia. VA testing with preferential looking shows reduced responses. Which diagnosis best fits?

- A. Infantile esotropia with amblyopia
- B. Latent nystagmus
- C. Cortical visual impairment (CVI)
- D. Congenital cataract

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Cortical Visual Impairment.** History of neurological insult (periventricular leukomalacia — white matter damage) + behavioral visual characteristics (preference, difficulty with complexity, visual latency) + normal-appearing anterior and posterior segments + reduced VEP-based VA = CVI. The diagnosis is behavioral and neuroimaging-based, not based on ocular structural findings.

</details>
