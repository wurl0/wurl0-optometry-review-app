## F7 — Low Vision and Rehabilitation

## The Simple Version

Low vision is vision loss that cannot be fully corrected with standard glasses, contact lenses, or medical treatment, yet leaves some usable vision. The goal of low vision care is not to restore 20/20 — it is to maximize the patient's functional vision for their specific daily tasks. This requires choosing the right magnification device, training the patient to use eccentric viewing if the fovea is damaged, and modifying the environment to support whatever vision remains.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "A patient with 20/200 VA wants to read the newspaper. What magnification do they need, and where does that number come from?"

---

## The Details

### Key Definitions

**WHO definition of Blindness:** VA ≤ 20/200 (or equivalent) in the better eye after best correction; OR visual field < 10° from fixation

**Legal Blindness (US standard, also used in PH exams):** VA ≤ 20/200 with best correction in the better eye; OR visual field ≤ 20° in the widest meridian

**WHO definition of Low Vision:** VA < 20/70 (6/18) but > 20/200 in the better eye after best correction; OR visual field < 20° from fixation, but some usable vision remains

**Functional definition (most clinically relevant):** A person with low vision cannot fully remediate their vision impairment with conventional spectacles, CLs, or medical intervention, and this causes restriction in daily life.

**ICD-11 Classification (2018):**

| Category | VA Range |
|---|---|
| Mild | 20/40 – 20/60 |
| Moderate | < 20/60 – 20/200 |
| Severe | < 20/200 – 20/400 |
| Blindness | < 20/400 (worse than 3/60) |

### Common Causes of Low Vision

**Adults over 50:**
1. Cataract (62.6%) — most common globally; avoidable
2. Refractive error (19.7%)
3. Glaucoma (5.8%)
4. Posterior segment disorders (4.7%)
5. Corneal opacity (0.9%)

**Children:**
1. Refractive error (33.3%)
2. Congenital anomalies (25%)
3. Retinal degeneration (16.7%)
4. Vitamin A deficiency (8.3%)

**Global:** 285 million visually impaired worldwide; 39 million blind; 246 million low vision; 80% of blindness is avoidable.

---

### Low Vision Rehabilitation Concepts

**Rehabilitation vs. Habilitation:**
- **Rehabilitation:** Re-teaching a skill to someone who had it and lost it (acquired vision loss)
- **Habilitation:** Teaching a skill to someone who never had it (congenital vision loss)

**The rehabilitation chain:**
Visual disorder → visual impairment (functional limitation) → visual disability (skill reduction) → visual handicap (psychosocial/economic consequence)

The goal of rehabilitation is to break the chain before it reaches handicap — maximize remaining function at the impairment level.

---

### Magnification — Principles and Calculation

**Why magnification works:** Enlarging the retinal image allows it to be processed by remaining functioning photoreceptors outside the damaged fovea.

**Four types of magnification:**

| Type | How It Works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Relative size | Make the object physically larger | Large-print books, enlarged font on phone |
| Relative distance | Move closer to the object | Bring reading material from 40 cm to 10 cm |
| Angular | Use a lens or optical device to enlarge the visual angle | Magnifiers, telescopes |
| Electronic | Digitally enlarge and enhance | CCTV, tablet with zoom |

**Calculating required magnification:**
```
M = Required VA / Present Near VA

Or using dioptric method:
M = Required add power / 4
(Reference standard: 4D = 1x at 25 cm)
```

**Example:**
- Patient best near VA: N24 at 40 cm
- Task demand: N8
- M = 24/8 = 3×
- Required add: 3 × 4 = +12.00D
- Working distance: 100/12 = 8.3 cm

### Low Vision Devices

**Near devices:**

| Device | Power Range | Advantage | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spectacle magnifier (high add) | +4.00D to +40.00D | Hands-free; best for sustained reading | Very short working distance |
| Handheld magnifier | Variable | Portable; flexible working distance | Needs steady hand; not hands-free |
| Stand magnifier | Variable | Stable; no hand steadiness needed; useful in tremor | Fixed focal distance; shorter field |
| Electronic magnifier (CCTV/tablet) | Variable electronic | Best for sustained reading; contrast control; adjustable | Expensive; less portable |

**Distance devices:**

| Device | Type | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Monocular telescope | Hand-held or spectacle-mounted | Spot viewing (TV, signs, boards) |
| Binocular telescope | Spectacle-mounted | Extended distance viewing |
| Telemicroscope | Telescope focused at near | Specific near tasks at a set distance |

**Non-optical aids:**

| Aid | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Typoscope | Black card with window cut out; reduces glare and isolates the reading line |
| Reading stand | Holds material at correct distance and angle; reduces fatigue |
| Filters / absorptive lenses | Reduces glare; improves contrast (CPF series) |
| Lighting control | Task lighting; lamp positioning; reduce veiling glare |
| Large-print materials | Relative size magnification |

### Eccentric Viewing (EV) Training

When the fovea is damaged (AMD, macular scar, macular dystrophy), the patient must learn to use a healthier peripheral retinal area as the preferred retinal locus (PRL).

**Training methods:**
- Steady eye strategy: teach patient to hold their eye steadily on a peripheral fixation point
- Clock face method: describe where the scotoma is on a clock face; teach patient to look "off-center" by a specific amount and direction
- Amsler grid: monitor the scotoma size and location

---

### Referral and Follow-Up in Low Vision

**When to refer:**
- VA worse than 20/70 after best correction that cannot be further improved medically
- Patient has a task they cannot perform with current correction

**Low vision team:**
- Low vision optometrist: device prescription and training
- Orientation and mobility specialist: safe travel training
- Occupational therapist: activities of daily living
- Social worker: psychosocial and financial support
- Teacher of the visually impaired: literacy and education

## Memory Anchors

**4 magnification types — RRАЕ:** Relative size, Relative distance, Angular, Electronic

**Magnification formula shortcut:** "Add ÷ 4 = times" — divide the needed add power by 4 to get the magnification, or multiply the magnification by 4 to get the add.

**Low vision device selection:** Near sustained work → spectacle magnifier; near spot reading → handheld; tremor patient → stand magnifier; distance spotting → monocular telescope

---

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** All patients with low vision need telescopes — Wrong. Telescopes are for distance tasks. Most low vision patients need near magnification first.
- **Trap:** Prescribing from uncorrected VA — Wrong. Magnification is calculated from best-corrected VA, not uncorrected. Always refract first.
- **Trap:** The goal of low vision care is 20/20 — Wrong. The goal is functional vision for the patient's specific needs. Trying to achieve 20/20 in low vision patients is unrealistic and misleading.
- **Trap:** Eccentric viewing training is only for AMD — Wrong. Any central scotoma (macular dystrophy, histoplasmosis, macular scar from any cause) may benefit from EV training.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A patient with AMD has best corrected near VA of N32 at 40 cm. Their goal is to read N8 print. What magnification and add power are needed?

- A. 2×; +8.00D add
- B. 4×; +16.00D add
- C. 3×; +12.00D add
- D. 2×; +12.00D add

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B. 4×; +16.00D add.** M = 32/8 = 4×. Equivalent add = 4 × 4 = +16.00D. Working distance = 100/16 = 6.25 cm. The patient must read at approximately 6 cm from the lens.

</details>

---

**2.** Which of the following best describes the goal of low vision rehabilitation?

- A. Restore best corrected VA to 20/20 through stronger lenses
- B. Maximize functional vision for the patient's specific daily tasks using optical and non-optical aids
- C. Replace all current spectacles with a single-lens magnifier system
- D. Provide training only for patients with VA better than 20/200

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Low vision rehabilitation aims to maximize the patient's functional use of remaining vision — not restore normal VA. It is task-specific and uses a combination of optical devices, non-optical aids, environmental modifications, and eccentric viewing training based on the patient's needs.

</details>
