## 1. Definitions and Classification

### Core Definitions

**Visual impairment** — any condition encompassing the continuum from partial sight to blindness.

**WHO definition of Low Vision:**
Visual acuity of 20/70 (6/18) or worse in the better eye after best possible correction, AND/OR visual field < 20° from the point of fixation — but some usable vision remains.

**Functional definition (most clinically relevant):**
A person with low vision is one who has an impairment of visual function for whom full remediation is not possible by conventional spectacles, contact lenses, or medical interventions, and which causes restriction in that person's everyday life.

**WHO definition of Blindness:**
Visual acuity of 20/400 (worse than 3/60) in the better eye after best possible correction; OR visual field < 5° from fixation.

**Legal Blindness — Philippines:**
Visual acuity equal to or worse than 20/400 even after standard refractive correction; OR visual field < 5° from fixation (better seeing eye).

**Legal Blindness — USA (also referenced in PH board exams):**
Visual acuity < 20/200 with best correction in the better eye; OR visual field ≤ 20° in the widest meridian.

---

### ICD-11 Classification of Visual Impairment (2018)

**Distance Vision Impairment:**

| Category | Visual Acuity Range |
|---|---|
| No impairment | Equal to or better than 6/12 (20/40) |
| Mild | Worse than 6/12 to 6/18 (20/40 to 20/70) |
| Moderate | Worse than 6/18 to 6/60 (20/70 to 20/200) |
| Severe | Worse than 6/60 to 3/60 or 6/120 (20/200 to 20/400) |
| Blindness | Worse than 3/60 (20/400) |

**Near Vision Impairment:**
Near visual acuity worse than N6 or M0.8 at 40 cm.

**Board hook:** The ICD-11 (2018) is the current classification. The older WHO 1966 classification is no longer the standard for board purposes.

---

### The Disorder → Handicap Chain

Understanding this chain is the conceptual backbone of low vision rehabilitation.

| Level | Definition | Example (Glaucoma) |
|---|---|---|
| Visual Disorder | Anatomical change in the visual organ | Optic nerve neuropathy from elevated IOP |
| Visual Impairment | Functional loss from the disorder | Reduced VA, restricted visual field |
| Visual Disability | Vision-related changes in skill and ability | Cannot read signage, difficulty driving |
| Visual Handicap | Psychosocial and economic consequences | Job loss, anxiety, social isolation |

**Rehabilitation** — re-teaching a lost skill (acquired vision loss).
**Habilitation** — teaching a skill never had (congenital vision loss).

The goal of low vision care: break the chain before it reaches disability and handicap — maximize remaining functional vision at the impairment level.

## 2. Demographics and Prevalence

**Global (WHO):**
- At least 2.2 billion people have vision impairment
- Of these, at least 1 billion have impairment that could have been prevented or has not yet been addressed
- Prevalence of vision impairment in low- and middle-income regions is estimated to be 4 times higher than in high-income regions
- 80% of all blindness is avoidable

**Leading causes globally:**
1. Age-related macular degeneration
2. Cataract
3. Diabetic retinopathy
4. Glaucoma
5. Uncorrected refractive errors

**Adults over 50 (Geriatric):**
1. Cataract — 62.6%

4. Posterior segment disorders — 4.7%

6. Others — 6.2%

**Pediatric:**
1. Refractive error — 33.3%
2. Congenital eye anomalies — 25%

4. Vitamin A deficiency — 8.3%

## 3. Functional Visual Impairment

These are the functional limitations that characterize a low vision patient:

- Reduced visual acuity or contrast sensitivity
- Visual field loss
- Photophobia
- Diplopia
- Visual distortion
- Visual perceptual difficulties

Causes can be:
- **Congenital** — prenatal or postnatal trauma; genetic or developmental abnormalities
- **Hereditary** — retinitis pigmentosa, Stargardt's macular degeneration
- **Acquired** — ocular infection or disease; trauma
- **Age-related** — systemic disease; structural aging changes

## 4. Visual Acuity in Low Vision

### Visual Acuity Measurement

VA provides a direct estimate of the magnification required to perform standard tasks.

**Distance VA:** Standard range is 6 meters (20 feet); can be moved to 3 meters or 1 meter. Moving from 6 m to 3 m gains a factor of two.

**M notation (Metric unit):** The standard system for low vision near VA. Louise Sloan introduced M-units. The reference distance is 6 meters for distance VA.

| Snellen | M Units (at 6 m) |
|---|---|
| 20/200 | 60 M |
| 20/100 | 30 M |
| 20/80 | 25 M |
| 20/70 | 21 M |
| 20/60 | 18 M |
| 20/50 | 15 M |
| 20/40 | 12 M |
| 20/30 | 10 M |
| 20/25 | 8 M |
| 20/20 | 6 M |

**Recording near VA for unknown print size:**
M units = Letter size in mm ÷ 1.45 mm

**N point to M unit reference (reading materials):**

| N Point | M Units | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 100 | 12.5 | Headlines |
| 40 | 5 | Sub-headlines |
| 20 | 2.5 | Large print books |
| 16 | 2 | Children's books |
| 12 | 1.5 | Books / typed materials |
| 10 | 1.3 | Magazines, books |
| 8 | 1.0 | Newspaper |
| 6 | 0.8 | Telephone book |
| 5 | 0.6 | Small advertisements, bibles |

**Conditions associated with poor VA:**
- High refractive errors (myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism)
- Conditions affecting the central retina: AMD, Stargardt's disease
- Conditions affecting transparent media: cataract, corneal opacity

---

### Refraction in Low Vision

**Why traditional refraction is not ideal:**
Poor fixation, eccentric viewing posture, or media opacities make conventional phoropter-based refraction less reliable.

**Preferred techniques:**
- **Radical retinoscopy** — working distance of 25–40 cm (shorter than conventional); deduct the working distance value from the gross findings; produces a brighter reflex for central cataracts/scars
- **Off-axis retinoscopy** — retinoscopy out of the client's line of sight
- **Bracketing technique** — trial frame with large changes in lens power (not phoropter); compare a range of lenses in large steps

## 5. Contrast Sensitivity

### Definition

**Contrast** — changes in luminance between regions not separated by definite borders.

**Contrast sensitivity** — the ability of the visual system to distinguish between an object and its background.

**Contrast threshold** — the smallest amount of contrast needed to detect a target.

**Spatial frequency** — the different sizes of gratings. Measured with repetitive black-and-white bars. Describes the person's ability to perceive visual information from fine to broad scale.

### Why contrast sensitivity matters

VA charts use high-contrast targets. A patient can have good VA but be visually impaired in real life due to poor contrast sensitivity. Contrast sensitivity tests the full visual spectrum — from fine to broad scale — and predicts real-world function (mobility, reading, face recognition).

### Contrast Sensitivity Tests

**1. Pelli-Robson Test**
- Single large letter size (20/60 optotype)
- 16 triples; contrast decreases 0.15 log units per triple
- Test distance: **1 meter**
- Score: last triple where ≥ 2 of 3 letters are correctly identified (log units)
- Normal: 1.80 log units (age 20–50); 1.65 log units (< 20 or > 50 years)
- Score < 1.5 = visual impairment; < 1.0 = visual disability
- Best for: cataract detection, IOL screening, driver vision screening
- Most common errors: not waiting ≥ 20 seconds; not pushing patient to guess; poor illumination

**2. Bailey-Lovie Contrast Sensitivity (Hi-Low Chart)**
- logMAR format calibrated at 6 meters
- Tests normal contrast (100%) and low contrast (10%)
- Equal number of optotypes per row; equal logarithmic change between rows
- Used for nighttime driving vision screening

**3. Vision Contrast Test System (VCTS) by Vistech — Gold Standard**
- Circular sine-wave gratings: 5 rows × 9 columns
- Spatial frequencies: 1.5, 3, 6, 12, and 18 cycles per degree
- Contrast decreases from left to right; patient identifies grating orientation (left/vertical/right)
- VCTS 6500: 3-meter test; VCTS 6000: close examination
- Lowest contrast grating correctly identified = sensitivity score for that spatial frequency
- Plots a complete contrast sensitivity function (CSF)

**4. Functional Acuity Contrast Testing (FACT)**
- Developed by Dr. Arthur Ginsburg
- 5 spatial frequencies; 9 contrast levels; test at 10 feet
- Rows A–E; patient reports grating orientation (up or left)
- Accurate and comprehensive; provides a full CSF

**5. Lea Paddles (Lea Gratings)**
- Preferential looking test for infants, children, adults with disabilities
- Detection acuity; 2% least contrast
- Two fields: paddle with no gratings (homologous) vs. paddle with gratings (heterologous)
- Distance: 0.5–1 meter; done binocularly
- Grating levels: 0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0 cycles per centimeter

**6. Hiding Heidi Contrast Test**
- Face contrast cards for infants and non-verbal patients
- Contrast levels: 100%, 25%, 10%, 5%, 2.5%, 1.25%
- Cards presented in pairs (hidden face vs. blank card) at the infant's communication distance
- Normal response: infant follows the face card, may smile at 10% contrast at 40 cm

**7. Cambridge Low Contrast Gratings**
- Rapid screening at 6 meters
- 12 plate pairs with varying contrast; 1 demonstration + 10 test plates
- Four series completed; total score converted to CS value from provided table

### Factors Affecting Contrast Sensitivity

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| Refractive error | Mild: loss at high spatial frequencies; severe: mid and low frequencies also affected |
| Age | Definite decrease; related to spherical aberration changes in lens and cornea |
| Cataract | Similar to refractive error; worse with glare source |
| Glaucoma | Mostly at middle spatial frequencies |
| Diabetic retinopathy | All grating sizes |
| Optic neuropathies (inc. MS) | Some or all sizes; MS can affect only middle sizes |
| Pituitary adenoma | Middle grating sizes |
| Contact lenses | Uncorrected residual astigmatism from soft CLs: loss at middle and higher frequencies |
| Refractive surgery | CS does not recover to pre-surgery level |
| Toxic chemicals | Occupational exposure; loss with normal VA |

### Contrast sensitivity-related functional problems

- Difficulty with night driving; cannot see traffic lights
- Problems when reading
- Eye fatigue watching television
- Cannot see spots on clothes, counters, or dishes

## 6. Visual Field and Visual Field Defects

### Normal Monocular Field Extent

- Superior: 60°
- Inferior: 75°
- Nasal: 60°
- Temporal: 90°

### Physiologic Blind Spot

- Located ~15° temporally from fixation (range: 12°–18°)
- ~1.5° below the horizontal midline
- Vertical diameter: 7–8°; Horizontal diameter: 5–6°
- Corresponds to the optic disc — no photoreceptors
- Absolute scotoma

### Types of Scotoma

**Relative scotoma:** Retinal sensitivity is decreased; target may appear blurry. Larger, brighter targets may still be detected.

**Absolute scotoma:** Retinal sensitivity is absent. Intensity and size of target will not produce a response.

**Positive scotoma:** The patient is aware of the defect (sees a dark spot).

**Negative scotoma:** The patient is not conscious of the defect (e.g., normal physiologic blind spot).

### Perimetry

**Kinetic perimetry** — stimulus moves until the patient first detects it; better for determining borders of large VF defects.

**Static threshold perimetry** — stimulus presented at varying intensity to find the local threshold per isopter; better at detecting small scotomas.

**Static supra-threshold perimetry** — stimulus presented at intensities calculated above threshold; two intensity levels; if missed first time = relative defect; if missed second brighter presentation = absolute scotoma.

### Visual Field Defects by Disease

| Disease | Visual Field Defect |
|---|---|
| Macular hole | Central scotoma |
| Retinitis pigmentosa | Field constriction (ring scotoma → tunnel vision) |
| Glaucoma | Arcuate scotoma → nasal step → tunnel vision |
| Diabetic/Hypertensive retinopathy | Scattered scotomas |
| Optic chiasm lesion (pituitary tumor) | Bitemporal hemianopsia |
| Optic tract/radiation lesion | Homonymous hemianopsia |

### Visual Pathway Summary

Retina → Optic nerve (CN II) → Optic chiasma (nasal fibers cross; temporal fibers do not) → Optic tract → Lateral geniculate body (relay station) → Optic radiation → Striate cortex (Brodmann area 17, medial occipital lobe)

- **Non-decussating fibers:** temporal half (lateral), project ipsilaterally
- **Decussating fibers:** nasal half (medial), cross at chiasma
- **Meyer's loop:** inferior temporal radiation fibers — lesion causes superior quadrantanopsia

## 7. Pupil Assessment

### Normal Anatomy

- **Sphincter pupillae:** circular muscle, 1 mm wide, near pupillary aperture, posterior iris surface. Innervated by PNS (CN III, ciliary ganglion, short ciliary nerves).
- **Dilator pupillae:** radial fibers from ciliary body to pupillary margin. Innervated by SNS (superior cervical ganglion, long ciliary nerves).

### Assessing the Pupil (5 Parameters)

1. **Number** — normally one; Diplocoria = 2 pupils in one eye; Polycoria = multiple pupils
2. **Location** — normally near-central; Corectopia = eccentric pupil
3. **Size** — normal: 3–4 mm depending on illumination; assess in light, darkness, and near stimulation
4. **Shape** — normally nearly circular; abnormal shapes below
5. **Color** — normally greyish-black

**Factors affecting pupil size:**
- Age: smaller in infants and elderly
- Sleep: smaller (parasympathetic dominance)
- Personality: smaller in calm/timid people
- Refraction: hyperopes have smaller pupils
- Iris color: darker iris = smaller pupil

**Anisocoria:** Difference in pupillary diameter ≥ 0.3 mm. Slight anisocoria (< 0.1 mm) is normal in a significant percentage of people.

**Pupil color guide:**

| Color | Condition |
|---|---|
| Black (normal) | Normal / aphakia |
| Greyish-white | Cataract |
| Brown | Nuclear cataract |
| Whitish | Retinoblastoma; pseudoglioma |
| Yellowish | Vitreous abscess (endophthalmitis, panophthalmitis) |
| Greenish | Acute congestive (angle-closure) glaucoma |
| Reddish | Albinism |

**Abnormal pupil shapes:**
- D-shaped: iridodialysis; neuropathy of short ciliary nerves
- Festooned (ribbon-shaped): posterior synechiae on dilation
- Pear-shaped: adherent leukoma (post-cataract surgery)
- Keyhole-shaped: sector iridectomy
- Tadpole-shaped: sector dilation in Horner's syndrome
- Scallop-shaped: sector iris atrophy in amyloidosis

### Pupillary Reflexes

**Light reflex:**
- **Direct:** ipsilateral pupil constricts when ipsilateral eye is stimulated
- **Consensual:** contralateral pupil constricts when ipsilateral eye is stimulated

**Near reflex (near triad):** Accommodation + Convergence + Miosis. Not a true reflex — synkinesis. Vision is not a prerequisite. The near reflex center is ill-defined.

**Darkness reflex:** Two mechanisms — (1) abolition of light reflex with relaxation of sphincter; (2) sympathetic contraction of dilator pupillae.

**Psychosensory reflex:** Dilation in response to sensory and psychic stimuli. Not present in newborns; develops by 6 months.

**Total Afferent Pathway Defect (Amaurotic Pupil):**
- Complete optic nerve or retinal lesion
- Affected eye: no light perception, no direct reflex
- Contralateral eye: no consensual reflex when affected eye is stimulated
- Both pupils remain dilated

**Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD / Marcus Gunn Pupil):**
- Partial afferent defect (optic nerve partial damage, severe retinal disease)
- Swinging flashlight test: paradoxical dilation of the affected eye when light is swung to it

## 8. Scotopic and Photopic Vision

### Retinal Structure Basics

| Feature | Rods | Cones |
|---|---|---|
| Count | 90–120 million | 5–7 million |
| Location | Peripheral retina | Fovea (concentrated centrally) |
| Minimum light trigger | 1 photon | 100+ photons |
| Primary function | Night and peripheral vision | Color and detail vision |
| Dark adaptation speed | Slow (~30 min) | Fast (adapts instantly) |

**Fovea:** 1 sq mm; capable of acute, detailed vision. Central fovea (foveola): 0.3 mm; composed almost entirely of cones. Blood vessels, ganglion cells, and inner nuclear layers are displaced to one side, allowing unimpeded light to cones.

### Visual Pigments (Photochemistry)

**Rhodopsin (rods):** scotopsin + 11-cis retinal. Visual purple. Decomposed by light energy → excited rhodopsin → nerve signal.

**Cone pigments / Iodopsin (cones):** three variants with different peak spectral sensitivities (L/M/S cones).

**Visual cycle (rods):**
1. Light decomposes 11-cis retinal → all-trans retinal
2. Retinal isomerase converts all-trans retinal back to 11-cis retinal
3. 11-cis retinal + scotopsin → rhodopsin reforms in darkness

**Role of Vitamin A:**
All-trans retinal → all-trans retinol (Vitamin A) → 11-cis retinol → 11-cis retinal + scotopsin = Rhodopsin

- Vitamin A stored in the liver (Ito cells) — deficiency takes months before night blindness develops
- Night blindness from Vitamin A deficiency can be reversed in < 1 hour with IV injection of Vitamin A

### Light and Dark Adaptation

**Light adaptation:** Prolonged exposure to bright light converts visual pigments to retinal + opsins; Vitamin A conversion further reduces pigment concentration → sensitivity decreases.

**Dark adaptation:** In darkness, Vitamin A converts back to retinal → visual pigment reforms → sensitivity increases. Cones adapt quickly but plateau at a lower sensitivity level. Rods adapt slowly but achieve a sensitivity threshold far lower than cones.

**Two curves of dark adaptation:**
1. Cone adaptation: rapid, plateau within 5–10 minutes
2. Rod adaptation: slow, continues for ~30 minutes; Kohlrausch kink visible where rods take over from cones

**Other mechanisms of light/dark adaptation:**
- Pupil constriction (photopic) and dilation (scotopic)
- Neuronal adaptation via GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter released by amacrine cells)

**Night blindness (Nyctalopia):** due to Vitamin A deficiency → decreased rhodopsin. Also a symptom of retinitis pigmentosa.

## 9. Color Vision and Color Vision Anomalies

### Definition

Color vision is the ability to discriminate light based on wavelength composition. Normal color vision (trichromacy) uses three functional cone types (L, M, S cones) — perceives approximately 1 million colors.

**Tetrachromacy:** Four functional cone types; perceives ~10 million colors. Found exclusively in females. Caused by an X-chromosome mutation (same mutation that causes color blindness in males).

### Classification of Color Vision Defects

| Category | Description | X-linked? | Sex Prevalence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anomalous trichromacy | One cone altered in spectral sensitivity | Yes (protan/deutan) | Mainly males |
| Dichromacy | One cone type absent/non-functioning | Yes (protan/deutan) | Mainly males |
| Achromatopsia (complete) | All cone types absent/non-functioning | No — autosomal recessive | Equal |
| Blue cone monochromacy | M and L cones absent; S only | X-linked | Males only |

---

### Anomalous Trichromacy (Deficiency — all 3 types present but altered)

| Type | Affected Cone | Description | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deuteranomaly | M (green) decreased | Most common color deficiency; green-weakness | ~5% white males, 0.25% females |
| Protanomaly | L (red) decreased | Red-weakness; red shifted toward green; orange/yellow appear paler | Less common |
| Tritanomaly | S (blue) decreased | Blue-weakness; yellow-blue confusion | < 0.01% |

---

### Dichromacy (One cone type absent)

| Type | Missing Cone | Description | Most Common? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Protanopia | L (red) missing | Cannot distinguish red from green; blue/green seen but mixed colors appear grey | Most common dichromacy |
| Deuteranopia | M (green) missing | Similar symptoms to protanopia; cannot distinguish red-green mixes | |
| Tritanopia | S (blue) missing | Cannot distinguish yellow from blue; rarest; 10 in 1 million | Rarest |

**Key:** Deutan, Protan, and Tritan terminology covers both anomalous trichromacy and dichromacy for each axis.

---

### Achromatopsia

| Type | Also Called | Description | Prevalence | Inheritance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Complete (rod monochromatism) | Total color blindness | All cone types absent/non-functional | ~0.003% (1/30,000) | Autosomal recessive; equal sex |
| Incomplete (cone monochromatism) | Cone monochromacy | One cone type only; VA may be preserved | Variable | Depends on type |
| Blue-cone monochromacy (S-cone) | Most common incomplete | M and L cones absent; only S cones | 1/100,000 | X-linked |
| Cerebral achromatopsia | | Caused by damage to Visual Area 4 (inferior-temporal extrastriate cortex) | | Acquired |

**Symptoms of complete achromatopsia (rod monochromacy):**
- Complete inability to distinguish colors
- Reduced visual acuity
- Hemeralopia (severe light sensitivity)
- Nystagmus
- Central scotoma

### Color Vision Tests

**Pseudoisochromatic Plates:**

**Ishihara (1917):**
- Tests red-green deficiencies ONLY
- 38 plates total; 14 screening + diagnostic plates
- Plate 1 (Demonstration): Everyone sees "12" — tests understanding of the task, not color vision
- Transformation plates (2–7): deficient patients see a different number
- Vanishing plates (8–13): only normal observers see the number
- Hidden digit plates (14–15): only color-deficient patients see the number
- Diagnostic plates (16–17): determine type and severity (protan vs. deutan)

| Plate 16 reading | Plate 17 reading | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| Normal sees: 26 | Normal sees: 42 | Normal |
| Protan sees: 6 | Protan cannot read | Strong protanomaly |
| Deutan sees: 2 | Deutan cannot read | Strong deuteranomaly |

**Hardy-Rand-Rittler (HRR) — 24 plates (first produced 1954):**
- Classifies ALL THREE deficiency types: protan, deutan, and tritan
- Unlike Ishihara, not limited to red-green defects
- Board hook: HRR = all 3 types; Ishihara = red-green only

**Arrangement Tests:**

| Test | Function |
|---|---|
| Farnsworth D15 Dichotomous | Separates strongly/moderately deficient from mildly deficient or normal |
| Lanthony Desaturated D15 | Harder than D15 (pale caps); detects subtle deficiencies; use only if D15 was passed |
| Farnsworth-Munsell 100 Hue | Most comprehensive; measures full color acuity; identifies type and axis of confusion |

**Lantern Tests (Vocational):**
- Simulate signal light recognition; high practical value
- Do NOT reveal type or severity of defect

| Test | Description |
|---|---|
| Holmes-Wright Lantern | 2 green, 2 red, 1 white light; shown in pairs at varying brightness |
| Farnsworth Lantern (Falant) | US military standard; designed to pass mild color deficiency |

**Anomaloscope — Most Accurate:**
- Distinguishes dichromats from anomalous trichromats; measures severity
- **Rayleigh match:** red + green mixture matched to yellow → tests protan/deutan axis
 - Protan uses MORE RED; Deutan uses MORE GREEN
- **Moreland match:** blue-green mixture → tests tritan defects

## 10. Predicting Reading Adds for Low Vision

Patients with low vision use shorter working distances. Three methods exist:

### Method 1: Kestenbaum's Rule

**Formula:** Add power = Reciprocal of distance VA (denominator/numerator)

**Example:** Distance VA = 20/200
- Reciprocal = 200/20 = 10
- Add power = **+10.00 D**

### Method 2: Reciprocal of Vision

**Formula:** (Denominator of distance VA ÷ Desired near VA denominator) × 2.50

**Example:** Distance VA = 20/200; Desired near = 20/50
- 200/50 × 2.50 = 4 × 2.50 = **+10.00 D**
- Reference: 40 cm standard → 2.50 D

### Method 3: Starting Near Visual Acuity (Most Accurate)

**Formula:**
```
Testing distance (m) = x
Letters read (M) Desired acuity (M)

New working distance (x) → Convert to diopters: D = 1/x
```

**Example:** Patient reads 4 M at 40 cm; goal is 1 M print
- 0.4/4 = x/1
- x = 0.4 ÷ 4 = 0.1 m
- D = 1/0.1 = **+10.00 D**

**Board hook:** Method 3 is most accurate because it uses near VA directly and requires no distance VA measurement.

---

### Reserved Acuity for Reading Tasks

| Reading task | Speed | Reserve needed | What this means |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spot reading | ~80 wpm | 1 line smaller than threshold | Can just barely read the target size |
| Fluent reading | ~160 wpm | 3 lines smaller | Target is 3 lines above threshold |
| Maximum reading | ~320 wpm | 5 lines smaller | Target is 5 lines above threshold |

## 11. Magnification

### Definition and Goal

Magnification: the process of increasing the apparent size of a target relative to its actual size.

**Goal: Bigger, Bolder, Brighter**

### General Magnification Formula

```
Magnification = Achieved VA (in M units)
 Goal VA (in M units)
```

**Example:** BCVA = 40 M; Need to read 10 M print
→ M = 40/10 = **4×**

---

### Type 1: Relative Size Magnification (RSM)

Object is made physically larger (large-print books, enlarged font).

```
RSM = S2 / S1
(S1 = original size; S2 = new size)
```

**Example:** Print enlarged from 2 M to 8 M → RSM = 8/2 = **4×**

---

### Type 2: Relative Distance Magnification (RDM) — "Approach Magnification"

Object moved closer to the eye; retinal image enlarges proportionally.

```
RDM = r / d
(r = reference/original working distance; d = new working distance)
```

**Example:** Book moved from 40 cm to 10 cm → RDM = 40/10 = **4×**

Practical for: young children (high amplitude of accommodation); myopes using unaided vision.
For most low vision patients: a high plus add is needed.

---

### Type 3: Angular Magnification (Optical Magnification)

Used when the object is too far away, too large to move, or too big to change in size. An optical system is interposed between the object and the eye.

**X notation (reference standard):**
- X = number of times the retinal image is enlarged
- For **spectacle magnifiers:** reference distance = **40 cm** → 1× = 2.50 D

| Distance (cm) | Magnification | Lens needed |
|---|---|---|
| 40 | 1× | +2.50 D |
| 20 | 2× | +5.00 D |
| 10 | 4× | +10.00 D |
| 5 | 8× | +20.00 D |

- For **hand and stand magnifiers:** reference distance = **25 cm** → 1× = 4.00 D

| Distance (cm) | Magnification | Lens needed |
|---|---|---|
| 25 | 1× | +4.00 D |
| 12.5 | 2× | +8.00 D |
| 6.25 | 4× | +16.00 D |
| 5 | 5× | +20.00 D |

**Higher magnification = shorter focal length = smaller field of view.**

---

### Type 4: Projection Magnification

Object enlarged by projecting it onto a screen (CCTV, smartphones, projectors).

```
PM = Size of screen image / Size of object
```

## 12. Telescopes

Telescopes improve distance vision for low vision patients.

**Key specifications:** magnification power and field of view.
**Inverse relationship:** ↑ Magnification → ↓ Field of view, and vice versa.
**Typical range:** 2× to 8× for low vision telescopes.
**All telescopes produce virtual images.**
**Structure:** Objective lens (always positive/convex) + Eyepiece lens (positive or negative).
**Tube length** = sum of focal lengths of objective and eyepiece lenses.

### Galilean vs Keplerian

| Feature | Galilean (Terrestrial) | Keplerian (Astronomical) |
|---|---|---|
| Objective lens | Convex (+) | Convex (+) |
| Eyepiece lens | Concave (−) | Convex (+) |
| Image | Erect (virtual) | Inverted (real) — needs prism |
| Weight | Lighter | Heavier |
| Cost | Less expensive | More expensive |
| Max magnification | Up to 4× | Up to 20× |
| Field of view | Smaller (brighter image) | Larger (less light transmission) |
| Design | Simpler | Complex (needs internal prism) |

### Telescope Magnification Formula

```
M = Fe / Fo

Where:
Fe = power of eyepiece (ocular) lens in diopters
Fo = power of objective lens in diopters

Focal length (f) = 100 / D (in centimeters)
```

**Galilean example:**
- Objective: +5.00 D, Eyepiece: −20.00 D
- M = 20/5 = **4×**
- Length = fo + fe = (100/5) + (100/−20) = 20 + (−5) = **15 cm**

**Keplerian example:**
- Objective: +4.00 D, Eyepiece: +15.00 D
- M = 15/4 = **3.75×**
- Length = 25 + 6.67 = **31.67 cm**

**Telescope with unknown power:**
```
M = Diameter of objective lens / Diameter of exit pupil
```

### Low Vision Telescope Notation (e.g., 4 × 12 12.5°)

- First number (4) = magnification
- Second number (12) = diameter of objective lens in mm
- Third value (12.5°) = field of view in degrees

### Criteria for Selecting a Telescope

- Magnification
- Transmission of light
- Field of view
- Patient's manual dexterity (tremor → spectacle-mounted)
- Cost
- Motivation of patient

### Determining Required Magnification

```
M = Achieved VA (denominator) / Required VA (denominator)
Example: VA = 6/60; Task requires 6/9 → M = 60/9 ≈ 6.7× → select 8× telescope
```

## 13. Low Vision Examination

### Process of Low Vision Assessment

1. Comprehensive Case History
2. Determining visual enhancement needs
3. Examination (refraction sequence + visual function tests + ocular health)
4. Determination of appropriate low vision devices
5. Magnification prescription
6. Rehabilitation plan and report

### Comprehensive Case History

- Ocular history and current status
- Health history and current status
- Developmental history
- Review of functional vision ability
- Educational history
- Functional task analysis
- Devices used in the past
- Working distance requirements
- Specific needs or goals

Sources: patient + family, employer, teacher, caregiver.

### Visual Function Tests in LV Exam

- Visual acuity (distance and near) — using M units
- Refraction (radical retinoscopy, bracketing)
- Binocular vision (motor assessment; stereopsis using Stereofly with Lea symbols)
- Contrast sensitivity (Pelli-Robson; Hiding Heidi; Bailey-Lovie Hi-Low; Lea Flip Chart)
- Glare testing (Brightness Acuity Testing — BAT)
 - Disability glare: reduction in visibility caused by intense light in the field of view
 - Discomfort glare: annoyance or pain from an overly bright source
- Ocular health examination (slit lamp; BIO; fundus camera)
- Visual field testing (Confrontation; Perimetry; Tangent screen; Amsler grid; Octopus)
- Color vision testing (Farnsworth D-15; color matching)

### Pre-requisites Before Prescribing LV Devices

- Patient seated comfortably
- Encourage eccentric viewing (where applicable)
- Material held at focal distance for clear view (especially with high add spectacles)
- Reading stand at focal distance (for hand and stand magnifiers)

## 14. Low Vision Aids

### Near Vision Devices

**Spectacle Magnifiers (Microscopic glasses):**
- Low powers: binocular use with base-in prisms
- High powers: monocular use only
- Advantages: hands-free; wider field of view; portable; sustained reading
- Disadvantages: fixed, close reading distance; binocularity impossible at high powers

**Telemicroscopes (Loupes):**
- Advantages: hands-free; greater working distance than spectacle magnifiers (patient can maintain good posture)
- Disadvantage: narrow field of view

**Hand Magnifiers:**
- Must be held at the focal length of the lens
- Advantages: variable working distance; inexpensive; portable; available with built-in lights; excellent for spot viewing
- Disadvantages: requires steady hands; decreases reading speed; difficult to maintain focus

**Stand Magnifiers:**
- Lens-to-object distance is fixed; patient moves the stand along text
- Advantages: fixed focal distance; helpful for poor motor control/tremor; available with built-in lights
- Useful for Parkinson's disease and cerebral palsy
- Disadvantages: narrow visual field; can block illumination; bulky

**Electronic Devices:**
- CCTV: high magnification; contrast reversal and enhancement; zoom; binocular image possible
 - Disadvantages: expensive; difficult to use; bulky
- TV readers: more affordable; limited, fixed magnification
- Pocket electronic magnifiers: popular with children and working-age adults; portable but limited magnification

### Distance Devices

- Handheld telescopes (short-term spotting)
- Spectacle-mounted telescopes (extended viewing; hands-free)
- Bioptics (spectacle-mounted superior telescope for mobility and driving)
- Electronic systems (head-mounted video; variable autofocus; not recommended for mobility)

### Field Expanders

For patients with contracted visual fields, hemianopsias, and scotomas:
- **Prisms** — expand peripheral field awareness
- **Mirrors** — redirect light from peripheral field
- **Reverse telescopes** — minify the image, widening the field
- **Minus lenses** — reduce image size to widen apparent field

### Non-Optical Devices

**Making things bigger:** larger font, large-print books, enlarged phone text

**Improving lighting:**
| Type | Properties |
|---|---|
| Fluorescent | Least expensive; good general illumination; scatters light |
| Incandescent | Most common; directional; can be dimmed; yellowish light; hot; costly to replace |
| Halogen | High intensity; directional; longer life than bulbs |
| Natural light | Free; cannot be controlled; glare and shadow problems |

**Glare reduction:** lamp shades; reposition lamp; thin curtains; blinds; hats/caps/visors; umbrellas; sunglasses and filters; typoscopes

**Contrast enhancement:**
- Black felt-tip pen on white paper
- White strips on dark stair edges
- Contrasting paint on doors, door frames, walls
- Contrasting chopping boards for food preparation
- Basic principle: choose colors that differ in saturation. Black and white = highest contrast. Avoid white/grey with other light colors.
- Devices: acetate overlays; highlighter pens; tinted lenses (yellow/orange); typoscopes

**Reducing visual clutter:** masking (typoscopes and writing guides); reduce objects in immediate area

**Sight substitution:** Braille; tactile devices; talking books and newspapers

**Mobility aids:** white canes

**Greater physical comfort:** appropriate posture; reading/writing stand; clipboard; standing lamp; proper chair/table

## 15. Conditions Causing Low Vision

### Diabetic Retinopathy (DR)

**Pathology:** Retinal vascular damage from chronic hyperglycemia; pericyte loss → microaneurysms → hard exudates → capillary non-perfusion → VEGF release → neovascularization → PDR.

**Leading cause of new blindness in 20–74 year olds (USA).**

**Duration correlation:**
| DM Type | Duration | Retinopathy status |
|---|---|---|
| Type 1 | > 10 years | 60% have some retinopathy |
| Type 1 | > 15 years | Virtually all have some DR; 25% progress to PDR |
| Type 1 | > 20 years | 50% have PDR |
| Type 2 | At diagnosis | 20% already have retinopathy |
| Type 2 | > 15 years | 60–80% have DR; up to 20% have PDR |

**Visual function affected:** scattered scotomas; reduced contrast sensitivity; reduced VA in advanced disease.

---

### Hypertensive Retinopathy (HR)

**Mechanism:** Persistent hypertension (> 140/90 for years) → arteriosclerosis; Hypertensive crisis (> 200/110) → acute retinal damage.

**Signs:**
- Copper-wiring: moderate arteriosclerosis; arterioles appear orange/yellow
- Silver-wiring: severe arteriosclerosis; arterioles appear white (occluded)
- AV nicking: indentation of veins by sclerotic arteries
 - Salus's sign: vein deflected at crossing
 - Gunn's sign: vein tapers on both sides of crossing
 - Bonnet's sign: vein banking distal to crossing
- Flame-shaped hemorrhages (superficial)
- Cotton-wool spots (microinfarctions from ischemia)
- Hard exudates (lipid residues from damaged capillaries)
- Optic disc edema/papilledema

**Keith-Wagner-Barker (KWB) Classification:**
| Grade | Features |
|---|---|
| 1 | Mild generalized constriction of retinal arterioles |
| 2 | Grade 1 + focal arteriolar narrowing + AV nicking |
| 3 | Grade 2 + flame hemorrhages + cotton-wool spots + hard exudates |
| 4 | Grade 3 (severe) + papilledema or retinal edema |

**Wong and Mitchell (2004):**
- None: no signs
- Mild: generalized arteriolar narrowing, focal narrowing, AV nicking, copper-wiring
- Moderate: hemorrhage, microaneurysm, cotton-wool spot, hard exudate, or combination

---

### Glaucoma in Low Vision

**Second leading cause of bilateral blindness worldwide.**

**Prevalence:** 57.5 million POAG; ~8.4 million bilaterally blind from glaucoma.

**Visual function affected:** restricted visual field (arcuate scotoma → nasal step → tunnel vision); decreased depth perception; reduced VA; blindness.

**Open-angle glaucoma:** chronic progressive decrease in aqueous outflow → elevated IOP → axonal compression and ischemia at optic nerve head.

**Angle-closure glaucoma:** peripheral iris blocks aqueous outreach to TM.
- With pupillary block: anatomic narrow angle (primary); or secondary (posterior synechiae, ectopia lentis, spherophakia)
- Without pupillary block: plateau iris (primary); rubeosis iridis/neovascular (secondary)

**Secondary OAG:** Pigmentary dispersion syndrome; pseudoexfoliation syndrome.

**Low vision considerations in glaucoma:** field expanders for contracted fields; training in eccentric viewing for central field loss; orientation and mobility training; lifestyle modifications.

---

### Retinitis Pigmentosa (RP)

**Most common inherited retinal dystrophy.** Rod-cone dystrophy — rods die first.

**Prevalence:** ~1:3,000–5,000; carriers ~1:100.

**Inheritance patterns:**
- Autosomal recessive (most common; onset teenage years)
- Autosomal dominant (onset early adulthood / 20s)
- X-linked
- Mitochondrial

**Syndromic associations:**
- **Usher syndrome:** RP + hearing loss; most common cause of deaf-blindness in the developed world
- **Bardet-Biedl syndrome:** RP + hearing loss + additional systemic features

**Classic hallmarks:**
1. Nyctalopia (night blindness) — earliest symptom
2. Bone spicule pigmentation (RPE cells migrate to perivascular areas after rod death → melanin deposits around vessels)
3. Arterial narrowing
4. Optic disc pallor
5. Reduced peripheral visual field (ring scotoma → tunnel vision)
6. Photopsia; glare; difficulty dark/light adapting

**ERG:** b-wave attenuation (photoreceptor-to-bipolar signal lost).

**Management:**
- Optimize remaining vision first
- Low vision assessment and aids
- Filters/sunglasses (for photopsia and glare)
- Large dose Vitamin A supplements discouraged (minimal benefit; worsens ABCA4-type RP)
- Gene therapy: **Luxturna (voretigene neparvovec)** — AAV vector containing correct RPE65 gene; FDA-approved January 2018; subretinal injection; for biallelic RPE65 mutation in patients > 1 year old
- Argus II bionic retinal prosthesis

---

### Achromatopsia

**Complete achromatopsia (rod monochromacy):**
- All cone types absent or non-functional
- Prevalence: ~0.003% (1/30,000)
- Autosomal recessive; equal sex prevalence
- Chromosomes 1, 2, 8, 10 affected (CNGA3, CNGB3, GNAT2, PDE6C genes)

**Incomplete achromatopsia (cone monochromacy):**
- Single cone type only; VA may be partially preserved (~20/60–20/200)
- Blue-cone monochromacy (most common incomplete form): M and L cones absent; S cones only; X-linked; 1/100,000

**Cerebral achromatopsia:** damage to Visual Area V4 (inferior-temporal extrastriate cortex).

**Symptoms:**
- Complete inability to distinguish colors
- Reduced VA (complete type); may be preserved in incomplete
- Hemeralopia (severe light/photosensitivity)
- Nystagmus
- Central scotoma

**Management:** Deep red filters/spectacles; magnifiers; gene therapy (early phase).

---

### Albinism

**Definition:** Inherited disorder resulting in little or no melanin production.

**Melanin** — natural pigment from tyrosine via melanocytes. Absorbs UV rays; protects cells.

**Types:**
- **Oculocutaneous albinism (OCA):** most common; affects skin, hair, and eyes; autosomal recessive
- **Ocular albinism (OA):** rarer; mainly eyes affected; X-linked (males affected; females are carriers)

**Types of OCA:**
- OCA1: tyrosinase deficiency (OCA1a = no melanin at all; OCA1b = some melanin)
- OCA2: OCA2 gene mutation; most common worldwide

**Symptoms:**
- Reduced VA (20/60–20/400); legally blind in severe cases
- Low contrast sensitivity
- Photophobia
- Nystagmus
- Foveal hypoplasia (lack of full foveal morphology development)
- Strabismus
- Poor depth perception
- Optic nerve misrouting (abnormal decussation at chiasma)

**Pupil color:** Reddish (due to lack of melanin in RPE — choroidal vessels visible through transparent iris/fundus)

**Why low vision occurs in albinism:** Melanin is absent in the RPE → light impinges on the retina and reflects in all directions → excites many receptors non-specifically → low vision; no UV protection for RPE cells.

**Management:**
- No cure; manage symptoms
- Comprehensive low vision exam
- Spectacles/CLs for refractive error
- Polarized sunglasses / tinted CLs for photophobia
- Prism spectacles / surgery for nystagmus
- Vision therapy / surgery for strabismus
- Not progressive — vision does not worsen over time

---

### Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP)

**Definition:** Potentially blinding disorder from abnormal retinal blood vessel growth in premature infants.

**Risk factors:**
- Premature birth: < 31 weeks gestation
- Low birth weight: < 1,500 g (3.3 lbs)
- High oxygen exposure during neonatal care
- Ethnicity: Asian and Black infants at higher risk

**Prevalence:** ~14% of all preterm infants develop ROP worldwide.

**Zones (where ROP develops):**
- Zone 1: radius = 2× macula-to-disc distance (most posterior, most severe)
- Zone 2: surrounds Zone 1; extends to ora serrata nasally
- Zone 3: crescent-shaped temporal retina (most anterior, mildest)

**Stages:**

| Stage | Finding |
|---|---|
| 1 | Demarcation line (white line between vascular and avascular retina) |
| 2 | Ridge (line becomes elevated with height and width) |
| 3 | Ridge with neovascularization (blood vessels proliferate into ridge) |
| 4 | Subtotal retinal detachment |
| 5 | Total retinal detachment — usually no treatment possible |

**Plus disease:** Retinal arterioles and venules near optic disc are dilated and tortuous; vitreous haze; poor pupillary dilation. Present at Stage 3 = treatment threshold.

**Aggressive posterior ROP:** Zone 1; flat neovascularization; plus disease always present; progresses rapidly to RD — treat within 48 hours.

**Symptoms of LV from ROP:** Reduced VA; diplopia; poor depth perception; low contrast sensitivity.

**Management:**
- Stages 1–2: observation (may resolve spontaneously)
- Laser photocoagulation (ablates abnormal vessels)
- Cryotherapy (peripheral retina)
- Anti-VEGF injections (intravitreal)
- Scleral buckling (traction relief)
- Vitrectomy (advanced RD)

---

### Anatomical Aging Changes and Low Vision

**Eyelids:**
- Loss of skin elasticity → blepharoptosis, ectropion, entropion
- Lid masses: xanthelasma (cholesterol deposits; yellow); seborrheic keratosis; squamous cell papilloma (most common benign lid lesion — fibroepithelial polyp/skin tag)

**Lacrimal system:**
- Decreased lipid (MGD), aqueous (lacrimal gland), and mucin (goblet cell) production
- Loose lids and conjunctival tissue affect tear spreading and drainage → dry eye

**Conjunctiva:**
- Thinning of epithelium (decreased cell proliferation; reduced goblet cell density; oxidative stress)
- Decreased elasticity
- Pinguecula and pterygium
- Conjunctival primary acquired melanosis (PAM): painless, flat, patchy conjunctival pigmentation; most cases benign but rare cases progress to melanoma

**Sclera:**
- Scleral thinning (decreased collagen content)
- Senile scleral plaque: slate-grey, oblong areas posterior to the limbus; uvea becomes visible through hyaline degeneration

**Cornea:**
- Corneal thinning; changing curvature → varying astigmatism
- Decreased corneal sensitivity
- **Arcus senilis (gerontoxon):** white/grey/bluish ring at corneal periphery; lipid deposits in stroma; most common peripheral corneal opacity; starts superior and inferior, progresses to complete ring
- **Limbal girdle of Vogt:** chalky-white crescentic deposits at nasal and temporal perilimbal cornea; elastotic degeneration
- **Hudson-Stahli line:** horizontal iron deposit in epithelium at junction of upper 2/3 and lower 1/3 of cornea
- **Hassel-Henle bodies:** endothelial guttae at the periphery; normal aging

**Lens and accommodation:**
- Lens hardens progressively → loss of elasticity → **presbyopia**
- Nuclear sclerosis → increased nucleus refractive index → acquired myopia (second sight/senopia)
- Lens thickens → ACD decreases → narrow angle risk (PACG)

**Retina/Optic nerve:**
- RNFL thins ~20–30% over lifetime
- Visual field sensitivity decreases peripherally
- Drusen accumulation in Bruch's membrane → AMD risk

## 16. High-Yield Cheat Sheet

### Definitions Fast Facts

| Term | Key Value |
|---|---|
| WHO LV cutoff | VA < 20/70 (6/18) in better eye after best correction |
| WHO blindness cutoff | VA < 20/400 (3/60) or VF < 5° |
| Legal blindness (PH) | VA ≤ 20/400 or VF < 5° |
| Legal blindness (US) | VA < 20/200 or VF ≤ 20° |
| ICD-11 moderate | VA worse than 6/18 to 6/60 |
| ICD-11 blindness | VA worse than 3/60 |
| Near vision impairment | Worse than N6 or M0.8 at 40 cm |

### Magnification Formula Summary

| Formula | Use |
|---|---|
| M = Achieved / Goal | General magnification needed |
| RSM = S2 / S1 | Relative size (object made larger) |
| RDM = r / d | Relative distance (object moved closer) |
| Telescope: M = Fe / Fo | Telescope magnification |
| Length = fo + fe | Telescope tube length |
| Kestenbaum: Add = D/N | Reciprocal of distance VA |
| Reciprocal of vision: (D2/D1) × 2.50 | Distance VA + desired near |

### Contrast Sensitivity Tests Summary

| Test | Distance | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Pelli-Robson | 1 m | Single letter size (20/60); log CS score; normal ≥ 1.80 |
| Bailey-Lovie | 6 m | logMAR format; 100% and 10% contrast |
| VCTS (Vistech) | 3 m / close | Gold standard; 5 spatial frequencies; orientation task |
| FACT | 10 feet | 5 SF × 9 contrast levels; plots full CSF |
| Lea Paddles | 0.5–1 m | Infants; preferential looking; 6 grating levels |
| Hiding Heidi | Communication distance | Infants; face contrast cards; 6 contrast levels |
| Cambridge Low Contrast | 6 m | Screening; 12 plate pairs; 4 series |

### Color Vision Test Summary

| Test | Detects | Unique Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Ishihara | Red-green only | Plate 1: everyone reads 12; plate 14–15: hidden digits |
| HRR | All 3 types | 24 plates; classifies protan, deutan, tritan |

| Lanthony D15 | Subtle deficiency | Desaturated caps; harder than D15 |

| Feature | Galilean | Keplerian |
|---|---|---|
| Eyepiece | Concave (−) | Convex (+) |
| Image | Erect (virtual) | Inverted — needs prism |
| Max power | Up to 4× | Up to 20× |

### LV Device Selection Guide

| Patient situation | Best device |
|---|---|
| Sustained reading; hands-free | Spectacle magnifier |
| Short-term spot reading | Hand magnifier |

| Need greater working distance near | Telemicroscope |
| Distance spotting (TV, boards) | Monocular telescope |
| Severe LV; contrast control needed | CCTV/electronic magnifier |

| Hemianopsia | Prism in glasses |

## 17. Practice Questions

**1.** A patient with VA of 20/400 needs to read 20/50 print. Using the reciprocal of vision method, what reading add is needed?
- A. +5.00 D
- B. +10.00 D
- C. +15.00 D
- D. +20.00 D

**Answer: D.** (400/50) × 2.50 = 8 × 2.50 = +20.00 D.

---

**2.** A patient reads 6 M at 40 cm. They need to read 1 M print (telephone directory). What is the required reading add?
- A. +10.00 D
- B. +12.00 D
- C. +15.00 D
- D. +20.00 D

**Answer: C.** Using starting NVA: 0.40/6 = x/1 → x = 0.40/6 = 0.067 m → D = 1/0.067 = +15.00 D.

---

**3.** A Galilean telescope has an objective lens of +8.00 D and an eyepiece of −24.00 D. What is the magnification and length of the telescope?
- A. 3×; 12.5 cm - B. 3×; 8.33 cm - C. 4×; 16.5 cm - D. 3×; 16.5 cm

**Answer: B.** M = 24/8 = 3×. fo = 100/8 = 12.5 cm. fe = 100/−24 = −4.17 cm. Length = 12.5 + (−4.17) = 8.33 cm.

---

**4.** A child presents with complete inability to distinguish colors, nystagmus, reduced VA, central scotoma, and hemeralopia. The family history is negative in the maternal line but both parents are phenotypically unaffected. What is the most likely diagnosis and inheritance pattern?
- A. Deuteranopia — X-linked recessive
- B. Blue-cone monochromacy — X-linked recessive
- C. Complete achromatopsia — autosomal recessive
- D. Retinitis pigmentosa — autosomal dominant

**Answer: C.** Complete achromatopsia (rod monochromacy) is autosomal recessive with equal sex prevalence. Both unaffected parents can be carriers. Blue-cone monochromacy is X-linked (males only clinically affected).

---

**5.** The VCTS/Vistech is described as the gold standard contrast sensitivity test. It uses sine-wave gratings and tests which of the following spatial frequency range?
- A. 1, 3, 6, 12, 18 cycles/degree
- B. 0.5, 1, 3, 6, 12 cycles/degree
- C. 3, 6, 12, 18, 24 cycles/degree
- D. 1.5, 3, 6, 12, 18 cycles/degree

**Answer: D.** The VCTS tests 5 spatial frequencies: 1.5, 3, 6, 12, and 18 cycles per degree. Gratings are presented in 5 rows × 9 columns, with contrast decreasing left to right.

---

**6.** On the Ishihara test, a patient reads "6" on plate 16 and cannot read plate 17. A colleague reads "26" on plate 16 and "42" on plate 17. What is the patient's diagnosis?
- A. Strong deuteranomaly
- B. Mild protanomaly
- C. Strong protanomaly
- D. Deuteranopia

**Answer: C.** Plate 16: Normal = 26; Protan = 6; Deutan = 2. Seeing only "6" = strong protan defect. The Ishihara cannot definitively distinguish anomalous trichromacy from dichromacy — that requires the anomaloscope.
