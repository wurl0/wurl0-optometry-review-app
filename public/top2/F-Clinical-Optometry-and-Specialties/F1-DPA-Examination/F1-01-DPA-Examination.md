## F1 — Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agents (DPA)
*Personal study notes | TOS: F.1 Clinical Optometry | 25 items*

---

## The Simple Version

DPAs are drugs that optometrists are legally authorized to use under RA 8050 to examine the eye more thoroughly. They dilate the pupil, paralyze accommodation, stain the cornea, numb the surface, or constrict the pupil — depending on which agent you use and why. Five agents are approved. Each one has a specific purpose, a receptor mechanism, and a set of conditions where you must not use it.

**Teaching angle:** Ask: "If DPAs are just eye drops, why do we need a law to authorize their use?" Because these drugs affect the autonomic nervous system and can cause systemic reactions — they are pharmaceutical agents with real risks, not just saline.

---

## But Why?

**Why does tropicamide dilate the pupil?**
Because it blocks the M3 muscarinic receptor on the sphincter pupillae.

**Why does blocking M3 cause dilation?**
Because the sphincter is tonically innervated by the parasympathetic system (CN3 → ciliary ganglion → short ciliary nerves → M3). Block M3 → sphincter stops contracting → dilator pupillae (sympathetically innervated, unopposed) pulls the iris open.

**Why does cycloplegia accompany some mydriatics?**
The ciliary muscle that controls accommodation is also innervated by M3. The same muscarinic blockade that dilates the pupil also paralyzes accommodation — the ciliary muscle relaxes, zonules tighten, lens flattens, and accommodation is abolished.

**Why does phenylephrine dilate without cycloplegia?**
Phenylephrine is an α1 agonist — it stimulates the dilator pupillae directly (α1 receptor) without touching the parasympathetic pathway. The ciliary muscle has no α1 receptors; it is purely under parasympathetic (M3) control. So phenylephrine produces mydriasis with zero effect on accommodation.

## The Details

### Terminology Unpacked

| Term | Roots / Meaning | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| Mydriasis | Greek: *mydros* = darkness (dilated pupil) | Pupil > 6 mm; produced by sympathomimetics or parasympatholytics |
| Cycloplegia | Greek: *kyklos* = circle (lens) + *plege* = stroke/paralysis | Paralysis of the ciliary muscle → loss of accommodation |
| Miosis | Greek: *meioun* = to lessen | Pupil constriction; M3 stimulation or α1 blockade |
| DPA | Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agent | Authorized under RA 8050; for examination use by optometrists |

### The Five Approved DPAs

#### A — Mydriatic WITHOUT Cycloplegia

**1. Phenylephrine 2.5%**
- Class: α1-adrenergic agonist (sympathomimetic)
- Receptor: α1 on dilator pupillae
- Action: contracts dilator → mydriasis; no effect on ciliary muscle (no cycloplegia)
- Onset: 15–20 minutes; duration 4–6 hours
- Use: dilation for fundus exam when accommodation must be preserved (e.g., elderly, glaucoma suspect)
- Contraindication: narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma (dilation can push iris against the trabecular meshwork, blocking aqueous outflow → acute angle-closure crisis)
- Systemic risk: hypertension, tachycardia (α1 systemic vasoconstriction) — use 2.5%, not 10%

#### B — Mydriatics WITH Cycloplegia

**2. Tropicamide 0.5% / 1%**
- Class: anticholinergic (competitive muscarinic antagonist)
- Receptor: M3 on sphincter pupillae AND ciliary muscle
- Action: blocks M3 → sphincter relaxes → mydriasis; ciliary muscle relaxes → cycloplegia
- Onset: 20–30 minutes; duration 4–6 hours
- The **preferred DPA in pregnancy** (FDA Category C, but tropicamide is chosen because of shortest duration and least systemic absorption among cycloplegics)
- Use: dilated fundus exam, cycloplegic refraction (especially in pseudomyopia)
- Contraindication: same as phenylephrine — narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma

**3. Cyclopentolate 0.5% / 1% / 2%**
- Class: anticholinergic
- Receptor: M3
- Stronger and longer cycloplegia than tropicamide
- Onset: 30–60 minutes; duration 12–24 hours
- Use: cycloplegic refraction in children (more reliable cycloplegia needed); suspected pseudomyopia
- Contraindication: narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma
- Pediatric caution: can cause CNS effects in young children (hallucinations, behavioral changes) — more common with 2% in infants; monitor for 30 minutes post-instillation

#### C — Local Anesthetic Agents

**4. Proparacaine 0.5%**
- Class: ester-type local anesthetic
- Mechanism: blocks sodium channels on corneal sensory nerve endings → topical anesthesia
- Onset: 30 seconds; duration ~15 minutes
- Use: tonometry, foreign body removal, corneal staining, contact lens fitting procedures requiring manipulation
- **Never prescribed for home use** — chronic use prevents pain sensation → patients unknowingly damage the cornea (neurotrophic keratopathy)
- Alternative esters: oxybuprocaine, benoxinate (0.4%)

#### D — Diagnostic Dye

**5. Fluorescein strips**
- Class: xanthene dye
- Mechanism: fluoresces green-yellow under cobalt blue light (465 nm) — accumulates where epithelial cells are missing
- Use: corneal abrasion detection, contact lens fluorescein pattern assessment, applanation tonometry (with proparacaine)
- Strips preferred over drops (less contamination risk)
- **Do not use with soft CLs in place** — fluorescein permanently stains hydrogel CLs; must remove CLs first

### The DPA Clinical Examination Workflow

```
Step 1: Take full history
 — Confirm no contraindications (narrow angle, allergy, systemic disease)

Step 2: Baseline assessment BEFORE instillation
 — Anterior chamber depth (oblique flashlight / Van Herick method)
 — Undilated fundus check if possible
 — Document pupil size and reactivity

Step 3: Instill DPA
 — Lower conjunctival fornix
 — Tip does not touch eye or skin
 — Apply nasolacrimal occlusion (NLO) immediately after instillation

Step 4: Nasolacrimal Occlusion (NLO)
 — Press the inner corner of the eye (lacrimal sac area) for 2 minutes
 — Prevents the drug from draining into the nasolacrimal duct and reaching systemic circulation
 — Most important step for minimizing systemic side effects

Step 5: Wait for onset
 — Tropicamide: 20–30 min
 — Cyclopentolate: 30–60 min
 — Phenylephrine: 15–20 min

Step 6: Confirm dilation and perform examination

Step 7: Document
 — Drug used, concentration, time instilled, eye(s) treated, patient response
 — Inform patient: blurred near vision and photosensitivity until drug wears off
 — Never drive immediately after dilation
```

### Contraindications Summary

| Condition | Contraindicated DPA | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Narrow angle / angle-closure glaucoma | Phenylephrine, tropicamide, cyclopentolate (all mydriatics) | Dilation pushes iris against TM → acute angle-closure |
| Pregnancy | All DPAs are FDA Category C | Insufficient safety data; use only when benefit > risk |
| Breastfeeding | Especially atropine; tropicamide preferred if must dilate | Systemic absorption excreted in breast milk |
| Allergy to ester anesthetics | Proparacaine, oxybuprocaine, benoxinate | Cross-reactivity within ester class |
| Children < 3 months | Cyclopentolate 2% — use 0.5% instead | CNS toxicity risk higher in neonates |
| Soft CLs in place | Fluorescein | Permanently stains hydrogel material |

---

### DPA in Pregnancy (Gap Lecture 08 Summary)

- All approved DPAs are **FDA Category C** in pregnancy (risk cannot be ruled out)
- If dilation is clinically necessary during pregnancy: **tropicamide is the preferred agent**
 - Rationale: shortest duration of action (4–6 hours vs. 24 hours for cyclopentolate); least systemic exposure; most data available
- Always apply NLO technique to minimize systemic absorption
- Breastfeeding: advise patient to pump and discard for 24 hours after cyclopentolate; tropicamide shorter clearance

## Board Traps

- **Trap:** Tropicamide causes mydriasis only — Wrong. It also causes cycloplegia (it is M3 antagonist). Phenylephrine causes mydriasis only.
- **Trap:** The strongest cycloplegic for children is tropicamide — Wrong. Cyclopentolate has stronger, more reliable cycloplegia; atropine is stronger still but not an approved DPA.
- **Trap:** All DPAs are contraindicated in pregnancy — Wrong. They are Category C (risk not ruled out), but if clinically necessary, **tropicamide is the preferred choice**, not all are absolutely contraindicated.
- **Trap:** Fluorescein can be used with soft CLs in place — Wrong. Remove soft CLs before applying fluorescein; they stain permanently.
- **Trap:** NLO prevents the drug from working — Wrong. NLO prevents systemic drainage; the drug still works locally on the eye.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A 32-year-old pregnant patient needs a dilated fundus exam. Which DPA is most appropriate?

- A. Cyclopentolate 1%
- B. Phenylephrine 10%
- C. Tropicamide 0.5%
- D. Proparacaine 0.5%

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Tropicamide 0.5%.** All DPAs are FDA Category C in pregnancy. When dilation is clinically necessary, tropicamide is the preferred agent because it has the shortest duration of action and least systemic exposure. Phenylephrine 10% is avoided due to its systemic cardiovascular risks. Cyclopentolate has a longer duration and more systemic exposure. Proparacaine is a local anesthetic, not a mydriatic.

</details>

---

**2.** Which of the following best explains why phenylephrine produces mydriasis without cycloplegia?

- A. It acts on M3 receptors selectively on the sphincter pupillae only
- B. It acts on α1 receptors on the dilator pupillae; the ciliary muscle has no α1 receptors
- C. It blocks acetylcholinesterase, preventing ACh from contracting the ciliary muscle
- D. It directly relaxes the ciliary muscle through a non-receptor mechanism

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Phenylephrine is an α1 agonist that stimulates the dilator pupillae. The ciliary muscle is under purely parasympathetic (M3) control — it has no α1 receptors. Therefore, phenylephrine produces mydriasis without any effect on accommodation. Options A and D are incorrect mechanisms; option C describes indirect cholinomimetics, not phenylephrine.

</details>
