F1 — Diagnostic Pharmaceutical Agents (DPA)

Personal study notes · Subject F, Clinical Optometry · read the refresher, then test yourself

▶ Read the refresher first (2 minutes)
  • 5 approved DPAs (PTCPF): Phenylephrine, Tropicamide, Cyclopentolate, Proparacaine, Fluorescein.
  • Phenylephrine 2.5% is an α1 agonist on the dilator. It dilates with no cycloplegia, because the ciliary muscle has no α1 receptors (it is pure M3).
  • Tropicamide and Cyclopentolate are M3 antagonists. They block the sphincter and the ciliary muscle, so they give both mydriasis and cycloplegia.
  • Cyclopentolate gives stronger, longer cycloplegia (12 to 24 h) and is the go-to for pediatric cycloplegic refraction. Tropicamide is shorter (4 to 6 h).
  • Preferred in pregnancy: tropicamide, because it has the shortest duration and least systemic exposure. All DPAs are FDA Category C.
  • Proparacaine 0.5% blocks Na+ channels on corneal sensory nerves. Never prescribe for home use: it masks pain and the cornea gets damaged unnoticed.
  • Fluorescein fluoresces under cobalt blue (465 nm). Never use with soft contact lenses in place, it stains them permanently.
  • NLO (nasolacrimal occlusion): press the inner corner for 2 minutes after instilling. It blocks systemic drainage, the single most important safety step.
  • Absolute contraindication for all mydriatics: narrow or angle-closure glaucoma. Dilation pushes the iris into the trabecular meshwork and can trigger an acute attack.
